A CONNECTICUT YANKEE IN KING ARTHUR'S COURT

A BedrockELA Adaptation

Based on the novel by Mark Twain (1889)

Adapted for 6th Grade Readers



CHAPTER 1

THE STRANGER'S TALE

I met the stranger at Warwick Castle in England.

He was a quiet, weathered American with gray hair and sharp eyes that seemed to be looking at something far away. The other tourists wandered through the ancient halls, admiring the armor and tapestries, but this man stood apart, studying a suit of chain mail with an expression I couldn't read — sadness, maybe, or recognition.

We fell into conversation. I mentioned that I was a writer, interested in old legends. His eyes flickered.

"Legends," he said. "I could tell you a story that would make every legend you've ever heard seem tame."

We sat by the fire that evening, and over a glass of whiskey, he told me his tale. His name was Hank Morgan, and he was from Hartford, Connecticut. He had been a foreman at the Colt Arms Factory — a practical, hardheaded Yankee who knew how to build anything, fix anything, and manage anything. He believed in science, progress, democracy, and common sense.

And he had spent several years living in the court of King Arthur.

Not a dream. Not a fantasy. He had actually been there — in sixth-century England, among knights and castles and sorcerers — and he had tried to change the world.

He reached into his coat and pulled out a small, yellowed manuscript — hundreds of pages, written in his own hand. "Read this," he said. "It's all true."

I took the manuscript to my room and began to read. What follows is Hank Morgan's story, in his own words.



CHAPTER 2

KNOCKED OUT

My name is Hank Morgan, and in 1879, I was the head superintendent at the Colt Arms Factory in Hartford, Connecticut. I knew everything there was to know about machinery, manufacturing, and modern technology. I could build a steam engine from scrap metal. I could wire a telegraph. I could make gunpowder, cement, and glass. I was a practical man in a practical age.

One day, I got into an argument with a worker named Hercules — a big, stupid fellow with fists like sledgehammers. The argument turned into a fight. Hercules hit me on the head with a crowbar.

I went down. The world went black.

When I woke up, I was lying in a field of grass under an oak tree. The sun was shining. Birds were singing. A man in a full suit of armor was sitting on a horse about twenty yards away, staring at me through the slit in his helmet.

"Fair sir," the knight said, "will you joust?"

I was wearing my factory clothes — work boots, denim trousers, a flannel shirt. I had no horse, no weapon, and no idea what was happening.

"Joust?" I said. "What year is this?"

"It is the nineteenth of June, in the year of our Lord 528."

He leveled his lance at my chest and charged. I ran. He chased me. Eventually he cornered me, his lance point an inch from my throat.

"Yield," he said.

I yielded. He tied my hands, put a rope around my neck, and led me like a dog down a dirt road toward a cluster of towers in the distance.

The towers belonged to Camelot.



CHAPTER 3

CAMELOT

Camelot was not what the stories made it sound like.

The castle was impressive enough — massive stone walls, towers, a great hall that could hold hundreds. But the town around it was a filthy, miserable collection of mud huts and open sewers. The people were dirty, ignorant, and afraid. They dressed in rags and lived worse than the pigs back in Hartford.

I was dragged into the great hall, where King Arthur sat on his throne, surrounded by his Knights of the Round Table. Arthur was a decent-looking man — big, blond, with an honest face. Merlin was there too — a skinny old man with a long beard who immediately began telling a story about Arthur pulling a sword from a stone. The knights looked bored.

The knight who had captured me — Sir Kay — announced that I was a dangerous foreign enchanter. Merlin studied me with suspicious eyes and agreed.

"He must burn," Merlin declared. "At noon tomorrow."

They stripped me to my underwear and threw me into a dungeon. Through a tiny window, I could see the courtyard where they were building the fire that would burn me alive in twelve hours.

Then I remembered something. A date. On June 21, 528 AD, there had been a total solar eclipse.

Tomorrow was June 21st.



CHAPTER 4

THE ECLIPSE

A boy came to my cell that night — a young page named Clarence, skinny and nervous, with big eyes and a quick mind. He had been sent to gawk at the prisoner, but I saw something useful in him: curiosity.

"What if I told you," I said, "that I am the greatest magician in the world? Tomorrow at noon, when they light the fire, I will blot out the sun."

Clarence ran to tell the court. The next day, they led me to the stake. The whole court was assembled — Arthur, Merlin, the knights, hundreds of common people.

I raised my arms and shouted: "I call upon the powers of darkness! Let the sun be destroyed!"

Nothing happened. Merlin laughed.

Then the edge of the moon began to creep across the face of the sun.

The light dimmed. Stars appeared in the middle of the day. People fell to their knees, weeping, praying, begging for mercy.

King Arthur rushed forward. "Name your terms! Whatever you want — just bring back the sun!"

"I want to be your chief minister. Second only to you. And I want Merlin locked up."

"Done! Anything!"

At the perfect moment, I raised my arms again. "Let the sun return!" The eclipse passed. Sunlight flooded back. The crowd erupted.

In five minutes, I had gone from a condemned prisoner to the most powerful person in England after the King. They called me "The Boss."



CHAPTER 5

THE BOSS

Within a week, I was installed in the finest rooms in Camelot, with servants, guards, and more power than I knew what to do with. Arthur trusted me completely.

I was an engineer in a world that hadn't invented the wheel yet. I knew things that wouldn't be discovered for another thousand years: electricity, printing, metallurgy, plumbing, democratic government, basic medicine.

The question was: what should I do with all of this knowledge?

The answer, to me, was obvious. I would civilize England. I would drag it out of the Dark Ages and into the modern world. I would build factories, schools, newspapers, and railroads. I would abolish slavery, establish equal rights, and create a republic. I would do in a few years what history took thirteen centuries to accomplish.

It never occurred to me that this might be arrogant. Or dangerous. Or impossible.

Clarence became my right-hand man. Together, we began what I privately called "the project": the secret transformation of England.

"The first thing we need," I told Clarence, "is a school. You can't build a civilization without education."

"What's a school?" Clarence asked.

We had a lot of work to do.



CHAPTER 6

THE FIRST REFORMS

I started in secret. In hidden valleys and remote corners of the kingdom, I built factories producing nails, wire, glass, and soap. I recruited smart young people and trained them as engineers, teachers, and craftsmen. I set up schools where they learned reading, writing, mathematics, and basic science.

I strung telegraph lines through the forests, disguised as vines. I built a printing press and started producing books — the first printed books in England, a thousand years before Gutenberg.

All of this was done quietly, away from the eyes of the knights, the nobles, and especially the Church. The Church was the most powerful institution in the land, and it would not take kindly to someone teaching people to think for themselves.

Meanwhile, I played the role expected of me: court magician, royal advisor, Arthur's right-hand man. I attended feasts, watched tournaments, and pretended to care about which knight had defeated which other knight in single combat.

The knights bored me. They were brave, certainly, but their entire lives revolved around fighting each other for honor that meant nothing. They couldn't read. They couldn't count. They believed everything Merlin told them.

But the peasants — the common people — were the ones who broke my heart. They lived in hovels, taxed into starvation by lords and the Church. They had no rights, no education, no hope. They accepted their suffering as God's will because no one had ever told them it could be different.

I was going to tell them.



CHAPTER 7

MORGAN LE FAY

Arthur sent me on a tour of the kingdom with a young woman named Sandy — brave, kind, and absolutely convinced the world worked on magic. We disagreed about everything, which made the journey both exhausting and interesting.

Our first stop was the castle of Morgan le Fay — Arthur's half-sister, a queen with absolute power and a reputation for cruelty. Inside, it was a house of horrors.

Morgan le Fay had dungeons full of prisoners — people locked up for years for offenses so minor they would be laughable in Hartford. A man imprisoned for twenty years for bumping into a nobleman's horse. A family who had forgotten what the sun looked like.

I freed them all. Morgan le Fay was furious but afraid of "The Boss."

Later, on the road, we found a hut where a woman and her daughter were dying of smallpox. No medicine. No doctor. Nothing I could do. I sat with them. Sandy sat with me. The mother died that night. The daughter died the next morning.

I buried them myself and stood over the graves thinking: I can build a steam engine, but I can't save a mother from a disease that, in my own time, had been nearly eliminated by a simple vaccine.

Sandy touched my arm. "You cannot save everyone, Boss."

"I know. But I can try to build a world where fewer people die like this."

That night I wrote plans for a medical school and sanitation system. The dungeons showed me the cruelty of the system. The smallpox hut showed me its cost. Together, they hardened my resolve: this world had to change.



CHAPTER 8

THE TOURNAMENT

Back at Camelot, I was challenged to a tournament by Sir Sagramor. In the medieval world, this meant a joust: two men in armor, on horseback, charging with lances.

I didn't own armor. I didn't know how to ride a war horse. So I improvised.

I rode into the lists wearing ordinary clothes, sitting on a regular horse, carrying a lasso. The crowd was stunned.

Sir Sagramor charged. I dodged. As he swept past, I threw the lasso, caught him around the shoulders, and yanked him off his horse. He crashed to the ground in a spectacular clatter of armor.

One by one, other knights challenged me. One by one, I lassoed them out of their saddles. The great champions of Camelot were beaten by a factory foreman with a rope.

Merlin watched from the stands, his face dark with fury. He whispered to the knights. He whispered to the Church. He was planting seeds.

But for now, I was invincible. The Boss had beaten the best knights in England with common sense and a length of rope.



CHAPTER 9

THE HOLY FOUNTAIN

The monks at the Valley of Holiness had a problem: their holy fountain had stopped flowing. Merlin had been trying to fix it for days with spells. They sent for me.

I found the problem immediately: a leaking underground pipe. Basic plumbing. But I couldn't just fix a pipe — I needed people to see magic.

So I spent two days building suspense. I had Clarence set up fireworks. I dressed in dramatic robes. I chanted nonsense. Then, at the climactic moment, I secretly opened the repaired pipe. Water burst forth. Fireworks blazed. The crowd fell to their knees.

My reputation soared. Merlin's crumbled. But I felt uncomfortable. I had beaten Merlin by being a better showman, not a better man. I had manipulated people's ignorance instead of curing it.

That was supposed to be Merlin's job, not mine.

Later, I demolished Merlin's tower with dynamite — a spectacular explosion that destroyed his last symbol of power. The old wizard stood in the rubble with hatred in his eyes. Not just defeat. Deep, patient, burning hatred.

I should have paid more attention to that look.



CHAPTER 10

THE YANKEE AND THE KING

I decided Arthur needed to see his own kingdom — not from a war horse but from the ground, as a common man.

We dressed in rough clothing and set out on foot. Arthur was terrible at being a peasant. He walked like a king — shoulders back, chin up, eyes commanding.

"You have to slump," I said. "Look at the ground. Act like the world has beaten you down."

"But the world hasn't beaten me down."

"It has beaten them down. That's the point."

Slowly, Arthur began to see. He saw villages where families owed half their food to lords who never visited. He saw children working in fields. He saw a man whipped for hunting a rabbit on his own family's land.

"Why does he not appeal to his lord for justice?" Arthur asked.

"His lord is the one who ordered the whipping."

When a nobleman rode past, his servant lashed Arthur across the shoulders with a whip — just a peasant in the way. Arthur stared in disbelief. "He struck me."

"He struck a peasant. That's what happens to peasants."

We sheltered with a charcoal burner and his wife — the poorest people I had ever met. They shared their soup without hesitation. Arthur ate and stared at the fire.

"When I return to Camelot," he said quietly, "things will change."

I believed him. But one good man at the top of a bad system doesn't fix the system. It just makes it slightly less awful while continuing to operate exactly as before.



CHAPTER 11

SOLD INTO SLAVERY

Our disguise worked too well. On the road, slave traders captured us and marched us to market. Arthur was outraged. "I am the King!" The traders laughed. "Last week I had a pig farmer who claimed to be the Pope."

We marched for three days in chains. Arthur's dignity was stripped away layer by layer. He was no longer a king. He was property.

At the slave market, people stood on wooden platforms while buyers examined their teeth and muscles like livestock. Arthur, the King of England, was sold for seven dollars. I was sold for nine.

Arthur was put to work hauling rocks. He worked in the mud until his hands bled. He did not complain. He talked to the other slaves — heard their stories, saw their scars. A woman separated from her children five years ago. A boy of twelve sold three times since he was eight.

"This is legal," Arthur said to me. "In my kingdom, this is legal."

"Yes."

"I will abolish it. I swear on my crown."

That was the moment I truly respected Arthur. Not because he was a king, but because he could look at a system that served him and say: this is wrong.



CHAPTER 12

THE RESCUE

They came on bicycles.

Five hundred of my trained men, equipped with products from my secret factories, riding bicycles at speeds no horse could match. Clarence was at the head of the column, grinning.

The slave traders fled. Every slave in the market was freed. Every chain was broken.

Arthur watched my men with wonder. "You built this."

"We built it," I said. "You and me and Clarence and every person who decided the world could be better."

We rode back to Camelot. The factories had expanded. The schools had more students. The telegraph network connected every major town. A railroad was being planned.

For the first time since I'd arrived in the sixth century, I felt genuinely hopeful.



CHAPTER 13

THREE YEARS OF PROGRESS

The next three years were the best of my time in Camelot.

With Arthur's full support, I brought my secret projects into the open. Factories appeared across England. Schools opened in every town. I built real roads, established a postal service, and introduced basic sanitation.

Disease dropped. Trade increased. Literacy spread. For the first time in English history, a common farmer could read a book, write a letter, and understand that the earth was round. I created a stock exchange — the capstone of a modern economy. The merchants and the new middle class thrived.

Not everyone was happy. The nobles saw their power eroding. The Church saw its authority challenged. Merlin, humiliated but not destroyed, whispered poison into every ear that would listen.

But for three years, progress outweighed resistance. England was changing — not just on the surface, but in the minds of its people. They were beginning to think. To question. To imagine a world where birth didn't determine destiny.

I was proud. Too proud, probably. I forgot that the old world doesn't die quietly. It fights back.



CHAPTER 14

THE NEWSPAPER

I introduced journalism because I believed an informed public was the foundation of democracy. I started four newspapers.

One was serious. One loved scandal. One was controlled by the Church. One was run by a former knight who used it to settle grudges.

The result was chaos. People who had never had access to information were suddenly drowning in it, with no way to tell truth from fiction. Rumors spread faster than facts. The coalition I had built — between the crown, the new middle class, and common people — began to crack.

"Maybe the newspapers were a mistake," Clarence said.

"The newspapers aren't the mistake," I said. "The mistake is thinking people will use freedom wisely just because you give it to them. Freedom is a skill. It has to be learned."

It was the most important thing I said during my entire time in Camelot.



CHAPTER 15

HANK AND SANDY

Somewhere in the middle of all this revolution, I married Sandy.

I hadn't planned on falling in love with a woman from the sixth century who believed in dragons and couldn't pronounce "Connecticut." But Sandy was brave, kind, and honest in a way that made my certainties feel small.

She didn't understand my technology. She didn't care about my factories. What she cared about was people — the specific human beings affected by my changes. When I talked about "progress" in the abstract, Sandy talked about the woman who could now read, the child who didn't die of fever.

She made me better. She made me see that changing the world isn't about systems and machines — it's about people.

We had a daughter. We named her Hello-Central, because I missed telephones. Sandy thought it was a beautiful name.

Late at night, I sometimes thought about Hartford. Was any of this real? Would I wake up one morning in 1879 with nothing but a headache and a strange story?

Sandy would reach for my hand in the dark, and the warmth of her fingers was the most real thing I had ever known.



CHAPTER 16

THE INTERDICT

The word hit like a thunderbolt: interdict.

The Church declared a religious ban against Hank Morgan and all his works. No baptisms. No marriages. No last rites. No salvation for anyone who supported me.

In a world where people believed their eternal soul depended on the Church, this was the ultimate weapon. It didn't attack bodies. It attacked souls.

Overnight, everything changed. Factories emptied. Schools closed. Telegraph operators walked away. Soldiers deserted. Not because they wanted to, but because they were terrified. The Church told them that supporting me meant eternal damnation.

I stood in my empty factory and understood what I had failed to see: I had changed the surface of England, but I hadn't changed its heart. Technology can build roads, but it can't build faith. Education can teach people to read, but it can't tell them what to believe.

The Church was older than Arthur. Older than Camelot. Older than England itself. And it would not be displaced by a Yankee with a factory.



CHAPTER 17

THE BATTLE OF THE SAND-BELT

With the Church against me and the nobles emboldened, thirty thousand knights marched on my fortified position in a narrow valley. I had fifty-two boys — the youngest of my soldiers, the ones too loyal to desert.

Fifty-two against thirty thousand. But I had electric fences, dynamite, Gatling guns, and land mines.

The knights came in waves. They charged on horseback, lances leveled, pennants flying, exactly the way they had for centuries. They were magnificent and doomed.

The first wave hit the land mines. The second hit the electric fences. The third was cut down by the Gatling guns. Thousands of knights — the flower of English chivalry — died in that narrow valley. Their armor meant nothing against modern weapons.

I watched from behind the fortification and felt sick.

I had wanted to bring civilization to England. Instead, I had brought the worst thing civilization ever invented: industrial warfare. The knights kept coming because their code demanded it, because they didn't know how to do anything else. They were the bravest men I had ever seen, and I was killing them by the thousands.

By morning, thirty thousand men lay dead. My fifty-two boys had won the battle. But we had lost everything else.

The dead surrounded us. Disease came. My boys fell sick, one by one, poisoned by the carnage our technology had created. They died until only Clarence and I were left.

Then Merlin came — disguised as an old woman. I was too broken to see through it. He laughed his thin, triumphant cackle and cast his spell.

"Sleep, Yankee. Sleep for thirteen centuries."

Darkness fell. Clarence found Merlin dead beside me — the old wizard had stumbled into one of my electric fences. Each of us destroyed by what the other represented.

Clarence wrote the final pages of my manuscript, sealed it in the cave with my sleeping body, and walked out into a world that no longer wanted what I had tried to give it.



CHAPTER 18

THIRTEEN CENTURIES LATER

I woke in 1879, in a hospital bed, with a headache that felt like the end of the world.

They told me I had been hit on the head at the Colt Arms Factory. They told me I had been unconscious for several minutes. They told me I would be fine.

I was not fine.

Everything was gone. Sandy was gone. Hello-Central was gone. Clarence was gone. Arthur, who had been the best king a bad system ever produced, was gone. The factories, the schools, the newspapers, the brief attempt to build a better world — all gone, buried under thirteen centuries of history.

Had it been real? The manuscript — my manuscript, yellowed and ancient — I found it among my possessions. How had it survived? How had it reached me?

I spent my remaining years caught between two worlds. I walked the streets of Hartford and saw Camelot. I heard factory whistles and remembered knights charging. I reached for Sandy in the night and found only empty air.

The stranger at Warwick Castle finished his story and fell silent. His eyes were focused on something I couldn't see.

"Was it real?" I asked.

He didn't answer. He reached out his hand, as if grasping for something, and whispered: "Sandy..."

Then Hank Morgan, the Connecticut Yankee who had tried to change the world, closed his eyes. His hand fell. And his story was over.

Whether he succeeded or failed — whether his revolution was a dream or a tragedy — is a question I leave to you.

But I will say this: he tried. In a world of darkness and ignorance and cruelty, he tried to build something better. He used everything he had — his knowledge, his courage, his stubborn American optimism — and he tried.

That, I think, is enough. That has to be enough.

THE END
