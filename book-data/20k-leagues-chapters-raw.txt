TWENTY THOUSAND LEAGUES UNDER THE SEA
A BedrockELA Adaptation
Based on the novel by Jules Verne (1870)
Adapted for 6th Grade Readers

CHAPTER 1
THE SEA MONSTER
In the year 1866, something strange was happening in the world's oceans.
Ships were reporting encounters with an enormous creature—something huge, fast, and glowing. A British steamer saw it in the Pacific. A French vessel spotted it near Australia. An American clipper ship reported that it nearly rammed them in the Atlantic, moving at impossible speed.
The thing was long, shaped like a spindle, and sometimes gave off a phosphorescent light—a greenish glow that lit up the water for hundreds of yards. It was far bigger than any whale ever recorded. Scientists argued about what it could be. Some said it was a giant narwhal—a type of whale with a long, spiraling tusk. Others insisted it was a sea serpent, a kraken, or something that had never been classified.
Then the creature attacked.
The Scotia, one of the finest ocean liners in the world, was crossing the Atlantic when something struck its hull below the waterline. The blow punched a perfectly triangular hole through iron plates that were nearly an inch thick. The ship barely made it to port.
After that, the world stopped arguing about whether the creature existed. Now the only question was: what was it, and how could it be stopped?
My name is Pierre Aronnax. I am a professor at the Museum of Natural History in Paris, and at the time of these events, I had just returned from a scientific expedition to the wilds of Nebraska. I had written a two-volume book on the mysteries of the ocean deep, and it had made me somewhat famous—at least among people who care about such things.
When the Scotia was attacked, newspapers around the world asked for my opinion. I published an article arguing that the creature was most likely a giant narwhal of extraordinary size and power—a real animal, not a myth. The article was widely read. Within weeks, I received a letter from the Secretary of the United States Navy.
The Americans were launching a warship, the Abraham Lincoln, to hunt the creature down. They wanted me aboard as the expedition's scientific advisor.
I could not resist.
"Conseil," I said to my servant, "pack our bags. We leave for New York tomorrow."
Conseil was a calm, loyal, thirty-year-old Flemish man who had been with me for ten years. Nothing surprised him. Nothing upset him. If I had told him we were going to the moon, he would have begun packing immediately.
"Very well, sir," he said. "Shall I pack your specimen jars?"
"Pack everything."
And so, within three days, we were aboard the Abraham Lincoln, steaming out of New York Harbor and into the unknown.

CHAPTER 2
THE ABRAHAM LINCOLN
The Abraham Lincoln was a fast frigate—a warship built for speed. Her captain, Commander Farragut, was a bold and determined man who had sworn not to return until the monster was dead or he had proven it didn't exist.
The ship was armed with every weapon the Navy could provide: cannons, rifles, harpoons, and a massive bow-mounted gun that could fire an explosive shell over a mile. If the creature was an animal, it wouldn't stand a chance. At least, that was the theory.
The crew was eager. The Navy had offered a reward of two thousand dollars to whoever first spotted the creature, and every sailor on board spent his free time scanning the horizon with a telescope. The lookouts barely blinked.
I spent my first days aboard studying my notes, reviewing the reports of sightings, and debating with a man who would become central to everything that followed: Ned Land.
Ned Land was a Canadian harpooner—the best in the world, by most accounts. He was about forty years old, tall and powerfully built, with a serious face and a temper that could flare without warning. He spoke little, but when he did, his words were blunt and direct.
"Professor," he said to me on the third day out, "I don't believe in your monster."
"You don't?"
"I've been whaling since I was a boy. I've harpooned every kind of creature that swims. If this thing were a whale or a narwhal, I'd have heard of it. It's nothing."
"The Scotia's hull would disagree with you, Ned."
He shrugged. "Rocks. Currents. Bad luck. Not a sea monster."
I admired his stubbornness. I would come to admire it even more.
Meanwhile, Conseil followed me everywhere with the patience of a shadow. He organized my papers, labeled my samples, and asked no questions. If I fell overboard, I was fairly certain Conseil would jump in after me without a word of complaint.
We searched for three months.
North Pacific, South Pacific, across the equator and back again. Nothing. The crew grew restless. Some began to agree with Ned—the creature was a myth. Commander Farragut's determination hardened into something that looked more like desperation.
Then, on November 5th, at three o'clock in the afternoon, Ned Land's voice rang out from the masthead:
"There it is! Dead ahead! The thing itself!"

CHAPTER 3
THE HUNT
Every man on deck rushed to the bow. There, in the fading light, about two miles ahead, the ocean was glowing. Just below the surface, a long, luminous shape moved through the water, leaving a trail of pale green fire.
"Full speed ahead!" Commander Farragut ordered.
The Abraham Lincoln surged forward, her engines roaring. But the creature moved faster—far faster than any whale had a right to move. It stayed just ahead of us, as if teasing us, its glow pulsing like a heartbeat beneath the waves.
For hours the chase continued. Every time we gained ground, the creature accelerated. It seemed to be playing with us. The crew fired at it—rifles, cannons, everything they had. The shots disappeared into the sea without effect.
Near midnight, the creature stopped. It floated motionless on the surface, its glow dimming to a faint shimmer. Steam rose from the water around it, as if the thing were breathing.
"Now!" Farragut shouted. "Ned, take your position!"
Ned Land stood at the bow, harpoon raised. The ship crept forward in the darkness.
"Steady," Ned murmured. "Steady..."
He hurled the harpoon with all his strength. It struck the creature with a ringing clang—the sound of metal on metal.
Metal?
In the same instant, the creature's light blazed to blinding intensity. Two enormous jets of water shot into the air, crashed down on the deck of the Abraham Lincoln, and swept across the ship like a tidal wave.
I was thrown over the railing.
The last thing I heard before the black water closed over my head was Conseil's voice shouting my name.

CHAPTER 4
PRISONERS OF THE SEA
I sank into the dark ocean, choking, tumbling. My heavy clothes dragged me down. I kicked and clawed my way back to the surface, gasping for air. The Abraham Lincoln was already a quarter mile away, her lights growing smaller. She hadn't seen me go over.
"Sir! Professor!"
A hand grabbed my collar. It was Conseil. He had jumped in after me—without hesitation, without being asked.
"Conseil! Are you insane?"
"I am in your service, sir. Where you go, I go."
We treaded water in the black ocean, watching the ship's lights shrink to pinpricks and vanish. We were alone.
For hours we floated, taking turns resting against each other. The cold crept into our bones. My legs went numb. Conseil never complained, but I could feel him shivering. We were going to die out here—drowned in the middle of the Pacific.
Then my foot struck something solid.
Not the ocean floor—we were miles from land. Something hard, something flat, something that rang under my boot like iron.
"What on earth—"
"Up here!" a voice called. It was Ned Land. He was sitting on top of the very creature we had been hunting, and he was grinning.
"It's not an animal," Ned said, pulling us up onto the metal surface. "It's a machine. A submarine."
He was right. Beneath us was a long, smooth hull made of riveted iron plates. No scales, no skin—just metal, bolted together by human hands. The "eye" that had glowed was a powerful electric light. The jets of water were exhaust vents.
We lay on the hull, catching our breath, trying to understand what we had found. A man-made vessel, shaped like a giant cigar, that could outrun the fastest warship in the world. It was impossible—and it was right beneath us.
A hatch opened with a clang. Eight men in dark uniforms emerged, grabbed us without a word, and dragged us inside.
The hatch sealed shut behind us. We were in absolute darkness.
We were prisoners.

CHAPTER 5
CAPTAIN NEMO
They locked us in a small, pitch-black room. For hours we sat in silence, unable to see each other, unable to tell if it was day or night. Ned pounded on the walls and shouted. No one answered.
Eventually the door opened and two men led us down a corridor lit by soft electric light—an extraordinary thing in 1866, when most of the world still used gas lamps and candles. The walls were polished metal. The air was clean and cool.
They brought us to a large, elegant room furnished with velvet sofas, paintings, a marble table, and shelves lined with thousands of books. A pipe organ stood in one corner. The floor was covered with thick carpet.
A man was waiting for us.
He was tall and broad-shouldered, with dark skin, a high forehead, and eyes that seemed to look straight through you. His hair was thick and black, swept back from his face. He had the bearing of someone who was used to giving orders and having them obeyed without question. His age was hard to guess—somewhere between thirty-five and fifty.
He studied us for a long moment in silence. Then he spoke—in perfect French.
"I am Captain Nemo," he said. "You are aboard my vessel, the Nautilus."
"Nemo?" I said. The word means "no one" in Latin.
A faint smile crossed his face. "I am no one. I have severed all ties with the world above. I have no country. I answer to no government. The sea is my only home."
Ned stepped forward aggressively. "You attacked our ship and nearly killed us. We demand to be released."
Nemo's eyes hardened. "You hunted me. Your ship fired on me. You tried to destroy me with cannons and harpoons. I owe you nothing." He paused. "However, I do not wish to be cruel. You have two choices. You may remain aboard the Nautilus as my guests. You will be fed, housed, and allowed to witness things no human being has ever seen. In exchange, you will never leave. You will live and die aboard this vessel."
"And the second choice?" I asked.
"There is no second choice. You know the Nautilus exists. I cannot allow that knowledge to reach the surface world."
Ned looked ready to fight, but I put a hand on his arm. We were unarmed, outnumbered, and trapped inside an iron machine at the bottom of the ocean. Fighting would accomplish nothing.
"We accept your hospitality, Captain," I said carefully. "For now."
"For now," Nemo repeated, with that same faint smile. "Come. Let me show you my ship."

CHAPTER 6
THE NAUTILUS
Nothing in my life had prepared me for the Nautilus.
Captain Nemo led us through the vessel like a proud father showing off a beloved child. The ship was enormous—about 230 feet long and 26 feet wide—built entirely of iron and steel, powered by electricity generated from seawater itself. Nemo had invented the process.
"Electricity lights the ship, heats it, powers the engines, and runs the clocks," he explained. "The sea provides everything I need. I am completely independent of the land."
The library held twelve thousand volumes—science, literature, history, poetry—in every language. I could have spent years in that room alone. Next to it was a drawing room filled with masterpiece paintings, sculptures, and rare musical instruments. In the center stood a magnificent pipe organ that Nemo played himself.
The dining room served food that came entirely from the sea: fish prepared a dozen different ways, sea vegetables, preserves made from seaweed, and desserts flavored with ocean plants I had never tasted. The "flour" for bread came from certain types of algae. The "milk" came from the fat of sea creatures. Nothing came from the land. Nothing.
But it was the salon—the viewing room—that stopped me in my tracks.
The walls on both sides were fitted with enormous glass panels, each one several inches thick and perfectly clear. Through them, the ocean spread out in all directions—an endless, blue-green world filled with more life than I had ever imagined. Schools of silver fish swept past like living curtains. Jellyfish drifted by, trailing long, delicate tentacles. A sea turtle glided through the light, ancient and unhurried.
I pressed my face to the glass like a child at a candy store.
"This is the window to the infinite," Nemo said quietly, standing beside me. "The sea is everything. It covers seven-tenths of the globe. Its breath is pure and healthy. It is an immense desert where a man is never alone, because he can feel life stirring on every side. The sea is the living infinite."
I could have stayed at that window forever.
Ned Land was not as impressed. He stared at the ocean with the expression of a man looking at the walls of his prison.
"Very nice," he said. "Now how do we get out?"

CHAPTER 7
THE UNDERWATER FOREST
A few days into our imprisonment, Captain Nemo invited me to join him on an excursion outside the ship.
"Outside?" I said. "You mean—in the water?"
"On the ocean floor, Professor. I think you will find it interesting."
He led me to a room filled with equipment I had never seen before: heavy diving suits made of rubber and metal, copper helmets with thick glass visors, and tanks of compressed air carried on the back. The boots were weighted with lead to keep us anchored to the bottom.
"These suits allow a man to walk on the ocean floor for several hours," Nemo explained. "The air supply is sufficient for a long excursion. We will carry electric lanterns and hunting rifles that fire glass bullets powered by compressed air."
Conseil begged to come along. Ned refused—he said he'd rather eat his boots than go for a walk at the bottom of the sea. So it was Nemo, two of his crewmen, Conseil, and me.
The airlock filled with water. The outer hatch opened. And I stepped onto the floor of the ocean.
I will never forget that moment.
The light filtered down from above in shifting columns of blue and green, growing dimmer as we descended a gentle slope. The ground beneath our heavy boots was fine sand, smooth as a beach, marked with ripples from the current. Small fish scattered at our approach, flashing silver in our lantern beams.
And then the forest appeared.
Not a forest of seaweed—a forest of living coral and giant sea plants that towered above us like trees. Their trunks were thick and rough, their branches spread wide, and from every limb hung curtains of marine vegetation in colors I had never seen: violet, rose, emerald, gold. Fish moved through the branches like birds, and the whole scene swayed gently in the current, as if breathing.
We walked among these underwater trees for two hours. The silence was extraordinary—a deep, cushioned quiet broken only by the rasp of our breathing inside the helmets and the crunch of sand under our boots. Nemo moved through this world with perfect ease, pointing out species I recognized from books and others I had never seen.
At one point he raised his rifle, aimed, and fired at something in the undergrowth. A large sea otter tumbled forward, struck by the glass bullet. Nemo's crewman picked it up—its fur was magnificent, thick and glossy.
"Dinner," Nemo said through a gesture—we couldn't speak through the helmets, but his meaning was clear.
We returned to the Nautilus as the light faded. I climbed out of my diving suit in a daze. I had walked on the bottom of the ocean. I had seen things no living scientist had ever witnessed.
And I began to understand the terrible beauty of Captain Nemo's bargain. How could I want to leave a place that showed me this?

CHAPTER 8
THE CORAL KINGDOM
The Nautilus continued south through the Indian Ocean. Each day brought new wonders. Through the salon windows, I watched the seafloor change from sand to rock to coral, an ever-shifting landscape more varied than any continent.
One morning, Nemo appeared at my door with a serious expression.
"Professor, I need to ask something of you and your companions. One of my crew has died from injuries. I wish to bury him. Will you accompany me?"
"Of course."
We suited up in our diving gear again—all of us this time, even Ned, who came grudgingly—and followed Nemo and his men as they carried the body, wrapped in cloth, out through the airlock and across the ocean floor.
Nemo led us into a forest of coral unlike anything I had seen before. The formations rose from the ground in fantastic shapes—towers, arches, branching structures that looked like the pillars of an ancient cathedral. The colors were extraordinary: deep red, orange, white, and pale pink, all growing slowly, year after year, adding layer upon layer of mineral.
In a clearing among the coral, several mounds marked previous graves. This was Captain Nemo's cemetery.
The men dug a shallow trench in the coral sand. They laid their companion to rest. Nemo stood over the grave in silence for a long time. Then he raised his hand in a slow gesture of farewell.
No words were spoken. None were needed.
As we walked back to the ship, I thought about what I had just witnessed. Nemo had built his own world down here—not just a ship, but a civilization. He had food, power, art, music, and now even a place to lay his dead. He truly had cut himself off from the surface world entirely.
Who was this man? What had driven him to abandon everything above and commit his life to the sea?
I watched his tall figure walking ahead of me through the underwater twilight, and I realized I might never know.

CHAPTER 9
THE TORRES STRAIT
The Nautilus entered the Torres Strait—the dangerous, shallow passage between Australia and New Guinea. The water was full of reefs, and the passage was so narrow in places that the ship had to thread its way like a needle.
Captain Nemo took the helm himself, guiding the Nautilus through the maze of coral with extraordinary skill. But even Nemo could not control everything. A sudden shift in the current caught us, and the Nautilus ground to a halt, wedged firmly on a reef of coral.
"We are stuck," Nemo announced calmly.
"Can't you reverse the engines?" I asked.
"The tide is going out. In six days, when the full moon brings a high tide, the water will rise enough to float us free. Until then, we wait."
Six days! Ned Land could barely contain his frustration. But the delay had one advantage: Captain Nemo gave us permission to go ashore on a nearby island to find fresh food.
The island was a paradise. Coconut palms swayed in the breeze. Breadfruit trees drooped with heavy fruit. The forest floor was carpeted with ferns and bright flowers. Brilliantly colored birds shrieked in the canopy.
Ned was in heaven. He hunted wild pigs, brought down birds with stones and sticks, and gathered enough fruit to fill a small boat. Conseil collected specimens of insects and butterflies with quiet delight. Even I felt the joy of standing on solid ground again, with the sun on my face and the wind in my hair.
For three days we explored, feasted, and almost forgot we were prisoners.
On the fourth day, everything changed.

CHAPTER 10
ATTACK OF THE SAVAGES
We were on the beach, roasting a wild pig over a fire, when Conseil noticed movement at the edge of the forest.
"Sir," he said quietly. "I believe we have company."
Men were emerging from the trees—dozens of them. They were tall, dark-skinned, and armed with bows, spears, and clubs. Their faces and bodies were painted in bold patterns. They did not look friendly.
"Back to the boat!" Ned snapped. "Now!"
We scrambled for our small dinghy, loaded it with as much food as we could grab, and rowed hard for the Nautilus. The warriors followed us to the waterline, shaking their weapons and shouting. Some waded into the shallows after us. A spear splashed into the water inches from our hull.
We reached the submarine and tumbled through the hatch, slamming it shut behind us. I went immediately to find Captain Nemo. He was in the salon, playing his organ, completely unconcerned.
"Captain! There are warriors on the beach. Dozens of them. They're hostile."
Nemo didn't stop playing. "Yes, I know. They've been watching us since we arrived."
"They followed us to the ship. What if they try to board?"
"They are welcome to try."
That night, through the salon windows, we could see canoes approaching in the darkness—thirty, forty, perhaps fifty of them. By morning, the beach was swarming with warriors. They crossed the reef at low tide and surrounded the Nautilus, pounding on the hull with their fists and weapons.
Ned readied a harpoon. Conseil looked worried. I looked at Nemo.
He finished his coffee and said, "Open the hatches."
"What?"
"Open the hatches. It is time to go. The tide will float us free today. But first—watch."
The crewmen opened the hatches as ordered. A warrior leaped onto the platform and grabbed the metal railing—and was hurled backward as if struck by lightning. He splashed into the water, convulsing.
The railing was electrified.
Every warrior who touched the ship received the same jolt. Within seconds, they were in full retreat, paddling their canoes away in terror.
Nemo sipped his coffee and watched them go. "The Nautilus has many defenses," he said mildly. "Violence is unnecessary when technology is sufficient."
The tide rose. The ship lifted free of the reef. The engines hummed to life, and we slipped beneath the waves and left the Torres Strait behind.

CHAPTER 11
THE PEARL DIVERS
The Nautilus entered the Indian Ocean and made for the island of Ceylon—modern-day Sri Lanka. Nemo announced that he wished to visit the pearl-diving banks off the coast, and he invited us to join him.
"Pearls?" Ned said, suddenly interested. "Real pearls?"
"The finest in the world," said Nemo. "The oyster beds off Manaar are legendary. Some pearls are worth a king's ransom."
We anchored near the coast and suited up for another underwater walk. This time, Nemo led us across a flat, sandy plain covered with thousands upon thousands of oysters. They lay in dense clusters, their rough shells half-buried in the sediment, each one potentially hiding a pearl.
But Nemo wasn't interested in ordinary pearls. He led us to a hidden cave, partially concealed by an overhang of rock. Inside, in a natural basin, sat the largest oyster I had ever seen—or imagined. It was the size of a dining table, its thick shell coated with algae and barnacles.
Nemo reached in and carefully pried the shell open. There, nestled in the creature's soft tissue, was a pearl the size of a coconut.
I nearly choked on my air supply.
It was flawless—perfectly round, gleaming with a soft, milky light. I calculated its value on the spot: it was worth millions of francs. Enough to buy a palace. Enough to fund a dozen scientific expeditions.
Nemo looked at it the way a gardener looks at a prized rose—with pride and patience. He closed the shell gently and let it be.
"I do not harvest this pearl," he said later, aboard the ship. "I allow it to grow. Year by year, layer by layer, it adds to its beauty. It is my treasure, and I will leave it in the sea where it belongs."
This was the mystery of Captain Nemo. He had access to wealth beyond imagination—pearls, gold from sunken ships, the riches of the entire ocean—and he wanted none of it for himself. Or rather, he used it, but not the way any normal person would.
As we would soon discover, Nemo had his own plans for that wealth. And they were not plans of a peaceful man.

CHAPTER 12
THE SHARK
The next day, Nemo took us back to the pearl banks to observe the local divers at work. These were poor men from the coast who dove without any equipment—no suits, no helmets, no air tanks. They held their breath, dove to the bottom, scooped up oysters, and swam back to the surface. It was dangerous, exhausting work, and many divers died young.
We watched from a distance, hidden among the rocks. One diver in particular caught my attention—a young man, thin but strong, who dove deeper and stayed down longer than any of the others. He was brave, but he was also reckless.
On his fifth or sixth dive, I saw it before he did.
A shadow passed over the sandy bottom—enormous, gray, and moving fast. A shark. Not just any shark—a great tiger shark, at least twenty feet long, with dead black eyes and a mouth that could swallow a man whole.
The diver looked up and saw it. His eyes went wide with terror. He tried to swim for the surface, but the shark was faster. It turned, came at him with its jaws open—
And Ned Land was there.
I don't know how he moved so fast. One moment he was beside me, the next he was hurtling through the water like a torpedo, his knife—a long, heavy whaling knife—held out in front of him. He collided with the shark just as it reached the diver, driving the blade into the creature's belly with both hands.
The water exploded into a cloud of blood and thrashing. The shark twisted, snapped, rolled—but Ned held on. He stabbed again and again, his face a mask of fury. Nemo was beside him in an instant, his own knife flashing.
Between them, they killed the shark. It took several long, terrible minutes. When it was over, the water was dark with blood and the great fish sank slowly to the bottom, dead.
The pearl diver floated nearby, unconscious but alive. Nemo swam to him, lifted him gently, and carried him to the surface. He reached into a waterproof pouch at his belt, pulled out a small bag of pearls, and pressed it into the unconscious man's hand.
When we were back aboard the Nautilus, drying off and tending to Ned's cuts and bruises, I said to Nemo: "That was extraordinarily generous."
He looked at me with those dark, unreadable eyes. "That man is one of the oppressed. As long as I live, I am his countryman."
It was the first time Nemo had given any hint about his past—about who he had been before he became "no one." He was not simply a scientist or an eccentric. He was a man with a cause. A man with a wound.
Ned Land said nothing about his own heroism. He just cleaned his knife, sharpened it on a stone, and went to bed.

CHAPTER 13
THE RED SEA
The Nautilus turned west and entered the Red Sea, that long, narrow body of water separating Africa from Arabia. Nemo was in unusually good spirits. He spent time in the salon with me, discussing marine biology, ocean currents, and the history of navigation. For a man who claimed to have abandoned humanity, he was remarkably well-informed about human affairs.
"Professor," he said one afternoon, "how do you suppose the ancient Egyptians traveled between the Red Sea and the Mediterranean?"
"The Suez Canal is nearly complete," I said. "But the ancients had no such channel."
"No. But nature provided one." His eyes gleamed. "There is an underground passage—a tunnel beneath the Isthmus of Suez—that connects the two seas. I discovered it myself. I call it the Arabian Tunnel."
I stared at him. "That's impossible."
"Many things I do are considered impossible. Come to the salon window in one hour."
I did. And I watched, speechless, as the Nautilus entered a dark, narrow passage in the rock, submerged completely, and traveled through an underwater tunnel for twenty minutes. When we emerged, the water had changed color. The temperature had shifted. The fish were different species entirely.
We were in the Mediterranean Sea.
We had crossed from the Indian Ocean to the Mediterranean in less than half an hour, through a passage that no one on the surface world knew existed.
Ned Land, when told, simply shook his head. "This ship gets stranger every day." But his eyes were calculating. The Mediterranean meant Europe. Europe meant home. I could see the escape plans forming behind his eyes.

CHAPTER 14
THE LOST CITY
Two nights later, Nemo woke me at midnight.
"Get dressed, Professor. I want to show you something."
We suited up in diving gear—just the two of us this time—and stepped out onto the ocean floor. It was pitch dark. Our electric lanterns cut narrow beams through the blackness. We were deep—very deep. The pressure squeezed my suit against my body, and every breath felt heavy.
Nemo led me up a steep, rocky slope. We climbed for what felt like an hour, picking our way over boulders and loose stone. The ground beneath us changed—it became too regular, too flat. I looked down and realized I was walking on carved stone blocks. Steps. I was climbing steps.
At the top of the ridge, Nemo turned off his lantern and pointed.
Below us, lit by the red glow of an underwater volcano, lay the ruins of a city.
Enormous columns lay toppled across wide streets. The outlines of temples and palaces were visible through the murk, their walls still standing after thousands of years beneath the sea. Statues, half-buried in sediment, stared up at us with blank stone eyes. An aqueduct stretched into the distance, its arches still intact.
I knew what I was looking at. Every scientist in the world would have given anything to stand where I stood.
Atlantis.
The lost continent. The civilization that Plato wrote about twenty-four hundred years ago—a great island empire that sank beneath the waves in a single day and night of catastrophe. Most scholars considered it a myth. But here it was, spread out before me like a map of the ancient world, preserved by the cold, dark water of the deep Atlantic.
I sank to my knees on the carved stone steps. Tears blurred my vision inside my helmet.
Nemo stood beside me, arms crossed, staring out at the drowned city. In the red light of the volcano, his face looked like a statue itself—ancient, proud, and impossibly sad.
We stayed for an hour. Neither of us wanted to leave. But the air supply demanded it, and we made the long walk back to the Nautilus in silence.
I did not sleep that night. I lay in my bunk, staring at the ceiling, trying to process what I had seen. The greatest archaeological discovery in human history—and I could never tell anyone about it.

CHAPTER 15
THE VOLCANIC FLOOR
The Nautilus continued south through the Atlantic, diving deeper than I would have believed possible. Nemo seemed drawn to the extremes of the ocean—the deepest trenches, the most remote waters, the places where no ship had ever ventured.
We passed over fields of underwater volcanoes—mountains of fire rising from the ocean floor, their peaks glowing orange and red, streams of lava flowing down their sides like slow rivers. The water around them shimmered with heat. Fish scattered. The hull of the Nautilus creaked and groaned under the temperature changes, but Nemo never hesitated.
"The ocean floor is alive," he told me, watching through the salon window as molten rock oozed across the seabed. "New land is being born down here every day. Islands are rising. Mountains are growing. The Earth is not finished building itself."
We entered the Sargasso Sea—a strange, calm region of the Atlantic where thick mats of floating seaweed covered the surface. The water was warm and eerily still. The Nautilus pushed through the weed like a plow through heavy soil.
"Ships have been trapped here for centuries," Nemo said. "The seaweed tangles their rudders. The calm steals their wind. Sailors call it the graveyard of ships."
Through the windows, I could see the evidence—the rotting hulls of ancient vessels, encrusted with barnacles and draped in weed, their masts broken and their cargo scattered across the sandy bottom. Some were centuries old. One looked like a Spanish galleon from the age of exploration, its cannons still visible through the murk.
Nemo looked at the wrecks without emotion. "The sea takes what it wants," he said. "And it keeps what it takes."

CHAPTER 16
THE DEEP DIVE
Captain Nemo had been dropping hints for days. He wanted to attempt something that had never been done—a dive to the deepest point of the ocean.
"The bottom," he said. "The absolute bottom. No one has ever been there. No one has ever seen it."
The Nautilus descended. The light faded—first blue, then indigo, then black. The water temperature dropped until the hull was cold to the touch. The pressure increased steadily, and I could hear the iron plates groaning under the strain.
Conseil stood beside me at the salon window, his face pressed to the glass, cataloging every creature we passed. Fish with enormous eyes. Jellyfish that glowed in the dark—bioluminescent, creating their own cold light in the eternal blackness. Creatures that looked like nothing in any textbook—transparent, boneless, drifting like ghosts.
At sixteen thousand feet, the last fish disappeared. We were beyond the zone of life—or so I thought.
At twenty thousand feet, the salon window showed nothing but blackness. The Nautilus was a bubble of light and warmth in an infinite void. The pressure gauge read numbers I didn't want to think about. If the glass cracked, if a single rivet failed, we would be crushed instantly.
At twenty-five thousand feet, the searchlights hit the bottom.
It was a flat, gray plain of fine sediment—the accumulated dust of millions of years of dead organisms drifting down from above. Nothing moved. Nothing grew. It was the most desolate landscape I had ever seen—and somehow, the most awe-inspiring.
"The floor of the world," Nemo whispered.
He planted a black flag on the seabed through a mechanical arm built into the hull. On it was a gold letter N.
"I claim this place," he said, "in my own name, and no other."
Then we rose. I breathed again only when the pressure gauge showed a safe number. Ned had spent the entire dive lying in his bunk with a pillow over his head, and I couldn't blame him.

CHAPTER 17
THE SOUTH POLE
Nemo turned the Nautilus south—far south, into the frozen waters of Antarctica. The water grew bitterly cold. Ice appeared—first as scattered chunks, then as a solid wall stretching from horizon to horizon.
"No ship has ever reached the South Pole by sea," Nemo said. "We will go under the ice."
The Nautilus dove beneath the ice shelf. Above us, the frozen ceiling stretched in every direction—a solid roof of white and blue, sometimes ten feet thick, sometimes a hundred. There was no way up. No way to surface for air.
The Nautilus carried enough compressed air for several days. But Nemo was gambling. He believed there would be open water at the Pole itself—a gap in the ice where we could surface and breathe.
For two days we traveled south under the ice. The crew grew quiet. The air recyclers worked overtime, but there was a staleness creeping into every breath. Ned paced the corridors like a caged animal.
On the third day, the sonar showed the ice thinning. Nemo pushed upward. The Nautilus struck the ice from below—once, twice, three times—and burst through into open air.
We climbed onto the hull and stood in the freezing wind. Before us stretched a barren landscape of snow and rock. Nemo took measurements with his instruments and announced what I already suspected:
"We are standing at the South Pole. The first human beings ever to set foot here."
He raised another black flag with the gold N. The wind snapped it straight. Penguins watched from a distance, unimpressed.
The moment of triumph was brief. Within hours, the temperature dropped further. The open water began to freeze around us. If we didn't leave immediately, the Nautilus would be trapped—and we would die.

CHAPTER 18
SUFFOCATION
We dove under the ice and headed north. But the ice had shifted. The passage we'd come through was blocked. Massive icebergs had drifted together overhead, sealing us in.
Nemo tried every direction. North, east, west—solid ice everywhere. The Nautilus was entombed.
"We have five days of air," Nemo said, his voice steady. "We must cut our way out."
The crew suited up and went outside into the freezing water with picks and axes. They hacked at the ice from below, working in shifts—two hours on, two hours off—around the clock. The water was so cold that men came back inside with their fingers blue and their lips white. But they kept going.
Inside the ship, the air grew thinner by the hour. Breathing became a conscious effort—each inhale felt incomplete, like trying to fill a glass with a trickle. My head pounded. My vision blurred at the edges. Conseil sat beside me, pale and gasping, but still trying to smile.
Ned worked alongside Nemo's crew outside the ship, swinging a pick with furious energy. Whatever his feelings about Nemo, whatever his desperate desire to escape, he was not a man who sat idle while others fought to survive.
By the fourth day, we were barely conscious. The air was so thin that a candle would not have stayed lit. Men lay on the floor, too weak to stand. I felt a terrible drowsiness creeping over me—a warm, peaceful desire to close my eyes and sleep forever.
On the fifth day, just hours before our air would have run out entirely, the last wall of ice gave way. The Nautilus surged upward through the gap and broke the surface. The hatches flew open. Arctic air—cold, sharp, magnificent—poured into the ship.
I have never tasted anything as sweet as that first breath.
Men wept. Men laughed. Men lay on the deck and simply breathed, over and over, marveling at the simple miracle of oxygen.
Captain Nemo stood at the bow, wind whipping his hair, staring north. His face showed nothing. But his hands, gripping the railing, were shaking.

CHAPTER 19
THE GIANT SQUID
The Nautilus entered warmer waters and continued north through the Atlantic. For a few days, life aboard settled into something almost like normalcy. I worked on my notes. Conseil organized specimens. Ned fished through the kitchen porthole and complained about captivity with renewed energy, which I took as a sign of good health.
Then came the squid.
Conseil spotted them first—dark shapes in the water, keeping pace with the ship. Through the salon window, we watched them approach: enormous creatures, each one forty to fifty feet long, with eight writhing arms and two longer tentacles, each lined with hundreds of suckers the size of dinner plates. Their eyes were huge and black and horribly intelligent.
There were seven of them.
"Giant squid," I breathed. "The kraken of legend."
The Nautilus tried to outrun them, but one of the creatures wrapped its tentacles around the propeller and jammed it. The ship shuddered and stopped. More squid latched onto the hull, their arms snaking across the metal plating, their beaks scraping against the rivets.
"We cannot use the electric charge," Nemo said grimly. "Their arms are too close to the hatches. We fight them hand to hand."
He opened the hatches. The crew surged out with axes, harpoons, and knives. The squid attacked immediately—tentacles as thick as tree trunks whipped through the air, grabbing at men, coiling around limbs, dragging sailors toward gaping beaks.
Ned was in the thick of it, his harpoon flashing. He drove it into the body of one squid, twisted, and yanked it free in a spray of black ink. Nemo fought beside him, hacking at a tentacle that had wrapped around a crewman's waist.
Then a scream—a crewman was lifted into the air by a massive arm, his legs kicking helplessly. The squid pulled him toward its beak. Nemo and three men threw themselves at the tentacle, chopping desperately—but the creature dragged the man under the water. He was gone.
The fight lasted fifteen minutes. It felt like hours. When it was over, the deck was slick with ink and blood. Six of the seven squid were dead or driven off. One crewman was lost.
Nemo stood at the railing, staring at the water where his man had disappeared. Tears ran down his face—the first and only time I ever saw Captain Nemo cry.

CHAPTER 20
THE STORM
The mood aboard the Nautilus changed after the squid attack. A darkness settled over Captain Nemo that deepened with each passing day. He stopped joining me for meals. He stopped playing the organ. He spent hours alone in the salon, staring through the windows at nothing, or pacing the corridors late at night. When I tried to speak with him, he gave short, distracted answers and walked away.
Meanwhile, Ned was growing desperate. We had been aboard the Nautilus for months. He could feel freedom slipping away with every mile.
"We have to escape, Professor," he said one night, his voice low and urgent. "This man is losing his mind. I can feel it. Something is building inside him, and when it breaks, we don't want to be here."
"Where would we go, Ned? We're in the middle of the ocean."
"We're not always in the middle of the ocean. We pass near coastlines. The dinghy on the hull—it's seaworthy. If we time it right, on a night when Nemo is distracted..."
I hesitated. Part of me—the scientist—never wanted to leave. The things I had seen aboard the Nautilus were beyond anything I had dreamed possible. But Ned was right about something: Nemo was changing. The noble, mysterious captain I had first met was being consumed by something—rage, grief, or madness.
A massive storm struck the Atlantic. The Nautilus surfaced to ride it out—or rather, Nemo surfaced deliberately. He climbed to the platform at the top of the ship and stood in the full fury of the hurricane, lashed by rain and wind, waves crashing over him. Lightning split the sky. Thunder shook the hull. And Nemo stood there with his arms spread wide, his face turned up to the storm, as if daring it to take him.
"He's completely mad," Ned said, watching from inside.
I wasn't sure Ned was wrong.

CHAPTER 21
NEMO'S REVENGE
The event Ned had feared came without warning.
Through the salon window, I spotted a ship on the surface—a large warship flying a flag I didn't recognize. Nemo appeared beside me. His entire body had gone rigid. His face was a mask of cold fury.
"That ship," he said, in a voice I had never heard before. "That flag."
"Captain, what—"
"Leave the salon, Professor. Now. Go to your cabin. Do not come out until I say."
"What are you going to do?"
He turned to me, and for the first time, I saw something in his eyes that frightened me more than the deep ocean, more than the squid, more than the ice: hatred. Pure, absolute, burning hatred.
"I am going to do what I was built to do," he said.
We were locked in our cabins. Through the walls, I heard the engines roar to full power. I felt the ship accelerate, diving and then rising, building speed. Then—a colossal impact. The Nautilus shook from bow to stern. A grinding, tearing sound of metal on metal.
Nemo had rammed the warship.
Through the small porthole in my cabin, I saw it—the warship, broken nearly in half, sinking fast. Men were in the water, clinging to wreckage, crying for help. The ship's bow pointed straight up at the sky for a long, terrible moment, and then it slid under the waves. Bubbles. Debris. Silence.
Nemo had killed them all.
When he finally let us out, I found him in the salon, standing before a portrait on the wall—a woman and two small children. He was weeping.
"That flag," he said, without looking at me. "That nation destroyed everything I loved. My family. My people. My country. They took it all."
I said nothing. There was nothing to say.
"I told you, Professor. I have severed all ties with the world above. But the world above has not severed its ties with me. It follows me. It hunts me. And when it finds me, I answer."
I looked at the portrait of the woman and children. I looked at this brilliant, tortured man. And I understood—not forgave, but understood—what had driven Captain Nemo to the bottom of the sea.

CHAPTER 22
NEMO'S SECRET
In the days that followed, the full picture emerged—in fragments, in half-sentences, in things Nemo said when he thought I wasn't paying close attention.
He had been a prince. A ruler of a colonized land—oppressed, invaded, and brutalized by a European power. His family had been killed. His people had been enslaved. He had fought back, lost everything, and fled to the sea with a group of loyal followers.
He had built the Nautilus in secret, on a deserted island, using his fortune and his genius. The ship was his weapon, his home, and his statement to the world: you cannot reach me here. You cannot control me. You cannot make me kneel.
The wealth he gathered from the ocean—pearls, gold from sunken ships, treasures beyond counting—he sent to the surface through secret channels, funding revolutions and resistance movements in oppressed nations around the world. The pearl diver he had saved was no accident. The bag of pearls was no random act of generosity. Nemo was financing a war against the empires of the world—a silent, invisible war waged from twenty thousand leagues beneath the sea.
He was a freedom fighter. He was also a murderer. He was a genius and a madman. He was the noblest person I had ever met, and the most dangerous.
I could no longer stay.
"Ned," I said. "Make your plan. The first opportunity we get—we run."
Ned's face broke into a fierce grin. "I've been waiting months to hear you say that."

CHAPTER 23
THE MAELSTROM
The opportunity came sooner than expected.
The Nautilus was traveling north, along the coast of Norway. Through my research, I recognized the waters—we were approaching the Lofoten Islands, home of the most terrifying natural phenomenon in the North Atlantic: the Maelstrom.
The Maelstrom was a massive whirlpool—a swirling vortex of water that could suck down entire ships. Sailors had feared it for centuries. Even Nemo, I thought, would steer clear.
He didn't. He drove straight toward it.
"He's going to kill us all," Ned said, his face white.
The sea began to spin. The current grabbed the Nautilus and pulled. Through the window, I could see the water spiraling downward in an enormous funnel, the walls of the vortex spinning faster and faster, debris whirling past—wood, rope, pieces of wrecked ships.
The Nautilus was caught. The engines screamed. The hull vibrated so violently that books flew off shelves and dishes shattered in the galley. Nemo was at the helm, his face blank, his hands steady on the controls. I couldn't tell if he was fighting the whirlpool or surrendering to it.
"Now!" Ned shouted. "It's now or never!"
We fought our way to the dinghy on the outer hull. The wind was deafening. The sea roared. Spray blinded us. Ned cut the ropes with his knife. We tumbled into the small boat as the Nautilus spun in the grip of the Maelstrom.
The last thing I saw was the black hull of the Nautilus, spiraling downward into the throat of the whirlpool, its lights still glowing, sinking into the dark.
Then the world went black.

CHAPTER 24
THE SURFACE
I woke in a fisherman's cottage on the coast of Norway.
Ned was there, bruised and battered but alive. Conseil was there too—calm as always, already organizing our few salvaged possessions into neat piles.
The fishermen told us they had found our small boat washed up on the rocks, with three half-drowned men inside. They had no idea where we had come from. We told them we had been shipwrecked. It was close enough to the truth.
For a long time, I sat by the window and stared at the sea. The gray water stretched to the horizon, cold and ordinary. Somewhere beneath that surface—if the Nautilus had survived the Maelstrom—Captain Nemo was still down there. Still exploring. Still running. Still fighting his invisible war.
Did the Nautilus survive? I don't know. The ship was built to withstand pressures that would crush any other vessel. If anyone could have navigated the Maelstrom, it was Nemo.
But I also remember the look on his face at the helm—that blank, peaceful expression. It was the look of a man who had made his decision. A man who was ready to stop running.
I wrote this account in the months that followed, working from the detailed notes I had managed to keep throughout our voyage. I have told the truth, as precisely as I can. Every depth, every creature, every wonder—it is all real. I was there. I saw it with my own eyes.
We traveled twenty thousand leagues beneath the sea. We walked the ocean floor. We visited the South Pole, explored the ruins of Atlantis, fought giant squid, and survived a voyage to the deepest point on Earth. We lived inside the greatest machine ever built, with the most extraordinary man I have ever known.
Conseil returned to his quiet life in Paris, organizing my specimens and asking no questions, as always.
Ned Land went back to Canada. He never spoke of the Nautilus again.
And I? I sit at my desk, writing these words, and I wonder. I wonder if Captain Nemo found peace at the bottom of the sea—or if he is still down there, somewhere in the endless dark, with his organ playing and his lights burning and his black flag planted on the floor of the world.
The sea keeps its secrets.
But I will keep mine, too—until the world is ready to hear them.
THE END
