A CONNECTICUT YANKEE IN KING ARTHUR'S COURT
A BedrockELA Adaptation
Based on the novel by Mark Twain (1889)
Adapted for 6th Grade Readers

CHAPTER 1
THE STRANGER'S TALE
I met the stranger at Warwick Castle in England.
He was a quiet, weathered American with gray hair and sharp eyes that seemed to be looking at something far away. The other tourists wandered through the ancient halls, admiring the armor and tapestries, but this man stood apart, studying a suit of chain mail with an expression I couldn't read — sadness, maybe, or recognition.
We fell into conversation. I mentioned that I was a writer, interested in old legends. His eyes flickered.
"Legends," he said. "I could tell you a story that would make every legend you've ever heard seem tame."
We sat by the fire that evening, and over a glass of whiskey, he told me his tale. His name was Hank Morgan, and he was from Hartford, Connecticut. He had been a foreman at the Colt Arms Factory — a practical, hardheaded Yankee who knew how to build anything, fix anything, and manage anything. He believed in science, progress, democracy, and common sense.
And he had spent several years living in the court of King Arthur.
Not a dream. Not a fantasy. He had actually been there — in sixth-century England, among knights and castles and sorcerers — and he had tried to change the world.
"You don't believe me," he said.
"I'm listening," I said.
He reached into his coat and pulled out a small, yellowed manuscript — hundreds of pages, written in his own hand. "Read this," he said. "It's all true. Every word."
I took the manuscript to my room and began to read. What follows is Hank Morgan's story, in his own words.

CHAPTER 2
KNOCKED OUT
My name is Hank Morgan, and in 1879, I was the head superintendent at the Colt Arms Factory in Hartford, Connecticut. I knew everything there was to know about machinery, manufacturing, and modern technology. I could build a steam engine from scrap metal. I could wire a telegraph. I could make gunpowder, cement, and glass. I was a practical man in a practical age.
One day, I got into an argument with a worker named Hercules — a big, stupid fellow with fists like sledgehammers. The argument turned into a fight. Hercules hit me on the head with a crowbar.
I went down. The world went black.
When I woke up, I was lying in a field of grass under an oak tree. The sun was shining. Birds were singing. A man in a full suit of armor was sitting on a horse about twenty yards away, staring at me through the slit in his helmet.
I stared back.
"Fair sir," the knight said, "will you joust?"
I was wearing my factory clothes — work boots, denim trousers, a flannel shirt. I had no horse, no weapon, and no idea what was happening.
"Joust?" I said. "What year is this?"
"It is the nineteenth of June, in the year of our Lord 528."
I laughed. Then I stopped laughing, because the knight leveled his lance at my chest and charged.
I ran. He chased me. I was faster on foot than he was on horseback — that armor slowed him down — but eventually he cornered me against a stone wall. His lance point hovered an inch from my throat.
"Yield," he said.
I yielded. He tied my hands, put a rope around my neck, and led me like a dog down a dirt road toward a cluster of towers in the distance.
The towers belonged to Camelot.

CHAPTER 3
CAMELOT
Camelot was not what the stories made it sound like.
The castle was impressive enough — massive stone walls, towers, a great hall that could hold hundreds. But the town around it was a filthy, miserable collection of mud huts and open sewers. The people were dirty, ignorant, and afraid. They dressed in rags and lived worse than the pigs back in Hartford. They bowed to every knight who rode past, not out of respect but out of terror.
I was dragged through the streets like a trophy and thrown into the great hall, where King Arthur himself sat on his throne, surrounded by his Knights of the Round Table.
Arthur was a decent-looking man — big, blond, with an honest face. He didn't seem cruel. But he was surrounded by the trappings of absolute power: the crown, the throne, the armed guards, and the courtiers who agreed with everything he said.
Merlin was there too — a skinny old man with a long beard who immediately began telling a story about Arthur pulling a sword from a stone. It was, I gathered, a story he told frequently. The knights looked bored. I felt their pain.
The knight who had captured me — Sir Kay — announced that I was a dangerous foreign enchanter. Merlin studied me with suspicious eyes and agreed.
"He must burn," Merlin declared. "At noon tomorrow."
The court murmured its agreement. Arthur nodded. And just like that, I was sentenced to death.
They stripped me to my underwear and threw me into a dungeon. The cell was dark, wet, and cold. Rats scurried in the corners. Through a tiny window, I could see the courtyard where they were building the fire that would burn me alive in twelve hours.
I sat in the dark and tried to think. I was Hank Morgan of Hartford, Connecticut. I could build a steam engine. I could wire a telegraph. Surely I could think my way out of this.
Then I remembered something. A date. A very specific date.
On June 21, 528 AD, there had been a total solar eclipse.
Tomorrow was June 21st.

CHAPTER 4
THE ECLIPSE
A boy came to my cell that night — a young page named Clarence, skinny and nervous, with big eyes and a quick mind. He had been sent to gawk at the prisoner, but I saw something useful in him: curiosity.
"What if I told you," I said, "that I am the greatest magician in the world? Greater than Merlin. Greater than any sorcerer who ever lived."
Clarence's eyes went wide. "Can you prove it?"
"Tomorrow at noon, when they light the fire to burn me, I will blot out the sun. I will turn day into night. The whole kingdom will see my power."
Clarence ran to tell the court. Within an hour, the entire castle was buzzing with fear and excitement. Could this strange foreigner really extinguish the sun?
The next day, they led me to the stake. The fire was laid. The whole court was assembled — Arthur, Merlin, the knights, hundreds of common people. Merlin was smirking. Arthur looked uneasy.
"Last chance," the executioner said.
I raised my arms to the sky and shouted in my most theatrical voice: "I call upon the powers of darkness! Let the sun be destroyed! Let eternal night fall upon England!"
Nothing happened. The crowd murmured. Merlin laughed.
Then the edge of the moon began to creep across the face of the sun.
The light dimmed. The temperature dropped. Shadows lengthened in strange directions. The crowd gasped. A woman screamed. The knights reached for their swords — but you can't fight the sky.
The eclipse deepened. The world went dark. Stars appeared in the middle of the day. People fell to their knees, weeping, praying, begging for mercy.
King Arthur rushed forward. "Name your terms! Whatever you want — freedom, gold, land — just bring back the sun!"
"I want to be your chief minister," I said. "Second only to you. And I want Merlin locked up."
"Done! Done! Anything!"
I waited. The timing had to be perfect. If I "restored" the sun too early, the effect would be weak. Too late, and they'd panic beyond reason.
At the perfect moment, I raised my arms again. "Let the sun return!"
The eclipse passed. Sunlight flooded back. The crowd erupted in cheers, tears, and terrified worship.
In five minutes, I had gone from a condemned prisoner to the most powerful person in England after the King.
Merlin was furious. The court was awed. And I was thinking: this is just the beginning.

CHAPTER 5
THE BOSS
They called me "The Boss."
Within a week, I was installed in the finest rooms in Camelot, with servants, guards, and more power than I knew what to do with. Arthur trusted me completely — partly because I had "controlled" the sun, and partly because I had a practical competence that no one else in the kingdom possessed.
I was an engineer in a world that hadn't invented the wheel yet. I was a factory foreman in an age where the most advanced technology was a crossbow. I knew things that wouldn't be discovered for another thousand years: electricity, printing, metallurgy, plumbing, democratic government, basic medicine.
The question was: what should I do with all of this knowledge?
The answer, to me, was obvious. I would civilize England. I would drag it out of the Dark Ages and into the modern world. I would build factories, schools, newspapers, and railroads. I would abolish slavery, establish equal rights, and create a republic. I would do in a few years what history took thirteen centuries to accomplish.
It never occurred to me that this might be arrogant. Or dangerous. Or impossible.
Clarence became my right-hand man. He was smart, loyal, and a fast learner — the best assistant I could have hoped for. Together, we began what I privately called "the project": the secret transformation of England.
"The first thing we need," I told Clarence, "is a school. You can't build a civilization without education."
"What's a school?" Clarence asked.
We had a lot of work to do.

CHAPTER 6
THE FIRST REFORMS
I started in secret.
In hidden valleys and remote corners of the kingdom, I built factories — small ones at first, producing nails, wire, glass, and soap. I recruited the smartest young people I could find and trained them as engineers, teachers, and craftsmen. I set up schools where they learned reading, writing, mathematics, and basic science.
I strung telegraph lines through the forests, disguised as vines. I dug mines and began smelting iron. I built a small printing press and started producing books — the first printed books in England, a thousand years before Gutenberg.
All of this was done quietly, away from the eyes of the knights, the nobles, and especially the Church. The Church was the most powerful institution in the land, and it would not take kindly to someone teaching people to think for themselves.
Meanwhile, I played the role expected of me: court magician, royal advisor, Arthur's right-hand man. I attended feasts, watched tournaments, and pretended to care about which knight had defeated which other knight in single combat.
The knights bored me. They were brave, certainly, but their entire lives revolved around fighting each other for honor that meant nothing. They couldn't read. They couldn't count. They believed everything Merlin told them and feared everything the Church said. They were the ruling class of England, and they were as ignorant as the peasants they ruled.
But the peasants — the common people — were the ones who broke my heart.
They lived in hovels. They were taxed into starvation by the lords and the Church. They had no rights, no education, no hope. If a nobleman wanted their land, he took it. If the Church wanted their money, they paid. They accepted their suffering as God's will because no one had ever told them it could be different.
I was going to tell them.

CHAPTER 7
MORGAN LE FAY
Arthur sent me on a tour of the kingdom with a young woman named Aloïse la Carteloise — I called her Sandy, because I couldn't pronounce her name, and she talked so much that I needed a short word to interrupt her with.
Sandy was kind, brave, and absolutely convinced that the world worked on magic. She believed in enchantments, curses, and dragons the way I believed in electricity and steam power. We disagreed about almost everything, which made the journey both exhausting and, I had to admit, interesting.
Our first stop was the castle of Morgan le Fay — Arthur's half-sister, a queen with absolute power over her lands and a reputation for cruelty.
The castle was magnificent on the outside. Inside, it was a house of horrors.
Morgan le Fay had dungeons full of prisoners — people locked up for years for offenses so minor they would be laughable in Hartford. A man imprisoned for twenty years because he accidentally bumped into a nobleman's horse. A woman chained to a wall because she had spoken without permission. A family who had been in the dungeon so long they had forgotten what the sun looked like.
I freed them all. Morgan le Fay was furious, but she was afraid of me — afraid of "The Boss," the man who had eclipsed the sun. She let me do it.
But as we rode away, I felt something I hadn't expected: not triumph, but anger. These people had been suffering for years, and no one — not Arthur, not the knights, not anyone — had cared enough to check.
The system wasn't just inefficient. It was evil. And the people at the top — the kings, the nobles, the Church — were either causing the evil or ignoring it.
I was going to tear the whole thing down and build something better.
Sandy looked at me as we rode. "You are troubled, Boss."
"I'm furious," I said.
"That is not the same thing," she said quietly. And she was right.

CHAPTER 8
THE SMALLPOX HUT
The worst thing I saw on the journey was a hut by the roadside.
Inside were a woman and her daughter, both dying of smallpox. Their skin was covered in sores. They were burning with fever. No one in the village would come near them — smallpox was a death sentence, and people believed it was God's punishment for sin.
There was no medicine. There was no doctor. There was nothing I could do.
I sat with them. Sandy sat with me. We gave them water and what comfort we could. The mother died that night. The daughter died the next morning.
I buried them myself, in the field behind the hut, and I stood over the graves and thought about all the things I knew. I could build a steam engine. I could wire a telegraph. I could blow up a castle with dynamite. But I couldn't save a woman and her child from a disease that, in my own time, had been nearly eliminated by a simple vaccine.
All my knowledge, all my technology, all my grand plans — and I couldn't prevent a mother from dying in a roadside hut.
Sandy touched my arm. "You cannot save everyone, Boss."
"I know," I said. "But I can try to build a world where fewer people die like this."
That night I wrote plans for a medical school, a sanitation system, and a public health program. None of it would come in time for the woman and her daughter. But it might come in time for someone else's mother. Someone else's child.

CHAPTER 9
THE TOURNAMENT
Back at Camelot, I was challenged to a tournament.
Sir Sagramor had taken offense at something I'd said — I honestly couldn't remember what — and demanded satisfaction through combat. In the medieval world, this meant a joust: two men in armor, on horseback, charging at each other with lances.
I didn't own armor. I didn't know how to ride a war horse. And I had absolutely no intention of letting a man in a tin suit poke me with a pole.
So I improvised.
When the day came, I rode into the lists wearing ordinary clothes, sitting on a regular horse, carrying a lasso. The crowd was stunned. The knights were offended. Arthur looked nervous.
Sir Sagramor charged. His lance was leveled at my chest. His horse thundered across the field.
I dodged. As he swept past, I threw the lasso, caught him around the shoulders, and yanked him clean off his horse. He crashed to the ground in a spectacular clatter of armor and lay there like an overturned turtle.
The crowd went insane. Half of them were outraged. The other half were laughing so hard they could barely stand.
One by one, other knights challenged me. One by one, I lassoed them out of their saddles. The great champions of Camelot — men who had spent their entire lives training for mounted combat — were beaten by a factory foreman with a rope.
Merlin watched from the stands, his face dark with fury. He whispered to the knights. He whispered to the Church. He was planting seeds that would bear terrible fruit later.
But for now, I was invincible. The Boss had beaten the best knights in England, and he had done it with common sense and a length of rope.

CHAPTER 10
THE HOLY FOUNTAIN
The monks at the Valley of Holiness had a problem. Their holy fountain — a sacred spring that pilgrims came from miles to see — had stopped flowing. Merlin had been trying to fix it for days with spells and incantations, and nothing worked.
They sent for me.
I examined the fountain and found the problem immediately: the underground pipe that fed the spring had developed a leak. It was basic plumbing — a problem I could fix in half a day with some cement and a wrench.
But I couldn't just fix it. If I simply repaired a pipe, people would see a man doing plumbing. I needed them to see magic — a miracle greater than anything Merlin could produce.
So I put on a show.
I spent two days building suspense. I had Clarence set up fireworks — rockets, sparklers, colored flames — hidden around the fountain. I dressed in dramatic robes. I chanted nonsense in a deep voice. I waved my arms and drew strange symbols in the air.
Then, at the climactic moment — with hundreds of monks and pilgrims watching — I secretly opened the repaired pipe, and the water burst forth in a glorious spray. At the same instant, Clarence set off the fireworks.
The fountain erupted. Colored fire blazed in the sky. The crowd fell to their knees, weeping with joy.
My reputation soared. Merlin's reputation crumbled. But as I stood there, watching the monks worship a plumbing repair, I felt uncomfortable. I had beaten Merlin by being a better showman, not a better man. I had manipulated people's ignorance instead of curing it.
That was supposed to be Merlin's job, not mine.

CHAPTER 11
MERLIN'S TOWER
Merlin challenged me directly. He announced that his tower — a massive stone structure at the edge of Camelot — was protected by the most powerful enchantments in the world. No force could destroy it.
I destroyed it with dynamite.
The explosion was spectacular — a thunderclap that shook the ground, followed by a column of dust and rubble that rose a hundred feet in the air. When the dust settled, Merlin's tower was a pile of broken stone.
The old wizard stood in the courtyard, his mouth hanging open, his reputation in ruins. The crowd stared at me with a mixture of awe and terror.
I had won. Merlin was finished as a rival. But the look on his face wasn't just defeat — it was hatred. Deep, patient, burning hatred.
"You haven't seen the last of me," his eyes said. And I should have listened. Because Merlin was old, and he was a fraud, but he understood something I didn't: this world operated on different rules than mine. Science and technology could build things, but they couldn't change what people believed in their hearts.
The Church was still the most powerful force in England. The nobles still held all the land. The people still bowed to authority. My factories and schools were growing, but they were growing in the shadows, and shadows don't last forever.
I was building a revolution. But I had forgotten the most important lesson of history: revolutions have enemies.

CHAPTER 12
THE YANKEE AND THE KING
I decided Arthur needed to see his own kingdom — not from the back of a war horse, surrounded by knights, but from the ground, on foot, as a common man.
"You've never seen how your people actually live," I told him.
"I know how they live. They live as God intends."
"With all due respect, Your Majesty, God doesn't intend for families to starve while the Church takes a third of their crops."
Arthur was offended. But he was also honest — one of the most genuinely honest men I had ever met. He agreed to travel in disguise, as a peasant, to see the truth for himself.
We dressed in rough clothing, smeared dirt on our faces, and set out on foot into the English countryside. No guards, no servants, no royal banner.
Arthur was terrible at being a peasant. He walked like a king — shoulders back, chin up, eyes commanding. He spoke to farmers as if addressing his court. He couldn't understand why people didn't immediately defer to him.
"You have to slump," I said. "Look at the ground. Mumble. Act like the world has beaten you down."
"But the world hasn't beaten me down."
"It has beaten them down. That's the point."
Slowly, reluctantly, Arthur began to see. He saw villages where every family owed half their food to a lord who never visited. He saw children working in fields instead of playing. He saw a man whipped for hunting a rabbit on land his family had lived on for generations.
"Why does he not appeal to his lord for justice?" Arthur asked.
"His lord is the one who ordered the whipping," I said.
Arthur went quiet. He stayed quiet for a long time. And I could see something working behind his eyes — the slow, painful realization that the world he ruled was not the world he thought it was.

CHAPTER 13
DISGUISED AS PEASANTS
Arthur tried his best, but he was the worst peasant in England.
When a nobleman rode past, Arthur instinctively stepped into the road and stood tall, expecting the rider to stop. Instead, the nobleman's servant lashed Arthur across the shoulders with a riding whip.
Arthur stared in disbelief. "He struck me."
"He struck a peasant," I said. "That's what happens to peasants."
"But I am the King!"
"Right now you're a nobody in dirty clothes. This is what your kingdom feels like from the bottom."
The lesson sank in — painfully, literally. Arthur was kicked, shoved, cursed, and ignored. He slept on the ground. He ate scraps. He saw a family evicted from their home for failing to pay taxes they couldn't possibly afford.
One evening, we sheltered with a charcoal burner and his wife — the poorest people I had ever met. They owned nothing except the clothes on their backs and a single iron pot. They shared their food with us without hesitation — thin soup with no meat, served in wooden bowls.
Arthur ate the soup and stared at the fire. He was beginning to understand something that I had been trying to explain since the day I arrived: the system was the problem. Not individual bad lords, not lazy peasants — the system. A system where one class of people had everything and another class had nothing, and the law existed to keep it that way.
"When I return to Camelot," Arthur said quietly, "things will change."
I believed him. He was a good man — better than most kings in any century. But one good man at the top of a bad system doesn't fix the system. It just makes the system feel slightly less awful while continuing to operate exactly as before.
I didn't say this to Arthur. He was having enough revelations for one week.

CHAPTER 14
SOLD INTO SLAVERY
Our disguise worked too well.
On the road near a market town, we were ambushed by slave traders — men who captured peasants and sold them at market. It was perfectly legal in Arthur's England. Slavery was one of the institutions I had been quietly working to abolish, but I hadn't gotten there yet.
The traders tied our hands, chained us to a line of other captives, and marched us to the nearest slave market. Arthur was outraged. "I am the King!" he shouted. The traders laughed.
"Every slave says something like that," the head trader said. "Last week I had a pig farmer who claimed to be the Pope."
We marched for three days in chains, sleeping on the ground, eating whatever the traders threw at us. Arthur's dignity was stripped away, layer by layer. He was no longer a king. He was property.
I had a plan — I always had a plan — but I needed time. I had secretly sent Clarence a message before we were captured, using one of my hidden telegraph stations. Help was coming. I just had to keep us alive until it arrived.
The hardest part was watching Arthur. He had never been powerless before. He had never been hungry, cold, and afraid with no one to help him. The experience was breaking something inside him — not his spirit, but his illusions. Every day, another piece of the world as he thought it was fell away, replaced by the world as it actually was.

CHAPTER 15
THE SLAVE MARKET
The slave market was held in a muddy field outside a town whose name I never learned. Dozens of people were being bought and sold — men, women, and children, standing on wooden platforms while buyers examined their teeth and muscles like they were livestock.
Arthur and I were put on the block together. The auctioneer called us "two able-bodied men, good for field work." Arthur, the King of England, was sold for seven dollars.
I was sold separately for nine dollars, which I found insulting — but at least it was more than Arthur.
The man who bought Arthur was a farmer who needed field hands. He put Arthur to work immediately, hauling rocks and digging ditches. The King of England, who had never done manual labor in his life, worked in the mud until his hands bled.
Arthur did not complain. That surprised me. He worked, and he watched, and he listened. He talked to the other slaves — men and women who had been in bondage for years. He heard their stories. He saw their scars.
One woman had been separated from her children five years ago. She didn't know if they were alive. A boy of twelve had been sold three times since he was eight. An old man had been free once, lost his land to debt, and been enslaved by his own creditor.
"This is legal," Arthur said to me that night. "In my kingdom, this is legal."
"Yes," I said.
"I will abolish it," he said. "I swear on my crown."
That was the moment I truly respected Arthur. Not because he was a king, but because he could look at a system that served him and say: this is wrong.

CHAPTER 16
THE RESCUE
They came on bicycles.
Five hundred of my trained men, equipped with the latest products of my secret factories, riding bicycles down the road at speeds that no horse in England could match. They wore uniforms I had designed — practical, efficient, nothing like the ridiculous armor the knights clanked around in.
Clarence was at the head of the column, grinning like a boy at Christmas.
The slave traders fled. The slave owners ran. Arthur's buyer dropped his whip and hid behind a haystack. In ten minutes, every slave in the market was freed, and every chain was broken.
Arthur stood in the muddy field, his hands still raw from digging, and watched my men with wonder. "You built this," he said.
"We built it," I said. "You and me and Clarence and every person who decided that the world could be better than it is."
We rode back to Camelot — Arthur on a horse, me on a bicycle, Clarence beside us, explaining everything that had happened while we were gone. The factories had expanded. The schools had more students. The telegraph network now connected every major town. A railroad was being planned.
Arthur listened. He asked intelligent questions. And for the first time since I'd arrived in the sixth century, I felt genuinely hopeful that my revolution might work.

CHAPTER 17
THREE YEARS OF PROGRESS
The next three years were the best of my time in Camelot.
With Arthur's full support, I brought my secret projects into the open. Factories appeared across England, producing iron, steel, glass, and textiles. Schools opened in every major town. A military academy trained a new kind of soldier — educated, disciplined, loyal to the nation rather than to individual lords.
I built roads — real roads, paved and drained, not the muddy tracks that passed for transportation. I established a postal service. I introduced basic sanitation: clean water, sewage systems, the radical concept of washing your hands.
The results were dramatic. Disease dropped. Trade increased. Literacy spread. For the first time in English history, a common farmer could read a book, write a letter, and understand that the earth was round.
Not everyone was happy. The nobles saw their power eroding and resented it. The Church saw its authority challenged and feared it. Merlin, humiliated but not destroyed, whispered poison into every ear that would listen.
But for three years, the forces of progress outweighed the forces of resistance. England was changing — not just on the surface, but in the minds of its people. They were beginning to think. To question. To imagine a world where birth didn't determine destiny.
I was proud of what we had built. Too proud, probably. I forgot that the old world doesn't die quietly. It fights back.

CHAPTER 18
THE NEWSPAPER
I introduced journalism because I believed that an informed public was the foundation of democracy. A free press would hold the powerful accountable, educate the masses, and give voice to the voiceless.
I was right about all of that. What I didn't anticipate was that a free press also publishes gossip, lies, and inflammatory nonsense that can tear a society apart.
The newspapers I founded — I started four of them — quickly developed their own personalities. One was serious and thoughtful. One was sensational and loved scandal. One was controlled by the Church and attacked everything I did. And one was run by a former knight who used it entirely to settle personal grudges.
The result was chaos. People who had never had access to information were suddenly drowning in it, with no way to tell truth from fiction. Rumors spread faster than facts. Political arguments erupted in every tavern. The carefully balanced coalition I had built — between the crown, the new middle class, and the common people — began to crack under the pressure of competing narratives.
"Maybe the newspapers were a mistake," Clarence said.
"The newspapers aren't the mistake," I said. "The mistake is thinking people will use freedom wisely just because you give it to them. Freedom is a skill. It has to be learned."
It was the most important thing I said during my entire time in Camelot. And I was only beginning to understand what it meant.

CHAPTER 19
HANK AND SANDY
Somewhere in the middle of all this revolution and reform, I married Sandy.
I hadn't planned on it. I hadn't planned on falling in love with a woman from the sixth century who believed in dragons and couldn't pronounce "Connecticut." But Sandy was brave, kind, and honest in a way that made my carefully constructed certainties feel small.
She didn't understand my technology. She didn't care about my factories. What she cared about was people — the specific, individual human beings who were affected by the changes I was making. When I talked about "progress" in the abstract, Sandy talked about the woman in the village who could now read, the child who didn't die of fever, the old man who was no longer a slave.
She made me better. She made me see that changing the world isn't about systems and machines — it's about people.
We had a daughter. We named her Hello-Central, because I missed telephones. Sandy thought it was a beautiful name. I didn't have the heart to explain.
Late at night, lying in our bed in Camelot, I sometimes thought about Hartford. About the factory, the crowbar, the life I had left behind. Was I dreaming? Was any of this real? Would I wake up one morning and find myself back in 1879, with nothing but a headache and a strange story?
Sandy would reach for my hand in the dark, and the warmth of her fingers was the most real thing I had ever known.

CHAPTER 20
THE STOCK EXCHANGE
My revolution reached its peak with the creation of the stock exchange.
I had transformed England's economy from a medieval feudal system into something approaching modern capitalism. Factories produced goods. Merchants traded them. A banking system provided credit. And the stock exchange — a marketplace where shares in companies could be bought and sold — was the capstone.
The knights, predictably, were confused. They didn't understand money. They understood land, honor, and sword fights. The idea that wealth could exist on a piece of paper was beyond them.
But the merchants understood. The craftsmen understood. The newly educated middle class — the people I had created — understood perfectly. They bought and sold shares. They invested. They built businesses.
For a brief, shining moment, I thought I had done it. I had dragged England out of the Dark Ages and into the modern world. I had proven that democracy and technology could triumph over ignorance and tyranny.
Then the Church made its move.

CHAPTER 21
THE INTERDICT
The word hit like a thunderbolt: interdict.
The Church — the most powerful institution in medieval England, more powerful than the King, more powerful than any army — declared an interdict against Hank Morgan and all his works. An interdict was a religious ban — a decree that cut a person, or an entire nation, off from the sacraments. No baptisms. No marriages. No last rites. No salvation.
In a world where people genuinely believed that their eternal soul depended on the Church's approval, an interdict was the ultimate weapon. It didn't attack bodies. It attacked souls.
Overnight, everything changed. The factories emptied. The schools closed. The telegraph operators walked away from their stations. The merchants shuttered their shops. The soldiers — my soldiers, the men I had trained — deserted.
Not because they wanted to. Because they were terrified. The Church told them that supporting Hank Morgan meant eternal damnation. Their wives begged them to stop. Their parents disowned them. Their children were refused baptism.
I stood in my empty factory, listening to the silence, and understood what I had failed to see: I had changed the surface of England, but I hadn't changed its heart. Technology can build roads and bridges, but it can't build faith. Education can teach people to read, but it can't tell them what to believe. I had given them tools, but I hadn't given them the courage to use those tools against the oldest, deepest power in their world.
The Church was older than Arthur. Older than Camelot. Older than England itself. And it would not be displaced by a Yankee with a factory.

CHAPTER 22
THE BATTLE OF THE SAND-BELT
With the Church against me and the nobles emboldened, the combined forces of old England marched on my remaining stronghold — a fortified position I had built in a narrow valley, surrounded by trenches and wire.
I had fifty-two boys — the youngest of my trained soldiers, the ones too young or too loyal to desert. Fifty-two against thirty thousand knights in armor.
It should have been hopeless. But I had something the knights didn't: technology.
I had electric fences. I had dynamite. I had Gatling guns — rapid-fire weapons that could cut down a charging army like a scythe through wheat. I had land mines buried in the sand belt that surrounded my position.
The knights came in waves. They charged on horseback, lances leveled, pennants flying, exactly the way they had charged for centuries. They were magnificent and doomed.
The first wave hit the land mines. The explosions tore through them. The second wave hit the electric fences. The third wave was cut down by the Gatling guns.
It was a slaughter. Not a battle — a slaughter. Thousands of knights, the flower of English chivalry, died in that narrow valley. Their armor, which had protected them in a thousand tournaments, meant nothing against modern weapons. They fell in heaps, in windrows, in mountains of steel and blood.
I watched from behind the fortification, and for the first time, I did not feel clever. I felt sick.
I had wanted to bring civilization to England. Instead, I had brought the worst thing civilization had ever invented: industrial warfare. I had given the medieval world a glimpse of the future — and the future was a killing field.
The knights kept coming. They were brave — I will never deny them that. They were the bravest men I had ever seen. They charged into certain death because their code demanded it, because they didn't know how to do anything else.
By morning, it was over. Thirty thousand men lay dead in the sand belt. My fifty-two boys had won the battle.
But we had lost everything else.

CHAPTER 23
THE CAVE
The dead surrounded us. Thirty thousand bodies, decomposing in the summer heat, created a barrier we couldn't cross. We were trapped in our own fortification, prisoners of our own victory.
Disease came. One by one, my boys fell sick — poisoned by the very carnage our technology had created. I nursed them. Clarence nursed them. But they died anyway, one after another, until only Clarence and I were left.
Then Merlin came.
He appeared at our gate disguised as an old woman, and I was too tired, too sick, and too broken to see through the disguise. He entered our camp, and he laughed — a thin, triumphant cackle.
"I told you," he said. "I told you this world runs on different rules."
He cast a spell — or what he called a spell. Whether it was real magic or whether my exhausted body simply gave out, I will never know. But darkness fell over me like a curtain, and Merlin's voice was the last thing I heard:
"Sleep, Yankee. Sleep for thirteen centuries."
Clarence found Merlin dead by my side — the old wizard had stumbled into one of my electric fences and been killed by the very technology he despised. Clarence wrote the final pages of my manuscript, sealed it in the cave with my sleeping body, and walked out into a world that no longer wanted what I had tried to give it.
I slept.
For thirteen hundred years, I slept.

CHAPTER 24
THIRTEEN CENTURIES LATER
I woke in 1879, in a hospital bed, with a headache that felt like the end of the world.
They told me I had been hit on the head in a fight at the Colt Arms Factory. They told me I had been unconscious for several minutes. They told me I would be fine.
I was not fine.
Everything was gone. Sandy was gone. Hello-Central was gone. Clarence was gone. Arthur, who had been the best king a bad system ever produced, was gone. The factories, the schools, the newspapers, the brief and shining attempt to build a better world — all of it, gone, buried under thirteen centuries of history.
Had it been real? Was it a dream? The manuscript — my manuscript, the one I had written in Camelot and Clarence had sealed in the cave — I found it among my possessions, yellowed and ancient. How had it survived? How had it reached me?
I spent my remaining years as a broken man, caught between two worlds. I walked the streets of Hartford and saw Camelot. I heard the factory whistles and remembered the sound of knights charging. I reached for Sandy in the night and found only empty air.
The stranger at Warwick Castle finished his story and fell silent. His eyes were focused on something I couldn't see — some distant, impossible place, thirteen centuries and an ocean away.
"Was it real?" I asked.
He didn't answer. He reached out his hand, as if grasping for something, and whispered: "Sandy..."
Then Hank Morgan, the Connecticut Yankee who had tried to change the world, closed his eyes. His hand fell. And his story was over.
Whether he succeeded or failed — whether his revolution was a dream or a tragedy — is a question I leave to you.
But I will say this: he tried. In a world of darkness and ignorance and cruelty, he tried to build something better. He used everything he had — his knowledge, his courage, his stubborn American optimism — and he tried.
That, I think, is enough. That has to be enough.
THE END
