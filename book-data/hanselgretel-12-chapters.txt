Chapter 1: Hard Times
Long ago, at the edge of a great forest, there lived a poor woodcutter with his wife and two children. The boy was named Hansel, and his sister was called Gretel.
The family had very little money and barely enough food to eat. The father worked hard every day, chopping wood to sell in the nearby village.
Hansel was eleven years old and very clever. Gretel was nine and always looked after her older brother, even though she was younger. They loved each other dearly.
Their mother had died when they were very small, and their father had remarried. Their stepmother was not kind to the children and often complained that they ate too much food.
One terrible year, there was very little rain. The crops in the fields dried up, and many people in the village went hungry. Food became scarce and expensive.
'We barely have enough bread for ourselves,' the stepmother grumbled every day. 'How can we possibly feed two children as well?'
The father loved Hansel and Gretel very much and tried his best to provide for them. But as winter approached, their situation became more and more desperate.

Chapter 2: The Terrible Plan
One night, Hansel and Gretel heard their parents talking quietly in the next room. The children should have been asleep, but they were too hungry to rest peacefully.
'We have only half a loaf of bread left,' said the stepmother. 'After that is gone, we will all starve. There is only one solution.'
'What do you mean?' asked the father, though he sounded worried about what his wife might suggest.
'Tomorrow morning, we must take the children deep into the forest and leave them there,' the stepmother said coldly. 'Then there will be enough food for the two of us.'
'I cannot abandon my children!' cried the father. 'They would never find their way home! Wild animals might hurt them!'
'Then we will all die of hunger,' replied the stepmother firmly. 'You must choose - save the children and lose everyone, or save yourself and me.'
Hansel and Gretel held hands tightly in their bed, tears rolling down their faces. How could their father agree to such a horrible plan?

Chapter 3: The Trail of Stones
While Gretel cried softly, Hansel tried to think of a plan to save them. 'Don't worry, little sister,' he whispered. 'I won't let anything happen to us.'
Late that night, when everyone was asleep, Hansel crept outside into the moonlight. The ground was covered with small white pebbles that glowed like silver coins.
Hansel filled his pockets with as many pebbles as he could carry. Then he quietly returned to bed, feeling more hopeful about their chances.
The next morning, the stepmother woke the children early. 'Get up!' she said. 'We're going into the forest to gather wood. Here's a piece of bread for each of you.'
As they walked deeper and deeper into the forest, Hansel secretly dropped white pebbles behind them. Every few steps, he would let another pebble fall to the ground.
'Why do you keep stopping and looking back?' asked his father.
'I'm looking at my white kitten,' Hansel replied cleverly. 'It's sitting on our roof, saying goodbye to me.' He didn't want his father to discover his plan.

Chapter 4: Finding the Way Home
After walking for many hours, they reached the very heart of the forest. The father built a fire and told the children to rest while he and their stepmother gathered wood.
'Stay here and don't wander off,' the stepmother said. 'We'll come back for you when we're finished working.' But Hansel and Gretel noticed the sadness in their father's eyes.
The children ate their small pieces of bread and waited by the fire. As the day grew late, they realized that their parents were not coming back.
'Are we really lost?' asked Gretel, her voice shaking with fear. 'How will we ever find our way home?'
'Don't be afraid,' said Hansel, hugging his sister. 'When the moon comes up, I'll show you how we can get home safely.'
When the full moon rose high in the sky, it shone down on the white pebbles that Hansel had dropped. They glowed like a path of stars leading through the dark forest.
Hand in hand, the children followed the trail of pebbles all night long. By morning, they had found their way back to their father's house.

Chapter 5: The Trail of Breadcrumbs
When their father saw Hansel and Gretel at the door, he was overjoyed. 'My dear children!' he cried, hugging them tightly. 'I was so worried about you!'
The stepmother was not happy to see them return, but she pretended to be glad. 'Oh, you found your way home!' she said with a false smile.
For a few weeks, the family stayed together. But soon the food ran out again, and the stepmother began complaining louder than ever.
'We must try again,' she told the father. 'This time, we'll take them even deeper into the forest where they can never find their way back.'
Once again, Hansel heard their plan. He tried to sneak outside to collect pebbles, but the stepmother had locked the door. There was no way to gather stones this time.
The next morning, they were given even smaller pieces of bread than before. As they walked into the forest, Hansel crumbled his bread and dropped the pieces behind them.
'Why do you keep stopping?' asked the stepmother suspiciously.
'I'm looking at my little dove,' Hansel said. 'It's sitting on our roof, saying goodbye.' He hoped his plan with the breadcrumbs would work as well as the pebbles had.

Chapter 6: Lost in the Forest
This time, the parents took them much deeper into the forest than before. They walked for most of the day until they reached a place the children had never seen.
Once again, the father built a fire and told them to wait. 'Rest here while we work,' he said sadly. 'We'll return before dark.' But Hansel and Gretel knew he was lying.
As night fell, the children waited for the moon to rise so they could follow their trail of breadcrumbs home. But when the moonlight came, they made a terrible discovery.
The breadcrumbs were gone! Birds and forest creatures had eaten every single piece. There was no trail to follow back home.
'What will we do now?' cried Gretel. 'We're truly lost! We'll never see our father again!'
'We must be brave,' said Hansel, though he was frightened too. 'Tomorrow, we'll look for a path out of the forest. Maybe we'll find someone who can help us.'
The children spent a cold, scary night huddled together under a big tree. They heard owls hooting and wolves howling in the distance.

Chapter 7: The Gingerbread House
For three days, Hansel and Gretel wandered through the dark forest. They ate berries and nuts when they could find them, but they grew weaker and more tired.
On the third day, they saw a beautiful white bird singing in a tree. The bird's song was so lovely that they decided to follow it deeper into the woods.
The white bird led them to a clearing where the most amazing house stood. The walls were made of gingerbread, and the roof was covered with frosting and candy.
The windows were made of clear sugar, and gumdrops decorated the doorway. Candy canes lined the garden path, and lollipops grew like flowers.
'It looks good enough to eat!' gasped Gretel, her stomach growling with hunger.
'Let's try just a little piece,' suggested Hansel. He broke off a piece of the gingerbread wall, while Gretel nibbled on a candy windowsill.
Suddenly, a voice called from inside the house: 'Nibble, nibble, like a mouse! Who is eating at my house?'

Chapter 8: The Hungry Witch
The door of the gingerbread house opened, and out came a very old woman. She had a crooked nose, beady red eyes, and long gray hair that hung in tangles.
'Oh, you poor little children!' the old woman said sweetly. 'Come inside, come inside! You must be cold and hungry after wandering in the forest.'
The children were too hungry and tired to be suspicious. The old woman led them into her house, which smelled like baking bread and sweet treats.
She gave them a wonderful meal of pancakes with honey, apples, nuts, and fresh milk. Then she tucked them into two little beds with clean white sheets.
'Sleep well, my dears,' the old woman said. But what the children didn't know was that she was really a wicked witch who ate children!
The witch only pretended to be kind. Her house was a trap to catch lost children. Her eyes were too weak to see well, but her nose could smell children from far away.
'These two will make a delicious dinner,' the witch thought to herself as she watched them sleep. 'But first, I must make them nice and fat.'

Chapter 9: Trapped
The next morning, the witch grabbed Hansel before he was fully awake and locked him in a cage behind the house. The cage was so small that he could barely move.
'Help me!' cried Hansel. 'Let me out of here!'
'You're staying right there,' cackled the witch, 'until you're fat enough to eat! And you,' she said, pointing at Gretel, 'will help me cook and clean!'
Every day, the witch fed Hansel the richest foods - cakes, pies, roasted meat, and sweet puddings. She wanted to make him as plump as possible.
'Stick out your finger,' the witch would say each morning. 'I want to feel if you're fat enough to cook yet.'
But clever Hansel always stuck out a thin chicken bone instead of his finger. The witch's eyes were so poor that she couldn't tell the difference.
'Still too skinny!' the witch would grumble. 'I must feed you more!' Meanwhile, poor Gretel had to scrub floors, wash dishes, and tend the fire from morning until night.

Chapter 10: Gretel's Clever Plan
After four weeks, the witch grew tired of waiting. 'Fat or thin,' she declared, 'I'm going to cook that boy today! Gretel, light the oven and make it very hot!'
Gretel's heart pounded with fear, but she tried to think of a way to save her brother. She pretended to have trouble with the oven.
'I don't know how to tell if the oven is hot enough,' Gretel said innocently. 'Could you show me how to check?'
'Stupid girl!' snapped the witch. 'The opening is big enough - look, I can fit inside myself!' She stuck her head and shoulders into the oven to demonstrate.
This was exactly what Gretel had hoped for. With all her strength, she gave the witch a hard push, sending her tumbling completely into the hot oven.
Quickly, Gretel slammed the oven door shut and locked it tight. The wicked witch was trapped inside, and she could never hurt another child again.
'Hansel! Hansel!' cried Gretel, running to the cage. 'The witch is gone! We're free!' She found the key and unlocked her brother's prison.

Chapter 11: The Great Escape
'My brave little sister!' said Hansel, hugging Gretel tightly. 'You saved our lives! How can I ever thank you?'
Before leaving the witch's house, the children decided to look for anything that might help them on their journey home.
In the witch's bedroom, they found boxes and chests filled with pearls, diamonds, emeralds, and gold coins. The witch had stolen these treasures from other victims.
'Let's take some of these,' said Hansel. 'If we ever find our way home, we can help our father.' They filled their pockets with jewels and gold.
Then they ran away from the gingerbread house as fast as their legs could carry them. They wanted to get as far away as possible from that terrible place.
After walking for several hours, they came to a wide river. There was no bridge to cross it, and the water looked too deep and dangerous to swim across.
'How will we get to the other side?' wondered Gretel. Just then, a beautiful white duck came swimming toward them.
'Please, kind duck,' called Gretel politely, 'will you help us cross this river? We're trying to find our way home to our father.'

Chapter 12: Home at Last
The friendly white duck agreed to help them. First, she carried Hansel safely across the river on her back. Then she returned for Gretel.
'Thank you, dear duck!' called the children as they waved goodbye from the other shore.
On the far side of the river, the forest looked familiar again. The children recognized trees and paths they had seen before. Their hearts filled with hope and joy.
After walking for most of the day, they finally saw smoke rising from a chimney in the distance. It was their father's house!
Their father was sitting outside, looking very sad and lonely. When he saw Hansel and Gretel running toward him, he could hardly believe his eyes.
'My children! My dear children!' he cried, running to meet them. 'I've missed you so much! I was so sorry that I left you in the forest!'
'Where is our stepmother?' asked Gretel. 'We have wonderful news to share!'
'She left months ago,' said their father sadly. 'After you disappeared, I couldn't bear to live with someone who had made me abandon my own children.'
Then Hansel and Gretel showed their father the witch's treasure. There were enough jewels and gold to keep their family comfortable for the rest of their lives.
From that day forward, the three of them lived happily together. They never went hungry again, and the father never let anyone convince him to abandon his beloved children.

The End
