
Alice's Adventures in
Wonderland
A BedrockELA Adaptation
Based on the novel by Lewis Carroll (1865)
Adapted for 4th–5th Grade Readers
 
 
Chapter 1: Down the Rabbit Hole

Alice was beginning to get very tired of sitting by her sister on the riverbank, and of having nothing to do.
Once or twice she had peeked into the book her sister was reading, but it had no pictures or conversations in it. "And what is the use of a book," thought Alice, "without pictures or conversations?"
She was considering in her own mind (as well as she could, for the hot day made her feel very sleepy) whether the pleasure of making a daisy-chain would be worth the trouble of getting up to pick the daisies, when suddenly a White Rabbit with pink eyes ran close by her.
There was nothing so VERY remarkable in that. Alice did not even think it was strange when she heard the Rabbit say to itself, "Oh dear! Oh dear! I shall be late!"
But when the Rabbit actually took a watch out of its waistcoat pocket, looked at it, and then hurried on, Alice jumped to her feet. She had never before seen a rabbit wearing a waistcoat, or carrying a pocket watch, or talking. Curious — extremely curious.
Without thinking about it for another moment, Alice ran after the Rabbit across the field and saw it pop down a large rabbit hole under the hedge. In another moment, Alice went down after it, never once considering how in the world she would get out again.
The rabbit hole went straight on like a tunnel for some way, and then dipped suddenly down — so suddenly that Alice had no time to think about stopping herself before she found herself falling down what seemed to be a very deep well.
Either the well was very deep, or she fell very slowly, for she had plenty of time to look about her as she went down. First, she tried to look down and make out what she was coming to, but it was too dark to see anything. Then she looked at the sides of the well and noticed that they were filled with cupboards and bookshelves. Here and there she saw maps and pictures hung upon pegs. She took down a jar from one of the shelves as she passed. It was labeled "ORANGE MARMALADE," but to her great disappointment it was empty. She did not want to drop the jar for fear of killing somebody underneath, so she managed to put it into one of the cupboards as she fell past it.
"Well!" thought Alice to herself. "After a fall like this, I shall think nothing of tumbling downstairs! How brave they'll all think me at home!"
Down, down, down. Would the fall NEVER come to an end?
"I wonder how many miles I've fallen by this time?" she said aloud. "I must be getting somewhere near the center of the earth. Let me see — that would be four thousand miles down, I think." Alice had no idea whether this was right, but she enjoyed saying clever things whether anyone was listening or not.
Down, down, down. There was nothing else to do, so Alice soon began talking again. "Dinah will miss me very much tonight, I should think!" Dinah was the cat. "I hope they'll remember her saucer of milk at tea-time."
And then — THUMP. She landed on a heap of dry leaves and sticks, and the fall was over.
Alice was not a bit hurt, and she jumped up on her feet in a moment. She looked up, but it was all dark overhead. Before her was another long passage, and the White Rabbit was hurrying down it. Away went Alice like the wind, and was just in time to hear it say, as it turned a corner, "Oh my ears and whiskers, how late it's getting!"
She turned the corner and found herself in a long, low hall, lit by a row of lamps hanging from the ceiling. There were doors all around the hall, but they were all locked. Alice walked sadly down the middle, wondering how she was ever to get out again.
Suddenly, she came upon a little three-legged table, all made of solid glass. There was nothing on it except a tiny golden key. Alice tried the key in all the doors, but it would not fit any of them. Then she noticed a small door, about fifteen inches high, hidden behind a low curtain she had not seen before. She tried the golden key in the lock, and to her great delight it fit!
Alice opened the door and found that it led into a small passage, not much larger than a rat-hole. She knelt down and looked along it into the loveliest garden you ever saw. Bright flowers bloomed in every direction, and cool fountains sparkled in the sunlight. But Alice could not fit through the tiny door. She was far too large.
"Oh, how I wish I could shut up like a telescope!" Alice said. "I think I could, if I only knew how to begin."
On the glass table, she found a small bottle with a label that read: "DRINK ME."
Alice was a sensible girl. She was not going to drink something just because a label told her to. She checked to make sure it was not marked "poison." It was not. So she tasted it.
It was delicious — a sort of mixed flavor of cherry-tart, custard, pineapple, roast turkey, toffee, and hot buttered toast.
And she was shrinking.
Down, down, down she went — not falling this time, but shrinking, getting smaller and smaller until she was just ten inches tall. The right size for the little door!
"What a curious feeling!" said Alice. "I must be shutting up like a telescope."
She hurried to the little door. But the door was locked, and the golden key was up on the glass table, far above her head. She could see it clearly through the glass, but she could not possibly reach it. She tried to climb one of the table legs, but it was too slippery.
Alice sat down and cried.
"Come, there's no use in crying like that!" she said to herself rather sharply. "I advise you to leave off this minute!" She generally gave herself very good advice, though she very seldom followed it.
Then she noticed a very small cake on the floor, with the words "EAT ME" beautifully marked on it in currants.
"Well, I'll eat it," said Alice, "and if it makes me grow larger, I can reach the key; and if it makes me grow smaller, I can creep under the door. Either way, I'll get into the garden, so it doesn't matter which happens."
She ate a little bit and waited to see what would happen.
 
Chapter 2: The Pool of Tears

"Curiouser and curiouser!" cried Alice. She was so surprised that for the moment she quite forgot how to speak proper English. "Now I'm opening out like the largest telescope that ever was! Goodbye, feet!"
She was growing. Rapidly. Her head hit the ceiling. Her feet were so far away she could barely see them. She was now more than nine feet tall, and still growing.
She grabbed the little golden key from the table — she could reach it easily now — but of course, at this size, the little door was useless. She could not even fit her eye against the keyhole. She was enormous, and the beautiful garden was more impossible than ever.
Alice sat down and began to cry again. She cried and cried, great splashing tears that rolled down her cheeks and pooled on the floor. Being nine feet tall meant her tears were proportionally large, and within minutes, she was sitting in a pool of salt water about four inches deep.
"I shall be punished for it now, I suppose, by being drowned in my own tears!" she said. "That WILL be a queer thing, to be sure! However, everything is queer today."
Just then, she heard the pattering of small feet, and the White Rabbit appeared again, trotting across the hall, muttering to itself.
"Oh! The Duchess, the Duchess! Oh! Won't she be savage if I've kept her waiting!" the Rabbit fretted.
"If you please, sir —" Alice began, in a low, timid voice.
The Rabbit looked up, saw the enormous girl looming above him, gave a terrified squeak, dropped the white kid gloves and the fan it was carrying, and scurried away into the darkness as fast as its legs could carry it.
Alice picked up the fan and began fanning herself. She was very hot and confused and enormous, and fanning seemed like the only sensible thing to do.
"Dear, dear!" she said. "How queer everything is today! And yesterday things went on just as usual. I wonder if I've been changed in the night? Let me think: was I the same when I got up this morning? I almost think I can remember feeling a little different. But if I'm not the same, the next question is, Who in the world am I? Ah, THAT'S the great puzzle!"
She tried to remember things she knew — multiplication tables, geography, poetry — to make sure she was still herself. But everything came out wrong. Four times five was not twelve, and London was not the capital of Paris, and the poems she had memorized came out with all the words changed.
"I'm sure those are not the right words," said Alice, and her eyes filled with tears again. "I must be somebody else entirely. But WHO?"
She noticed she was getting smaller again. The fan! It was the fan that was shrinking her. She threw it away just in time, or she might have disappeared entirely.
She was now about two feet tall and still shrinking — but she slipped on the wet floor and SPLASH! She was up to her chin in salt water.
"I wish I hadn't cried so much!" said Alice, paddling about and trying to find her way out. "I shall be punished for it now, I suppose."
She was swimming in her own tears. They had become a pool — a salty, ridiculous pool created by her own enormous crying.
She was not alone in the pool. A Mouse was swimming near her, paddling with tiny, desperate strokes.
"O Mouse!" Alice called out. "Do you know the way out of this pool? I am very tired of swimming."
The Mouse looked at her with suspicion but said nothing.
"Perhaps it doesn't understand English," Alice thought. "I daresay it's a French mouse." So she tried the first sentence in her French lesson-book: "Où est ma chatte?"
This, unfortunately, meant "Where is my cat?" The Mouse gave a great leap out of the water and trembled with fright.
"Oh, I beg your pardon!" cried Alice hastily, afraid of hurting the poor animal's feelings. "I quite forgot you wouldn't like cats."
"Would YOU like cats if you were my size?" the Mouse demanded, in a voice that was surprisingly deep and indignant for such a small creature.
"Perhaps not," Alice said in a soothing tone. "I promise not to mention them again."
They swam to the edge of the pool together and climbed out, dripping wet, onto dry ground, where they found a whole crowd of strange creatures that had fallen into the pool: a Duck, a Dodo, an Eaglet, and several other curious animals.
Alice looked at the gathered creatures — soaking wet, shivering, and all staring at her as though she were the strangest thing THEY had ever seen — and she wondered, not for the first time, what on earth she had gotten herself into.
 
Chapter 3: A Caucus Race

The first question was, of course, how to get dry.
The Mouse, who seemed to be a person of authority among the assembled creatures, cleared its throat importantly and said: "Ahem. I shall tell you a very dry story. That should do the trick."
"A dry story?" Alice asked.
"The driest I know," the Mouse said. And it began to recite a long, complicated passage about English history — William the Conqueror, the Archbishop of Canterbury, something about earls and bishops — in a voice so flat and so dull that within two minutes, everyone was more bored than wet.
"I beg your pardon," said the Dodo, a large, round bird with a serious expression and an air of great self-importance, "but I believe there is a better way to get dry. I propose a Caucus Race."
"What IS a Caucus Race?" Alice asked.
The Dodo waved a wing vaguely. "The best way to explain it," the Dodo said, "is to do it."
It marked out a rough circle on the ground, placed the participants around it — Alice, the Mouse, the Duck, the Eaglet, and several others — and said, "Run."
"But when do we start?" Alice asked.
"You already have," the Dodo said.
They ran. There was no starting signal, no set direction, and no particular speed. Everyone ran in whatever direction they liked, some fast and some slow, some in circles and some in zigzags. The Dodo walked. The Mouse scurried. Alice jogged. The Duck waddled. The Eaglet hopped.
After about half an hour, the Dodo called out, "The race is over!"
Everyone stopped, panting.
"But who won?" Alice asked.
The Dodo thought about this for a long time. Then it said, "EVERYBODY has won, and ALL must have prizes."
"But who is to give the prizes?" a chorus of voices asked.
"She will," the Dodo said, pointing at Alice.
Alice reached into her pocket and found a box of sweets. She handed them around — there was exactly one for each creature, which was fortunate. But there was nothing left for Alice.
"She must have a prize herself, you know," the Mouse said.
"Of course," the Dodo agreed. "What else have you got in your pocket?"
Alice pulled out a thimble.
"Hand it over here," the Dodo said. Then, with great ceremony, the Dodo presented the thimble back to Alice, saying: "We beg your acceptance of this elegant thimble."
Everyone cheered. Alice thought the whole thing was very ridiculous, but it seemed rude to say so, so she bowed as solemnly as she could and accepted her own thimble with as much dignity as the situation allowed.
"This is the most absurd thing I have ever been part of," Alice thought. But then she thought: "Actually, I rather enjoyed it."
And that was the strange thing about Wonderland. The rules made no sense. The logic was upside down. And yet, there was a kind of joy in the nonsense — a freedom in letting go of the way things were supposed to work and simply running in circles until everyone won.
Alice was beginning to understand that Wonderland operated on its own logic. It was not the logic she had learned in the schoolroom. But it was logic, of a sort. You just had to squint at it sideways.
 
Chapter 4: The Rabbit Sends a Little Bill

Alice had barely caught her breath when the White Rabbit came trotting back, peering anxiously in every direction and muttering, "The Duchess! The Duchess! Oh my dear paws! Oh my fur and whiskers! She'll have me executed, as sure as ferrets are ferrets!"
He spotted Alice and cried, "Why, Mary Ann, what ARE you doing here? Run home this moment, and fetch me a pair of gloves and a fan! Quick, now!"
Alice was so startled that she ran off at once in the direction he pointed, without even stopping to tell him she was not Mary Ann.
"He took me for his housemaid," she said as she ran. "How surprised he'll be when he finds out who I really am! But I'd better take him his gloves and fan — at least, I'll try."
She found a neat little house with "W. RABBIT" on a brass plate by the door. She went in and hurried upstairs, where she found a pair of white kid gloves and a fan. She also found a little bottle, and though it did not have a "DRINK ME" label, she drank from it anyway.
"I know SOMETHING interesting is sure to happen," she said, "whenever I eat or drink anything in this place."
Something interesting did happen. She began to grow. And grow. And grow. Within a minute, she filled the entire room. One arm was stuck out the window. One foot was jammed up the chimney. Her head was pressed against the ceiling.
"I can't get any bigger than this," she said hopefully. But she could. She kept growing until her elbow was pressing out one window and her knee was breaking through another.
"It was MUCH pleasanter at home," Alice thought, "when one wasn't always growing larger and smaller, and being ordered about by mice and rabbits. I almost wish I hadn't gone down that rabbit hole — and yet — and yet — it's rather curious, you know, this sort of life."
The Rabbit arrived outside and tried to get in, but the door was blocked by Alice's enormous body. She heard it calling, "Bill! Bill! Come here! There's an arm sticking out the window!"
Bill turned out to be a small lizard. The other animals decided to send him down the chimney, which seemed to Alice like a very bad plan for Bill. She gave a little kick, and the next thing she heard was a chorus of voices crying, "There goes Bill!" as the poor lizard went sailing through the air.
Eventually, the creatures outside began throwing pebbles through the window at Alice. The pebbles, when they hit the floor, turned into small cakes.
"If I eat one of these," Alice reasoned, "it will probably change my size. Since I can't possibly get any LARGER, it must make me smaller."
She was right. She shrank quickly and ran out of the house before the Rabbit and his friends could catch her, diving into a thick wood where the trees came up to about her current height — which was not very high at all.
"The first thing I've got to do," said Alice to herself, as she wandered among the trees, "is to grow to my right size again; and the second thing is to find my way into that lovely garden. I think that will be the best plan."
It was an excellent plan. The only difficulty was that she had not the smallest idea how to carry it out.
She was wandering through the wood, looking up at the great mushrooms that grew like tables above her head, when she noticed a large blue caterpillar sitting on top of one of them, calmly smoking a long hookah and taking not the smallest notice of her or of anything else.
 
Chapter 5: Advice from a Caterpillar

The Caterpillar and Alice looked at each other for some time in silence. The Caterpillar was sitting on top of a large mushroom with its arms folded, puffing on a hookah with an air of supreme indifference. It was, Alice decided, the most self-satisfied creature she had ever seen.
At last, the Caterpillar took the hookah out of its mouth and addressed her in a languid, sleepy voice.
"Who are YOU?" it said.
This was not an encouraging opening for a conversation. Alice replied, rather shyly, "I — I hardly know, sir, just at present. At least I know who I WAS when I got up this morning, but I think I must have been changed several times since then."
"What do you mean by that?" said the Caterpillar sternly. "Explain yourself!"
"I can't explain MYSELF, I'm afraid, sir," said Alice, "because I'm not myself, you see."
"I don't see," said the Caterpillar.
"I'm afraid I can't put it more clearly," Alice replied very politely, "for I can't understand it myself, to begin with. Being so many different sizes in a day is very confusing."
"It isn't," said the Caterpillar.
"Well, perhaps you haven't found it so yet," said Alice. "But when you have to turn into a chrysalis — and then after that into a butterfly, I should think you'll feel it a little queer, won't you?"
"Not a bit," said the Caterpillar.
Alice felt rather cross. The Caterpillar had a way of contradicting everything she said, and it was extremely irritating. She drew herself up and said, very gravely: "I think you ought to tell me who YOU are, first."
"Why?" said the Caterpillar.
This was another puzzling question. Alice could not think of any good reason, and as the Caterpillar seemed to be in a very unpleasant state of mind, she turned away.
"Come back!" the Caterpillar called after her. "I've something important to say!"
Alice turned back, hopeful.
"Keep your temper," the Caterpillar said.
"Is THAT all?" said Alice, swallowing down her anger as well as she could.
"No," said the Caterpillar. It uncoiled itself from the mushroom and began crawling away. "One side will make you grow taller, and the other side will make you grow shorter."
"One side of WHAT?" Alice thought to herself.
"Of the mushroom," said the Caterpillar, as if it had read her thoughts. And then it was gone, leaving Alice alone with the mushroom and the question that lingered in the air like smoke:
Who ARE you?
Alice thought about this as she carefully broke off pieces from both sides of the mushroom. It was a question she had been asked many times before — by teachers, by relatives, by the Mouse, by the Caterpillar. But she had never really thought about the answer.
Who WAS she? She was Alice. But what did that mean? She had been tall and short and enormous and tiny, all in the same day. She had been mistaken for a housemaid. She had swum in her own tears. She had given herself her own prize. In Wonderland, nothing stayed the same for more than five minutes, including Alice herself.
"I suppose," Alice said slowly, nibbling a piece of mushroom and watching herself grow to a more comfortable size, "that who I am does not depend on how tall I am. It depends on something else."
She was not sure what that something else was. But the question had planted itself inside her, and it would keep growing, like a seed in dark soil, all the way through Wonderland.
 
Chapter 6: Pig and Pepper

Having gotten herself to a reasonable height — about a foot tall, which was short but manageable — Alice set off through the wood and came upon a little house.
Standing outside was a Fish-Footman, dressed in full livery, holding an enormous letter. He was knocking on the door, which was opened by a Frog-Footman, also in full livery.
"For the Duchess," said the Fish-Footman, in a solemn voice. "An invitation from the Queen, to play croquet."
"From the Queen," the Frog-Footman repeated, in an equally solemn voice. "An invitation for the Duchess, to play croquet."
They bowed to each other so deeply that their wigs got tangled together.
Alice laughed so hard she had to hide behind a tree so they wouldn't hear her. By the time she composed herself, the Fish-Footman was gone and the Frog-Footman was sitting on the doorstep, staring up at the sky with blank, bulging eyes.
"How am I to get in?" Alice asked.
"Are you to get in at all?" the Frog-Footman said. "That's the first question, you know."
"I shall just go in," Alice said, and she did.
The kitchen was full of smoke and chaos. The Duchess sat on a three-legged stool in the middle of the room, holding a baby that was howling at the top of its lungs. A cook was stirring an enormous pot of soup with one hand and throwing pepper into it with the other. Pepper filled the air like fog — everyone was sneezing except the cook and a large cat sitting on the hearth, grinning from ear to ear.
"Why does your cat grin like that?" Alice asked politely, sneezing.
"It's a Cheshire Cat," the Duchess said, "and that's why."
"I didn't know cats could grin," Alice said.
"You don't know much," the Duchess said. "And that's a fact."
Alice did not like being told she didn't know much, especially by someone sitting in a kitchen full of pepper. But before she could respond, the Duchess thrust the baby at her.
"Here! You may hold it if you like. I've got to get ready for croquet with the Queen." And she bustled out of the room.
Alice carried the baby outside, which seemed like the safest place for it given the state of the kitchen. But as she walked, the baby began to change. Its nose turned up. Its eyes grew small. It grunted instead of crying.
Alice looked down. She was holding a pig.
"If you're going to turn into a pig," Alice said, setting it down gently, "I'll have nothing more to do with you."
The pig trotted away into the wood, looking, Alice thought, quite content with the arrangement.
She was standing alone, considering her next move, when she noticed the Cheshire Cat sitting on the branch of a tree, grinning down at her.
"Would you tell me, please, which way I ought to go from here?" Alice asked.
"That depends a good deal on where you want to get to," said the Cat.
"I don't much care where —" said Alice.
"Then it doesn't matter which way you go," said the Cat.
"— so long as I get SOMEWHERE," Alice added.
"Oh, you're sure to do that," said the Cat, "if you only walk long enough."
Alice felt that this could not be denied, but it was not very helpful. She tried another question. "What sort of people live around here?"
"In THAT direction," the Cat said, waving its right paw, "lives a Hatter. And in THAT direction," waving the other paw, "lives a March Hare. Visit either you like — they're both mad."
"But I don't want to go among mad people," Alice remarked.
"Oh, you can't help that," said the Cat. "We're all mad here. I'm mad. You're mad."
"How do you know I'm mad?" said Alice.
"You must be," said the Cat, "or you wouldn't have come here."
And then, slowly, starting from the tip of its tail and ending with the grin, the Cheshire Cat vanished.
Alice stared at the spot where it had been. She had seen a cat without a grin. But a grin without a cat? That was the most curious thing she had seen yet.
She decided to visit the March Hare. "The Hatter might be more mad," she said to herself. "Besides — it's May. Perhaps the March Hare won't be quite so mad as it was in March."
She walked on, and before very long, she came upon the maddest tea party in the history of tea.
 
Chapter 7: A Mad Tea-Party

There was a table set out under a tree in front of the March Hare's house, and the March Hare and the Hatter were having tea at it. A Dormouse was sitting between them, fast asleep, and the other two were using it as a cushion, resting their elbows on it and talking over its head.
"Very uncomfortable for the Dormouse," thought Alice. "Only, as it's asleep, I suppose it doesn't mind."
The table was enormous — long enough for a dozen guests — but the three were all crowded together at one corner of it.
"No room! No room!" they cried out when they saw Alice coming.
"There's PLENTY of room!" said Alice indignantly, and she sat down in a large armchair at the head of the table.
"Have some wine," the March Hare offered in an encouraging tone.
Alice looked all over the table, but there was nothing on it but tea. "I don't SEE any wine," she said.
"There isn't any," said the March Hare.
"Then it wasn't very civil of you to offer it," said Alice angrily.
"It wasn't very civil of you to sit down without being invited," said the March Hare.
The Hatter opened his eyes very wide. He was a peculiar little man with a large head and wild eyes, wearing an enormous top hat with a price tag still stuck in the band. He leaned across the table and said: "Why is a raven like a writing-desk?"
"A riddle!" said Alice. "I believe I can guess that."
"Do you mean that you think you can find out the answer to it?" asked the March Hare.
"Exactly so," said Alice.
"Then you should say what you mean," the March Hare went on.
"I do," Alice hastily replied. "At least — at least I mean what I say — that's the same thing, you know."
"Not the same thing a bit!" said the Hatter. "You might just as well say that 'I see what I eat' is the same thing as 'I eat what I see'!"
"You might just as well say," added the March Hare, "that 'I like what I get' is the same thing as 'I get what I like'!"
Alice had no answer for this. The logic was absurd, and yet — she could not find the flaw. Saying what you mean and meaning what you say SOUNDED like the same thing, but perhaps they were not. Language, in Wonderland, was slippery.
"What day of the month is it?" the Hatter asked, looking at his watch.
"The fourth," said Alice.
"Two days wrong!" the Hatter sighed. "I told you butter wouldn't suit the works!" he added, looking angrily at the March Hare.
"It was the BEST butter," the March Hare meekly replied.
"Yes, but some crumbs must have got in as well," the Hatter grumbled.
Alice looked at the watch. It was a watch that told the day of the month but not the hour. "What a funny watch!" she said.
"Why should it?" the Hatter said. "Does YOUR watch tell you what year it is?"
"Of course not," Alice replied. "But that's because it stays the same year for such a long time together."
"Which is just the case with MINE," said the Hatter.
Alice was dreadfully puzzled. The Hatter's words seemed to make sense, but they didn't — or perhaps they did, but in a way her brain couldn't quite follow. It was like trying to catch water in your hands.
"The Dormouse is asleep again," the Hatter observed, and poured a little hot tea onto its nose.
The Dormouse shook its head impatiently and said, without opening its eyes, "Of course, of course. Just what I was going to remark myself."
"Tell us a story!" the March Hare demanded.
"Yes, please do!" Alice pleaded.
"And be quick about it," added the Hatter, "or you'll be asleep before it's done."
 
Chapter 8: The Dormouse's Story

"Once upon a time," the Dormouse began in a great hurry, "there were three little sisters, and their names were Elsie, Lacie, and Tillie, and they lived at the bottom of a well —"
"What did they live on?" Alice asked. She always took a great interest in questions of eating and drinking.
"They lived on treacle," said the Dormouse, after thinking a moment.
"They couldn't have done that, you know," Alice gently remarked. "They'd have been ill."
"So they were," said the Dormouse. "VERY ill."
"But why did they live at the bottom of a well?"
"It was a treacle-well," the Dormouse said.
"There's no such thing!" Alice was getting very cross.
"If you can't be civil," said the Dormouse, "you'd better finish the story yourself."
"No, please go on!" Alice said, more humbly. "I won't interrupt again."
"One, indeed!" said the Dormouse indignantly. But it continued: "These three little sisters — they were learning to draw, you know."
"What did they draw?" asked Alice, forgetting her promise not to interrupt.
"Treacle," the Dormouse replied, without even thinking.
"But I don't understand," Alice said. "Where did they draw the treacle from?"
"You can draw WATER out of a water-well," said the Hatter, "so I should think you could draw TREACLE out of a treacle-well — eh, stupid?"
"But they were IN the well," Alice said.
"Of course they were," said the Dormouse. "Well in."
Alice gave up. The story made no sense, the Dormouse kept falling asleep in the middle of sentences, the March Hare was putting butter in the Hatter's watch, and the Hatter was asking riddles with no answers.
"Why is a raven like a writing-desk?" she asked again. "Have you guessed the riddle yet?"
"No, I give it up," Alice replied. "What's the answer?"
"I haven't the slightest idea," said the Hatter.
"Nor I," said the March Hare.
Alice sighed wearily. "I think you might do something better with the time than waste it asking riddles that have no answers."
"If you knew Time as well as I do," said the Hatter, "you wouldn't talk about wasting IT. It's HIM."
"I don't know what you mean," Alice said.
"Of course you don't!" the Hatter said, tossing his head contemptuously. "I dare say you never even spoke to Time!"
"Perhaps not," Alice cautiously replied. "But I know I have to beat time when I learn music."
"Ah! That accounts for it," said the Hatter. "HE won't stand beating. Now, if you only kept on good terms with him, he'd do almost anything you liked with the clock. For instance, suppose it were nine o'clock in the morning, just time to begin lessons: you'd only have to whisper a hint to Time, and round goes the clock in a twinkling! Half-past one, time for dinner!"
Alice had had enough. The tea party was going in circles — literally and figuratively. The Hatter and the March Hare were moving around the table, taking the next set of cups, because they had quarreled with Time and it was always six o'clock, always tea-time, never any other time at all.
"This is the stupidest tea-party I ever was at in all my life!" Alice said, and she got up and walked off into the wood.
The last thing she saw was the Hatter and the March Hare trying to stuff the Dormouse into the teapot.
"At any rate, I'll never go THERE again!" said Alice as she picked her way through the wood. "It's the stupidest tea-party in the world!"
But even as she said it, she noticed a door in one of the trees. A door that led to the long, low hall she had been in before. And on the table — the glass table — was the golden key.
This time, Alice knew what to do. She nibbled the mushroom until she was the right size, took the key, opened the little door, and — at last — stepped into the beautiful garden.
 
Chapter 9: The Queen's Garden

The garden was even lovelier than Alice had imagined from peeping through the keyhole. Bright flowers bloomed in perfect beds. Fountains sparkled in the sunshine. The air smelled of roses.
But something was wrong.
Near the entrance, three gardeners were clustered around a rose tree. They were shaped like playing cards — flat, rectangular, with arms and legs and painted faces. These particular cards were the Two, the Five, and the Seven of Spades, and they were painting the roses.
They were painting white roses red.
"Would you tell me," Alice said, very shyly, "why you are painting those roses?"
Five and Seven said nothing, but looked at Two. Two began, in a low voice, "Why, the fact is, you see, Miss, this here ought to have been a RED rose-tree, and we put a white one in by mistake. And if the Queen was to find it out, we should all have our heads cut off, you know."
"The Queen?" Alice said.
"The Queen of Hearts," Two whispered. "She ordered red roses. We planted white ones. If she sees them —" He drew his finger across his neck in a gesture that needed no explanation.
"That seems very excessive," Alice said.
"That's the Queen," Five said gloomily.
At that moment, a great procession entered the garden. First came ten soldiers carrying clubs — they were all shaped like playing cards, just like the gardeners. Then came ten courtiers covered in diamonds. Then the royal children — ten of them, decorated with hearts. Then came the guests — mostly kings and queens from other decks of cards. Then came the Knave of Hearts, carrying the King's crown on a red velvet cushion. And last of all came the KING AND QUEEN OF HEARTS themselves.
The Queen was a small, furious woman with a red face and a voice that could shatter glass. Her eyes darted over the garden like searchlights, looking for something — anything — to be angry about.
She stopped in front of Alice.
"Who IS this?" the Queen demanded, pointing.
"My name is Alice, so please your Majesty," Alice said politely. She added to herself: "Why, they're only a pack of cards, after all. I needn't be afraid of them."
"And who are THESE?" the Queen said, pointing at the three gardeners, who were lying flat on their faces on the ground.
"How should I know?" said Alice, surprised at her own courage. "It's no business of MINE."
The Queen turned crimson with fury. "Off with her head! Off —"
"Nonsense!" said Alice, very loudly and decidedly.
The Queen was silent.
Everyone was silent.
Nobody in Wonderland had ever said "Nonsense" to the Queen before. It was such a novel experience that the Queen did not seem to know how to react. She stood there, mouth open, for a full five seconds.
Then the King tugged her sleeve and murmured, "Consider, my dear: she is only a child!"
The Queen turned away, and Alice breathed.
"Can you play croquet?" the Queen shouted at Alice.
"Yes!" shouted Alice.
"Come on, then!" the Queen roared, and the entire court swept away toward the croquet ground, leaving Alice to follow in a daze.
"They're only cards," she told herself again. But her heart was pounding all the same.
 
Chapter 10: The Queen's Croquet Ground

Alice had never seen such a curious croquet ground in her life.
The ground was all ridges and furrows. The croquet balls were live hedgehogs. The mallets were live flamingos. And the hoops were soldiers who had to bend over and stand on their hands and feet to form arches.
Alice's flamingo kept twisting its neck around to look at her with a puzzled expression, as if to say: "What exactly do you expect me to do?" The hedgehog, meanwhile, uncurled itself and crawled away every time Alice tried to hit it. And the soldiers kept standing up and walking to different parts of the ground, so the hoops were never in the same place twice.
"It's very hard to play," Alice said, "when nothing stays where you put it."
The Queen did not seem to find it hard at all. She played with terrifying intensity, slamming her flamingo at hedgehogs with the force of a battering ram. Whether she actually hit anything or whether the animals simply scurried out of her way in terror was hard to say. But the Queen won every point, because the Queen always won every point, because anyone who suggested otherwise would lose their head.
"Off with his head!" the Queen shouted at a soldier who had sneezed during her swing. "Off with her head!" she screamed at a courtier who had looked in the wrong direction. "Off with EVERYBODY'S head!" she roared, apparently just for the exercise.
Alice noticed that no one's head was actually removed. The King quietly pardoned everyone the moment the Queen's back was turned. It was, Alice thought, rather like a game of pretend played by someone who did not know it was pretend.
"How are you getting on?" the Cheshire Cat asked, appearing suddenly as a floating grin above the croquet ground.
"I don't think they play at all fairly," Alice complained. "And they all quarrel so dreadfully you can't hear yourself think — and they don't seem to have any rules. Or if they have, nobody follows them."
"How do you like the Queen?" said the Cat in a low voice.
"Not at all," said Alice. "She's so extremely —" At that moment, the Queen appeared behind her, and Alice quickly finished: "— likely to win, that it's hardly worth finishing the game."
The Queen smiled and passed on.
The Cheshire Cat watched her go, its grin widening. "Who ARE you talking to?" the King asked, peering at the floating cat head with obvious distaste.
"It's a friend of mine — a Cheshire Cat," Alice said. "Allow me to introduce it."
"I don't like the look of it at all," the King said. "It must be removed."
"Off with his head!" the Queen called, without even looking.
"How do you cut off a head," the executioner argued, "when there is no body to cut it from?"
This created a magnificent argument. The King said anything that had a head could be beheaded. The executioner said you couldn't cut off a head without a body underneath it. The Queen said that if something wasn't done about it in less than no time, she'd have EVERYBODY beheaded, including the executioner.
The Cat solved the problem by slowly vanishing, beginning with the tip of its tail and ending with the grin, which remained for some time after the rest of it had gone.
Alice decided it was time to leave the croquet ground. She slipped away while the Queen was busy threatening someone else and wandered through the garden, feeling tired and confused and more than a little homesick.
Wonderland, she was beginning to realize, was not just absurd. It was unfair. The Queen made rules that no one could follow, then punished people for breaking them. The game was rigged. The logic was backwards. And the only way to survive was to agree with whatever the most powerful person said, whether it made sense or not.
"I don't like this at all," Alice said to herself. "Rules should make sense, and punishments should fit the crime, and people should be allowed to disagree."
She had not yet realized that this idea — this simple, brave, radical idea — was the most important thing she would discover in all of Wonderland.
 
Chapter 11: The Mock Turtle's Story

The Duchess found Alice in the garden and seized her arm with uncomfortable familiarity.
"You're thinking about something, my dear," the Duchess said, "and that makes you forget to talk. I can't tell you just now what the moral of that is, but I shall remember it in a bit."
"Perhaps it hasn't got one," Alice ventured.
"Tut, tut, child!" said the Duchess. "EVERYTHING'S got a moral, if only you can find it." And she squeezed Alice's arm harder.
Alice was rescued by the Queen, who appeared and screamed at the Duchess until the Duchess fled. Then the Queen grabbed Alice and marched her to a sunny spot on the shore, where two creatures sat on a rock: a Gryphon (a creature with the head and wings of an eagle and the body of a lion) and a Mock Turtle.
The Mock Turtle was a sad creature. He had the body of a turtle and the head and tail of a calf, and he was weeping — great, salty tears that rolled down his cheeks and dripped into the sea.
"What's the matter with him?" Alice asked the Gryphon.
"It's all his fancy, that," the Gryphon whispered. "He hasn't got no sorrow, you know. Come on!"
They went to the Mock Turtle, who looked at them with large, mournful eyes.
"This here young lady," said the Gryphon, "she wants to know your history, she do."
"I'll tell it her," said the Mock Turtle in a deep, hollow tone. "Sit down, both of you, and don't speak a word till I've finished."
They sat. The Mock Turtle sighed deeply and began.
"Once," he said, "I was a real Turtle."
A long pause.
"When we were little," the Mock Turtle went on, more calmly, "we went to school in the sea. The master was an old Turtle — we used to call him Tortoise."
"Why did you call him Tortoise, if he wasn't one?" Alice asked.
"We called him Tortoise because he taught us," said the Mock Turtle angrily. "Really you are very dull!"
Alice bit her lip. The wordplay was maddening. Everything in Wonderland was a pun, a riddle, or a trick of language.
"We had the best of educations," the Mock Turtle continued. "We studied Reeling and Writhing, of course, to begin with, and then the different branches of Arithmetic — Ambition, Distraction, Uglification, and Derision."
"I never heard of 'Uglification,'" Alice ventured.
"You know what to BEAUTIFY is, I suppose?" the Gryphon said.
"Yes," Alice said.
"Well, then," the Gryphon continued, "if you don't know what to UGLIFY is, you ARE a simpleton."
Alice decided not to ask any more questions about the curriculum. The Mock Turtle's school seemed to operate on the same logic as everything else in Wonderland: it SOUNDED right if you didn't think about it too hard, but the moment you examined it closely, it fell apart.
And yet — was it so different from Alice's own schooling? She had memorized multiplication tables without understanding them, recited poems without feeling them, learned geography without ever seeing the places on the map. Was the Mock Turtle's school really more absurd, or was it just more honest about its absurdity?
Alice filed this uncomfortable thought away and listened as the Mock Turtle described his eleven hours of lessons a day.
"Eleven hours a day?" Alice said. "That's a great deal."
"Ten hours the first day," said the Mock Turtle, "nine the next, and so on."
"What a curious plan!" Alice exclaimed.
"That's the reason they're called lessons," the Gryphon remarked. "Because they lessen from day to day."
Alice sat on the rock by the sea and listened to the Mock Turtle cry about a school that might never have existed, taught by a Tortoise who was not a tortoise, in subjects that were not subjects, and she thought: this is what happens when you stop asking whether things make sense and just accept them.
She was not going to do that.
 
Chapter 12: The Lobster Quadrille

"Shall we try the Lobster Quadrille?" the Gryphon suggested. "Or would you rather the Mock Turtle sang you a song?"
"Oh, a song, please, if the Mock Turtle would be so kind," Alice replied. She was curious what a Mock Turtle's song would sound like.
The Mock Turtle sang in a slow, melancholy voice. It was a song about soup — beautiful soup, rich and green, waiting in a hot tureen. He sang it with such deep feeling that Alice felt moved, though she was not entirely sure why a song about soup should be emotional.
"Thank you," Alice said when he finished. "It's a very interesting song."
"Shall I sing you the second verse?" the Mock Turtle asked eagerly.
"I'd rather see the Lobster Quadrille first," said the Gryphon. "Go on, old fellow, show her."
The Mock Turtle sighed and began to describe the dance, but the Gryphon interrupted: "The thing is, you advance twice —"
"Each with a lobster as a partner!" cried the Mock Turtle.
"Of course," said the Gryphon. "Advance twice, set to partners —"
"— change lobsters, and retire in same order," continued the Mock Turtle.
"Then, you know," the Gryphon went on, "you throw the —"
"The lobsters!" shouted the Mock Turtle, bouncing up and down.
"— as far out to sea as you can —"
"Swim after them!" screamed the Mock Turtle, turning somersaults.
"Turn a somersault in the sea!" cried the Gryphon.
"Change lobsters again!" yelled the Mock Turtle.
"Back to land again, and that's all the first figure," said the Gryphon, dropping his voice.
Alice watched, bewildered, as the Mock Turtle and the Gryphon demonstrated the dance — hopping and spinning and waving their limbs in patterns that seemed to follow no logic whatsoever. They crashed into each other, stepped on each other's feet, and nearly fell into the sea twice.
"It must be a very pretty dance," Alice said, politely.
"Would you like to try it?" the Mock Turtle asked.
"Very much indeed," said Alice, which was not entirely true but seemed like the right thing to say.
They tried to teach her the steps, but the steps kept changing. Every time Alice thought she understood the pattern, the Gryphon would add a new move, or the Mock Turtle would insist that she had done it wrong, even though he could not agree with the Gryphon on what "right" was.
Alice tried her best. She hopped when they said hop. She spun when they said spin. She threw imaginary lobsters into an imaginary sea. And somewhere in the middle of this ridiculous exercise, she started to laugh.
Not a polite laugh. Not a careful, proper English girl's laugh. A real laugh — deep and helpless, the kind that makes your sides ache and your eyes stream. Because the dance was absurd, and the teachers were absurd, and Wonderland was absurd, and Alice was absurd for trying so hard to make sense of a place that had no sense to make.
The Gryphon and the Mock Turtle stopped dancing and stared at her.
"What's so funny?" the Mock Turtle asked, offended.
"Everything," Alice said. "Absolutely everything."
And she meant it as the highest compliment, though neither of them understood.
Just then, a cry came from the distance: "The trial's beginning!"
"Come on!" the Gryphon cried, seizing Alice by the hand and dragging her away at a run, without waiting for the end of the song or the dance or the Mock Turtle's tears.
"What trial?" Alice asked, panting.
"Come on!" was all the Gryphon said.
 
Chapter 13: Who Stole the Tarts?

The King and Queen of Hearts were seated on their thrones when Alice arrived at the court, with a great crowd assembled around them — all sorts of birds and beasts, as well as the whole pack of cards. The Knave of Hearts stood before them, in chains, looking miserable. And near the King was the White Rabbit, with a trumpet in one hand and a scroll of parchment in the other.
In the very middle of the court was a table with a large dish of tarts upon it. They looked so good that Alice felt quite hungry just looking at them.
"I wish they'd get the trial done," she thought, "and hand round the refreshments!"
The White Rabbit blew three blasts on the trumpet and read from the scroll:
"The Queen of Hearts, she made some tarts, all on a summer day. The Knave of Hearts, he stole those tarts, and took them quite away!"
"Consider your verdict," the King said to the jury.
"Not yet, not yet!" the Rabbit hastily interrupted. "There's a great deal to come before that!"
The jury was writing busily on their slates. Alice noticed, with surprise, that they were writing down their own names, as though they might forget who they were before the trial was over. One of them did not know how to spell his name and had to ask his neighbor.
"Call the first witness," the King said.
The first witness was the Hatter.
He came in with a teacup in one hand and a piece of bread-and-butter in the other. "I beg pardon, your Majesty," he began, "for bringing these in: but I hadn't quite finished my tea when I was sent for."
"You ought to have finished," the King said. "When did you begin?"
The Hatter looked at the March Hare, who had followed him into the court, arm-in-arm with the Dormouse. "Fourteenth of March, I THINK it was," he said.
"Fifteenth," said the March Hare.
"Sixteenth," said the Dormouse.
"Write that down," the King said to the jury, and the jury eagerly wrote down all three dates on their slates and then added them up and reduced the answer to shillings and pence.
"Take off your hat," the King said to the Hatter.
"It isn't mine," said the Hatter.
"STOLEN!" exclaimed the King, turning to the jury, who instantly made a memorandum of the fact.
"I keep them to sell," the Hatter explained hastily. "I've none of my own. I'm a hatter."
Alice watched the trial with growing dismay. It was the most disorganized, unfair, and ridiculous proceeding she had ever witnessed. The witnesses contradicted each other. The jury wrote down whatever they wanted. The King made rulings based on nothing. And the Queen sat on her throne shouting "Off with his head!" at regular intervals, whether it was relevant or not.
"This isn't a trial," Alice thought. "It's a farce."
But she said nothing. Not yet.
 
Chapter 14: Alice's Evidence

The next witness was the Duchess's cook, who carried a pepper-box and made everyone in the court sneeze. She refused to give her evidence, the King got flustered, and the Queen got furious.
Then the White Rabbit read the next name on his list.
"Alice!"
Alice jumped up so quickly that she knocked over the jury box with the edge of her skirt, spilling the jurors all over the floor. She scrambled to pick them up and put them back, though she accidentally put one in upside down.
"What do you know about this business?" the King asked Alice.
"Nothing," said Alice.
"Nothing WHATEVER?" persisted the King.
"Nothing whatever," said Alice.
"That's very important," the King told the jury.
"UN-important, your Majesty means, of course," the White Rabbit said hastily.
"Unimportant, of course, I meant," the King said. "Important — unimportant — unimportant — important —" as if he were trying out which word sounded best.
Some of the jury wrote down "important" and some wrote down "unimportant." Alice could see them from where she stood. "It doesn't matter a bit," she thought.
"Let the jury consider their verdict," the King said, for about the twentieth time that day.
"No, no!" said the Queen. "Sentence first — verdict afterwards."
"Stuff and nonsense!" said Alice loudly. "The idea of having the sentence first!"
"Hold your tongue!" said the Queen, turning purple.
"I won't!" said Alice.
The court gasped. The jury dropped their slates. The Knave of Hearts stared. Even the Dormouse woke up.
"OFF WITH HER HEAD!" the Queen shrieked.
Alice felt something happening. She was growing. Right there in the courtroom, she was getting taller and taller, and as she grew, something else was growing too — something inside her that had been building since the moment she fell down the rabbit hole.
Her courage.
 
Chapter 15: Sentence First

The trial continued, though "continued" was a generous word for what was happening. The King called for evidence that did not exist. The Queen demanded executions for crimes that had not been committed. The jury wrote down things that had not been said, crossed them out, and wrote down different things instead.
A letter was produced — supposedly written by the Knave of Hearts — that proved nothing at all. It was not in the Knave's handwriting. It was not signed. It was not even addressed to anyone. It was, as far as Alice could tell, a nonsense poem.
"That PROVES his guilt, of course," said the Queen.
"It proves nothing of the sort!" said Alice. She was much taller now — nearly her normal height — and she was getting less afraid by the minute.
"It does!" said the Queen.
"It doesn't!" said Alice.
"Read the poem," the King ordered.
The White Rabbit put on his spectacles and read the poem aloud. It was about someone giving something to someone else, but nobody knew who the someones were or what the something was, and the poem contradicted itself in every other line.
"That's the most important piece of evidence we've heard yet," said the King, rubbing his hands.
"If any one of them can explain it," said Alice — she had grown so much in the last few minutes that she had quite lost her fear — "I'll give him sixpence. I don't believe there's an atom of meaning in it."
The jury wrote down "she doesn't believe there's an atom of meaning in it," but none of them seemed to have any idea what it meant, either.
"If there's no meaning in it," said the King, "that saves a world of trouble, you know, as we needn't try to find any."
Alice stared at him. The King of Hearts — the highest authority in Wonderland — had just declared that a meaningless poem was the most important evidence in a trial, and then admitted that its meaninglessness was actually convenient.
This, Alice realized, was how Wonderland worked. Not by logic, not by justice, not by any principle that Alice could recognize — but by the whims of whoever happened to be in charge. The Queen shouted. The King agreed. The jury wrote it down. And the Knave stood in chains, about to be sentenced for a crime that may or may not have happened, based on evidence that proved nothing.
"Sentence first — verdict afterwards," the Queen had said.
And Alice understood, with sudden clarity, that this was not just nonsense. It was the opposite of everything that was right. A sentence should follow a verdict. A verdict should follow evidence. Evidence should follow truth. And truth should be the foundation of everything.
In Wonderland, the foundation was missing. And Alice was done pretending it wasn't.
 
Chapter 16: Growing Taller

Alice was growing, and she could not stop.
She was already taller than the King. Taller than the Queen. Taller than the soldiers, the courtiers, the witnesses, and the jury combined. Her head was approaching the ceiling. Her feet were pushing the furniture aside. She was becoming, once again, enormous — but this time, she was not frightened.
"You're doing it again," she told herself. "Growing when you should be sitting still. But perhaps growing is exactly what I should be doing."
Because it was not just her body that was growing. It was her mind. Her voice. Her willingness to say what she thought, even when the most powerful person in the room was screaming at her.
She had arrived in Wonderland small, confused, and obedient. She had drunk when told to drink, eaten when told to eat, run when told to run. She had answered questions politely, followed rules that made no sense, and kept her opinions to herself because the creatures around her were louder and more certain than she was.
But certainty, Alice was learning, was not the same as being right. The Queen was the most certain person in Wonderland, and she was almost always wrong. The Caterpillar had been certain. The Hatter had been certain. Everyone in Wonderland was absolutely, unshakably certain about things that were completely and obviously absurd.
Alice was the only one who had been uncertain. She was the only one who had asked questions, challenged answers, and admitted when she did not understand. She had thought this made her weak.
It did not. It made her the only honest person in Wonderland.
"Off with her head!" the Queen screamed again.
Alice looked down at the Queen of Hearts — this small, red-faced, furious woman who ruled through fear and shouting and the constant threat of beheading. The Queen who made rules no one could follow, played games no one could win, and held trials where the verdict came before the evidence.
Alice had been afraid of the Queen. She was not afraid anymore.
"You can't cut off my head," Alice said calmly. "You can't even reach it."
The Queen sputtered.
"And even if you could," Alice continued, "it wouldn't change the fact that this trial is unfair, this evidence is meaningless, and the Knave of Hearts may very well be innocent."
The court was utterly silent. No one had ever spoken to the Queen this way. No one had ever stood in the middle of Wonderland's most powerful court and said: this is wrong.
Alice looked at the pack of cards assembled before her — soldiers and courtiers and Kings and Queens and Knaves — and she felt something settle inside her. Not anger. Not fear. Something simpler and stronger than both.
Clarity.
 
Chapter 17: "You're Nothing But a Pack of Cards!"

"Hold your tongue!" the Queen said, for what felt like the hundredth time.
"I will NOT!" said Alice. She was her full size now — her real, actual, proper size — and she was looking down at the Queen of Hearts the way you look down at a small and unreasonable child.
"Off with her head!" the Queen screamed.
Nobody moved.
"Who cares for you?" said Alice — and as she said it, she felt a great rush of something she could only describe as truth — "You're nothing but a pack of cards!"
At this, the whole pack rose up into the air and came flying down upon her.
Alice gave a little scream — half of fright, half of excitement — and tried to beat them off. The cards swirled around her in a whirlwind of hearts and diamonds and clubs and spades, fluttering and spinning and slapping against her arms and face. The Queen was screaming. The King was shouting. The jury was throwing their slates. The Hatter was waving his teacup. The White Rabbit was blowing his trumpet. The Cheshire Cat's grin floated above the chaos, wider than ever.
And then — everything began to dissolve.
The cards thinned. The colors faded. The screaming grew distant. The croquet ground, the garden, the courthouse, the tea table, the mushroom, the pool of tears, the long fall down the rabbit hole — all of it was collapsing, folding in on itself, becoming less real with every heartbeat.
Alice felt herself rising. Not growing — rising. Floating upward out of Wonderland like a bubble rising through water, leaving behind the mad creatures and the nonsense rules and the beautiful, terrible, ridiculous world she had been lost in.
The last thing she saw was the grin.
Then everything was quiet.
 
Chapter 18: Waking Up

Alice opened her eyes.
She was lying on the riverbank, with her head in her sister's lap. Dead leaves were fluttering down from the trees and landing on her face — that was what the cards had been. Just leaves.
"Wake up, Alice dear!" said her sister. "What a long sleep you've had!"
"Oh, I've had such a curious dream!" said Alice, and she began to tell her sister all about it — the White Rabbit, the pool of tears, the Caucus Race, the Caterpillar, the Duchess, the Cheshire Cat, the mad tea party, the Queen's croquet, and the trial. She told it all, tumbling over her words in her excitement to get every detail out.
Her sister listened with a patient smile. The sun was warm. The river sparkled. The afternoon was golden and peaceful and completely, perfectly ordinary.
"It was just a dream," Alice's sister said gently.
"I know," Alice said. She sat up and brushed the leaves from her dress. "But it was a very REAL sort of dream."
She looked around at the riverbank — the grass, the daisies, the book her sister was reading. Everything was exactly as it had been before she fell asleep. Nothing had changed.
And yet Alice felt different.
She felt as though she had traveled a great distance and come back, not to a different place but to the same place seen with different eyes. The riverbank was the same. The daisies were the same. But Alice was not.
She had been to a world where nothing made sense, where the rules changed every five minutes, where the most powerful people were the least reasonable, and where a simple question — "Why?" — was the most dangerous thing you could say. She had been small and large and confused and frightened and brave. She had been told she was nobody and told herself she was somebody. She had stood before a Queen and said: you are only a pack of cards.
"Was it really just a dream?" Alice asked.
"What else would it be?" her sister said.
Alice considered this. Dreams are strange things. They feel real while you are in them. They teach you things you did not know you were learning. And sometimes, when you wake up, the person who opens their eyes is not quite the same person who closed them.
"I don't know what else it would be," Alice said. "But I know it meant something."
Her sister smiled and went back to her book. Alice lay on the grass and watched the clouds move across the sky, and she thought about Wonderland — not as a place she had visited, but as a question she was still answering.
Who are you?
She was Alice. That much she knew. But who Alice WAS — that was a question she would spend the rest of her life exploring, and the answer would keep changing, and that was exactly as it should be.
 
Chapter 19: The Duchess's Morals

Alice's sister had gone inside for tea, leaving Alice alone on the riverbank with her thoughts. And what thoughts they were.
She found herself going back through Wonderland piece by piece, examining it the way she might examine a puzzle box — turning it over, pressing its edges, looking for the hidden mechanism that made it work.
She thought about the Duchess, who found a moral in everything. "Be what you would seem to be," the Duchess had said. Or, put more simply: "Never imagine yourself not to be otherwise than what it might appear to others that what you were or might have been was not otherwise than what you had been would have appeared to them to be otherwise."
Alice had not understood this at the time. Now, turning it over in her mind, she decided it still made no sense — but the Duchess's impulse was not entirely wrong. She WANTED things to have meaning. She just couldn't find it clearly.
"Perhaps," Alice thought, "the problem isn't that Wonderland has no meaning. The problem is that everyone in Wonderland INSISTS on the wrong meaning — or insists that their nonsense IS meaning — instead of admitting when something doesn't make sense."
The Duchess found morals in everything, whether they belonged there or not. The Hatter asked riddles with no answers and blamed Alice for not guessing them. The Queen declared sentences before verdicts and called it justice. Everyone in Wonderland was absolutely certain about things they had no right to be certain about.
Alice had been the uncertain one. She had asked questions. She had said "I don't understand." She had admitted when she was confused.
And that, Alice was beginning to realize, was not weakness. It was honesty.
The world — the real world, not Wonderland — was full of people who talked like the Duchess: confidently, loudly, in sentences that sounded important but meant nothing. It was full of people who made rules like the Queen: arbitrary, unfair, enforced by fear instead of reason. It was full of Hatters, asking questions they couldn't answer, and Mock Turtles, mourning things that never existed.
But it was also full of people like Alice. People who asked why. People who challenged nonsense. People who stood up in the middle of a crooked court and said: this is wrong.
Those people were rare. And they were important. More important than any Queen.
Alice picked a daisy and pulled its petals off, one by one. "He loves me. He loves me not." She stopped. "That's nonsense too," she said. "A flower doesn't know anything about love."
She laughed. Then she pulled the last petal anyway, because some nonsense is worth keeping.
 
Chapter 20: The Rules of Wonderland

Alice made a list in her head of all the rules she had encountered in Wonderland.
Rule 1: Drink things labeled "DRINK ME." Eat things labeled "EAT ME." (This was how you changed size, which was how you got through doors, which was how you got anywhere at all. The first rule of Wonderland was: accept what you are given and see what happens.)
Rule 2: Everyone is mad. The Cheshire Cat had said so, and as far as Alice could tell, the Cat was right. Madness in Wonderland was not an exception — it was the standard. The sane were the strangers.
Rule 3: Time can be broken. The Hatter had quarreled with Time, and Time had stopped at six o'clock forever. In Wonderland, time was not a fact but a relationship. If you offended it, it could punish you. If you pleased it, it could do anything you liked.
Rule 4: Words do not mean what you think they mean. They mean what the speaker WANTS them to mean, which is different for everyone, which is why conversations in Wonderland always went in circles.
Rule 5: The Queen is always right, even when she is wrong. Especially when she is wrong.
Rule 6: Sentence first, verdict afterwards.
Alice examined this list and frowned. These were not really rules. They were the ABSENCE of rules — a world where nothing was stable, nothing was fair, and nothing could be relied upon.
And yet, Wonderland had one rule that was real, even if no one said it out loud:
Rule 7: The person who asks questions is more powerful than the person who demands answers.
The Queen demanded. The Hatter puzzled. The Caterpillar interrogated. The Duchess moralized. Everyone in Wonderland TOLD Alice things. But Alice was the only one who ASKED.
"Who ARE you?" the Caterpillar had demanded. It was the most important question in Wonderland, and the Caterpillar had asked it as a challenge — as if Alice should be ashamed of not knowing.
But Alice was not ashamed. She was ten years old. She was still becoming who she would be. Not knowing who you are is not a failure. It is a beginning.
"I am Alice," she said to the empty riverbank. "I am the girl who asks questions. And that is enough."
The river flowed on. The sun moved across the sky. And Alice sat on the bank and felt, for the first time since waking up, that she understood something important — not about Wonderland, but about herself.
 
Chapter 21: Who Is Alice?

The Caterpillar's question would not leave her alone.
Who are you?
Alice thought about all the different versions of herself she had been in Wonderland. She had been tiny — small enough to swim in her own tears. She had been enormous — big enough to fill an entire house. She had been polite to creatures who were rude to her and rude to creatures who were polite to her. She had been brave and frightened, curious and confused, loud and silent, all in the same afternoon.
Which one was the real Alice?
"All of them," she decided. "I was all of those things, and I am all of those things. A person is not one size. A person is not one mood. A person is the whole collection — the tall days and the small days and the brave days and the frightened days, all stacked together."
This was a very grown-up thought for a ten-year-old, and Alice was a little surprised at herself for thinking it.
She thought about the creatures she had met. The White Rabbit, who was always late and always anxious. The Caterpillar, who was always certain and always rude. The Hatter, who was always mad and always stuck at six o'clock. The Queen, who was always angry and always threatening.
Each of them was ONE thing. They were fixed, rigid, unchanging. The Rabbit would always be late. The Queen would always be furious. The Hatter would always be at tea.
Alice was different. Alice CHANGED. She grew and shrank and learned and questioned and stood up and sat down and made mistakes and corrected them. She was the only character in Wonderland who was not stuck being one thing.
"That's what the Caterpillar didn't understand," Alice said. "He asked 'Who are you?' as though the answer should be permanent. But I am not permanent. I am growing. And growing means the answer keeps changing."
She thought about the chrysalis — how the Caterpillar would someday become a butterfly. Even HE would change, whether he admitted it or not.
"Perhaps," Alice thought, "the real question is not 'Who are you?' but 'Who are you becoming?' And the real answer is: I don't know yet. And that's all right."
The wind blew across the riverbank, ruffling her hair. The daisies nodded. A rabbit — an ordinary brown rabbit, not wearing a waistcoat or carrying a watch — hopped across the grass and disappeared into a hole.
Alice watched it go and smiled.
"Not that hole," she said. "Not today."
 
Chapter 22: Sense and Nonsense

Alice's sister came back with tea and biscuits and found Alice sitting on the riverbank with a very thoughtful expression.
"You look serious," her sister said. "Are you still thinking about your dream?"
"I'm thinking about nonsense," Alice said.
"Nonsense?" Her sister raised an eyebrow.
"In my dream, everything was nonsense. The rules didn't make sense. The conversations didn't make sense. The trial didn't make sense. And at first, I was very frustrated, because I kept trying to make sense of things that HAD no sense."
"And now?"
"Now I think nonsense is more complicated than I thought." Alice bit into a biscuit and chewed thoughtfully. "Some nonsense is just silliness — like the Dormouse's story about living at the bottom of a well. That kind of nonsense is fun. It makes you laugh."
"And the other kind?"
"The other kind is dangerous," Alice said. "The Queen's kind. When someone in charge makes rules that don't make sense and punishes you for not following them — that's nonsense too. But it's not funny. It's cruel."
Her sister set down her teacup. She was looking at Alice with an expression of quiet surprise — the look of someone who has just realized that the child in front of them is not quite a child anymore.
"The trick," Alice continued, "is knowing which kind of nonsense you're dealing with. The Hatter's nonsense is harmless. The Queen's nonsense is not. And the only way to tell the difference is to ask yourself: is this nonsense hurting anyone?"
"That's very wise, Alice."
"I learned it in Wonderland," Alice said. "Though I don't think Wonderland meant to teach me."
They sat together on the riverbank, sipping tea and watching the river flow. The world was quiet and sensible and beautifully boring, and Alice appreciated it in a way she had not appreciated it that morning.
Because now she knew what the alternative looked like. She had been to a world without sense, without fairness, without logic. And she had learned that these things — sense, fairness, logic — were not automatic. They had to be built, and maintained, and defended.
Every time someone asked "Why?", they were defending sense against nonsense.
Every time someone said "That's not fair," they were building something.
Every time a girl looked at a Queen and said "You're nothing but a pack of cards," the world got a little more honest.
Alice finished her biscuit and reached for another.
"I think I should like to go back to Wonderland someday," she said.
"Whatever for?" her sister asked.
"Because there's still so much nonsense to sort out," Alice said. "And I'm rather good at it."
 
Chapter 23: Alice Remembers

That evening, Alice sat in her room and tried to write down everything she remembered about Wonderland.
She wrote about the fall — the long, slow tumble through the earth, past cupboards and bookshelves and empty marmalade jars. She wrote about the pool of tears and the Caucus Race and the Caterpillar on his mushroom. She wrote about the mad tea party where it was always six o'clock and the Dormouse lived in a teapot. She wrote about the Queen's croquet ground, where the rules changed with every swing and the penalty for everything was beheading.
She wrote about the creatures she had met, and as she wrote, she realized something: she missed them.
Not the Queen. Definitely not the Queen. But the others — the Hatter with his riddles, the Cheshire Cat with his vanishing grin, the Mock Turtle with his melancholy songs, even the bossy Caterpillar with his infuriating question. They were maddening and absurd and completely impossible, but they had been, in their strange way, alive. More alive than the people Alice saw every day in the real world, who followed rules without thinking and answered questions without listening and never, ever admitted that they were confused.
The creatures of Wonderland were confused all the time. They were also rude, illogical, and frequently wrong. But they were never boring, and they were never pretending to be something they were not.
"Except the Queen," Alice thought. "The Queen was pretending to be powerful. But she was only a card."
Alice set down her pen and looked at what she had written. It was messy and rambling and full of crossed-out words, but it was hers. Her record of Wonderland. Her proof that it had happened — if not in the world, then in her mind, which was perhaps the more important place.
She thought about the question that had followed her through the whole adventure: Who are you?
She picked up her pen and wrote, at the bottom of the last page:
"I am Alice. I am the girl who fell down a rabbit hole and found a world where nothing made sense, and instead of pretending it did, I said so. I am the girl who was small and became large and became small again, and learned that my size does not determine my worth. I am the girl who stood before a Queen and told the truth. I am still growing. I am still changing. I do not know who I will be tomorrow. But today, I am Alice, and that is enough."
She closed the notebook, turned off the lamp, and went to sleep.
She did not dream of Wonderland. She dreamed of gardens and rivers and a cat who grinned, and when she woke up, she was smiling.
 
Chapter 24: The Girl Who Asked Questions

The next morning, Alice went downstairs to breakfast and found the world exactly as she had left it.
The toast was in the toast rack. The tea was in the pot. Her sister was reading the same book — the one without pictures or conversations. The cat, Dinah, was washing her face by the fire. Everything was ordinary and predictable and precisely as it should be.
Alice sat down and poured her tea, and she thought about how strange it was to be back in a world where tea was just tea and cats were just cats and nothing turned into anything else without warning.
"Alice, you're very quiet this morning," her sister said.
"I'm thinking," Alice said.
"About what?"
"About questions."
Her sister smiled. "What kind of questions?"
"The kind that don't have answers. Or the kind that have MANY answers. Or the kind that change depending on who's asking."
Her sister put down her book. This was, she thought, not entirely the sort of thing one expected to hear over breakfast.
"For instance," Alice said, buttering her toast with great deliberation, "why is a raven like a writing-desk?"
"I haven't the faintest idea," her sister said.
"Neither has anyone else," Alice said. "But isn't it wonderful that the question exists?"
She ate her toast. She drank her tea. She fed Dinah a small piece of fish. And then she went outside, into the garden, into the ordinary, sensible, beautiful world.
The morning was bright. The sky was very blue. Birds sang in the apple tree. The flowers in the garden were red and white and yellow, and none of them needed painting. There were no soldiers standing on their heads, no flamingos being used as mallets, and no one was shouting "Off with her head!" It was, by any reasonable standard, a much better garden than the Queen's.
Alice walked through it slowly, touching the flowers, listening to the birds, feeling the sun on her face. She was still Alice — the same girl who had sat on the riverbank yesterday, bored and sleepy and waiting for something to happen.
But she was also the Alice who had fallen through the earth and swum in her own tears and talked to a caterpillar about identity and stood before a queen and refused to be silent. She was the Alice who had learned that curiosity is the bravest thing a person can have, and that asking questions is more powerful than knowing answers, and that the most important words in any language are: "Why?" and "Who says so?" and "I don't think that's right."
She was the girl who had gone to Wonderland and come back with the most important souvenir of all: the knowledge that she did not have to accept the world as it was presented to her. She could question it. She could challenge it. She could look at a Queen and see a playing card.
"I wonder," Alice said aloud, to no one in particular, "what would happen if I went looking for another rabbit hole."
She did not go looking. Not today. Today, the ordinary world was enough.
But she kept her eyes open. Because Alice had learned the most important lesson of all: the extraordinary is always there, hiding just beneath the ordinary, waiting for someone curious enough to notice.
All you have to do is ask the right question.
All you have to do is follow the rabbit.
All you have to be is Alice.
