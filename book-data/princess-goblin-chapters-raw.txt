THE PRINCESS AND THE GOBLIN

A BedrockELA Adaptation

Based on the novel by George MacDonald (1872)

Adapted for 6th Grade Readers



CHAPTER 1

THE PRINCESS ON THE MOUNTAIN

Princess Irene was eight years old, and she lived in a castle on the side of a mountain.

It was not a grand, glittering castle like the ones in fairy tales. It was a large, old, stone house — part fortress, part farmhouse — built into the rocky slope above a mining town. The town had no name that anyone remembered. People just called it "the town at the mountain," and the mountain had no name either, except among the miners, who called it the King's Mountain because the king owned everything underneath it.

Irene's father, the king, did not live in the mountain house. He was far away, governing the kingdom from the capital city, and Irene saw him only a few times a year. She lived with her nurse, Lootie, and a handful of servants who looked after her, fed her, and kept her safe.

Safe from what? Irene didn't know exactly. She only knew that she was never allowed outside after dark, and that when the sun began to set, Lootie would hurry her inside with a nervous look over her shoulder, as if the shadows themselves were dangerous.

"Why can't I stay out?" Irene would ask.

"Because it isn't safe, princess. That's all you need to know."

Irene was a good-natured child — trusting, curious, and braver than she looked. She had dark hair, bright eyes, and a way of asking questions that made adults uncomfortable, because her questions were often the ones nobody wanted to answer.

The mountain house was enormous and mostly empty. Dozens of rooms stood unused, their doors shut, their furniture covered in white sheets like sleeping ghosts. Irene had explored many of them during the long, rainy days when she couldn't go outside. She knew every corridor on the lower floors, every creaking step on the main staircase, every window seat where she could curl up with a book and watch the rain.

But the upper floors were different. They were older, dustier, and more confusing — a maze of narrow hallways and unexpected staircases that seemed to lead nowhere. Irene had been told not to go up there alone.

She went anyway.



CHAPTER 2

THE MYSTERIOUS STAIRCASE

On a rainy afternoon when Lootie was napping and the servants were busy in the kitchen, Irene slipped through a door she had never noticed before and found a staircase.

It was narrow and winding, built of old stone, and it spiraled upward into the tower. Irene had never been in this part of the house. The air smelled of dust and something else — something faintly sweet, like dried roses.

She climbed. The stairs went up and up, past landings with locked doors, past windows so narrow that only a sliver of gray light came through. Her shoes made soft sounds on the stone.

At the top, she found a door. It was small and wooden, with a simple iron latch. She lifted the latch and pushed.

The room beyond was not what she expected.

It was a large, round room, lit by a warm glow that had no visible source. In the center sat a very old woman at a spinning wheel, spinning thread so fine it was almost invisible. The woman had silver hair that fell to the floor and eyes that were young and bright — far too young for her ancient face.

Irene stared. The old woman smiled.

"Come in, my dear," she said. Her voice was gentle, like music heard from far away.

"Who are you?" Irene asked.

"I am your great-great-grandmother."

Irene's eyes went wide. "But — you would be hundreds of years old."

"Yes," the old woman said simply. "I would be."

She went back to her spinning. The wheel turned silently. The thread caught the light and gleamed like a line of silver.

Irene sat down on a stool and watched, and something about the room — its warmth, its stillness, the steady rhythm of the spinning wheel — made her feel safer than she had ever felt in her life.



CHAPTER 3

THE GREAT-GREAT-GRANDMOTHER

Irene visited every day that week. Each time, the staircase was in a different place — or seemed to be. She would wander the upper hallways, open doors she didn't remember seeing before, and somehow find her way to the round room at the top of the tower.

The grandmother — that was what Irene called her — was always there, spinning. She answered some of Irene's questions and smiled at others without answering. She told Irene about the thread she was spinning.

"This thread is special," she said. "When the time comes, it will guide you. It will lead you where you need to go, even in the dark."

"What do you mean, 'when the time comes'?"

"You will know."

The grandmother was mysterious, but not in a frightening way. She was mysterious the way the stars are mysterious — vast and unknowable, but constant. Irene trusted her completely, the way a child trusts a parent, without needing reasons.

The grandmother showed Irene a ring — a simple gold ring with no stone. "When the time comes," she said, "I will give you this ring. The thread will be tied to it. If you are lost, if you are afraid, follow the thread. It will always lead you to safety."

"But how will I see the thread? It's almost invisible."

"You won't see it. You'll feel it. That's what trust means — following something you can't see, because you know it's real."

Irene nodded. She didn't fully understand, but she believed.

Believing, the grandmother seemed to say, was the first step.



CHAPTER 4

NO ONE BELIEVES HER

Irene told Lootie about the grandmother. It was a mistake.

"There is no old woman in the tower," Lootie said firmly. "I have lived in this house for years, and I have never seen anyone up there."

"But I saw her! She has silver hair, and she spins thread, and she says she's my great-great-grandmother!"

Lootie's face went pale, then red. "Princess, you must stop making up stories. There is no one in the tower. The upper rooms are empty."

"Come with me. I'll show you!"

They went together. Irene led Lootie through the hallways, up the stairs, through the door — and into an empty room. Dusty, cold, with no spinning wheel, no grandmother, no warm glow. Just bare stone walls and a single window looking out at the rain.

Irene stared. "She was here. She was right here."

"There is no one here, princess." Lootie's voice was kind but firm. "You had a dream. That's all."

Irene didn't argue further. But she didn't stop believing, either. She knew what she had seen. She knew what she had felt. The grandmother was real — as real as the stone under her feet — and the fact that Lootie couldn't see her didn't change that.

This was Irene's first experience with a truth that would define her life: some things are real even when other people can't see them. And believing in those things — trusting what you know in your heart — requires more courage than believing in things everyone can see.



CHAPTER 5

CURDIE THE MINER

Below the castle, in the town at the foot of the mountain, a boy named Curdie Peterson worked in the mines with his father.

Curdie was twelve years old, practical and strong, with rough hands and a sharp mind. His father, Peter Peterson, was one of the king's miners — men who dug deep into the mountain for gold and precious stones that were sent to the capital to fill the royal treasury.

The work was hard and dangerous. The tunnels were narrow, dark, and prone to collapse. The air was thick with dust. Miners worked by candlelight, swinging picks until their arms ached, filling carts with ore that other men hauled to the surface. Boys Curdie's age worked alongside men twice their size, because in a mining town, everyone worked.

But the mine held a secret that most people in the town preferred not to talk about.

Deep beneath the mountain, below the levels where the miners worked, there were other tunnels — older tunnels, carved not by human hands but by something else. Something that lived in the dark.

Goblins.

The miners knew about the goblins the way people know about earthquakes or wolves — a danger that was always present but rarely discussed. The goblins had lived under the mountain for centuries, driven underground long ago by the humans who had settled the surface. They were small, twisted creatures — stronger than they looked, clever in a cruel way, and filled with a bitter hatred for the people who lived above them.

The goblins kept to their own tunnels, mostly. They came out at night sometimes, creeping through the darkness, but they fled from light and noise. The miners stomped their feet and sang loud songs when they worked near goblin territory, because goblins hated the sound of singing and the vibration of stamping feet.

Curdie wasn't afraid of goblins. He was cautious — his father had taught him to be cautious — but he wasn't afraid. He had grown up in the mines. He knew the tunnels better than most grown men. And he had a quality that would prove more valuable than strength or speed: he paid attention.



CHAPTER 6

THE GOBLINS BELOW

One evening, Curdie stayed late in the mines — later than he should have, following a vein of quartz that glittered in his candlelight. He wandered deeper than usual and found himself in a part of the tunnel system he didn't recognize.

The walls were smoother here. The air was warmer. And there was a sound — a low, rhythmic murmur, like many voices speaking at once.

Curdie pressed himself against the wall and crept forward. The tunnel opened into a large cavern, lit by a sickly greenish glow. And in the cavern, seated on a rough stone throne, was the Goblin King.

He was larger than the other goblins — nearly the size of a man, but wider and heavier, with a flat, broad face and small, mean eyes. His queen sat beside him, tall and thin, with feet so long and flat that she walked on them like snowshoes. Around them, hundreds of goblins crowded the cavern floor.

They were having a council.

Curdie couldn't hear everything, but he caught enough. The Goblin King was angry. He was tired of hiding underground. He was tired of the miners digging into his territory, stealing the mountain's treasure. He wanted revenge.

And he had a plan.

The goblins had been tunneling — secretly, quietly, for months — toward the castle on the mountainside. They were going to break through the castle floor, storm the building, and capture the princess.

"The king's daughter," the Goblin Queen hissed. "She will marry our prince. Then we will have a claim to the surface. Then the mountain will be ours."

Curdie's blood went cold. He backed away slowly, his heart hammering, and ran for the surface.



CHAPTER 7

THE GOBLIN PLAN

Curdie told his father everything. Peter Peterson listened carefully, his face grim.

"I've suspected something for weeks," his father said. "We've been hearing noises in the lower tunnels — tapping, scraping, as if something is digging. The other miners have noticed too."

"We have to warn the castle."

"We will. But Curdie — be careful what you say and who you say it to. The castle servants don't take kindly to miners showing up with wild stories about goblins. They'll think you're trying to cause trouble."

His father was right. The people in the castle and the people in the mines lived in different worlds, even though they shared the same mountain. The castle folk looked down on the miners — literally and figuratively. A miner's son claiming that monsters were tunneling toward the princess would be laughed out of the building.

But Curdie also learned more about the goblins' weaknesses. They were strong in the dark, but their feet were their vulnerable point — soft, tender, and unprotected. A good stamp on a goblin's foot would send it howling. And they couldn't stand singing — real singing, with melody and heart. It disrupted them, confused them, drove them back like light drives back shadows.

Curdie filed this information away. He was practical, like his father. He didn't waste time being afraid of problems. He prepared for them.



CHAPTER 8

CURDIE'S WARNING

Curdie went to the castle. He cleaned his face, put on his best clothes — which were still rough and patched — and walked up the mountain road to the great stone house.

The servants turned him away at the gate.

"The princess doesn't receive miners' boys," the guard said, not unkindly. "Go back to your tunnels, lad."

"But there's danger — the goblins are digging toward the castle. They're planning to break through the floor—"

"Goblins?" The guard snorted. "There haven't been goblin problems in years. Off with you."

Curdie tried again the next day. And the next. Each time, he was turned away. The castle servants couldn't imagine that a miner's boy might know something they didn't. Their world was the world they could see — the castle, the princess, the well-ordered life above ground. The world below — the dark, the danger, the goblins — was not their concern.

Curdie was frustrated, but he didn't give up. If the castle wouldn't listen to him, he would protect the princess himself. He began spending his nights in the tunnels, tracking the goblins' progress, mapping their route, looking for any advantage he could find.

He was one boy against an army of goblins. But he was a miner's son, and miners don't quit when the rock is hard. They keep digging.



CHAPTER 9

THE PRINCESS AND THE MINER

They met by accident.

Irene and Lootie were walking on the mountainside one evening when Lootie made the mistake of staying out too late. The sun dropped below the ridge, and suddenly the shadows were full of movement — small, dark shapes creeping between the rocks.

Goblins.

Lootie screamed and ran, dragging Irene by the hand. But they were going the wrong way — downhill, toward the goblin territory, instead of uphill toward the castle. The creatures followed, chattering and laughing in their harsh, grinding voices.

Then a boy appeared out of nowhere. He was carrying a miner's pick and singing — singing loudly, fiercely, a rough mining song with a stomping rhythm. The goblins flinched. They fell back. The boy stamped his feet as he sang, and the goblins scattered like cockroaches when a light is turned on.

"Run!" the boy shouted. "Up the hill! To the castle!"

Irene ran. The boy followed, singing and stamping, keeping the goblins at bay until they reached the castle gates. The guards let them in and barred the door.

Irene turned to the boy. He was about twelve, dirty, with calloused hands and a defiant expression.

"What's your name?" she asked.

"Curdie. Curdie Peterson. I'm a miner."

"I'm Princess Irene. Thank you for saving us."

Curdie looked at the princess — small, dark-haired, with bright eyes that showed no fear, only gratitude — and felt something shift inside him. He had come to warn the castle about goblins. Now he had a face to protect. It was different, somehow. More real.

"You should be more careful," he said gruffly. "The goblins are getting bolder."

"Will you come back and tell me about them?"

"They won't let me in the castle. I'm a miner's boy."

"I'm a princess," Irene said. "I'll let you in."



CHAPTER 10

GOBLIN CREATURES

The goblins weren't the only danger on the mountain. They kept creatures — animals as twisted and strange as the goblins themselves.

These goblin creatures had once been ordinary animals — dogs, cats, rats — that had been taken underground generations ago. Over the years, living in darkness with the goblins, they had changed. They were larger, fiercer, and stranger than their surface cousins. Dogs with no eyes but an uncanny sense of smell. Cats with claws like fishhooks. Rats the size of small dogs.

The creatures began appearing on the mountainside at night, ranging further from the goblin tunnels than they ever had before. Livestock disappeared. A miner's dog was found dead. The people of the town grew nervous.

Curdie tracked the creatures through the forest and back to their source — a new tunnel entrance the goblins had dug, closer to the surface than any Curdie had seen. The goblins were expanding their territory, pushing upward, preparing for something big.

He brought this news to Irene. They met in secret, at the castle gate, speaking through the bars like prisoners.

"They're getting closer," Curdie said. "The tunnel I found is only a few hundred yards from the castle wall."

"What should we do?"

"I'll keep watching. You stay inside after dark. And if anything strange happens — any sound from below the floors, any cracking or shaking — run. Get out of the castle and go uphill. The goblins won't follow into open ground above the tree line."

Irene nodded. She was eight years old, and she was being asked to prepare for an invasion. She didn't cry. She didn't panic. She filed the information away, the same way Curdie did.

They were alike, these two — the princess and the miner. Different worlds, different lives, but the same practical courage.



CHAPTER 11

THE GRANDMOTHER'S THREAD

Irene found her way to the grandmother's room again.

The old woman was waiting, as if she had known Irene would come. The spinning wheel turned. The thread gleamed.

"The time has come," the grandmother said.

She placed the gold ring on Irene's finger. It was warm — warmer than metal should be — and it fit perfectly, as if it had been made for her.

"The thread is tied to this ring," the grandmother said. "You cannot see it. You cannot feel it with your fingers. But it is there, and it will guide you."

"Where will it lead me?"

"Wherever you need to go. Into the dark, if necessary. Through places that frighten you. But always — always — toward safety. The thread will never lead you wrong."

"How do I follow something I can't see?"

The grandmother smiled. "You follow it with your heart. You trust it. Even when everything around you says it's not there — even when people you love tell you you're foolish — you trust the thread."

"What if I'm scared?"

"You will be scared. Courage doesn't mean you're not afraid. Courage means you follow the thread anyway."

Irene looked at the ring on her finger. She could see nothing attached to it — no thread, no line, no gleam of silver. But when she closed her eyes and reached out with her heart instead of her hands, she felt it. A gentle tug. A direction. A quiet voice that said: this way.

"I'll follow it," Irene said.

"I know you will," the grandmother said. "That's why I chose you."



CHAPTER 12

FOLLOWING THE THREAD

The night came sooner than Irene expected.

She was in her room, getting ready for bed, when she felt the thread pull. Not a physical pull — she couldn't have described it to anyone — but a certainty, deep in her chest, that she needed to go. Now. Right now.

She put on her shoes, wrapped herself in a cloak, and followed the thread.

It led her out of her room, down the hallway, through a door she had never noticed, and into a passageway that sloped downward — below the castle, into the rock of the mountain itself. The air grew cold. The darkness was absolute.

Irene was terrified. Every instinct told her to turn back, to run to Lootie, to hide under her blankets and pretend none of this was happening. She was eight years old, alone in the dark, in tunnels that led into goblin territory.

But the thread pulled, and she followed.

She walked for what felt like hours. The tunnel twisted and branched. She could hear sounds in the darkness — dripping water, distant tapping, the scurrying of goblin creatures. Once, she heard goblin voices, harsh and close, and she pressed herself against the wall and held her breath until they passed.

The thread never wavered. It led her left when she would have gone right. It led her down when she wanted to go up. It led her deeper into the mountain, into the very heart of goblin territory, and every step was an act of faith.

Faith in the grandmother. Faith in the thread. Faith that the invisible could be more real than anything she could see or touch.



CHAPTER 13

RESCUING CURDIE

The thread led her to Curdie.

He was trapped in a goblin cell — a small, rough-hewn chamber with a stone door that had been rolled across the entrance. The goblins had caught him spying on their tunnels and locked him away.

Irene heard his voice first — singing. Even trapped, even alone, Curdie was singing, because he knew it kept the goblins at bay.

"Curdie!" she whispered through a crack in the stone. "It's me! Irene!"

"Princess? What are you doing down here?"

"I've come to rescue you."

"You? Rescue me?" Despite everything, he almost laughed. "How did you find me?"

"My grandmother's thread led me."

There was a pause. "Your what?"

Irene felt along the stone door. It was heavy — far too heavy for an eight-year-old girl to move. But the thread tugged her hand to a specific spot, and when she pushed there, the stone shifted. It was balanced on a pivot, like a secret door, and with the right pressure in the right place, it swung open.

Curdie stared at her — this small girl in a nightgown and cloak, standing in the blackest part of the goblin tunnels as if she were walking through a garden.

"How did you know where to push?" he asked.

"The thread showed me."

"What thread? I don't see any thread."

"I know," Irene said. "But it's there."

They ran. Irene led, following the thread back through the twisting tunnels, and Curdie followed, stamping his feet and singing to keep the goblins back. Together — her faith and his fight — they made it to the surface.



CHAPTER 14

CURDIE DOESN'T BELIEVE

They sat on the hillside in the moonlight, catching their breath, and Irene told Curdie everything — the grandmother, the spinning wheel, the thread, the ring.

Curdie listened. His face was polite, but his eyes were skeptical.

"I believe that you believe it," he said carefully. "But I didn't see any thread. I didn't see any grandmother. You found me because you're brave and clever, not because of magic."

Irene felt a sharp pain in her chest — not anger, but disappointment. She had risked her life to save him, guided by something she trusted more than anything in the world, and he didn't believe her.

"Come with me," she said. "I'll take you to the grandmother. You can see for yourself."

They climbed to the castle and up the stairs. Irene led Curdie through the hallways, found the door, climbed the winding staircase — and opened the door to an empty room.

No grandmother. No spinning wheel. No warm glow. Just bare stone and dust.

"She was here," Irene whispered.

Curdie said nothing. He didn't mock her. He didn't call her a liar. He simply looked at the empty room and said, "I believe you're brave, Princess. That's enough for me."

But it wasn't enough for Irene. She knew the grandmother was real. She knew the thread was real. And she knew that someday, Curdie would believe it too.

The question was whether that day would come soon enough.



CHAPTER 15

THE GOBLIN ATTACK

The goblins attacked on a night without moon.

They had been tunneling for months, and they finally broke through — not into the castle, as Curdie had feared, but into the cellars beneath it. The stone floor cracked, then caved in, and goblin warriors poured up through the hole like water through a broken dam.

The castle was thrown into chaos. Servants screamed. Guards grabbed swords and rushed to the cellar stairs. The goblin creatures — the eyeless dogs, the enormous rats — swarmed through the lower floors, knocking over furniture, tearing at doors.

Irene was in her room when the floor trembled. She knew instantly what was happening. She grabbed her cloak, woke Lootie, and led her to the upper floors, away from the goblins.

Curdie was already in the castle — he had been sleeping in the servants' quarters, keeping watch as he had for weeks. When the floor collapsed, he grabbed his pick and ran toward the breach.

The battle was desperate. The goblins were strong in numbers, and they fought with a savage fury born of centuries of resentment. The castle guards were braver but unprepared. They had never fought goblins — most of them had never even seen one.

Curdie rallied the guards. "Stamp on their feet!" he shouted. "Sing! They can't stand singing!"

The guards thought he was insane. Then they tried it — stomping, shouting, singing any song they could think of — and the goblins flinched. They fell back. The singing didn't kill them, but it confused them, disrupted their coordination, and gave the humans an advantage they desperately needed.

But the goblins kept coming. There were hundreds of them, and more were pouring through the breach every minute.



CHAPTER 16

THE FLOOD

Curdie knew the mountain.

He knew every tunnel, every shaft, every underground stream. And he knew that above the goblin tunnels, behind a thin wall of rock, ran an underground river — a powerful current that the miners had been careful to avoid for years.

If he could break through that wall, the river would flood the goblin tunnels. It would drive the goblins back, collapse their passages, and cut off their route to the castle.

It would also be incredibly dangerous. The flood would be uncontrollable. Anyone in the tunnels when the water came would be swept away.

Curdie didn't hesitate. He grabbed his pick, fought his way through the goblins in the cellar, and plunged into the breach — back into the tunnels, back into the dark, running through passages he knew by feel.

The goblins chased him. He stamped and sang, but they were close — too close. He could hear their feet slapping on the stone behind him.

He found the wall. He could hear the river behind it — a deep, rushing roar that vibrated in the rock. He swung his pick. Once. Twice. Three times. Cracks appeared. Water seeped through.

He swung again. The wall gave way.

The river exploded through the opening with the force of a cannon. Water filled the tunnel in seconds — a roaring, churning flood that swept everything before it. Goblins were carried away like leaves. Their creatures tumbled in the current. The tunnels filled with dark, freezing water.

Curdie was caught in the flood too. The water slammed him against the tunnel wall, spun him, dragged him. He grabbed a rock ledge and held on with both hands as the river raged past him.

When the water finally subsided, the goblin tunnels were flooded. The goblins' route to the castle was destroyed. The breach in the cellar floor was sealed by water and debris.

Curdie, soaked and battered but alive, climbed out of the tunnels and back into the castle.



CHAPTER 17

THE BATTLE WON

The goblins who had already entered the castle were trapped — cut off from their tunnels by the flood, surrounded by guards who had learned to stamp and sing. Without reinforcements, without their creatures, and without a way to retreat, they were defeated within the hour.

By morning, the castle was quiet. The breach was sealed. The goblin army was scattered, driven back into the deepest parts of the mountain where the floodwaters hadn't reached.

Curdie was a hero. The guards who had dismissed him as a miner's boy now clapped him on the back and called him "lad" with genuine respect. The servants brought him food, dry clothes, and a warm place by the fire.

Irene found him in the great hall, sitting by the hearth, staring at the flames. He looked exhausted — not just physically, but in some deeper way, as if the night had cost him something he couldn't name.

"You saved us," Irene said.

"We saved each other," Curdie said. "You saved me from the goblin cell. I stopped the tunnel. We're even."

"How did you know about the river?"

"I'm a miner. I know the mountain." He paused. "But how did you know where to find me? In the dark, in goblin territory — how did an eight-year-old girl navigate tunnels that grown miners get lost in?"

Irene looked at him steadily. "The thread."

Curdie stared at the fire. He wanted to dismiss it — his practical, mining brain wanted to find a rational explanation. But he had seen Irene walk through absolute darkness as if she could see. He had seen her find the hidden pivot in a stone door. He had seen an eight-year-old girl do something that should have been impossible.

Maybe the thread was real. Maybe the grandmother was real. Maybe there were things in the world that a miner's pick couldn't uncover and a miner's lamp couldn't illuminate.

"Maybe," Curdie said quietly. "Maybe I believe you."



CHAPTER 18

THE GRANDMOTHER'S KISS

The next day, Irene went to find the grandmother.

She climbed the stairs — the stairs that were never in the same place twice — and opened the small wooden door. The round room was warm and glowing. The spinning wheel turned. The grandmother sat in her chair, her silver hair cascading to the floor, her young-old eyes bright with something that might have been pride.

"You followed the thread," the grandmother said.

"Yes."

"You were afraid."

"Yes."

"But you followed it anyway."

"You told me courage means following the thread even when you're scared."

The grandmother stood and took Irene's face in both hands. Her touch was warm — warmer than fire, warmer than sunlight. She kissed Irene on the forehead.

"That kiss will protect you," she said. "Not from danger — I can't promise you that. But from the thing that is worse than danger: the temptation to stop trusting."

"Will Curdie believe someday?"

"Curdie is honest and brave. Honest, brave people always come to believe, in time. But they must find belief through their own path, not through someone else's. You followed the thread because you trusted me. Curdie will follow when he trusts himself enough to see what's really there."

Irene hugged the grandmother. The old woman's arms were strong — stronger than they looked, strong enough to hold the whole mountain, strong enough to hold the world.

"Will I see you again?" Irene asked.

"Whenever you need me," the grandmother said. "I am always here. The thread is always tied. All you have to do is follow it."

Irene walked back down the stairs and out into the morning sunlight. The mountain was peaceful. The town was stirring. Somewhere below, Curdie was in the mines, working alongside his father, keeping watch.

The ring glowed faintly on Irene's finger. She couldn't see the thread, but she could feel it — a gentle tug, a quiet certainty, a line of trust stretching from her heart to something vast and invisible and absolutely, unshakably real.

She would follow it wherever it led.

THE END
