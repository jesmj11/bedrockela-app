

ANNE OF GREEN GABLES

A YA Adaptation

Based on the novel by L.M. Montgomery

7th Grade ELA — BedrockELA

Theme: Who You Want to Be vs. Who You Actually Are





Chapter 1

The Wrong Child



Matthew Cuthbert was not a man who liked surprises. He liked his farm, his horse, his sister Marilla, and the quiet of Prince Edward Island in the early evening. He did not like strangers, conversation, or unexpected developments. So when he arrived at the Bright River station to collect the orphan boy he and Marilla had arranged to adopt — a boy who would help on the farm — and found instead a girl sitting alone on a bench, his first instinct was to turn around and go home.

She was about eleven years old, with a thin face covered in freckles and two long braids of red hair. She wore a dress that was too short and too tight, and she had a battered carpet bag at her feet that looked like everything she owned in the world was inside it. She was sitting very straight, as if posture might compensate for everything else, and she was looking at the world around her with enormous, interested eyes.

She saw him looking and stood up immediately.

"I suppose you are Mr. Matthew Cuthbert?" she said. "I'm very glad you're here, because I was beginning to be afraid no one was coming for me, and I was imagining all the things that might have happened — perhaps you had been thrown from your buggy and were lying injured in a ditch somewhere, or perhaps there had been a miscommunication and you'd come yesterday instead and I'd been left here entirely by mistake. I've been imagining for the past hour."

Matthew said, carefully, that no such thing had occurred.

"I'm relieved," she said. "I've been told I imagine too much. But I find it better than not imagining at all, don't you? If you don't imagine things, you just sit there with things as they are, and things as they are can be rather dull."

Matthew, who had no experience with children and especially not with children who talked like this, said that he supposed so.

He could not bring himself to tell her, on the drive to Green Gables, that there had been a mistake. She was so obviously happy — happy with the trees, the sky, the road, the horse, the smell of apple blossoms. She had never been in a place this beautiful, she told him. She had never had anyone come to meet her anywhere before.

"I know I'm not what you wanted," she said, at one point, as if she could read the thought he hadn't voiced. "I know you wanted a boy. People always want boys when they want help. But I'll try very hard to be useful, and I have a very strong imagination, which I think could be an asset in some situations."

Matthew was not accustomed to children speaking frankly about their own situations. He found, to his considerable surprise, that he rather liked it.

He said nothing about the mistake. He brought her home. He told Marilla she'd have to deal with it.

Marilla was not pleased.





Chapter 2

Marilla Makes Up Her Mind



Marilla Cuthbert was a woman of sharp edges and practical views. She believed in plain food, plain speech, and plain dress, and she was deeply suspicious of anything that seemed designed to make life more pleasant than it needed to be. She was not unkind — she would have been shocked to be called unkind — but she expressed care through action rather than words, and she had no patience for sentiment.

When Matthew brought home a girl instead of a boy, Marilla's first response was brisk and practical: the girl would have to go back.

She said this to the child directly, because she did not believe in softening things unnecessarily.

"You'll have to be returned to the orphanage," she told Anne — for the girl had told them her name, and given the full version of it with the e spelled out, because she felt the e made a considerable difference. "We asked for a boy. We need help on the farm. You're not what we arranged for."

Anne went very still. She had the quality, when something hurt her, of becoming entirely motionless, as if the stillness might contain it.

"I understand," she said. "I'm used to not being wanted."

She said it quietly, without self-pity. It was worse, somehow, than crying would have been.

Marilla went to bed that night intending to send Anne back in the morning. She woke up still intending it. But she found, as the day went on, that the intention was harder to carry out than she'd expected.

Anne was strange — there was no question of that. She had a habit of going off into long, flowing descriptions of things she found beautiful. She named a lane full of white cherry trees "the White Way of Delight" and a pond "the Lake of Shining Waters" and when Marilla pointed out these were not their real names, Anne said yes, but the real names weren't good enough for the places, and the places deserved better names. This was not logic Marilla could entirely argue with, though she tried.

She also prayed differently than Marilla expected. When asked to say grace at dinner, Anne said she would try her best, and produced a prayer that began "Dear God, I'm so thankful for this beautiful world and for the cherry blossoms and for being here instead of the orphanage and also for the fact that my hair is not black, because I think that would be worse somehow."

Marilla said that was not a proper grace.

Anne said she'd always thought grace should be honest, and she was honest about what she was grateful for.

Something shifted in Marilla, slightly. Not enough to name. But enough to pause.

She wrote to the orphanage to ask about the circumstances of the mistake. She received a letter that told her of three other families who had turned Anne back over the years. Not because she was bad, the letter said. She was bright and eager and full of ideas. It was just — she was a lot. And most people wanted children who were less.

Marilla read this letter twice. Then she folded it and put it in her pocket and went to find Anne.

"You'll stay," she said. "On trial. We'll see how it goes."

Anne looked at her with a combination of hope and disbelief that was painful to look at directly.

"On trial," she repeated. "That's — I'll be so good, Mrs. Cuthbert. Or I'll try to be. I may not always succeed because I have a very bad temper and sometimes I say things I shouldn't. But I'll try."

"I know you'll try," Marilla said. "That's all anyone can do."

She went back to her work and thought no more about it.

Or so she told herself.





Chapter 3

Green Gables



Green Gables was the most beautiful place Anne had ever seen, and Anne had imagined a great many beautiful places.

It was not grand — not the kind of beautiful that required money or display. It was a farmhouse, white with green trim, sitting on a slight rise with apple orchards on one side and a birch woods on the other. The kitchen had a low ceiling and smelled of baking. The garden was full of flowers that Marilla grew for the pleasure of growing them, not because she would ever have said she kept a garden for pleasure. The spare room where Anne would sleep faced east, which meant morning light, which Anne considered practically miraculous.

She stood in the doorway of the room on her first night and looked at it with an expression that made Matthew, passing in the hall, stop and look away.

"It's not much," he said gruffly, as an apology.

"It's perfect," Anne said. "I've shared rooms all my life. I've never had a room of my own."

She put her few things away with great care and stood at the window for a long time watching the apple trees.

She told Marilla, at breakfast, that she had decided to love Green Gables.

"You've decided," Marilla said.

"I was afraid to," Anne said. "In case it didn't work out and I had to leave. But then I thought — it's better to love a place and lose it than to have had it and never let yourself care. So I've decided."

Marilla looked at her over the coffee pot and said nothing.

In the days that followed, Marilla set Anne to work. She was firm on this point: Anne was not a pet and not a guest. If she was to stay at Green Gables, she would earn her place. There were dishes to wash, vegetables to bring in, sewing to learn, cooking to be supervised. Anne attacked all of it with a kind of desperate enthusiasm, though she was not skilled at any of it. She burned the porridge because she was thinking about a story she was inventing. She dropped an entire bowl of peas because she was watching a butterfly.

"You must keep your mind on what you're doing," Marilla said, more than once.

"I know," Anne said. "I keep it there and then it wanders off again. It's very inconvenient. I don't do it on purpose."

She was also given an education. Marilla quizzed her on what she knew and found that Anne had read widely but irregularly — she knew things about history and poetry and literature that children twice her age didn't know, and she didn't know things that children half her age did. She learned the catechism reluctantly, because she could see no poetry in it, and learned the multiplication tables enthusiastically because she found patterns beautiful.

"You're a strange child," Marilla said one evening.

"I know," Anne said. "People have always said so. I used to mind it. Now I think I'd rather be strange and interesting than ordinary and dull."

Marilla said she wouldn't go that far.

But that night she told Matthew that the child was settling in, and there was something in her voice that Matthew, who knew his sister, understood to be considerably warmer than her words.





Chapter 4

The Incident with the Brooch



Marilla had an amethyst brooch that she valued above all other possessions — not because it was worth a great deal of money, but because it had been her mother's.

Anne admired it openly, with the directness she applied to everything. "It's the most beautiful thing I've ever seen," she said. "The purple is exactly the color I've always imagined amethysts should be."

Marilla allowed her to look at it, but instructed her firmly that she was not to touch it.

The next morning the brooch was gone.

Marilla searched the room. She searched the bureau, the windowsill, the floor, the pocket of her good dress. The brooch was not there. She came downstairs with a face like a thundercloud and found Anne at the table with a book.

"Did you take my brooch?"

Anne looked up with the alert, slightly nervous expression she wore when she sensed trouble. "I only held it for a moment," she said. "I wanted to see how it felt. It was so beautiful. I put it back."

"You admit you took it."

"I held it," Anne said. "For just a moment. And then I put it back on the bureau, exactly where it was."

Marilla did not believe her. This was not because she thought Anne was a bad child, but because the brooch was missing, Anne had touched it, and Marilla was a woman who followed evidence to its conclusions.

"You will not go to the Sunday school picnic," she said, "until you confess what you did with it."

The picnic was the great social event of the season. Anne had been anticipating it with a fervor that bordered on religious. Marilla knew this.

Anne said she had nothing to confess.

The stand-off lasted two days. Anne remained in her room, increasingly desperate. Marilla remained firm, increasingly uncomfortable. Matthew said nothing, but his face said a great deal.

On the second morning, Anne appeared before Marilla with the look of someone who has made a terrible decision and knows it.

"I confess," she said. "I took the brooch and I was wearing it and I leaned over the bridge to look at my reflection and it fell in and sank."

Marilla looked at her. Anne looked back, her chin high.

Marilla sent her to her room and went to put on her other brooch — the plain one. The amethyst brooch was in the folds of her shawl, which she had overlooked in her searching. She had found it ten minutes earlier.

She went upstairs.

"The brooch was not lost," she said. "I found it. Why did you confess to something you didn't do?"

Anne looked at her with complete simplicity. "Because you said I couldn't go to the picnic until I confessed. And I wanted to go to the picnic more than I've ever wanted anything. So I confessed." A pause. "I'm sorry. I know it was wrong. But the picnic —"

Marilla sat down on the edge of the bed. She was, unexpectedly, trying not to smile.

"You may go to the picnic," she said. "And we will say no more about the brooch."

She went downstairs and allowed herself, in the privacy of the kitchen, a very small smile.

The child was impossible. The child was also — she would not say it aloud — rather wonderful.





Chapter 5

School



Anne went to school in Avonlea and immediately made two things: a best friend, and an enemy.

The best friend was Diana Barry, who lived nearby and had dark hair and rosy cheeks and a sweet disposition that was almost the opposite of Anne's, which made them, somehow, perfectly complementary. Anne decided within five minutes that Diana was a kindred spirit, which was her term for people who understood the world the same way she did. Diana was not entirely sure what a kindred spirit was, but she was quite certain she liked Anne.

The enemy was Gilbert Blythe, who was handsome and clever and used to being the most interesting person in the room.

On Anne's first day, he reached across the aisle and tugged one of her braids and said "Carrots."

The class laughed. Anne went white, then red. Red hair had been a source of suffering all her life — she minded it more than almost anything, which was illogical, she knew, but feelings frequently are.

She picked up her slate and brought it down on Gilbert's head with a crack that silenced the entire room.

The teacher kept her after school and made her stand in the corner for the afternoon. Anne stood with her back perfectly straight and did not cry.

Gilbert tried to apologize after school. Anne walked past him without looking at him.

"I said sorry," he called.

"Some things," Anne said, without turning around, "cannot be apologized for."

She meant it. The grudge was complete and she had every intention of keeping it.

She was, she knew, being unreasonable. She didn't care. The red hair was the particular wound that had never healed, and Gilbert Blythe had poked it deliberately.

School itself, once she had established this enmity, she found mostly wonderful. She loved literature with a love that was almost embarrassing. She loved history except for dates. She did not love mathematics except when it became a problem she could think her way through. She threw herself into every lesson with the kind of whole-hearted attention that made some teachers delighted and others exhausted.

And she made Diana her devoted companion. They walked to school together and home again and spent the long summer afternoons in a raspberry cane field they had claimed as their own, reading to each other from books they passed back and forth, inventing elaborate games and stories that neither of them ever wrote down.

"You're my best friend," Anne told Diana solemnly, one afternoon. "I've never had a best friend before. I used to pretend I had one — a girl in the reflection in the glass. Her name was Katie Maurice. But she couldn't answer back, which was a limitation."

Diana promised, equally solemnly, to be Anne's best friend for as long as she lived.

Anne was so happy she couldn't sleep that night. She lay in her room with the east window open and thought: I have a friend. A real one. I have a home and a friend.

It seemed almost too much to be true, and she was afraid to trust it.

But in the morning Diana was there at the gate with her schoolbag, exactly as promised.

And Anne walked to school beside her, and for once the red hair didn't matter.





Chapter 6

The Apology



Anne's temper was her most significant character flaw, and she knew it. She did not know how to hold it. When it came, it came like weather — sudden and total — and by the time she could think clearly again, the damage was usually done.

The worst occasion of the whole first year at Green Gables was the incident with Mrs. Rachel Lynde.

Mrs. Lynde was the neighborhood's most accomplished observer of other people's business. She sat at her front window and kept thorough track of all comings and goings in Avonlea, and she had opinions about all of them. She was not malicious — she was, in her own way, kind — but she was entirely without the understanding that some things, spoken plainly, hurt.

She came to tea in the autumn and looked at Anne across the table and said, cheerfully, that Anne was "terribly skinny and homely" and that her hair was "as red as carrots."

Anne rose from her chair.

What she said to Mrs. Rachel Lynde was extensive, impassioned, and extremely personal. It touched on Mrs. Lynde's own appearance, her small-mindedness, and her considerable lack of business being in other people's lives. It was delivered without pausing for breath and with a passion that transfixed the room.

Then she ran upstairs.

Marilla sent Mrs. Lynde home with profuse apologies and went upstairs to deal with the problem.

"You will apologize," she said.

"She said I was skinny and homely," Anne said, from her position face-down on the bed. "She said my hair was the color of carrots. She said it in front of you as if I weren't there."

"She shouldn't have said it," Marilla said. "It was unkind. But two wrongs don't make a right."

"I know that," Anne said, muffled by the pillow. "But she started it."

"That is not the point."

Anne sat up. Her face was red and her eyes were still bright with temper. "I find it very difficult," she said, "to apologize for things that were deserved."

Marilla said that was precisely why she needed to do it.

Anne thought about this. She thought about it for a long time — long enough that Marilla had to wait, which she was not accustomed to doing.

"All right," Anne said finally. "I'll apologize. But it will be a full apology. If I'm going to apologize, I'll do it properly."

She went to Mrs. Lynde's house that afternoon. She knelt — actually knelt — on the floor and produced an apology of such elaborate and heartfelt remorse that Mrs. Lynde was simultaneously moved and somewhat overwhelmed. She forgave Anne completely and then told Marilla afterward that the child was unusual but she thought she would probably turn out all right.

Walking home, Anne's step was lighter.

"How was it?" Marilla asked.

"It was actually rather satisfying," Anne said. "If you're going to humble yourself, you might as well do it completely. There's no point in a small apology."

Marilla considered this. "That's — not a bad way to look at it."

"Also," Anne said, "Mrs. Lynde called my hair distinctive afterward. Which I think is better than carrots."

Marilla said nothing. But she thought: this strange, difficult, impossible child might just be all right.





Chapter 7

The Haunted Wood



Anne had one significant weakness that she was somewhat ashamed of: she was afraid of the dark, and specifically of a stand of woods between Green Gables and the Barry farm that she had, in a moment of literary inspiration, named the Haunted Wood.

The naming had been a mistake. Once you named a place the Haunted Wood, the imagination supplied the rest without being asked.

She had begun seeing things there. Not real things — she knew they weren't real things, which was almost worse. A pale shape among the trees. Something at the edge of the path that retreated when she looked directly at it. She stopped going through the wood after dark.

Then she told Diana about the hauntings, because she told Diana everything.

And then Diana told three other people, because Diana's gift for keeping secrets was not quite equal to her other gifts.

And then the haunting of the wood became a neighborhood legend, and Avonlea children started daring each other to walk through it at night, which they did, and reported various atmospheric experiences, none of which were supernatural.

Marilla found out, as Marilla found out everything.

"You've been telling ghost stories," she said.

"It started as imagination," Anne said. "It got out of hand."

"You terrified half the children in the neighborhood."

"I may have also terrified myself," Anne said, with honesty.

Marilla told her to stop going around with her head in the clouds and to remember that imaginary things had real effects on real people.

This stayed with Anne. She had always thought of her imagination as purely a gift — something that made dull things interesting and difficult things bearable. She had not entirely considered that it could mislead her, or affect others in ways she didn't intend.

She walked through the Haunted Wood in the daytime and looked at it carefully. It was birch and maple, the ordinary lovely woods of Prince Edward Island in autumn, the light coming through the leaves in pale gold columns. There was nothing there. There had never been anything there.

"I did that myself," she told Diana, on the walk home. "I made myself afraid of trees."

"We all did," Diana said fairly. "We all wanted there to be something there."

"Why?"

Diana thought about it. "I suppose," she said, "because something being there would have been more interesting than just trees."

Anne thought about this for the rest of the walk home. It was a truth about herself she had not quite faced: that she sometimes preferred the more dramatic version of things, even when the dramatic version caused trouble. She liked the story better than the facts, and this was not always a virtue.

She told Marilla she had sorted out the Haunted Wood.

"Good," Marilla said. "Try not to invent any new ones."

Anne thought she probably would, eventually. But she would try to be more careful about who she told.





Chapter 8

The Puffed Sleeves



Anne wanted, above all other things, a dress with puffed sleeves.

Puffed sleeves were the fashion. Every girl of any standing in Avonlea had puffed sleeves. Diana had puffed sleeves. Even the minister's daughter, who was not generally stylish, had puffed sleeves. And Anne had the two dresses Marilla had made for her — practical, sensible, made of serviceable fabric in useful colors, with sleeves as flat and plain as could be arranged.

She did not say anything about the puffed sleeves. She was trying very hard to be grateful for what she had, because she knew very well what it was to have nothing. But she could not stop noticing them, everywhere, on everyone who was not her.

Matthew noticed. Matthew, who noticed nothing about domestic matters as a rule, and who would no more have gone to the dressmakers on his own than he would have flown to the moon, noticed because he watched Anne.

He went to town on an errand and came back having purchased the most alarming amount of fabric in a color that was clearly chosen by a man who did not shop for fabric — a brownish something that was not quite any color at all. He gave it to Mrs. Lynde, who had by now become Anne's most ardent defender, and asked her, in a voice so low he could barely be heard, to make a dress with the sleeves that all the girls were wearing.

The dress appeared on Christmas morning. It was brown — the fabric was still unfortunate — but the sleeves were magnificent. The most extravagant, beautiful, impractical puffed sleeves that had ever appeared in Avonlea.

Anne put it on and looked in the mirror for a long time.

"Matthew," she said, when she came downstairs.

He looked at her.

"You knew," she said. "You noticed."

He went red. He said it was nothing.

"Nobody has ever noticed that kind of thing about me before," she said. "Nobody has ever paid that kind of attention."

Matthew was extremely uncomfortable and said he supposed the sleeves were fine.

Anne understood that she was embarrassing him. She stopped talking about it. But she thought about it all day — this small, careful act of seeing. She had been seen. Not for what she should be or what they'd wanted, but for what she actually was: a girl who wanted, among the things she wanted, puffed sleeves.

She wore the dress to church and felt, for the first time in her life, entirely right in her own clothes.

On the way home, Diana said it was the nicest dress she'd seen all season.

"Matthew chose it," Anne said.

Diana looked surprised. "Mr. Cuthbert? But men never —"

"I know," Anne said. "That's why."

She looked back at Matthew walking behind them — large, quiet, entirely unconscious of having done anything remarkable — and felt something she had never felt for anyone in her eleven years: the feeling of being someone's priority.

She carried it home carefully, like something she didn't want to spill.





Chapter 9

The Rivalry



Anne refused to speak to Gilbert Blythe for the rest of the school year, all of the summer, and well into the following autumn. She was consistent about this in a way she was not always consistent about other things.

Gilbert, for his part, tried several more times to apologize. He came to it from different angles — direct, joking, indirect, through intermediaries. Anne acknowledged none of these attempts. She walked past him in the school yard as if he were a fence post. She answered his comments in class as if he were a general member of the air.

What she could not do — what annoyed her considerably — was stop competing with him academically.

Gilbert Blythe was clever. He was the kind of clever that had always meant something in Anne's world: quick, disciplined, confident in his intelligence. He was used to being the best in the class. Anne was also used to being the best in the class — or would have been, if there had been a class to be best in before Avonlea. Their teacher, Mr. Phillips, had never encountered two students who cared this much about being better than each other at spelling.

Anne won the spelling prize. Gilbert won the arithmetic medal. Anne won the essay competition. Gilbert won the geography award. They circled each other at the top of the class list like two stars caught in each other's orbit, unable to move apart.

Anne told herself she didn't care about Gilbert specifically — she just cared about being excellent. This was mostly true. But she did notice when he did well, and she did feel something particular when she edged him out by a single mark. She was not sure what to call that feeling. It was nothing like friendship.

Diana watched all of this with patient amusement.

"He likes you," she said.

"He called me carrots," Anne said.

"He teases the people he likes. All boys do."

"That," Anne said, "is an excuse. If he likes me he should not tease me. He should say so directly and accept the consequences."

"That's not how boys work."

"Then boys should work better."

Diana had no answer for this.

The competition continued. Anne pushed herself harder than she would have without Gilbert there. She wrote better essays, studied more carefully, read more widely. She would not have said he made her better. But she was better that year than she'd been the year before, and he was one of the reasons, and somewhere under the stubborn grudge she almost knew it.

She won the final class award at the end of the term — the one that went to the overall best student, by points across all subjects. She stood to receive it and, without quite meaning to, looked across the room at Gilbert.

He was already looking at her. He smiled — not mockingly, but the real smile, the one she'd seen him give his friends.

She looked away. She did not smile back.

But the look had happened.





Chapter 10

The Unfortunate Affair of the Hair Dye



Anne's red hair remained the great trial of her life.

She had learned to accept it, in the way you accept a chronic inconvenience — not with peace exactly, but with a certain pragmatic resignation. She would never have brown hair. She would never have black hair. She had red hair, and it was very red, and that was the fact of the matter.

Then a peddler came to Avonlea.

He had, among his other goods, a bottle of hair dye that he guaranteed would turn red hair black. He showed Anne the label, which was convincing, and named a price, which was everything she had saved from the previous three months of small errands. Anne looked at the bottle for a long moment.

She bought it.

She applied it at home, alone, on a Saturday morning when Marilla was out and Matthew was in the fields. She followed the instructions carefully. She waited the prescribed time. She washed it out.

Her hair was green.

Not a subtle green. A vivid, unmistakable, arresting shade of green that had no business being on a human head.

She sat very still in front of the mirror for a long time.

She considered her options. She could say it had happened accidentally. She could say she had found something in her hair and tried to treat it. She could claim the water had done something unusual.

She could tell the truth.

She told Marilla the truth. She told it all — the peddler, the bottle, the money, the applying, the waiting, the washing, the green. She left nothing out.

Marilla sat down.

"Well," she said, after a considerable pause. "That's — there's nothing for it. It'll have to be cut."

Anne looked at her long braids. They were the thing she liked most about her appearance. They were the thing she'd spent the most time wishing were a different color.

"All of it?" she said.

"All of it. There's no fixing this otherwise."

They cut it in the kitchen. Marilla worked quickly. The braids came off and were set aside, and Anne sat in the chair with short, green, ear-length hair and felt what could only be described as complete humiliation.

"You should have known better," Marilla said.

"I know," Anne said.

"What possessed you?"

Anne was quiet for a moment. "I wanted to be different than I am," she said simply.

Marilla looked at her. There was something in Anne's voice — a plainness, underneath all the usual expressiveness — that Marilla recognized. The want to be other than you are. The embarrassment of the want, as well as the failure.

"Your hair," she said carefully, "was always perfectly fine."

She said it very quickly, and went back to the kitchen without looking at Anne.

Anne sat with that for a long while.

It was the first time Marilla had said anything like it.





Chapter 11

Diana's Disgrace



Anne's friendship with Diana Barry suffered a severe interruption in the winter, and it was entirely Anne's fault, though she had not intended any of it.

Diana's mother, Mrs. Barry, was a careful and somewhat rigid woman who held strong opinions on the raising of children. She had allowed the friendship between Diana and Anne because Diana was happy, and Anne, whatever her faults, was clearly a good-natured and imaginative companion.

Then Anne, minding Diana while Mrs. Barry was out, gave Diana three full glasses of what she believed was raspberry cordial.

It was not raspberry cordial. It was currant wine, which Marilla had made and placed in exactly the same type of bottle and left on exactly the same shelf. The bottles were indistinguishable. Anne had served the wine in good faith.

Diana went home flushed, laughing in an unusual way, and fell asleep immediately after dinner. Mrs. Barry sent a very cold note to Green Gables the next morning saying that Diana was not to associate with Anne any further.

Anne was devastated.

She had not — the injustice of it was almost unbearable — done anything intentionally wrong. She had served what she thought was cordial. She was eleven years old and had not previously encountered wine in any context whatsoever and could not have been expected to know the difference. All of this was true. None of it seemed to matter to Mrs. Barry.

She went to the Barrys and tried to explain. Mrs. Barry closed the door.

She wrote Diana a letter. Diana sent back a note that was clearly dictated, saying she was not permitted to correspond.

Anne went home and sat in her room and was quieter than she had ever been. Marilla, who had never seen Anne truly quiet, came to check on her.

"I've lost my only friend," Anne said.

Marilla said there would be other friends.

"There will not be another Diana," Anne said. "Diana knows me. She knows what I imagine and she doesn't think it's foolish and she calls me Anne with an e without being reminded. You don't replace that."

Marilla could not argue with this. She said, gently, that she was sorry it had happened.

Anne looked at her. "I couldn't have done anything differently," she said. "I mean — I couldn't. I didn't know it was wine. I just didn't know."

"I know," Marilla said. "It wasn't your fault."

This was not something Marilla said easily — assigning fault or its absence was a serious matter, and she didn't do it carelessly. Anne understood this.

"Thank you," she said, in a small voice.

They sat together in the room, not saying much. Outside the window the birch trees were bare against the winter sky, and the field beyond them was white with snow.

It was the closest thing to comfort Marilla had ever offered, and Anne received it as what it was.





Chapter 12

The Queen's Class



Anne was thirteen when she decided she wanted to go to Queen's Academy in Charlottetown to get her teacher's license.

It was a significant decision. Queen's was the path to becoming a teacher, which was the path to independence, which was the thing Anne wanted most underneath all the other things she wanted. She wanted not to depend on the goodwill of others for her existence. She had depended on it all her life and it had not always been there, and she intended to be able to take care of herself.

Marilla agreed to the plan. Matthew was delighted, because Matthew was always delighted by whatever Anne decided to do. But the preparation for the entrance examination meant entering the Queen's class at school, which meant working alongside Gilbert Blythe in the direct and organized competition for top marks.

Anne put aside, for the purpose of this task, the question of whether she was speaking to Gilbert. She was not. But she was also preparing for the Queen's examination, and she could not do both of those things without noticing him, because he was the person she would have to beat.

The class was demanding. Mr. Philips was replaced by a new teacher, Mr. Amos Barr, who was rigorous and fair and had high expectations for everyone in the class. He made them work harder than they had worked before.

Anne found something unexpected happening: she didn't just want to beat Gilbert. She wanted to understand the material. She wanted to actually know things — not just win the prize but have the thing the prize was meant to represent. For the first time, the competition felt less like the point and more like the byproduct of something more important.

She began to tutor some of the younger students on her own time, because she'd discovered that explaining a thing was the best way to understand it. She found she could hold mathematics in her mind when she had to show someone else how to use it.

Gilbert, she noticed, was doing much the same thing — staying after to help younger students, studying with the same focused intensity she brought to the material.

She did not comment on this. She did not acknowledge it. But she noticed.

"Do you think," she asked Marilla one evening, "that a person can respect someone and still not like them?"

"Yes," Marilla said. "Happens fairly regularly."

"How do you tell the difference between not liking someone and just being too stubborn to give up an old feeling?"

Marilla looked at her. "That's a question only you can answer."

Anne went back to her studying.

She did not have an answer yet. But asking the question was something.





Chapter 13

The Scholarship



The results of the Queen's entrance examination came back in July, and Anne stood first in the whole of Prince Edward Island.

She read the notice three times, because she couldn't entirely believe it. She had worked for it — she had known she'd worked for it — but the whole island. She had beaten every student in every school in every county, and her name was at the top of the list, and Gilbert Blythe's name was directly below hers, tied with another student for second.

She ran to tell Matthew. Matthew beamed at her with such uncomplicated delight that she had to look away. Then she ran to the fence between their property and the Barrys' to call for Diana, who was allowed to speak with her again — had been allowed since the winter, when Diana's little sister had croup and the doctor was hours away and Anne had administered the right medicine in the right amount and saved the girl's life, and Mrs. Barry had admitted that she had perhaps been mistaken about Anne's character.

Diana shrieked. She told her mother. Her mother sent over a cake.

Marilla read the notice when Anne brought it home and set it on the table.

"Well," she said. "You've done it."

"We've done it," Anne said. "You let me stay. You made me work. You didn't let me give up when the Latin was terrible."

Marilla said she'd only done what was sensible.

But she kept the notice. Anne didn't know this for a long time — didn't find it until much later, when she was older and sorting through Marilla's things. She found the clipping carefully folded in the back of Marilla's Bible, alongside a lock of red hair that had been kept since the day they'd had to cut it off.

For now, though, there was just this summer afternoon and the notice on the table and the cake from the Barrys and Matthew's beam and Marilla's careful practicality, and Anne felt something she had no adequate word for — the sensation of a thing you hoped for so hard it frightened you coming completely, absolutely true.

She went to her room and stood at the east window and looked at the apple orchards for a long time.

Then she went back downstairs and helped make dinner and was, for once, almost completely in the present moment, not imagining anything at all.

The real thing, she was discovering, was sometimes better than what you imagined.





Chapter 14

Queen's



Charlottetown was nothing like Avonlea, and everything like what Anne had always imagined a city should be.

It had streets full of people and shops she could look at for hours and a harbor she could see from the dormitory window of Queen's Academy if she leaned at a certain angle. It had professors who treated the students as capable of serious thought, which was new and exhilarating. It had a library larger than anything Anne had seen in her life.

It also had a great deal of loneliness, which she had not anticipated.

She had Diana's letters, which arrived with faithful regularity and were long and detailed and warm. She had Matthew's short notes that always included some piece of news from the farm — the cow had calved, the apple trees were looking good this year, the weather had been unusually fine — and underneath the news, something else, the way Matthew communicated: I am thinking of you. She had Marilla's letters, which were practical and contained instructions she hadn't asked for and ended, every single time, with "we hope you are eating properly."

She was not particularly eating properly. She was busy. She was studying in a way she had never had to study before — not the effortless absorption she'd managed at Avonlea school, but the grinding, organized, daily work of really difficult material. Mathematics especially was a struggle. Latin she loved but found the grammar tortuous.

Gilbert Blythe was also at Queen's.

He had a group of friends and he was easy in it, in the way some people are naturally easy in company. He said good morning to her once, in the corridor outside the lecture hall. She said good morning back before she'd thought about it, and then felt odd about it for the rest of the day.

She made a friend at Queen's — a girl named Priscilla Grant, who was from the country like Anne and equally overwhelmed by the city and equally determined not to show it. They studied together and walked along the harbor on Sunday afternoons and were, in the particular way of people who are thrown together and discover a natural understanding, immediately close.

"You're always looking at that boy," Priscilla said one afternoon.

"I'm not," Anne said.

"You are. The tall one."

"I'm watching the competition," Anne said. "He's very good. He's my main rival."

"That's not what it looks like," Priscilla said.

Anne said it absolutely was what it looked like, and changed the subject.

But she thought about it later. What did it look like?

She had been watching him for two years. She told herself it was competitive vigilance. She had not, she realized, entirely convinced herself.





Chapter 15

The Avery Scholarship



There were prizes at Queen's, and the greatest of them was the Avery Scholarship — a full year's study in English at Redmond College in Nova Scotia, awarded to the top student in English and composition.

Anne had not known about the Avery when she came to Queen's. She learned about it in the second month, from a notice board, and stood reading the description of it for much longer than it required.

Redmond College. A full year. English and composition, which were the things she was best at, the things she actually loved rather than just worked at. Nova Scotia — farther from home than she'd ever been, farther than she'd imagined going.

She wanted it with a fierceness that surprised her.

She began writing with more intention than she had before. She had always written easily — too easily, she realized, looking back. She had relied on the natural outpouring of a mind that ran to language, and this had carried her through so far, but it was not the same as really working at a piece. Really working meant revising, which she had always resisted, because revisions meant admitting that the first version wasn't right.

Her teacher, a professor named Dorinda Mitchell who was severe and encouraging in almost equal measure, read her essays and said: "You have a genuine talent. You are also lazy with it. Fix that."

Anne fixed it. She revised. She wrote drafts and threw them away and wrote better ones. She began to hear the difference between the version she wanted to write and the version she needed to write to actually achieve what she was after.

She wrote the competition piece in early spring. It was about Green Gables — not by its name, but by its quality: the particular light of an east-facing window, the smell of apple orchards, the feeling of arriving at a place and knowing, without being told, that it was home.

She handed it in and tried not to think about it.

The results were announced in May. Professor Mitchell read the list of awards beginning with the smaller prizes and working upward. Anne's name appeared twice in the lower prizes. When she got to the Avery, she paused — she was that sort of professor, she liked a moment — and then said: "Miss Anne Shirley."

Anne heard it. She did not immediately react, because she was not sure she had heard correctly. Then the girl beside her touched her arm.

She had heard correctly.

She sat very still for a moment. Then she thought: Matthew. She had to tell Matthew.





Chapter 16

Matthew



She came home for the summer with the Avery Scholarship and both teaching certificates and a heart full of plans for Redmond College in the autumn, and the first thing she did was look for Matthew.

He was in the field. He looked older than when she'd left — she noticed this and dismissed it, because she did not want to think about what it meant. He was tired in a way that went past ordinary tiredness. But he smiled when he saw her, the full uncomplicated smile, and she put her arm through his and walked with him back to the house telling him everything.

He listened to all of it with his whole attention. He asked questions — careful, good questions — about the scholarship and the college and what she would study. He said he was proud of her, which he almost never said directly, and when he said it she felt it land in her chest and stay there.

That night she woke to the sound of something in the kitchen.

She found Marilla sitting at the table with a telegram in her hand.

The bank had failed. The Cuthbert savings — everything they had — were gone.

She sat down across from Marilla.

"The farm," Marilla said. Her voice was very steady, which meant it was costing her something. "We'll manage the farm. Matthew can — "

In the morning they found Matthew in the yard.

He had died in the night. His heart, which had never been particularly strong, had given out. He was found by the fence, peaceful, as if he had simply stopped.

Anne sat with the fact of it and could not make it real.

She had come home with her whole future planned and now she stood in the kitchen of Green Gables while Matthew was still and the farm was still and Marilla was trying very hard not to break apart, and the world had reorganized itself entirely overnight.

She did not cry immediately. She went through the motions of the day — the neighbors coming, the arrangements, the food that appeared on the table that she could not eat. She sat with Marilla in the evenings and they didn't talk very much.

One afternoon she went out to the field where he had been.

She stood there for a long time.

Matthew had never once looked at her and seen something lacking. He had never wished she were different, or a boy, or less strange, or less talkative, or less red-haired. He had just — seen her. The way a person sometimes, rarely, sees you completely.

She had been seen, fully, exactly once in her life before Green Gables, and that had been by Matthew.

She went back inside and told Marilla she was not going to Redmond.

"I'm staying," she said. "I'm not leaving you with the farm and your eyes the way they are. I'm going to teach at the Avonlea school. It's arranged. I spoke to the trustees this morning."

Marilla looked at her.

"Anne —"

"It's decided," Anne said. "Don't argue."

She had Matthew's voice when she said it. They both heard it.





Chapter 17

What Marilla Said



The weeks after Matthew died were the hardest of Anne's life, and she had had some hard weeks.

It was not simply grief, though there was grief — a clean, uncomplicated grief for the man who had driven to Bright River station to collect a boy and come home with her instead, and had never seemed to mind. It was also the whole shape of the future rearranging itself. Redmond was gone. The scholarship would be held, she'd been told — she could apply again — but the timing was off now and she could not leave Marilla.

She taught herself not to think about Redmond. She was not always successful.

Marilla's eyesight was failing. The doctor had told her so in the spring, and the crisis at the bank and Matthew's death had accelerated something that was already happening. She needed rest from close work, from reading, from anything requiring focus. She needed, the doctor said, to not spend the next year alone.

So Anne stayed.

She set up her classroom at Avonlea school and learned to be a teacher in the way she learned most things — by doing it badly at first and then better, by paying attention to what worked and discarding what didn't. She discovered she loved it with a love that was different from anything she'd expected. Children were strange and various and endlessly interesting, and teaching required her to understand not just what she knew but how to make someone else understand it, which was harder and more rewarding.

One evening in September Marilla called her into the sitting room.

"Sit down," she said.

Anne sat. Marilla stood at the window with her back to the room. This was unusual. Marilla generally faced the things she had to say.

"When you came," Marilla said, "I didn't want you. I don't think I told you that plainly. I wanted to send you back."

"I know," Anne said.

"I was wrong to. Though I couldn't have known that then." She paused. "I have not always been — I am not an easy person, Anne. I know that. I have not always said the things I should have said."

Anne waited.

"I want you to know," Marilla said, "that whatever I have not said — I have felt. All of it." A pause. "You have been the best thing that happened to this house."

She still didn't turn around.

Anne looked at her back — the straight, slightly rigid back of a woman who said hard things looking the other way — and felt something give way in her chest, quietly.

"I know," she said. "I've always known."

"I wasn't sure."

"I know you," Anne said. "I've learned to know you. The way you say things without saying them."

Marilla turned around then. Her face was composed, but her eyes were the eyes of someone who has just handed something over.

"Good," she said. "Then I don't need to say more."

"No," Anne said. "You don't."

They sat together as the evening came in through the windows, and Anne thought: this is home. Not because it's perfect — because I know the people in it. Because they know me.





Chapter 18

Gilbert Again



Gilbert Blythe came to see her in October.

She knew he was coming — he'd sent a note asking if she'd receive him, which was formal enough that she'd read it twice to make sure it meant what she thought it meant. She'd said yes, because the grudge was six years old and she was tired of carrying it, and because Matthew was gone and she had been thinking lately about the uses of stubbornness versus the uses of letting things go.

He came on a Saturday afternoon. He sat in the parlor and they drank tea and looked at each other across the considerable history of their acquaintance.

"I wanted to tell you," he said, "that I relinquished my claim on the Avonlea school so you could have it."

She'd known this. She'd been told by the school trustees. She hadn't known how to feel about it.

"You gave up the school," she said.

"You needed it more. I've arranged something at White Sands." A pause. "Also I've been trying to apologize for six years. I thought it might be a way to start."

Anne looked at him. He was the same — quick and dark and clever — and also different in the way people are after years of difficulty. There was something steadier in him than there had been at twelve.

"It was carrots," she said.

"I know. It was unkind. I was twelve and I was trying to make you look at me and I chose the wrong way."

"Why did you want me to look at you?"

He raised his eyebrows slightly. "You were the most interesting person in the room from the moment you walked in. I'd never met anyone who actually cared that much about everything. I wanted you to notice me."

Anne sat with this for a moment.

"You could have just said hello," she said.

"Yes," he agreed. "I could have done that."

They were quiet for a moment. Outside the window the October leaves were going, the oaks burning red against the pale sky.

"I forgive you," Anne said. "I think I forgave you some time ago, really. I was just too committed to the grudge to admit it."

"I know," he said. "I could tell." A pause. "Can we — would you like to try being friends? We've been rivals for six years. I think we'd be better as something else."

Anne thought about the long years of watching him across classrooms. The way she'd pushed herself harder because he was there. The note he'd sent, formal and careful, because he didn't want to take her by surprise.

"I think we might," she said. "Though you should know I'm still planning to be better than you at most things."

Gilbert laughed — the real laugh, not the school laugh. "I know," he said. "I'm counting on it."

She walked him to the gate when he left. The evening was coming in cool and clear, the first real chill of autumn.

"Friends, then," he said.

"Friends," she agreed.

She watched him go down the lane. She felt, for the first time in a long time, that the future — the actual future, not the imagined one — might be interesting.





Chapter 19

Teaching



Anne taught at Avonlea school for two years, and was good at it.

She was not immediately good at it. She was, at first, inconsistent — too lenient with students whose stories interested her, too demanding of students who seemed like they should be doing better. She didn't know how to manage a classroom that didn't want to be managed, which was most classrooms most of the time. She made mistakes and learned from them slowly and made different mistakes.

But she understood something about children that not every teacher understood: that they were small people with full inner lives, and that what happened in those lives mattered more to them than anything happening at the front of the classroom. She learned to read a room — to see who was unhappy today and make allowance for it without excusing everything, to see who was bored and needed a different kind of challenge.

She was the teacher who let children read ahead if they finished early, and the teacher who made them go back and do it again if they finished by not doing it properly. She was the teacher who noticed which students were struggling and found a different way to explain things, and which were struggling because something was wrong at home and needed a different kind of attention.

She also kept writing. She had put it aside for the months immediately after Matthew died — there was too much else, and the words felt thin against the weight of things. But they came back, eventually. She wrote in the mornings before school, in a notebook she kept in her desk drawer. Essays, mostly. Observations. Stories she started and didn't finish. Descriptions of things: the way light hit the apple trees in November, the particular quality of silence in a schoolroom before the students arrived.

She sent two pieces to a newspaper in Charlottetown. Both were accepted.

She showed Marilla the payment she received — a small sum, but real money.

Marilla looked at the amount. "It's a start," she said.

"It is," Anne agreed.

"Your father would have been —" Marilla stopped. She had a policy about using that language, because it was the kind of thing that got said carelessly and meant nothing. She corrected herself. "Matthew would have been very proud."

"I know," Anne said. "I could tell."

She put the money in the farm account and went back to her desk.

The words that came now were different from the words that had come at Queen's — less eager, less trying. She had suffered some things she would not have chosen to suffer, and the suffering was in the words now, grounding them. They had weight.

She was twenty years old and she was beginning, slowly, to understand what she was actually good at.





Chapter 20

Redmond After All



In the spring of her second year of teaching, Marilla told Anne she was going to Redmond.

Anne looked up from her marking.

"Your eyes —" she said.

"Are managing," Marilla said. "Better than expected. The doctor says I've been careful enough." She sat down across from Anne with the air of someone who has prepared a case. "Also, Miss Barry has offered to stay with me when needed — she likes the countryside and she likes me well enough. Also, there's a girl in the village who comes to clean and who could help in the mornings. Also, I've arranged it."

"You've —"

"I wrote to Redmond. You still have your scholarship. I wrote to ask if it could be deferred a second year. They said yes. I wrote to accept." She folded her hands. "You are going in September."

Anne stared at her.

"Marilla, you can't —"

"I have," Marilla said. "It's done. You're not going to argue me out of something I've already done."

Anne opened her mouth. She closed it. She thought about arguing. She thought about all the reasons she'd given herself for staying — Marilla's eyes, the farm, the practicalities. She looked at Marilla, who was sitting very straight and looking very firm and had clearly thought about all of those reasons too and had an answer for each of them.

"You planned this," Anne said.

"For several months. I wanted to be sure it would work before I told you."

"You went to all that trouble —"

"You gave up Redmond for me. The least I could do was find a way to give it back." Marilla picked up her knitting, which was what she did when a conversation was over. "You'll write every week."

"Of course."

"And come home for holidays."

"Obviously."

"And you'll eat properly."

"Marilla —"

"You'll eat properly," Marilla repeated, with the tone of someone who considers this non-negotiable.

"Yes," Anne said. "I'll eat properly."

She sat there for a moment, the marking forgotten, looking at the woman who had said she'd wanted to send her back and had kept a newspaper notice in the back of her Bible and had spent several months quietly arranging for Anne to have the thing Anne had given up.

She could not find words for it. The feeling was too large.

"Thank you," she said.

Marilla said it was only sensible. She kept knitting.

Anne went upstairs and stood at the east window for a long time, looking at the apple trees. The world had changed on her several times, in directions she hadn't chosen. But she was going to Redmond. She was going to write. She was going to teach and learn and become the person she was building herself into, one difficult year at a time.

She thought of Matthew. She thought of the puffed sleeves.

She went back downstairs to finish her marking.





Chapter 21

Redmond



Redmond College was everything Queen's had been and more — larger, more rigorous, more alive with the particular energy of people who have chosen a serious thing and are doing it together.

Anne arrived in September with her scholarship and her two teaching years and the writing she'd done in the mornings and the things the teaching had taught her about how people worked. She was different from the girl who had left for Queen's four years ago. She knew this and was glad of it.

She studied English literature with a passion that had not diminished over the years but had deepened. She had a professor named Dr. Harmon Andrews who had read everything and had opinions about all of it and pushed his students to have opinions of their own — not to parrot back what the critics said but to read and think and feel and know what they actually believed. This was harder than it sounded. It required reading something and asking yourself, honestly, what you thought, and then being able to say why. Anne found it was the most interesting thing she'd ever been asked to do.

She wrote for the Redmond literary magazine. Her pieces were received well — well enough that the editor, a small intense girl named Stella Maynard, told her the magazine was better with her in it. Anne received this with the mixture of pleasure and discomfort that praise always carried for her, as if she hadn't quite earned the right to enjoy it yet.

Gilbert was at Redmond too, studying medicine in the science college on the other side of the common. They were friends now — genuinely friends, the way the best friendships come out of the hardest old enmities. They studied together sometimes, walked together sometimes, argued about books and science and whether feeling or reason should govern a life, which was the argument she would not have been able to have without the six years of competition behind it.

She liked him. She had known this for a while and was done pretending otherwise. She had not said so. She was not sure what it amounted to, or what she would do about it, or whether he felt anything similar. She had enough to think about without adding that.

She wrote to Marilla every Sunday. She wrote to Diana every other Sunday. She got letters back that kept Green Gables real and present even at this distance — the farm, the neighbors, the seasons turning. She carried it with her, Green Gables, the way you carry a home you've chosen. Not as a weight but as an anchor.

She was becoming something. She wasn't sure exactly what. But she could feel it happening, and it did not frighten her anymore.





Chapter 22

The Proposal



Gilbert asked her to marry him in April, in the garden of the house where Anne boarded on the edge of the Redmond campus.

He had not, she would reflect later, done it entirely by surprise. He had been moving toward it for some time — she'd seen it, the careful approach, the conversations that went a bit deeper than they needed to, the way he looked at her when he thought she wasn't looking. She had seen it and not commented, because she wasn't certain of her own feelings and she did not say things she wasn't certain of.

He said it simply and clearly: that he loved her, that he'd loved her since approximately the moment she'd broken a slate over his head in fifth grade, that he was aware this was a somewhat unusual basis for a relationship, that he would like to marry her if she was willing.

Anne looked at him for a long moment.

She was not swept away. She had imagined, at eleven, that love would feel like being swept away — like the romances she'd read, the dramatic sudden certainty. This was not dramatic. This was Gilbert, who she knew as well as she knew anyone outside of Green Gables, who she had competed with and been infuriated by and eventually forgiven and eventually befriended. This was a person she respected and liked and in whose company she thought better than alone.

Was that love?

She thought it might be something better than the swept-away kind.

But she was not ready. She knew this too, and she knew he deserved an honest answer.

"No," she said. "Not yet. I'm not — I need more time."

He looked at her steadily. "More time, or no?"

"More time," she said. "I am — I think — but I'm not sure, and I won't say yes to something I'm not sure about, because that would be unfair to both of us."

He nodded slowly. "All right."

"Are you —"

"I'd rather know the truth," he said. "Whatever it is. We've been honest with each other since we stopped being enemies. I'm not going to stop now."

She looked at him. This was why, she thought. This was the thing underneath everything else — the honesty, the willingness to sit with an uncertain answer and not make it into something it wasn't.

"I'll know when I know," she said. "I promise."

"That's enough," he said.

He walked her back to the house and they talked about other things and it was almost exactly normal, which was strange and also reassuring. They were not going to break apart over this. They were too old and too well-matched for that.

She went to her room and thought about it for a long time.

She was not sure. But she was thinking about it.

That was something.





Chapter 23

Anne's Writing



The story that won the Redmond literary prize was the best thing Anne had written.

She knew it when she finished it — not with pride exactly, but with the particular certainty of a piece that has gone where it was trying to go. She had tried to write it three times before. The first two versions were too clever, too pretty, too aware of themselves. The third version was about a woman going back to a place she had loved as a child and finding it smaller than she remembered, and what it meant that the love hadn't diminished even though the place had.

It was not about Green Gables. It was entirely about Green Gables.

She had learned something in the year at Redmond that she'd suspected before but couldn't have articulated: that the best writing is both specific and universal, that the more precisely you describe your own particular experience, the more other people recognize it as their own. That "Green Gables" meant nothing to a reader who had never been there, but "the place you return to and find is still home" meant something to almost everyone.

She handed it in without telling anyone, including Gilbert. The prize was announced in March at the literary society meeting, and she heard her name and thought, with a feeling she recognized as satisfaction, that Matthew would have been glad.

She had written, over the course of the year, a dozen essays and two short stories and the beginnings of something larger that she wasn't ready to name yet. The professor who ran the literary society told her she had a genuinely distinctive voice, which was the kind of compliment that was both satisfying and a responsibility — because a distinctive voice obliged you to use it, to keep working, to not waste it.

She thought about what she was going to do after Redmond.

She could teach — she'd done it, she was good at it, it provided a stability that mattered when you had grown up without stability. She could write — she was doing it, it was what she loved, it did not yet provide anything like an income. She would probably do both, for a while. Most writers did something alongside the writing.

She thought about Gilbert's question. She thought about it differently now than she had in April — not as an either/or between love and independence, but as a question about what kind of life she wanted and whether the two things could coexist.

She thought they could. She thought she'd been wrong to frame it as a choice.

She didn't tell him this yet. She was still thinking.

But the thinking had changed direction, and she knew what that meant.





Chapter 24

Home Roads



Anne came home to Green Gables in June, two years after she'd left, and stood at the gate and looked at the white farmhouse with the green trim and the apple orchards she had loved since the first morning she'd woken to them.

She was twenty-two years old. She had her degree. She had won two prizes. She had published six pieces in real publications and been paid for five of them. She had been a teacher and a student and a friend and she was about to become something she didn't have a full word for yet, something she was building on her own terms.

Marilla came to the door before she was halfway up the path.

She looked older. Anne noticed this and allowed herself to notice it without flinching away. Marilla was older. Time passed. The people you loved aged and would continue to age, and that was simply true, and you could either spend your life braced against it or you could be present in the time you actually had.

She hugged Marilla, who was not a hugger, who allowed it anyway.

"You look well," Marilla said.

"You look wonderful," Anne said, which was a departure from the usual truth-telling, but meant something different: I see you. You are still here.

Diana came that afternoon with her husband and her new baby, and they sat in the garden in the long June light and talked about everything. The farm was doing better — Marilla had managed, as she always managed. The Avonlea school had a new teacher who was, by local report, quite good. The Barry family had expanded. The Haunted Wood was still just trees.

Gilbert came in the evening.

He came to the gate and she went to meet him, and they stood for a moment in the road with the June evening settling around them, the familiar road, the familiar fields.

"I've been thinking," she said.

He waited.

"When you asked me, in April — I said I needed time because I wasn't sure. I've been thinking since then." She looked at him. "I'm sure now."

He was very still.

"Yes," she said. "My answer is yes."

He looked at her the way Matthew used to look at her — the uncomplicated, full, seeing look — and she thought: here is someone who has seen me clearly for ten years and has not looked away.

"You're certain?" he said.

"I am a person," she said, "who imagines things. Who names places and gets ideas and sees stories in everything. I am also a person who, when I am sure, am completely sure. And I am sure."

He took her hand. The road stretched behind them toward Green Gables and ahead of them toward everywhere else.

She had been a girl nobody wanted, and then a girl on trial, and then a girl who belonged, and then a woman who was building a life she had chosen. She had lost things and gained things and changed in ways she would not always have predicted and would not change back.

She was exactly who she had been becoming all along.

The evening was warm. The apple orchards smelled of summer. The east window of Green Gables caught the last of the light.

Anne Shirley looked at the road ahead and was not afraid of any of it.

She never had been, really. She just hadn't always known it.
