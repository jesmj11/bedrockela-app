The Hunchback of Notre-Dame
A BedrockELA Adaptation
Vocabulary Guide — 2 Words Per Chapter (48 Words Total)

Chapter 1: The Bells of Notre-Dame

cathedral  (noun)
A large, important church, often the main church of a city. Cathedrals are usually grand and ancient buildings.
From the story: Notre-Dame was more than a building — it was the heart of Paris, the place where kings were crowned and the city gathered in times of trouble.
Think about it: Why does the author describe the cathedral as the 'heart' of Paris? Can a building be more than just stone and glass?

gargoyle  (noun)
A carved stone creature, often ugly or monstrous, placed on the outside of a building to drain rainwater. Gargoyles also serve as decoration.
From the story: The gargoyles perched on every ledge and rooftop, staring down at the city with frozen expressions that seemed to be laughing at a joke no one else could hear.
Think about it: Gargoyles are ugly but they protect the building from water damage. How might this connect to a character in the story who is ugly but protective?

Chapter 2: The Foundling

foundling  (noun)
A baby or young child who has been abandoned by their parents and found by someone else.
From the story: The foundling bed was a place where parents who could not care for their babies left them, hoping the church would take them in.
Think about it: Being a foundling means starting life without a family. How does this shape who Quasimodo becomes?

deformity  (noun)
A part of the body that is an unusual shape or does not work normally. A physical difference that others may notice.
From the story: The priests recoiled from the baby's deformity — his hunched back, his enormous head, his twisted limbs.
Think about it: The priests judge the baby by his appearance before knowing anything about him. Why do people do this? Is it fair?

Chapter 3: The Bell Ringer

solitude  (noun)
The state of being completely alone, especially for a long time. Isolation from other people.
From the story: Quasimodo lived in almost total solitude — his only companions were the bells, the gargoyles, and the pigeons.
Think about it: Is solitude always bad? Can someone be alone and content? Or does Quasimodo's solitude feel more like a prison?

agile  (adjective)
Able to move quickly and easily. Nimble and light on your feet despite physical challenges.
From the story: Despite his hunched body, Quasimodo was as agile as a cat — he could climb walls and leap between ledges that would terrify an ordinary person.
Think about it: People see Quasimodo's body and assume he is clumsy and weak. His agility proves them wrong. What other assumptions about him might be wrong?

Chapter 4: The Festival of Fools

spectacle  (noun)
An impressive or dramatic display that people watch. Also: something or someone being stared at, often in a humiliating way.
From the story: Quasimodo went from being celebrated as a spectacle to being mocked as one — the crowd's love turned to cruelty in an instant.
Think about it: The word spectacle has two meanings: an amazing show AND someone being humiliated publicly. How does Quasimodo experience BOTH meanings in this chapter?

jeering  (verb/noun)
Making rude, mocking comments or sounds at someone. Laughing at someone to hurt or embarrass them.
From the story: The cheering became jeering — the crowd threw rotten vegetables and mocked his hunched back and twisted limbs.
Think about it: The crowd cheers and jeers within minutes of each other. What does this tell you about mobs? Can a crowd change from kind to cruel that quickly?

Chapter 5: The Dancer

compassion  (noun)
A feeling of deep sympathy for someone who is suffering, combined with a desire to help them.
From the story: While the crowd mocked Quasimodo, Esmeralda showed compassion — she pushed through the mob, wiped the mud from his face, and treated him like a person.
Think about it: Compassion requires action, not just feeling sorry for someone. How is Esmeralda's compassion different from simply pitying Quasimodo?

dignity  (noun)
A sense of pride and self-respect. The quality of being worthy of honor and fair treatment.
From the story: Esmeralda restored Quasimodo's dignity by treating him as a human being when everyone else treated him as a joke.
Think about it: Can someone give you back your dignity, or is dignity something you always have even when others refuse to see it?

Chapter 6: The Poet

outcast  (noun)
A person who has been rejected by society or a group. Someone who does not belong to the accepted community.
From the story: The Court of Miracles was home to everyone Paris had rejected — the outcasts who had nowhere else to go.
Think about it: Who decides who is an outcast? Is it always fair? Can a group of outcasts form their own community that is better than the one that rejected them?

vouch  (verb)
To say that someone can be trusted. To take responsibility for another person.
From the story: Esmeralda vouched for the poet Gringoire, saving his life by taking responsibility for him even though she did not know him.
Think about it: Vouching for a stranger is risky — if they cause trouble, it falls on you. Why does Esmeralda take this risk?

Chapter 7: The Judge

authority  (noun)
The power to give orders and make decisions. The right to control others.
From the story: Frollo held absolute authority over the cathedral and everyone in it — he decided what was sin and what was virtue.
Think about it: Is authority the same as being right? Can someone have enormous power and still be completely wrong?

manipulation  (noun)
Controlling someone through cleverness and deception rather than force. Making people do what you want without them realizing it.
From the story: Frollo's manipulation of Quasimodo was masterful — not with shouting, but with quiet, relentless control that made Quasimodo believe the world hated him.
Think about it: Why is manipulation sometimes more dangerous than force? How has Frollo manipulated Quasimodo his entire life?

Chapter 8: Frollo's Command

obedience  (noun)
Doing what you are told without questioning. Following orders because you believe the person giving them has the right to command you.
From the story: Quasimodo obeyed Frollo's command to capture Esmeralda, even though something inside him whispered it was wrong.
Think about it: Is obedience always a good thing? When does obedience become harmful? At what point should a person stop obeying and start thinking for themselves?

conscience  (noun)
The inner sense of right and wrong. The small voice inside you that tells you when something is not right.
From the story: Something inside Quasimodo — a small, new feeling like a seed breaking through soil — whispered that capturing Esmeralda was wrong.
Think about it: Quasimodo's conscience is waking up for the first time. What caused it to stir? Why has he not felt it before?

Chapter 9: The Capture

magistrate  (noun)
A judge or official who has the power to make legal decisions and punish people.
From the story: Quasimodo was brought before a magistrate who saw a monster and sentenced him without listening.
Think about it: A magistrate is supposed to deliver justice. Does this magistrate deliver justice or prejudice? What is the difference?

prejudice  (noun)
Judging someone before you know them, usually based on how they look or where they come from.
From the story: The judge's prejudice against Quasimodo's appearance made a fair trial impossible — he had decided guilt before hearing a word.
Think about it: How is prejudice different from simply forming an opinion? What makes prejudice dangerous, especially when the person with prejudice has power?

Chapter 10: The Pillory

humiliation  (noun)
The feeling of being made to look foolish or worthless in front of others. Public shame.
From the story: The pillory was designed to humiliate — the prisoner stood helpless while the crowd did its worst.
Think about it: Why is public humiliation considered a punishment? Why does it sometimes hurt worse than physical pain?

indifferent  (adjective)
Having no interest or concern. Not caring about someone's suffering.
From the story: Frollo stood in the crowd and turned away — indifferent to the suffering of the person he had raised.
Think about it: Is being indifferent to someone's pain as bad as causing it? Frollo did not throw stones, but he turned his back. Which is worse?

Chapter 11: A Cup of Water

mercy  (noun)
Compassion or forgiveness shown to someone you could punish or ignore. Choosing kindness when cruelty would be easier.
From the story: Esmeralda's cup of water was an act of mercy — she helped the person who had hurt her, not because he deserved it, but because he needed it.
Think about it: Is mercy something a person earns, or is it given freely? Does Quasimodo deserve Esmeralda's mercy?

gratitude  (noun)
A deep feeling of thankfulness. Appreciation for kindness received, especially when unexpected.
From the story: The tear that rolled from Quasimodo's eye was not from pain but from gratitude — the overwhelming realization that kindness existed.
Think about it: Why is unexpected kindness more powerful than expected kindness? How does gratitude change a person?

Chapter 12: The World Below

community  (noun)
A group of people who live together, share resources, and take care of each other.
From the story: The outcasts of Paris had built a real community in the Court of Miracles — with shared food, their own laws, and people who cared for each other.
Think about it: What makes a community? Is it a place, or is it the people? Can a group of outcasts have a stronger community than a wealthy city?

naive  (adjective)
Innocent and trusting, sometimes to the point of not seeing danger. Lacking experience with the harsh parts of the world.
From the story: Esmeralda was not naive — she knew her freedom was fragile and that men like Frollo wanted it destroyed.
Think about it: Some people mistake kindness for naivety. Esmeralda is kind but not naive. What is the difference?

Chapter 13: Frollo's Fury

hypocrisy  (noun)
Pretending to have beliefs or standards that you do not actually follow. Saying one thing and doing another.
From the story: Frollo's greatest hypocrisy was claiming to serve justice while building a tower of lies to destroy an innocent girl.
Think about it: What makes hypocrisy especially harmful when the hypocrite has power? How does Frollo use the appearance of justice to commit injustice?

fabricated  (adjective/verb)
Made up. Invented and presented as true even though it is false.
From the story: Frollo fabricated the witchcraft charges against Esmeralda — every piece of evidence was a lie.
Think about it: Fabricated evidence looks real from the outside. How can people tell the difference between real evidence and fabricated evidence? What happens when they can't?

Chapter 14: The Trap

accusation  (noun)
A claim that someone has done something wrong or illegal. A formal charge of wrongdoing.
From the story: The accusation of witchcraft was impossible to disprove — how do you prove you are NOT a witch?
Think about it: Some accusations are designed to be impossible to fight. Why is a false accusation one of the most powerful weapons a person in authority can use?

bystander  (noun)
A person who watches something happen without getting involved. Someone who sees wrongdoing but does not act.
From the story: The crowd was full of bystanders — people who knew Esmeralda was innocent but were too afraid to speak.
Think about it: Is being a bystander the same as being guilty? When you watch an injustice happen and say nothing, whose side are you really on?

Chapter 15: Sanctuary!

sanctuary  (noun)
A safe place where someone is protected from harm. In medieval times, a person who reached a church could claim sanctuary and be legally untouchable.
From the story: Quasimodo carried Esmeralda into the cathedral and shouted the word that would protect her: SANCTUARY!
Think about it: Why is it powerful that the same building that imprisoned Quasimodo becomes the thing that protects Esmeralda? How has the meaning of the cathedral changed?

defied  (verb)
Openly refused to obey someone in authority. Stood against a powerful person or rule.
From the story: The hunchback had defied Frollo — for the first time in his life, he made a choice his master did not make for him.
Think about it: Defying someone you have always obeyed is one of the hardest things a person can do. What gave Quasimodo the courage to defy Frollo?

Chapter 16: The Cathedral

refuge  (noun)
A safe place to go when you are in danger. Shelter from threats.
From the story: The cathedral became a true refuge — not just for Esmeralda's body but for Quasimodo's spirit.
Think about it: A refuge protects you from outside threats. But can a refuge also heal inside wounds? How does sharing the cathedral with Esmeralda change Quasimodo?

marvel  (noun/verb)
Something that fills you with wonder and amazement. To be astonished by something extraordinary.
From the story: Esmeralda marveled at the view from the tower — all of Paris laid out below her like a painting.
Think about it: Quasimodo sees this view every day, but sharing it with someone makes it new. Why does sharing something beautiful make it more beautiful?

Chapter 17: The Monster and the Angel

assumption  (noun)
Something you believe to be true without proof. A judgment you make before having all the facts.
From the story: The crowd assumed Quasimodo was a monster because of his appearance — they never looked deeper.
Think about it: What assumptions do people make about Quasimodo? What assumptions do they make about Esmeralda? Which assumptions are wrong?

extraordinary  (adjective)
Very unusual, remarkable, or exceptional. Far beyond what is ordinary or expected.
From the story: Esmeralda told Quasimodo that the most extraordinary people in the world are often the most ordinary-looking — and the reverse is also true.
Think about it: What makes someone truly extraordinary — their appearance or their character? Can someone who looks ordinary be extraordinary inside?

Chapter 18: Frollo's Visit

ultimatum  (noun)
A final demand or statement of terms — do this, or face consequences. A threat with a deadline.
From the story: Frollo delivered an ultimatum: Esmeralda must leave the cathedral, or he would find a way to take her by force.
Think about it: An ultimatum is a weapon of power. How does Quasimodo respond to Frollo's ultimatum? What makes his 'no' so powerful?

emancipation  (noun)
The process of being set free from control or oppression. Gaining independence.
From the story: Quasimodo's refusal to hand over Esmeralda was his emancipation — the moment he stopped being Frollo's puppet and became his own person.
Think about it: Emancipation can be physical (leaving a prison) or mental (stopping someone from controlling your thoughts). Which kind of emancipation is Quasimodo experiencing?

Chapter 19: The Siege

siege  (noun)
An attack where forces surround a place and try to force the people inside to surrender.
From the story: Frollo sent fifty soldiers to lay siege to the cathedral and take Esmeralda by force.
Think about it: How is a siege different from a direct attack? Why does Frollo choose force after his manipulation and legal tricks have failed?

violation  (noun)
Breaking a rule, law, or sacred right. Acting against something that should be protected.
From the story: Attacking the cathedral was a violation of sanctuary — a right older than any law Frollo could claim.
Think about it: Frollo uses the law when it helps him and breaks it when it doesn't. What does this reveal about his real relationship with justice?

Chapter 20: Quasimodo's Defense

formidable  (adjective)
Inspiring fear or respect through being impressively large, powerful, or capable.
From the story: Quasimodo was formidable in defense — one man held off fifty soldiers for three hours using stones, boiling water, and sheer determination.
Think about it: The same qualities that made people fear Quasimodo — his size, his strength — now make him a hero. How does context change whether a quality is seen as frightening or admirable?

tenacity  (noun)
The quality of not giving up. Stubborn determination to keep going no matter what.
From the story: Quasimodo's tenacity amazed the people of Paris — he was bleeding, exhausted, and outnumbered, but he refused to stop.
Think about it: Where does Quasimodo's tenacity come from? Is it strength, love, or both?

Chapter 21: Betrayal

treachery  (noun)
Betrayal of trust. Acting against someone who trusted you, especially through deception.
From the story: Frollo's treachery was complete — he used Esmeralda's exhaustion against her, pretending to offer help while leading her into a trap.
Think about it: Treachery by someone in authority is especially devastating. Why is betrayal from someone you should be able to trust worse than an attack from a known enemy?

deception  (noun (revisited))
You learned this word in Chapter 13 (Musketeers). Frollo uses deception here — pretending to help Esmeralda while actually leading her into a trap.
From the story: Frollo's deception was chilling — he made his voice almost human, almost kind, as he led Esmeralda to the waiting soldiers.
Think about it: Frollo uses kindness as a disguise. How is this similar to what Milady does in The Three Musketeers? Why is false kindness more dangerous than open cruelty?

Chapter 22: The Rooftop

confrontation  (noun)
A direct, face-to-face conflict between opposing people. The moment when tensions finally come to a head.
From the story: The rooftop confrontation between Quasimodo and Frollo was sixteen years in the making — the foundling finally facing the man who had raised and controlled him.
Think about it: Why does this confrontation HAVE to happen on the rooftop of the cathedral? What does the setting add to the scene?

reckoning  (noun)
A time when someone must face the consequences of their actions. A moment of judgment and truth.
From the story: Frollo's reckoning came on the rooftop — the place where he had kept Quasimodo imprisoned for sixteen years became the place where his power finally ended.
Think about it: The cathedral was Frollo's source of power. It is fitting that his reckoning happens here. How does the setting mirror the story's themes?

Chapter 23: Justice

vindication  (noun)
Being proven right or innocent after being accused or doubted. The clearing of someone's name.
From the story: Esmeralda's vindication came when Frollo's own papers proved her innocence — every charge had been fabricated.
Think about it: Vindication comes late in this story — after imprisonment, false accusations, and a siege. Is late justice still justice? What does the delay cost?

conscience (revisited)  (noun)
You learned this in Chapter 8. Now the city of Paris is experiencing a collective conscience — the uncomfortable realization that they allowed injustice to happen.
From the story: The people were horrified by what Frollo's papers revealed. Their conscience — too quiet when it mattered — now screamed.
Think about it: The people feel guilty AFTER the crisis. Is guilt useful if it only arrives after the harm is done?

Chapter 24: The Bells Ring Again

transformation  (noun)
A complete change in appearance, character, or situation. Becoming something fundamentally different.
From the story: The bells rang with a new sound — not the dutiful ringing of a servant, but the free expression of a heart that had been transformed by friendship.
Think about it: Quasimodo's transformation is not physical — he still looks the same. What has actually changed? Is inner transformation more meaningful than outer change?

belonging  (noun)
The feeling of being accepted and valued as part of a group. Knowing that you have a place in the world.
From the story: For the first time, Quasimodo felt a sense of belonging — not because the world had changed, but because he had found a friend who saw him truly.
Think about it: Does belonging come from the world accepting you, or from finding even one person who sees you for who you really are? Which matters more?
