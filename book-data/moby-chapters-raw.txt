

MOBY DICK

A YA Adaptation

Based on the novel by Herman Melville

7th Grade ELA — BedrockELA

Theme: Who You Want to Be vs. Who You Actually Are





Chapter 1

Call Me Ishmael



Call me Ishmael.

That is not necessarily my name. But it will do. Names are less important than what you do with the time you have, and I have a story to tell that matters more than what to call the person telling it.

I go to sea when the world becomes too much. Other men go to church, or to their mothers, or to the bottom of a whiskey glass. I go to the ocean. There is something about standing at the rail of a ship, watching the land fall away, that organizes the chaos inside a person. The sea does not care about your problems. It is not unkind about this — it is simply vast, and vastness has a way of putting things in proportion.

I had come to New Bedford in the late fall, with very little money and a strong desire to ship out on a whaler. Not a merchant vessel, not a ferry — a whaler. I wanted the long voyage, the far seas, the work that was genuinely hard and genuinely dangerous. I wanted, if I am honest, to feel small. I had been feeling too large for my own life in a way I could not explain to anyone, and I thought the ocean might be a cure.

New Bedford in winter was cold and gray and smelled of rendered oil and old wood. The wharves were busy with the machinery of the whale trade — barrels being rolled, rope being coiled, men in boots arguing about prices. It was not a romantic place. It was a working place, which I found more honest than romance.

I needed somewhere to sleep. The inns were full or expensive, and I was directed to a place called the Spouter Inn, which was owned by a man named Peter Coffin. The name seemed like a poor omen, but I had nowhere else to go.

The Spouter Inn was dim and smelled of the sea and whale oil and old wood and tobacco. The walls were hung with harpoons and a painting that might have been a ship in a storm or a whale breaching or possibly both at once — it was difficult to say. I asked about a room.

There was one room left, Peter Coffin told me, but I would have to share it. With a harpooner. Who was currently out.

I said that would be fine. I had shared rooms before.

I did not know, sitting in the smoky common room eating chowder that night, that the harpooner I would share a room with would become one of the most important people in my life. I did not know that the ship I was about to sign onto would take me to the edge of the world and show me things that would mark me permanently. I did not know, in short, anything at all.

I was twenty-two years old and I thought the ocean would solve my problems. It did not solve my problems. It gave me much larger ones to look at, which had roughly the same effect.

That is, I have come to believe, how most genuine education works.





Chapter 2

Queequeg



The harpooner arrived at midnight.

I was half asleep in the big bed when I heard the door open. I saw, in the candlelight, a figure that was unlike anything I had prepared myself for: a large man, tattooed from crown to ankle in patterns I could not read, with a shaved head and a face that had been marked in ways that made his expressions difficult to interpret at first. He was carrying, of all things, a shrunken human head, and a harpoon that he leaned against the wall with the casual ease of someone setting down an umbrella.

He saw me in the bed. He made a sound I could not interpret.

I made a sound back. I may have been somewhat alarmed.

He lunged. I yelled. Peter Coffin appeared in the doorway and sorted things out, and after some conversation it became clear that we were both reasonable people who had merely startled each other, and that the bed was large enough for two, and that there was no alternative anyway.

His name was Queequeg. He was from an island called Rokovoko in the South Sea — a place so remote it appeared on no map, because the best things never do, he told me later, or at least I chose to believe he was telling me something like that, because my Fijian was poor and his English was limited, but we communicated well enough through tone and gesture and the patient goodwill that develops between people who have decided to trust each other.

By morning I had decided I liked him considerably.

By the end of the second day I had decided he was the finest man I had met in years. This was not despite his being foreign and tattooed and the owner of a shrunken head. It was simply about who he was: calm, competent, unexpectedly gentle, with a quality of attention — he listened to what you actually said rather than what he expected you to say — that was rare in any man of any background.

He smoked his pipe. He carved his idol. He sharpened his harpoon. He offered me half of everything he had without being asked, which was the custom of his people, and I was grateful for it and tried to offer half of everything I had in return.

He agreed to sail with me. He had been looking for a ship himself, and there was an understanding between us already that where one went, the other would go.

On Sunday we went to church together. Father Mapple stood in a pulpit shaped like the bow of a ship and preached on Jonah — the man who ran from what he was meant to do and was swallowed by a whale and spent three days in darkness before being given a second chance. Father Mapple's voice was the voice of a man who had been to sea himself and knew the difference between a sermon about the ocean and the ocean itself.

I thought about Jonah for a long time afterward. The man who tried to escape what he was meant to do. The whale that was not a punishment but a passage.

I signed on to the Pequod the following morning. Queequeg signed on beside me.

We did not know, either of us, what we were sailing into. We were young enough to prefer not knowing.





Chapter 3

The Pequod



The Pequod was old and she looked it.

She had been built for endurance rather than elegance, and she wore her years honestly — her hull dark with tar, her deck worn smooth by uncountable sailors, her masts tall and spare against the winter sky. She was trimmed with whale teeth and bones along her taffrail, trophies of past voyages, which gave her a quality that was part monument and part warning.

Two men owned her. Captain Peleg handled the practical business of the ship — large, blunt-spoken, direct. Captain Bildad was older and quieter and had the particular austerity of a man who believed deeply in frugality as a spiritual practice. Between them they embodied the serious, Quaker-inflected character of Nantucket's whaling trade.

Neither of them captained the Pequod. That was the third man.

I asked about the captain.

Peleg's face changed slightly. He said the captain's name was Ahab — after the biblical king. He said Ahab was a great whaleman, one of the greatest living. He said Ahab was currently ashore, recovering from an injury sustained on the last voyage.

What injury?

His leg, Peleg said. He lost a leg. Taken off at the knee by Moby Dick.

I had heard of Moby Dick. Anyone connected to the sea trade had heard of Moby Dick: the great white sperm whale, enormous and apparently unkillable, that had been encountered by ships all over the Pacific and had accumulated a mythology all its own. Some men said it was an ordinary whale grown exceptionally large. Others said it was something else entirely.

I asked if the captain was recovered.

Peleg said he was recovered in body. He did not say anything else, but the way he did not say it communicated something.

We signed the articles. As we were leaving the dock, an old man appeared from nowhere — ragged and wild-eyed, with the quality of someone who had been outside for a very long time.

He asked us if we had shipped on the Pequod.

We said we had.

He said we should think about what it meant to ship with a man who had a vengeance to prosecute. That a ship sailed for profit, not for revenge, and the two did not mix well.

He was gone before we could ask what he meant.

His name, I learned later, was Elijah.

The Pequod sailed on Christmas Day.





Chapter 4

The Captain



We did not see Ahab for the first several days at sea.

He was in his cabin, they said. Brooding, others suggested, with the careful vagueness of men who had served under him before and did not want to say too much. The Pequod was well-run — the officers competent, the crew experienced, the daily routine established and orderly. But there was a quality in the air, a waiting, that I felt from the first week. As if the voyage had not properly begun.

Then one morning Ahab appeared on the quarterdeck.

He was a tall man, broad through the shoulders, with a face so weathered by sun and salt and years that it looked carved rather than grown. His beard was iron-gray. His eyes were dark and steady and had the particular quality of a man who has looked at something so large that ordinary things have permanently lost their scale.

What I noticed first was his leg.

Where his left leg should have been, he had a prosthetic fashioned from whale ivory — from the jaw of a sperm whale, polished smooth, fitted with the precision of long use. It made a sharp knock with each step on the deck. That sound became, over the weeks that followed, one of the organizing sounds of life on the Pequod — heard before he was seen, tracking his position through the ship the way you track a person through a house by their footsteps.

He stood at the rail and looked at the sea. He did not speak for a long time.

The crew gathered the way people gather when something is happening they feel compelled to watch. Starbuck, the first mate — a lean, composed man from Nantucket who took everything seriously, including himself — stood slightly apart with an expression of careful neutrality.

When Ahab finally spoke, his voice was measured and deep. He asked questions about the weather and the ship's position. He received answers. He nodded.

Then he dismissed everyone except the senior officers.

I went back to my work. But I was thinking about his eyes. The eyes of a man who has decided something — not recently, but long ago, and has been living with the decision so long that it has become simply who he is.

I found, to my surprise, that I was not afraid. I was paying attention in a way I had not paid attention to anything in years.





Chapter 5

The Quarter-Deck



Three weeks out of Nantucket, Ahab called the crew to the quarter-deck.

He came up from below with the ivory leg knocking on the boards, and he stood at the rail and looked at us all — all forty of us, from Starbuck and Stubb and Flask at his right to the harpooners Queequeg and Tashtego and Daggoo at his left, to the ordinary seamen ranked behind. His face was the face of a man arriving at something he has been waiting a long time to arrive at.

He held up a gold coin — a large Spanish doubloon, bright as fire in the winter light.

'What do you see, men?' he said.

'Gold,' someone said.

'Gold,' Ahab agreed. 'And I will nail this coin to the mast, and it belongs to the first man among you who raises Moby Dick.'

He said the name the way you say the name of a country or a mountain — something that existed at a scale beyond argument. And then he described the whale: white, enormous, with a wrinkled brow and a crooked jaw, the one that had destroyed more boats than any other in the Pacific. The one that had taken his leg.

'It is Moby Dick we sail for,' Ahab said. 'God hunt us all if we do not hunt him.'

The crew cheered. It was the cheer of men given a mission they could understand — a clear enemy, a named target. The aimlessness of ordinary whaling was replaced in that moment by something simpler and more powerful: a story, a chase, a named thing.

I cheered too, I am not ashamed to say.

Only Starbuck did not cheer.

He stood slightly apart, and when the noise died down he spoke quietly to the captain. He said he had signed on for a commercial voyage. That his duty was to the owners and the ship. That vengeance against a whale was a kind of madness he was not comfortable with.

Ahab turned to him with those eyes. He said the whale had taken his leg. That the pain of it was not something he would calculate into profit and loss. That there was a justice to this that Starbuck could not understand because Starbuck had never been broken by something he could not fight back against.

Starbuck said no more. But his face said a great deal.

I watched him walk back to his station and thought: here is the most important question on this ship. Not whether we will find the whale. But whether Starbuck can stop what is coming.





Chapter 6

Starbuck



Starbuck was the most decent man on the Pequod, and the most tormented by it.

He was a Nantucket man through and through — Quaker-raised, careful in all things, with the sober integrity of someone who genuinely believed that the right thing and the practical thing were usually the same thing. He was brave in the way that good officers are brave: not recklessly, but with a clear-eyed assessment of what risks were necessary. He had a wife and children in Nantucket. He thought about them every day.

He did not want to chase Moby Dick.

He was not afraid of the whale. He was afraid of what the whale had made Ahab. He had shipped under difficult captains before — the sea produces a type of commanding personality that does not always translate well to ordinary human interaction. But Ahab was something different. The obsession was not a personality trait. It was an organizing principle. Everything on the ship ran through it.

I had several conversations with Starbuck during the early weeks. He was a man precise with his words, who said what he meant and meant what he said, and who listened to you as if you were worth listening to. He was not warm exactly, but he was honest, and honesty done consistently is its own kind of warmth.

He told me about his father. Lost at sea. His brother. Lost at sea. He had grown up understanding the ocean as a place where people you loved did not always come back from. He had gone to sea anyway, because it was the only trade he knew and he was good at it, and he had made peace with the risk in a particular way: by being excellent, by never taking an unnecessary chance, by being the officer who brought men home.

'What about Ahab?' I asked.

Starbuck was quiet for a moment. 'Ahab is looking for extra,' he said. He did not say more, but the weight behind the words told me this was not a new thought. That he had been thinking about what to do with it for a long time and had not yet arrived at an answer.

What Starbuck did not yet know was how far Ahab's obsession would take us before it found its end. He was calculating the risk as a man of his training would: as a variable, something to be weighed and managed. He had not yet understood that the calculus of obsession does not yield to ordinary management.

He stood at his watch that evening, looking at the dark sea, and I watched him and thought: he is the only man on this ship who might stop this. And I do not think he knows how.





Chapter 7

The Whiteness of the Whale



I spent a great deal of time on that voyage thinking about what Moby Dick meant.

This may seem like a strange occupation for a sailor whose job was to help find and kill whales, not to think about them philosophically. But the sea produces a contemplative state in certain minds — the long watches, the unchanging horizon, the rhythm of the work — and I had that kind of mind. Queequeg was patient with it. He would sit beside me while I thought aloud, and he would occasionally say something that reframed everything, and then go back to carving his idol.

What troubled me about Moby Dick was the white.

Whiteness is supposed to be pure. In most traditions it carries the meaning of innocence, of cleanliness, of beginnings. A white flag means surrender and peace. A white dove means hope. We dress brides and holy things in white.

But there is another quality to whiteness that I could not shake. The white of polar wastes. The white of fog that hides everything. The white of blank paper, which contains all possibilities and therefore makes no promises. White is also the absence of color, the erasure of particular things into no-particular-thing. A great white whale moving through the dark water is not a symbol of innocence. It is a symbol of something that gives no information — that reflects back whatever you bring to it.

That was what troubled me about Ahab.

Moby Dick had taken his leg. That was real and it had been terrible. But Moby Dick did not know Ahab existed. The whale did not hate him. The whale did not remember him. The whale was a whale.

What Ahab had done — what the whiteness of the whale had allowed him to do — was pour everything into it. Every frustration, every loss, every wound the universe had dealt him before the whale and since. The whale became the vessel for all of it, a target on which to project every darkness that had no other shape. You cannot fight the randomness of the world. You cannot harpoon fate. But you can follow a white whale.

This is what obsession does. It takes the shapeless and gives it a shape. It takes the uncontrollable and gives it an address.

The relief of that — the relief of having a named enemy, a chase, a mission — is real. I had felt it on the quarter-deck when Ahab held up the gold coin. The whole crew had felt it.

But the relief was a lie. The whale was not the thing that had broken Ahab.

The thing that had broken Ahab was the world's indifference to his suffering.

You cannot harpoon that.





Chapter 8

Life at Sea



Let me tell you how it actually was.

It was cold, and then hot, and then cold again, as we made our way south and east toward the cruising grounds of the Pacific. The work was constant and physical and occasionally dangerous and frequently tedious, in the way that real work involves long stretches of routine interrupted by intense effort. The sea was enormous and most of it looked the same.

We ate salt beef and hardtack and dried fish and, when the officers were generous, something approaching a stew. We slept in hammocks in the forecastle, which smelled of men and sea water and the staleness of a closed space. We stood watch in four-hour shifts, staring at the horizon for the spout of a whale.

The crew of the Pequod was one of the most various groups of human beings I have ever been among. Thirty men from at least a dozen different countries, speaking five or six languages, practicing various religions or none, with histories that would each fill a book.

There was Tashtego, a Wampanoag man from Gay Head on Martha's Vineyard, who was the best spotter on the ship and moved through the rigging with a confidence that made the rest of us look earthbound. There was Daggoo, from Africa — so large that ordinary ship's furniture was too small for him, with a quiet dignity that made racism slide off without purchase. There was the cook, Fleece, who had been sailing forty years and had the comprehensive world-weariness of someone who had stopped being surprised by people's behavior.

And there was Pip.

Pip was the ship's boy — the youngest of the crew, from Alabama, quick and cheerful and musical, who played the tambourine and made people laugh and was, I thought, too gentle for the life he had chosen. He would become important later in ways I did not then anticipate.

Between watches I talked to Queequeg. We had developed by this point the particular communication of two people who have spent enough time together that words become shorthand and silence becomes conversation. He was sewing his own coffin — a Fijian custom, he explained, preparing for death before it comes so that it cannot surprise you. He sewed it carefully, with the attention he brought to everything.

'Are you not afraid?' I asked.

'The whale?' he said.

'Any of it. The hunt. Ahab.'

Queequeg considered this. Then he said something I understood to mean: that a man who chose to go to sea accepted the sea's authority. The danger was the agreement. What mattered was who you were within it.

I thought about this for the rest of the watch.





Chapter 9

The First Lowering



The first time we lowered the boats for a whale, I understood what whaling actually was.

From the deck, it looked manageable. The whale spouted. The captain gave the order. The boats went down — three of them, each crewed by six men including a harpooner at the bow and a mate at the stern. They rowed toward the whale. The whale was visible, large but comprehensible.

Then I was in the water.

Not because we capsized — not yet. Because the whale sounded, dove straight down, and the line connected to Queequeg's harpoon went out with such violence that it smoked against the oarlock and pulled the boat through the water at a speed no rowing could match. This was the Nantucket sleigh ride: being towed by a harpooned whale through the ocean, unable to do anything but hold on and wait for it to end one way or another.

It ended, that first time, without serious injury. The whale tired. The whale was killed and lashed to the ship, and the long, bloody, industrial work of converting a living creature into oil began — which was what we were actually there for.

Starbuck ran his boat with the quiet authority of a man who had done this hundreds of times. He did not shout. He said what needed to be done and his crew did it. Stubb ran his with a breezy confidence that made the work seem almost casual, smoking his pipe in the chase, smiling during things that were not particularly funny. Flask ran his with pure aggression and no apparent concern for personal safety.

I was in Starbuck's boat. Queequeg was our harpooner. He stood in the bow and balanced in the moving boat with the easy confidence of a man standing on solid ground, harpoon ready, watching the water with complete attention.

When he threw, it was fast and certain and had a quality of inevitability, as if the harpoon had always been going to go exactly where it went.

That night, processing the whale on deck, covered in blood and oil in the dark, with the lanterns swinging and the ocean everywhere, I felt something I had not felt in years: entirely, specifically alive.





Chapter 10

What Whaling Is



Here is what I understood about whaling after the first month:

It was the most dangerous legal occupation in the world. The creature being hunted was vastly larger than the boats hunting it, was capable of destroying those boats, and would sometimes do so deliberately when wounded. The men who did this work were not specially heroic — they were working men who had made a particular career choice and were good at their jobs. The heroism was incidental to the employment.

It was also, in its own way, magnificent.

A sperm whale is not simply a large fish. It is an intelligence — not human, but genuine — that navigates the deepest parts of the ocean using sound, that communicates with others of its kind across distances we are only beginning to understand. I did not know all of this then. I felt some version of it, observing them.

There was a contradiction in the work that I never resolved. The whale was extraordinary. The whale was also what we had come to kill, because the oil inside it powered the lamps of cities, because the commerce of the whale trade was woven into everything. We could not do without the oil. The whale could not survive the hunting. These two things were both true and I held them both without finding a way to reconcile them.

Ahab did not think about this. Ahab thought about one whale.

The rest of the crew thought about wages and the voyage's end. They were professionals with families and opinions about tobacco, who happened to work in a field that required them to chase the largest animals on earth. Their relationship with the grandeur of their work was mostly practical.

I was somewhere between these positions — too much of a thinker to be purely practical, not obsessed enough to be Ahab.

What I thought about, increasingly, was Starbuck.

Starbuck was a man of genuine principle in a situation that was testing those principles to their limit. He had signed onto a commercial voyage. He was being used as the officer of a revenge mission. He knew this. He disagreed with it. And yet the ship sailed on and Starbuck held his watch and ran his boat and did his job.

Why?

I asked him, once, directly.

He thought about it for a long time before answering. Then he said: 'Because the moment I refuse, the ship has no check on him at all. A bad officer is better than no officer.'

I said: 'Is that enough?'

He said: 'I do not know yet.'

He walked away. I watched him go and thought about the difference between the decision you make in principle, in the abstract, and the decision you make when you are on the ship and the captain is already at the helm and the coast is three months behind you.





Chapter 11

The Gam



When two whaling ships met at sea, they held what was called a gam — an exchange of visits and news from shore. It was the social life of the deep ocean.

The Pequod gammed with several ships during the voyage. These meetings told a story that Ahab refused to read.

The first was the Albatross. She had been at sea for four years and her crew looked it — gaunt faces, faraway eyes, the weariness of people who had been away from home so long they had stopped expecting to return. Her captain called across the water to Ahab: had they seen the white whale?

Ahab called back: had they?

The Albatross captain said they had not. He began to say something else — something that looked, from the distance, like a warning — but the wind took his voice away.

The Jeroboam was more direct. She carried a man who had installed himself as prophet on her decks — who told Ahab that Moby Dick was a divine messenger and pursuing him meant death. The Jeroboam had lost its first mate to Moby Dick on their last encounter.

Ahab said he did not believe in prophets. He said the white whale was a whale, not an agent of anything. He said this crisply, as a man states a fact.

I was not sure he believed it.

The Samuel Enderby was most significant. Her captain had lost his right arm to Moby Dick — he received Ahab in his cabin with the stump between them like a third guest. The English captain had given up the chase after losing the arm. He was alive. He seemed, if anything, relieved.

He advised Ahab to let it go.

Ahab received this advice with the politeness of a man who has already decided and is merely waiting for the conversation to end. He left the Enderby's boat, climbed back aboard the Pequod, and gave the order to sail on.

Starbuck watched from the deck. His face was a controlled nothing.

I watched Starbuck watching and thought: he is counting. He is noting each warning, each testimony from those who have met what we are sailing toward. He is building a case.

For what purpose I could not yet say.





Chapter 12

Ahab Alone



As the voyage deepened, Ahab ate less with the officers.

He began to spend his meals alone on the quarter-deck, eating without tasting in the way of a man whose mind is elsewhere. He slept less. The ivory leg was heard on the deck in the small hours of the night, knocking its irregular rhythm, and those of us who woke to it lay in our hammocks and listened and felt, though none of us would have said so, something between pity and fear.

He had a compass he kept on him at all times — a physical compass he consulted rather than trusting the ship's. This was partly practicality and partly, I came to think, something else: a need to be the one who knew which direction things were going. To not be dependent on instruments he had not personally verified.

The crew felt the change in him and responded the way crews respond to a captain's mood — by organizing themselves around it, becoming careful in his presence. The atmosphere of the ship changed as we moved further from port.

Queequeg remained himself. This was perhaps the most remarkable thing about him: he was entirely impervious to the psychological weather of the ship. He worked when work was required, rested when rest was possible, and met each day with the calm of someone who has made peace with his mortality and finds the rest of existence relatively manageable in comparison.

I found him one evening mending a sail, the needle moving through the canvas with characteristic precision. I sat beside him.

'Are you afraid of him?' I asked.

Queequeg considered the question with his usual seriousness.

Then he said something I understood to mean: that a captain was like weather. You did not fear the weather as a personal matter. You prepared, you watched, you went where you needed to go and accepted what came.

'And if the weather is a hurricane?' I asked.

He said: you are in the boat either way. You might as well row.

I went back to my watch. I thought about what it would mean to row toward a hurricane with perfect equanimity and no illusions.

I thought Queequeg had worked out something about how to live that most people spend their whole lives trying to find.





Chapter 13

Pip



Pip fell out of a boat.

It was during a chase — one of the ordinary whale chases, not Moby Dick, just a sperm whale that had been spotted and brought the boats down into the water in the usual rush. Pip was in Stubb's boat as the extra hand, and when the whale surged and the boat lurched, Pip went over the side.

This happens sometimes. The protocol is clear: the harpooner cuts the line if a man is overboard, because a man is worth more than a whale. Stubb's harpooner cut the line. The whale was lost. They circled back for Pip.

Stubb was furious. He told Pip that next time he would not cut the line. That whales were worth more per pound than Pip, which was a cruel joke, and Pip should think about that before falling out of the boat again.

Pip fell out again.

This time Stubb did not cut the line immediately. He ran out a hundred yards before turning back. It was a minute, perhaps two. Not long by any ordinary measure.

It was long enough.

When they pulled Pip out of the water he was alive. He was not, quite, the same person who had gone in. He had been alone in the open ocean — vast and moving and nothing but water in every direction. He was very small and the ocean was very large and the time alone in it had done something to his mind that could not be undone.

He was not mad, exactly. He was elsewhere — occasionally present, occasionally in some other place, moving between the two states with a freedom that could look, at a distance, like cheerfulness. He still played his tambourine sometimes. He still laughed. But the laughs were at things no one else could see.

Ahab found him on the deck one evening and sat with him.

This surprised everyone. Ahab had not been soft with anyone since the voyage began. But he sat with Pip and talked to him quietly and held his hand, and his face during this had a quality I had not seen there before: not obsession, not intensity. Something more like grief.

He told Pip to stay close to him. That he would keep him safe.

Pip looked at him with the clear eyes of someone who has no performance left. 'The sea is big,' he said.

'Yes,' Ahab said.

'Bigger than us,' Pip said.

Ahab did not answer. He looked at the ocean.

I think Pip said what Ahab already knew. The difference was that Pip could live with it, and Ahab could not.





Chapter 14

The Carpenter and the Blacksmith



The ship had its craftsmen, and the craftsmen had their work, and the work went on regardless of the captain's state of mind.

The carpenter was a spare, practical man who could make anything from wood and nails and would fix anything that broke with untroubled efficiency. He had made Ahab's ivory leg — had made two of them over the course of their shared voyages — and when Ahab's current leg cracked, he made a third. He asked no questions about where they were going. He did what was in front of him with the tools he had.

The blacksmith was Perth, a large, quiet man with scarred hands. He had come to sea later than most — after losing his family to a series of misfortunes that left him with nothing. He hammered iron. His face had the quality of a man who has burned down to something essential and found, on the other side of the burning, a kind of peace. Or at least an absence of complaint.

Ahab went to Perth one evening with an unusual request.

He had been collecting from the crew the steel nails from their razors — the hardest steel on the ship. He brought them to Perth and asked him to forge a special harpoon. A harpoon for Moby Dick.

Perth made it. You brought him materials and told him what you needed and he made it.

When the harpoon was finished, Ahab needed to temper it. The usual method was water. Ahab refused. He used blood: a drop from each of the three harpooners, drawn from their arms in a ritual that had the quality of a dark ceremony, a binding thing.

I watched this from a distance. Starbuck watched it too. He watched it with the face of a man adding a final calculation.

'Do you not fear God?' he said quietly to Ahab, when the others had dispersed.

Ahab looked at him. 'I am not afraid of God,' he said. 'I am defying Him. There is a difference.'

Starbuck had no answer for this.

I thought about it for a long time. The difference between fear and defiance. What it would take to cross from one to the other.

We were not on a commercial voyage. We were on a crusade.





Chapter 15

The Chart



Ahab had a chart.

He had been working on it since before the voyage began — mapping the known positions of Moby Dick, the dates and locations of every reported sighting, the testimony of every ship that had encountered the whale and survived. It was a document of obsessive precision, covered in lines and notations and calculations.

What Ahab had worked out — through years of study, through the patterns of whale migration and ocean current — was where Moby Dick would be, and when. The whale was not random. No animal is entirely random. Given enough data, a mind like Ahab's could predict where the white whale would be in any given season.

He showed it to no one. But he consulted it every day.

Starbuck found him with it one night, alone in his cabin, the chart spread across the table, the lamp making his face strange in its shadows. Ahab explained it in the flat tone of a professor explaining something that should be obvious to any competent student.

Starbuck asked how long he had been working on it.

Since before the last voyage, Ahab said. That voyage was three years ago.

Starbuck looked at the chart and then at Ahab. He told me later that he had stood at the chart and felt, for the first time, the full weight of what they were part of. This was not a plan developed in anger. This was a plan developed over years, with extraordinary care and intelligence, for the sole purpose of finding and killing one whale.

All that intelligence. All that precision. All that mental energy, which could have been applied to a hundred other things.

He had asked Ahab what he would do after.

After the whale was dead.

Ahab had looked at him as if the question made no sense. As if the territory beyond the whale's death had never been mapped.

That was when Starbuck knew that the real problem was not the whale. The real problem was that Ahab had made the whale the meaning of his life. Kill the whale and there was no map for what came next.

He could not stop a man from doing what the man had decided was the only reason he was still alive.

He went back to his watch.





Chapter 16

What Starbuck Almost Did



There was a night, when we were two months from what we did not yet know would be the end, when Starbuck stood outside Ahab's cabin with a musket in his hands.

He had not planned this. He had come to the cabin for a legitimate purpose — to report a weather change. And he had found Ahab asleep with his face in the chart, the ivory leg hooked around the leg of the chair. And the musket rack was right there, and the thought came to him with terrible clarity.

He could end it. One shot, one decision, and the Pequod would turn for home. The crew would live. He would be the murderer of a captain, with all the legal and moral consequences of that — but the consequences could be managed, argued, perhaps justified. And the crew would live.

He stood there for a long time.

He did not shoot.

He told me later that he had looked at Ahab sleeping and had seen — not the obsessed captain, not the commander of the death voyage — but a man. An old man with a white leg and a ruined face who was asleep with his cheek on a map.

'He looked tired,' Starbuck said. 'Not frightening. Just tired.'

'That changed your mind?'

'It reminded me that I was thinking about killing a person,' he said. 'Not an idea. A person.' He paused. 'I am not capable of killing a person who is asleep.'

He put the musket back. He went to report the weather.

I have thought about this moment many times since. Starbuck had the opportunity, the weapon, the justification, and the will — until he looked directly at the human being in the chair, and the human being was enough to stop him.

Was he right?

If he had acted, a hundred men would have come home.

If he had acted, he would have been a murderer.

I cannot tell you what the right answer is. I was not the one standing there. I was not the one who had to choose between the lives of a hundred men and the life in the chair.

What I know is that Starbuck chose the man. And the choice was completely who he was. And it was also the choice that allowed what happened next to happen.

That is the kind of thing that makes morality complicated.





Chapter 17

The Pacific



We entered the Pacific through the Straits and the ocean opened before us like a held breath releasing.

The Pacific is not like the Atlantic. The Atlantic has weather and history and the presence of continents on either side giving it edges. The Pacific is simply enormous. It covers more of the earth than all the land combined. Standing at the bow and looking out at it, you understood things about the scale of the world that no map can convey.

Ahab became more himself in the Pacific. He was at the rail more hours of the day. He consulted his chart. He spoke to the crew with a directness that had a quality of imminence, as if the thing he had been sailing toward was now close enough to smell.

He climbed the masthead himself some mornings, which captains normally did not do. He stood in the crow's nest with his spyglass and looked at the ocean.

He was looking for white.

The rest of us looked too — not with his intensity, but with the natural vigilance of men at sea who know that spotting a whale means a share of profit. Every splash examined. Every disturbance in the water considered.

I was at the masthead one afternoon when I saw — or thought I saw — something large and pale beneath the surface. I held my breath. I looked again. It was a trick of the light, a shadow, a wave.

But for one moment it had been there: something vast and white in the green water, moving with the purposeful ease of something that owned the ocean.

I said nothing. I had not seen it clearly enough to report.

But I thought about it for the rest of my watch, and I thought: I understand now. Not the obsession — that I still could not justify — but the whale itself. The white shape in the water that was real and enormous and belonged to a world that existed entirely without reference to us.

There was something about that which was both terrible and magnificent. A thing that large, that indifferent, that genuinely other.

I thought: if I were less sane than I am, I might need to pursue that to its end too.

I went down from the masthead and said nothing to anyone.





Chapter 18

The Rachel



The Rachel was a Nantucket ship, and her captain was a Nantucket man, and he came alongside the Pequod with a desperation that was visible from fifty yards.

He had lost a boat. Two days before, in an encounter with Moby Dick, a boat had been cut off and lost, and the captain's youngest son had been in that boat. He was twelve years old. He had shipped for the adventure of it, the way boys do, and he was now somewhere in the Pacific on a boat with five other men and two days' worth of food and water if they were lucky.

He asked Ahab to help search.

The Pacific is very large. Two days in an open boat could mean a fifty-mile radius in any direction. But two ships searching had twice the eyes and covered twice the ocean. And the boy was twelve years old.

Ahab said no.

He said it with his arms crossed and his face set and his eyes already on the horizon. He said they could not delay. He said they were close. He said the course was fixed.

The Rachel's captain did not move. He stood in his boat at the Pequod's side and looked up at Ahab and said, man to man, father to father — Ahab had a boy of his own in Nantucket, had he not?

Ahab's face did something. Something behind the obsession moved, briefly.

Then it closed.

'I will not delay,' he said.

The Rachel fell behind. Her captain stood at the rail watching the Pequod sail away, and even at a distance you could see what was in his face.

Starbuck came to stand beside me at the rail.

He said nothing for a long time. Then, quietly, almost to himself: 'God keep us.'

I had never heard him pray before.

I thought about the boy in the boat. I thought about what kind of man says no to that request. And then I thought — and this is the thought that troubled me most — that I understood how Ahab had gotten to this point. Not agreed. Not excused. But understood.

A man who decides that one thing is the most important thing in the world, who builds his entire existence around it, cannot stop for anything. If he stops once, for any reason, the structure collapses. The whole architecture of the obsession requires every piece to hold.

The boy in the boat was a reason to stop.

The whale was a reason to keep going.

He chose the whale.





Chapter 19

The First Day



On the first day of the chase, the lookout's cry came at dawn.

'There she blows! White water — white whale!'

The cry went through the ship like a current. Men came from below, from their hammocks, from every corner of the Pequod, and within minutes the whole crew was on deck and Ahab was at the rail with his spyglass.

White. There was white in the water. Something large and pale, moving on the surface a mile ahead.

Ahab's face transformed. I had watched it carefully for months — the controlled intensity, the long patience, the suppressed fire. All of that resolved, in the moment of sighting, into something pure. He was, in that moment, exactly where he had been going for three years.

The boats went down. Four of them — Starbuck's, Stubb's, Flask's, and Ahab's own, which he had begun commanding himself in the late weeks of the voyage.

I was in Starbuck's boat.

We rowed toward the white shape on the water. As we drew closer I understood, in my body rather than my mind, what I had only understood abstractly before. The whale was real. Not a metaphor, not a symbol, not the vessel of Ahab's rage. A living creature of extraordinary size, moving through the water with the ease of something entirely at home.

His head broke the surface. The brow was wrinkled, exactly as described. The jaw was slightly crooked. His skin was a mottled gray-white, luminous in a way that was strange and beautiful and alarming.

He seemed to see us.

He turned toward us, and the boats scattered.

He came through Ahab's boat first — not even particularly deliberately, just moving through the space where the boat was. Men in the water. The whale turned again.

Ahab surfaced, swimming. The ivory leg was gone — snapped off in the impact. He swam to Starbuck's boat with strong, furious strokes and was pulled aboard, and he said, immediately: 'The chase. Do not stop.'

His face was extraordinary. Soaked, bleeding, deprived of his leg again, having just watched his boat destroyed.

He was alive.

He was not stopping.





Chapter 20

The Second Day



On the second day the carpenter made Ahab a new leg before dawn.

He worked through the night, and in the gray predawn Ahab stood on the deck with a fresh peg of ivory and looked at the ocean.

'He is still out there,' he said. Not a question.

He was.

The second day was different from the first. The whale had not run. He was still in the vicinity — visible, intermittently, on the surface — and he had become, overnight, more aggressive. Whatever the whale understood about being hunted, it understood that this was different.

The boats went down again.

Moby Dick destroyed all three.

Not by accident — with apparent intention. He turned on each boat in sequence, came to them, struck them. Men in the water. Men on floating wreckage. Lines tangled in the legs of swimming sailors.

The Pequod picked up the survivors. Three men were badly hurt. Flask's boat was completely destroyed.

Starbuck came to Ahab on the deck.

This time he did not speak quietly. He said, in the flat controlled voice of a man who has passed through fear and come out the other side: 'We are destroyed. Not the boat — us. Three boats gone. Men injured. One more day and there is no crew left to sail this ship home. I am asking you. Please.'

Ahab looked at the ocean.

'Tomorrow,' he said. 'One more day.'

Starbuck looked at him for a long moment. He looked at him the way you look at something you are trying to memorize. Then he walked back to his post.

I do not know if Starbuck slept that night. I did not sleep. The ship moved on the dark ocean and the sounds of it were the sounds they had always been — and also different, charged with what tomorrow meant.

I lay in my hammock and thought about the boy in the Rachel's boat. I thought about Starbuck's face. I thought about Pip, who slept nearby and sometimes murmured things in his sleep that sounded like conversations with the ocean.

I thought about Queequeg, who had finished sewing his coffin and slept well.





Chapter 21

The Third Day



On the third day the sky was clear.

The whale was still there — as if he had been waiting, as if he too understood that this was the last day and was prepared to meet it. He was visible from the masthead at first light, white and enormous, moving slowly on the surface a mile to the south.

Ahab climbed the masthead himself. He stood up there for a long time, looking.

When he came down he called Starbuck to him. They stood together at the rail, close enough that I could see their faces but not hear what was said.

Starbuck later told me what Ahab said.

Ahab said he had been thinking, in the night, about his boy in Nantucket. About what it would mean to that boy to grow up without a father. He said he had thought, for a few hours, about turning home.

Starbuck's face, Starbuck told me, had opened in that moment.

And then Ahab said: but he could not. That the white whale was ahead of them and the iron was in his blood and the hunt was not finished and he did not know how to be the man who turned away from it.

He asked Starbuck's forgiveness, for what was coming.

Starbuck said nothing for a while. Then: 'You are a good man, Ahab. That is the tragedy of it.'

Ahab returned to the quarter-deck. The boats went down.

I was in Starbuck's boat. We rowed toward the whale in the morning light and the ocean was flat and the white shape ahead was visible and enormous, and I held my oar and thought of everything and nothing.

The whale turned.

He came at the Pequod herself — not the boats, the ship. He hit the hull with a force that stopped the world for a moment. He came around and hit it again. The Pequod began to fill with water.

Ahab saw it from his boat. He threw his harpoon and the harpoon struck the whale and the line ran out and in the second he did not see coming, it coiled around his neck.

It took him with it.

The sea closed over him.

The Pequod went down. The vortex of its sinking pulled the boats down with it, and the men, and everything on the ship — the charts, the gold doubloon still nailed to the mast, the carpenter's tools, Queequeg's coffin — all of it went down into the Pacific.

The ocean was very large. It was very quiet.





Chapter 22

The Orphan



I am the only one who survived.

The Pequod sank and took everyone with it and I was the last one in the water when the sinking was done.

I survived because of Queequeg.

His coffin had been lashed to the deck. When the Pequod went down, the suction pulled me under, and when I came back up, gasping, the coffin was there. It had come up from the vortex and it floated. I grabbed it. I held on. I floated on the coffin my friend had made while he was alive, which he had made as an act of preparation and peace, and which had become, in the end, the thing that kept me alive.

I floated for a day and a night.

I was found by the Rachel.

She was still searching for her captain's son, moving in slow circles across that section of ocean, unwilling to give up what any reasonable calculation would call lost. She was a ship that refused to stop looking for what was missing. She found me instead.

I was taken aboard. I was given water and food and a blanket and a bunk. The Rachel's captain came to see me himself. He asked what had happened. I told him.

He did not ask about his son. He did not need to.

I lay in the bunk and looked at the ceiling and tried to account for what I had survived.

Forty men. One ship. Three years of Ahab's planning and the whale's ocean and the particular machinery of obsession that had set all of this in motion. And here I was: one man on a different ship, alive because of a coffin, heading back toward a world that had continued its ordinary business while I had been away.

I thought about Queequeg. About the quality of attention he brought to everything — the harpoon throw, the sail-mending, the coffin-sewing. The calm that came from making peace with his own death. He had lived entirely in the present tense, and the present tense had been enough.

I thought about Starbuck. About the night with the musket. About the request he made on the third day that Ahab heard and could not act on.

I thought about Ahab. Not with hatred — I could not hate him. He had been a great man made into something diminished by the particular wound the universe had given him and his inability to put it down. He had been looking for something real — justice, meaning, a world that responded to suffering with something other than indifference — and he had looked for it in the wrong place, with the wrong tool, at the cost of everything.

The whale was still in the Pacific. White, enormous, indifferent, moving through the deep water with the ease of something that had never needed to be anything other than what it was.

I was alive. That was the whole of what I knew.





Chapter 23

What the Ocean Kept



The Rachel brought me to Nantucket.

I went ashore on a gray afternoon in November, nearly a year after we had sailed, and I walked the familiar streets of a town that had sent many ships to sea and received fewer back. It knew how to absorb loss. The town itself had a quality of continuing — the commerce, the streets, the church steeples — that said: things end, life goes on, this is not a betrayal, it is simply how things are.

I went to the offices of the Pequod's owners. I told Peleg and Bildad what had happened. I told it plainly, beginning to end. They listened with the attention of men who have heard bad news from the sea before and know there is nothing to do with it but hear it.

Bildad sat in silence for a long time after I finished. Then he said: 'He knew.'

I asked what he meant.

He said that Ahab had known, before the voyage, that this was how it would end. That the certainty in Ahab was not the certainty of someone who expected to win. It was the certainty of someone who has decided to see a thing through regardless of outcome. He had told Bildad, in one of their last conversations, that he thought the whale would kill him. He had gone anyway.

I did not know how to hold this information.

A man who went knowingly toward his own death, for a reason he could not explain that made sense to anyone outside himself.

Was he brave? Was he mad? Was there a difference?

I left the owners' office and walked to the harbor and looked at the ships.

There were ships preparing to sail. The whale trade continued because the lamps needed oil and men would sign on tomorrow and next month and next year and most of them would come back and some of them would not and the ocean would keep what it kept.

I thought about Starbuck's wife and child. About how they had been waiting. About what it meant to wait for someone and have someone else come to tell you the news.

I thought about Pip, who had seen the ocean from inside it and had never fully come back.

I thought about Queequeg, who had made his peace with death and therefore been able to live without fear, and whose coffin had kept me alive.

I sat on the dock for a long time and watched the water.

I did not go back to sea for a year.





Chapter 24

What Ishmael Learned



I am writing this down because it needs to be written down.

Not for anyone in particular. For whoever comes after, who might find it useful. The ocean is always accepting new passengers and they deserve to know what they are getting into — not the danger, which is obvious, but the other thing, the thing beneath the danger.

What I learned on the Pequod:

I learned that obsession is seductive. Ahab on the quarter-deck with the gold coin was genuinely moving. The crew cheered because he had given them a story with a shape — a clear enemy, a defined goal, the prospect of resolution. Humans need stories with shapes. Ahab's madness felt, from inside it, like clarity. The test of a story is not whether it is compelling. It is whether it is true.

I learned that the opposite of obsession is not indifference. Queequeg was not indifferent. He cared — about his work, about the people beside him, about the moment he was in. But his caring was proportionate. It was care without ownership, attention without possession. He could hold things lightly because he had already decided the fundamental question — that he would die eventually, as everything dies, and that this was fine — and everything else arranged itself around that decision.

I learned that a good man in a bad situation is still a good man. Starbuck did not stop what happened. He could not stop it without becoming something he was not. And what he was — careful, principled, genuinely brave in the ways that do not make noise — was real and it mattered even though it was not enough. The fact that goodness sometimes fails does not make it less worth having.

I learned that the ocean is not against us. It is simply the ocean. The whale was not Ahab's enemy. It was a whale. The universe is not organized around our suffering, which is both the worst thing about it and the most liberating. If it is not organized against you, you are free.

I learned that I wanted to live. This sounds simple. It is not simple. I had gone to sea at twenty-two to feel something, because the ordinary world had not been large enough to fill the space inside me. The Pequod was larger than I had asked for. It gave me things I will carry for the rest of my life, including the memory of people I loved who are gone.

The coffin kept me alive. Queequeg made the coffin. The Rachel was still looking.

I go to sea sometimes still, on shorter voyages, merchant ships. I stand at the rail and watch the water and think about all of it. The whiteness of the whale. Ahab's face at the moment of sighting. Starbuck at the end. Pip talking to the ocean.

The water shows me none of it back. It is too large for mirrors.

That is, I have decided, exactly right.

Call me Ishmael.

I was there.

I survived to tell you.
