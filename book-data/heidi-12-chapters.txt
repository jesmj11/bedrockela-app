Chapter 1: Up the Mountain
Five-year-old Heidi climbed the steep mountain path, holding tightly to her Aunt Dete's hand. The little girl carried all her belongings in a small bundle.
'Where are we going, Aunt Dete?' asked Heidi, looking up at the tall pine trees and rocky cliffs around them.
'To see your grandfather,' Aunt Dete replied, breathing heavily from the climb. 'He lives high up on this mountain, all by himself.'
Heidi had never met her grandfather. She had lived with Aunt Dete in the village below since her parents died when she was very small.
'Why does Grandfather live alone?' Heidi asked. The mountain air was fresh and cool, and she could hear birds singing in the trees.
Aunt Dete frowned. 'He's a grumpy old man who doesn't like people. But I got a new job in the city, and I can't take you with me.'
As they climbed higher, the village became smaller and smaller below them. Heidi could see tiny houses and a church with a pointed steeple.

Chapter 2: Meeting Grandfather
At the top of the mountain sat a small wooden hut. An old man with a long gray beard was sitting outside, carving a piece of wood with a sharp knife.
'Uncle Alp,' called Aunt Dete nervously. 'I've brought Heidi to live with you. She's your granddaughter, and you must take care of her now.'
The old man looked up with fierce blue eyes. He stared at little Heidi, who stared back at him without fear. She had never seen anyone who looked so wild and strong.
'I don't want a child here,' Grandfather said gruffly. 'Take her back to the village where she belongs.'
But Aunt Dete had already set down Heidi's bundle and was walking quickly back down the mountain path. 'She's your responsibility now!' she called over her shoulder.
Heidi and her grandfather stood looking at each other in silence. The wind whistled through the pine trees, and somewhere far below, a church bell was ringing.
'Well,' said Grandfather finally. 'I suppose you're here now. But don't expect me to change my ways for a child.'

Chapter 3: A New Home
Grandfather's hut was simple but clean. There was one large room with a fireplace, a wooden table, and two chairs. A ladder led up to a loft where hay was stored.
'You can sleep up there,' Grandfather said, pointing to the loft. 'I'll make you a bed in the hay. It's softer than it looks.'
Heidi climbed the ladder and looked around the cozy loft. Through a small round window, she could see the whole valley spread out below like a green carpet.
'It's beautiful!' she exclaimed. 'I can see everything from up here - the village, the church, even the river!'
For dinner, Grandfather gave Heidi fresh bread, creamy goat cheese, and cold milk. They sat outside on wooden stools, watching the sun set behind the mountains.
'I like it here,' Heidi said simply. 'The air smells like flowers, and I can hear the wind singing in the trees.'
Grandfather looked surprised. Most people were afraid of the lonely mountain. But this little girl seemed to understand its beauty.

Chapter 4: Peter and the Goats
The next morning, Heidi woke to the sound of bells jingling outside. She looked out her window and saw a boy about eleven years old leading a herd of goats up the mountain.
'That's Peter the goatherd,' Grandfather explained. 'He takes the goats to pasture every day. Two of those goats are mine - Little Swan and Little Bear.'
Heidi ran outside to meet Peter. He had dark hair and wore simple clothes, but his eyes were kind. The goats gathered around Heidi, sniffing her curiously.
'Would you like to come with me today?' Peter asked. 'The goats need to eat fresh grass on the high meadows, and it's a beautiful day for walking.'
Grandfather nodded his permission, and Heidi skipped along beside Peter and the goats. She had never seen such a wonderful sight - dozens of goats with bells around their necks, all heading up the mountain together.
'This is Little Swan,' Peter said, pointing to a white goat. 'And that's Little Bear, the brown one. They give the best milk on the mountain.'
Heidi laughed as the goats playfully butted their heads against her hands, looking for treats. She felt like she had found a whole new family.

Chapter 5: Life on the Mountain
Every day, Heidi went with Peter to the high pastures. They would sit in the sunshine while the goats grazed on sweet mountain grass and wildflowers.
Peter taught Heidi the names of all the peaks and valleys. 'That's Eagle's Peak,' he said, pointing to a tall, rocky mountain. 'And down there is Mayenfeld, where I live with my mother and grandmother.'
Heidi learned to milk the goats and make cheese with Grandfather. She helped him repair tools and gather firewood for winter. The old man began to smile more often.
'You're a good helper,' Grandfather told her one evening as they watched the stars come out. 'I haven't been this happy in years.'
Heidi had never been happier either. She loved the clean mountain air, the sound of cowbells in the distance, and the way the sunrise painted the peaks pink and gold each morning.
At night, she would lie in her hay bed and listen to the wind in the pine trees. It sounded like a lullaby, singing her to sleep under the bright mountain stars.
'I want to stay here forever,' she whispered to herself. 'This is the most beautiful place in the whole world.'

Chapter 6: The Village Below
One day, Peter took Heidi down to his village to meet his grandmother. The old woman was kind but very poor, and she was slowly going blind.
'I can't read my prayer book anymore,' Grandmother said sadly. 'The words are too blurry, and we have no money for glasses.'
'I wish I could read,' Heidi said. 'Then I could read your book to you every day.' She had never learned to read because there was no school on the mountain.
'The pastor says all children should go to school,' Peter's mother said. 'But your grandfather won't let you come to the village school.'
When Heidi asked Grandfather about school, he frowned. 'You don't need book learning,' he said. 'The mountains will teach you everything important.'
But Heidi couldn't stop thinking about Peter's blind grandmother. If only she could learn to read, she could help the lonely old woman.
'Knowledge is a gift,' she thought. 'Maybe someday I'll find a way to learn, so I can share that gift with others.'

Chapter 7: Taken Away
Two years passed happily on the mountain. Heidi grew taller and stronger, with rosy cheeks and bright eyes. She knew every path and meadow for miles around.
One morning, Aunt Dete appeared at the hut door, dressed in fine city clothes. 'I've come for Heidi,' she announced. 'I found her a wonderful opportunity in Frankfurt.'
'A rich family needs a companion for their sick daughter,' Aunt Dete explained. 'Heidi will live in a mansion, wear beautiful dresses, and get an excellent education.'
'I don't want to go!' cried Heidi. 'I want to stay here with Grandfather and Peter and the goats!'
But Aunt Dete was firm. 'This is a chance for a better life. You're eight years old now - old enough for proper schooling and city manners.'
Grandfather's face was like stone. 'Take her then,' he said coldly. 'But don't expect me to welcome her back when the city breaks her spirit.'
With tears streaming down her face, Heidi was led away from her mountain home. She looked back at the hut until it disappeared behind the trees.

Chapter 8: The Big City
Frankfurt was huge and noisy, full of tall buildings and busy streets. Heidi had never seen so many people and carriages rushing in all directions.
The Sesemann house was like a palace, with marble floors, crystal chandeliers, and rooms full of expensive furniture. Heidi felt lost in the grand hallways.
Miss Rottenmeier, the strict housekeeper, looked Heidi up and down with disapproval. 'This wild mountain child needs proper training,' she sniffed.
'Her name is Heidi, and she'll be my friend,' said a gentle voice. A pale girl in a wheelchair rolled into the room. This was Clara, the daughter of the house.
Clara had been sick for many years and couldn't walk. She spent her days in the big house, reading books and doing needlework, with no friends her own age.
'Tell me about the mountains,' Clara said eagerly. 'I've never seen anything but city streets from my window.'
As Heidi described the alpine meadows and snowy peaks, Clara's eyes sparkled with interest. Maybe having a friend would make them both less lonely.

Chapter 9: Clara's Friend
Heidi tried her best to fit into city life. She learned to read and write with Clara's tutor, Mr. Candidate. She wore fancy dresses and practiced proper table manners.
But everything felt strange and uncomfortable. The house was always stuffy, the streets were noisy, and she could never see the sky properly through the tall buildings.
Clara loved hearing Heidi's stories about mountain life. 'Tell me about the goats again,' she would say. 'And the way the sunrise looks on Eagle's Peak.'
Heidi drew pictures of her mountain home for Clara. She described how the wind sounded in the pine trees and how fresh the morning air smelled after rain.
'I wish I could see your mountains,' Clara sighed. 'They sound so much more beautiful than anything here in the city.'
'Maybe someday you can visit,' Heidi said hopefully. 'Grandfather would like you, and the mountain air might make you feel stronger.'
The two girls became best friends, but Heidi grew sadder each day. She missed her simple life on the mountain more than words could say.

Chapter 10: Homesick
As months passed, Heidi became pale and thin. She couldn't eat the rich city food and had trouble sleeping in the soft feather bed.
Every night, she dreamed of her mountain home. She could hear the goat bells ringing and feel the cool mountain breeze on her face. Then she would wake up in the stuffy city house.
'What's wrong, Heidi?' Clara asked with concern. 'You look so sad lately, and you hardly eat anything.'
'I miss my home,' Heidi whispered. 'I miss Grandfather and Peter and the goats. I miss the mountains and the fresh air and the way everything smells like flowers.'
Clara's father, Mr. Sesemann, was a kind man who noticed how unhappy Heidi had become. He called the family doctor to examine her.
'This child is homesick,' the doctor said after talking with Heidi. 'She needs to return to her mountain home, or she may become seriously ill.'
Though Clara would miss her friend terribly, Mr. Sesemann knew the doctor was right. 'We must send you home to your grandfather,' he told Heidi gently.

Chapter 11: Return to the Mountain
The journey back to the mountains felt like waking up from a long dream. As the train climbed higher into the hills, Heidi's spirits began to lift.
When she saw the familiar peaks and valleys, she could barely contain her excitement. 'I'm almost home!' she cried, pressing her face to the train window.
Peter met her at the village station, grinning from ear to ear. 'Heidi! You're back! Everyone has missed you so much, especially your grandfather.'
They climbed the mountain path together, just like old times. The goats seemed to remember Heidi and crowded around her, bleating happily.
Grandfather was waiting at the hut door. When he saw Heidi running up the path, his stern face broke into the biggest smile she had ever seen.
'My dear child,' he said, wrapping her in a warm hug. 'I've been so lonely without you. The mountain hasn't been the same.'
That night, Heidi slept in her hay bed again, listening to the familiar sound of wind in the pine trees. She was finally home where she belonged.

Chapter 12: Clara's Visit
A few months later, a wonderful surprise arrived. Mr. Sesemann brought Clara to visit the mountains, hoping the fresh air would improve her health.
Clara was amazed by the beauty around her. 'Heidi, you were right!' she exclaimed. 'This is the most beautiful place I've ever seen!'
Every day, Peter helped carry Clara up to the high meadows in her wheelchair. She watched the goats graze and breathed the clean mountain air.
'I feel stronger already,' Clara told Heidi. 'The sunshine and fresh air are like medicine. I think I might even try to walk a little.'
With Heidi and Peter's encouragement, Clara practiced standing on her weak legs. Day by day, she grew stronger and more confident.
By the end of summer, Clara was walking short distances on her own. 'It's a miracle!' her father said happily. 'The mountain air has healed her!'
When it was time for Clara to return to Frankfurt, both girls cried. But they promised to write letters and visit each other every summer.
Heidi stayed on her beloved mountain, where she continued to grow up healthy and happy, surrounded by the natural beauty she loved best of all.

The End
