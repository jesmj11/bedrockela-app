Alice's Adventures in Wonderland
A BedrockELA Adaptation
Vocabulary Guide — 2 Words Per Chapter (48 Words Total)

Chapter 1: Down the Rabbit Hole

curious  (adjective)
Eager to know or learn something. Also: strange or unusual.
From the story: Alice found the rabbit hole curious in both senses — strange enough to notice, and interesting enough to follow.
Think about it: The word curious means both 'wanting to know' and 'strange.' How does Alice embody BOTH meanings throughout this story?

proportion  (noun)
The size or amount of something compared to something else. The correct relationship between parts.
From the story: After drinking from the bottle, Alice shrank out of all proportion — her body no longer matched the world around her.
Think about it: When things are out of proportion, they feel wrong. How does Alice's constantly changing size make everything in Wonderland feel unstable?

Chapter 2: The Pool of Tears

identity  (noun)
Who a person is. The qualities, beliefs, and personality that make someone themselves.
From the story: Alice wondered: 'If I'm not the same, the next question is, Who in the world am I?' Her identity was the great puzzle of Wonderland.
Think about it: If you changed size ten times in one day, would you still feel like yourself? What makes you YOU — your body, your thoughts, your memories, or something else?

bewildered  (adjective)
Completely confused and unable to understand what is happening.
From the story: Alice was bewildered — she had grown enormous, shrunk to nothing, and was now swimming in her own tears.
Think about it: Being bewildered means you've lost your sense of how things work. How is Wonderland designed to bewilder everyone who enters it?

Chapter 3: A Caucus Race

absurd  (adjective)
Wildly unreasonable or illogical. So silly that it is almost funny.
From the story: The Caucus Race was completely absurd — everyone ran in random directions, everyone won, and everyone got prizes.
Think about it: Is the Caucus Race truly absurd, or does it have its own kind of logic? If the point is to get dry, does it matter who wins?

ceremony  (noun)
A formal event with specific rituals and traditions. An official, often serious, occasion.
From the story: The Dodo presented Alice with her own thimble with great ceremony, as if awarding her the most important prize in the world.
Think about it: The ceremony is ridiculous — Alice receives her own thimble back. But is this more or less ridiculous than real ceremonies where people receive trophies and awards? What gives a ceremony meaning?

Chapter 4: The Rabbit Sends a Little Bill

impulsive  (adjective)
Acting quickly without thinking about the consequences. Doing things on a sudden urge.
From the story: Alice impulsively drank from the bottle in the Rabbit's house without reading the label — and grew so large she filled the entire room.
Think about it: Alice keeps eating and drinking things without thinking. Is this reckless, or is it necessary in a world where the rules keep changing?

predicament  (noun)
A difficult, unpleasant, or embarrassing situation that is hard to get out of.
From the story: Being stuck inside a house with her arm out the window and her foot up the chimney was quite a predicament.
Think about it: Alice has been in one predicament after another since entering Wonderland. What does she do when she's stuck? Does she panic, or does she think?

Chapter 5: Advice from a Caterpillar

languid  (adjective)
Slow, relaxed, and showing little energy or interest. Moving or speaking in a lazy, unhurried way.
From the story: The Caterpillar spoke in a languid, sleepy voice, as though Alice's entire existence were barely worth noticing.
Think about it: The Caterpillar is completely unimpressed by Alice. Why does his languid manner make Alice so frustrated? What does it feel like when someone refuses to take you seriously?

chrysalis  (noun)
The protective shell a caterpillar forms around itself before transforming into a butterfly.
From the story: Alice pointed out that the Caterpillar would feel strange when it turned into a chrysalis and then a butterfly — but the Caterpillar disagreed.
Think about it: A chrysalis is where transformation happens. The Caterpillar will literally become a completely different creature. How does this connect to Alice's own transformations in Wonderland?

Chapter 6: Pig and Pepper

chaos  (noun)
Complete disorder and confusion. A state where nothing is organized or predictable.
From the story: The Duchess's kitchen was pure chaos — pepper in the air, a baby screaming, a cook throwing things, and a cat grinning from ear to ear.
Think about it: Wonderland is full of chaos. But is all chaos bad? Can chaos be creative or freeing? What's the difference between dangerous chaos and playful chaos?

vanish  (verb)
To disappear suddenly and completely, as if into thin air.
From the story: The Cheshire Cat vanished slowly, starting with its tail and ending with its grin — which remained floating in the air after the rest was gone.
Think about it: The Cat vanishes but its grin stays. Can an expression exist without a face? Carroll is playing with the idea that qualities can be separated from the things they belong to.

Chapter 7: A Mad Tea-Party

contradiction  (noun)
A combination of statements or ideas that are opposite or inconsistent with each other.
From the story: The Hatter said: 'I see what I eat' is NOT the same as 'I eat what I see.' What seemed like a contradiction turned out to be a real logical difference.
Think about it: The Hatter's word games sound like nonsense, but they reveal real truths about language. 'I say what I mean' and 'I mean what I say' ARE different. When has a seeming contradiction turned out to be true?

perpetual  (adjective)
Never ending or changing. Continuing forever without stopping.
From the story: The tea party was perpetual — stuck at six o'clock forever because the Hatter had quarreled with Time.
Think about it: Being stuck in one moment forever sounds fun for about five minutes. Why would a perpetual tea party actually be a kind of prison?

Chapter 8: The Dormouse's Story

treacle  (noun)
A thick, sweet syrup made from sugar. (In the story, it represents something that sounds real but is actually nonsense.)
From the story: The Dormouse claimed three sisters lived at the bottom of a treacle-well, drawing treacle. Every detail made less sense than the last.
Think about it: The treacle-well story is complete nonsense — but the Dormouse tells it with total confidence. When someone tells a nonsense story with certainty, does that make it more or less convincing?

contemptuously  (adverb)
In a way that shows you think someone or something is worthless or beneath you. With scorn.
From the story: The Hatter tossed his head contemptuously when Alice didn't understand his logic.
Think about it: Many characters in Wonderland speak contemptuously to Alice. Why do they treat her as if SHE is the one who doesn't make sense? Who is really confused — Alice or Wonderland?

Chapter 9: The Queen's Garden

tyranny  (noun)
Cruel and oppressive rule. The use of power in a harsh, unfair way.
From the story: The Queen's rule was pure tyranny — she threatened beheading for the smallest mistake, and everyone lived in fear.
Think about it: The Queen rules through fear, not respect. How is tyranny different from real leadership? What makes people obey a tyrant?

decisive  (adjective)
Making decisions quickly and firmly. Not hesitating.
From the story: When the Queen screamed 'Off with her head!' Alice was decisive — she said 'Nonsense!' without a moment's hesitation.
Think about it: Alice's decisiveness surprises everyone, including herself. Where does her courage come from? Is being decisive the same as being brave?

Chapter 10: The Queen's Croquet Ground

arbitrary  (adjective)
Based on random choice rather than reason. Rules that change depending on someone's mood.
From the story: The Queen's croquet rules were entirely arbitrary — she changed them whenever she was losing and punished anyone who noticed.
Think about it: Arbitrary rules feel unfair because there's no way to follow them correctly. How does living under arbitrary rules affect the people in Wonderland? Have you ever experienced arbitrary rules?

rigged  (adjective)
Arranged dishonestly to produce a particular result. A game that is set up so one person always wins.
From the story: The croquet game was rigged — the Queen always won because the rules changed to suit her, and anyone who scored against her lost their head.
Think about it: A rigged game isn't really a game at all. It's just power pretending to be fairness. Where else in the story — or in real life — do you see rules that are rigged?

Chapter 11: The Mock Turtle's Story

parody  (noun)
An imitation of something that exaggerates its features for comic effect. A funny version of something serious.
From the story: The Mock Turtle's school subjects are parodies of real subjects — 'Reeling and Writhing' instead of Reading and Writing, 'Uglification' instead of Multiplication.
Think about it: Carroll is parodying the education system. By making school sound ridiculous, what might he be saying about how children were taught in his time?

melancholy  (adjective/noun)
A deep, persistent sadness. A feeling of thoughtful sorrow.
From the story: The Mock Turtle was melancholy — weeping constantly about a past that may never have existed.
Think about it: The Mock Turtle is sad about something he can't explain. Is his sadness real even if his reasons don't make sense? Can you feel genuine melancholy about something imaginary?

Chapter 12: The Lobster Quadrille

quadrille  (noun)
A formal dance for four couples, popular in the 18th and 19th centuries, with set movements and patterns.
From the story: The Lobster Quadrille was a ridiculous version of a formal dance — with lobsters as partners and somersaults in the sea.
Think about it: A quadrille has strict rules and patterns. The Lobster Quadrille has none. What happens when you take something formal and make it chaotic? Is it more fun or less meaningful?

absurdity  (noun)
The quality of being wildly unreasonable or ridiculous. Complete nonsense.
From the story: Alice finally laughed at the absurdity of everything — the dance, the teachers, the rules, and herself for trying so hard to make sense of it all.
Think about it: Alice's laughter is a breakthrough. Instead of fighting the absurdity, she accepts it. Is accepting absurdity the same as giving up, or is it a kind of wisdom?

Chapter 13: Who Stole the Tarts?

testimony  (noun)
A formal statement, especially one given in a court of law. Evidence given by a witness.
From the story: The witnesses' testimony at the trial was useless — the Hatter couldn't remember when he started his tea, and the jury wrote down dates that made no sense.
Think about it: Testimony is supposed to establish facts. What happens when testimony is unreliable? How can a trial find the truth if the witnesses don't make sense?

verdict  (noun)
The decision reached by a jury at the end of a trial. The final judgment of guilty or not guilty.
From the story: The King wanted the verdict immediately, even though no real evidence had been presented.
Think about it: A verdict should follow evidence. In Wonderland, the verdict comes first. What is Carroll saying about justice systems that decide guilt before examining the facts?

Chapter 14: Alice's Evidence

evidence  (noun)
Facts or information used to prove whether something is true. The basis for believing something.
From the story: Alice was called to give evidence, but she had none — and she said so honestly, which was more than anyone else in the courtroom had done.
Think about it: Alice is the only person at the trial who tells the truth: she knows nothing. In a court full of lies and nonsense, is honest ignorance better than confident falsehood?

authority  (noun)
The power to give orders and make decisions. The right to be obeyed.
From the story: The Queen's authority was based on fear alone — she screamed 'Off with her head!' but Alice was growing too tall to be afraid.
Think about it: Authority can come from respect, from knowledge, or from fear. Which kind does the Queen have? Which kind is strongest in the long run?

Chapter 15: Sentence First

injustice  (noun)
Unfairness. A situation where people are treated wrongly or without regard for their rights.
From the story: The Queen's demand of 'Sentence first — verdict afterwards' was the clearest example of injustice in all of Wonderland.
Think about it: Injustice often hides behind the appearance of proper procedure. The trial LOOKS like a trial — judge, jury, witnesses — but it IS injustice. How can you tell the difference between real justice and the appearance of justice?

meaningless  (adjective)
Having no meaning, purpose, or value. Empty of real content.
From the story: The poem presented as evidence was completely meaningless — it proved nothing about anyone — yet the King called it 'the most important piece of evidence.'
Think about it: When powerful people declare meaningless things important, everyone goes along with it. Why? What gives something meaning — its actual content or the authority of the person presenting it?

Chapter 16: Growing Taller

clarity  (noun)
The quality of being clear, easy to understand, and free from confusion.
From the story: Alice felt clarity settle inside her — not anger, not fear, but a clear understanding that Wonderland's rules were wrong and she did not have to accept them.
Think about it: Clarity is the opposite of bewilderment. Alice started the story bewildered and ends it with clarity. What experiences gave her this clarity?

certainty  (noun)
The state of being completely sure about something. Absolute confidence.
From the story: Everyone in Wonderland was full of certainty about things that were completely wrong. Alice was the only one who admitted uncertainty — and that made her the most honest person there.
Think about it: Is certainty always a good thing? The Queen is certain. The Caterpillar is certain. The Hatter is certain. They are all wrong. Alice is uncertain — and she is right. What does this teach about the relationship between certainty and truth?

Chapter 17: "You're Nothing But a Pack of Cards!"

revelation  (noun)
A surprising and important discovery. The moment when something hidden becomes clear.
From the story: Alice's revelation — 'You're nothing but a pack of cards!' — shattered the illusion of Wonderland's power.
Think about it: Alice has known the court is made of cards since she arrived. But SAYING it out loud is different from knowing it silently. Why does speaking the truth have more power than just thinking it?

illusion  (noun)
Something that appears real but is not. A false impression or belief.
From the story: Wonderland was an illusion — its power, its rules, its terrors were all made of paper and paint, and Alice finally said so.
Think about it: Many things in life are illusions that seem powerful only because we agree to believe in them. What 'illusions' exist in the real world?

Chapter 18: Waking Up

ordinary  (adjective)
Normal, usual, with no special or distinctive features. Everyday and unremarkable.
From the story: The riverbank was perfectly ordinary — grass, daisies, a book. Everything was exactly as it had been. And yet Alice felt different.
Think about it: The ordinary world hasn't changed. Alice has. How does a person's inner transformation change how they see the same world they've always known?

perspective  (noun)
A particular way of seeing or thinking about something. Your point of view shaped by your experiences.
From the story: Alice's perspective had shifted. The riverbank was the same, but she saw it with eyes that had been to Wonderland.
Think about it: Two people can look at the same thing and see it differently because of their perspective. How has Wonderland changed Alice's perspective on the ordinary world?

Chapter 19: The Duchess's Morals

moral  (noun)
A lesson about right and wrong learned from a story or experience. The deeper meaning behind events.
From the story: The Duchess found a moral in everything — but her morals were forced and false. Alice learned that real morals can't be crammed into everything.
Think about it: The Duchess insists everything has a moral. But sometimes things just ARE, without a lesson attached. When is it right to look for a moral, and when is it forcing meaning onto something that doesn't have one?

conviction  (noun)
A firmly held belief or opinion. The strong feeling that something is true.
From the story: Alice spoke with quiet conviction when she said 'I don't think that's right.' Not loudly, not angrily — just certainly.
Think about it: Conviction is different from certainty. Certainty can be wrong. Conviction is earned through experience. How did Alice earn her convictions?

Chapter 20: The Rules of Wonderland

logic  (noun)
Reasoning based on rules and consistency. The process of using facts and reason to reach conclusions.
From the story: Alice discovered that Wonderland had its own logic — it wasn't HER logic, but it followed its own strange internal consistency.
Think about it: Is something illogical just because you don't understand the rules? Wonderland has logic — it's just different from ours. When you encounter something that doesn't make sense, is the problem the thing itself or your expectations?

navigate  (verb)
To find your way through a difficult or complex situation. To steer a course.
From the story: Alice navigated Wonderland by asking questions, admitting confusion, and refusing to pretend she understood things she didn't.
Think about it: What tools does Alice use to navigate Wonderland? Not strength, not magic — questions. How are questions a navigation tool in real life?

Chapter 21: Who Is Alice?

fixed  (adjective)
Not changing. Permanent and set in place.
From the story: Every creature in Wonderland was fixed — the Rabbit was always late, the Queen was always angry, the Hatter was always at tea. Only Alice changed.
Think about it: The creatures of Wonderland are stuck being one thing forever. Alice grows, changes, and learns. Why is the ability to change more valuable than being permanently defined?

evolve  (verb)
To develop gradually over time. To change and grow into something new.
From the story: Alice evolved throughout her journey — from a bored girl on a riverbank to someone who understood the difference between sense and nonsense.
Think about it: Evolution is not sudden — it happens gradually through experience. Can you trace the moments that caused Alice to evolve?

Chapter 22: Sense and Nonsense

discernment  (noun)
The ability to judge well. The skill of seeing differences that others miss, especially between what is true and what is false.
From the story: Alice developed discernment — the ability to tell harmless nonsense from dangerous nonsense, silly rules from unjust ones.
Think about it: Discernment is one of the most important skills a person can have. How does Alice's journey through Wonderland build her discernment? Can discernment be taught, or must it be learned through experience?

defend  (verb)
To protect something from attack. To argue in support of something you believe in.
From the story: Alice realized that sense, fairness, and logic are not automatic — they have to be defended by people willing to ask 'Why?'
Think about it: Alice becomes a defender of sense. What does she defend against? What tools does she use? Is asking questions a form of defense?

Chapter 23: Alice Remembers

reflect  (verb)
To think deeply and carefully about something, especially past experiences. To look back with thought.
From the story: Alice reflected on Wonderland and realized she missed the creatures — not the nonsense, but the aliveness of a world where nothing was boring.
Think about it: Reflection is different from remembering. Remembering is recalling facts. Reflecting is finding meaning. What does Alice find when she reflects on Wonderland that she couldn't see while she was there?

authentic  (adjective)
Real, genuine, true to itself. Not fake or pretending.
From the story: The creatures of Wonderland were maddening but authentic — they never pretended to be something they weren't.
Think about it: Is being authentically mad better than being falsely sensible? The Hatter is genuinely himself. Many 'normal' people pretend to be something they're not. Which is more honest?

Chapter 24: The Girl Who Asked Questions

extraordinary  (adjective)
Very unusual, remarkable, beyond what is ordinary. Far exceeding the common.
From the story: Alice learned that the extraordinary is always hiding just beneath the ordinary — waiting for someone curious enough to notice.
Think about it: The extraordinary was under a hedge (the rabbit hole) the entire time. Alice found it because she was curious enough to look. What extraordinary things might be hiding in YOUR ordinary world?

wonder  (noun)
A feeling of amazement and admiration, caused by something beautiful, unexpected, or difficult to understand. Also: to think curiously about something.
From the story: Alice returned to ordinary life carrying wonder with her — the ability to see questions where others saw only answers.
Think about it: Wonder has two meanings: amazement AND curiosity. Alice has both. How are these two meanings connected? Does being amazed make you more curious? Does being curious lead to amazement?
