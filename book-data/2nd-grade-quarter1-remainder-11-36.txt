Text 11: Frogs
Frogs are amphibians, which means they can live both in water and on land. Most frogs start their lives as eggs laid in ponds or streams. When the eggs hatch, tiny tadpoles come out. Tadpoles look very different from adult frogs – they have long tails and breathe through gills like fish do.
As tadpoles grow, they go through an amazing change called metamorphosis. They develop legs, lose their tails, and grow lungs so they can breathe air. Adult frogs eat insects, worms, and other small creatures by catching them with their long, sticky tongues. Frogs are important to the environment because they eat many insects that could harm crops and spread diseases.

Text 12: Mail Carriers
Mail carriers are the people who bring letters, packages, and other mail to homes and businesses every day. They work for the postal service and have special trucks or bags to carry all the mail. Mail carriers learn the addresses in their area so they know exactly where each piece of mail should go. They work in all kinds of weather – hot summer days, cold winter mornings, and even in rain or snow.
Being a mail carrier requires good physical fitness because they walk many miles each day and carry heavy bags of mail. They must also be very careful and responsible since people depend on them to deliver important letters and packages safely. Mail carriers often get to know the people on their routes and become friendly faces in the neighborhood. Some mail carriers drive trucks to deliver mail, while others walk from house to house.

Text 13: Clouds
Clouds are made of tiny water droplets or ice crystals that float high in the sky. They form when warm air rises from the ground and cools down as it gets higher. As the air cools, the water vapor in it turns back into tiny droplets that stick to dust particles in the air. Millions of these tiny droplets cluster together to make clouds that we can see.
There are many different types of clouds, and each type can tell us about the weather. Fluffy white clouds usually mean nice weather, while dark gray clouds often bring rain or storms. Some clouds are high and wispy like feathers, and others are low and thick like blankets covering the sky. Meteorologists study clouds to help predict what the weather will be like.

Text 14: Doctors
Doctors are trained medical professionals who help people stay healthy and treat them when they get sick or injured. They go to medical school for many years to learn about the human body, diseases, and different treatments. Doctors examine patients, ask questions about how they feel, and use special tools to check their health. They can also order tests like X-rays or blood tests to help figure out what might be wrong.
There are many different types of doctors who specialize in different parts of the body or types of medicine. Pediatricians take care of children, cardiologists focus on the heart, and surgeons perform operations. Family doctors treat people of all ages for common illnesses and injuries. All doctors work hard to help their patients feel better and live healthier lives.

Text 15: Owls
Owls are birds of prey that hunt mainly at night when most other birds are sleeping. They have special features that make them excellent nighttime hunters. Their large eyes can see very well in the dark, and their ears are positioned differently on each side of their head, which helps them locate sounds precisely. Owls also have soft feathers that allow them to fly silently through the air.
Most owls eat small animals like mice, rats, rabbits, and insects. They use their sharp talons to catch their prey and their hooked beaks to tear it apart. Owls live in many different places around the world, including forests, deserts, and even cities. They make their nests in tree holes, old buildings, or sometimes in burrows in the ground. Baby owls are called owlets and are covered in fluffy down feathers.

Text 17: Seasons
Earth has four seasons: spring, summer, fall, and winter. These seasons happen because Earth is tilted as it travels around the sun. When our part of Earth is tilted toward the sun, we get more direct sunlight and warmer weather, which gives us spring and summer. When our part is tilted away from the sun, we get less direct sunlight and cooler weather, creating fall and winter.
Each season brings different weather and changes in nature. Spring is when plants start growing again, flowers bloom, and baby animals are born. Summer is the warmest time with long days and lots of sunshine. In fall, leaves change colors and fall from trees, and many animals prepare for winter. Winter is the coldest season when some animals hibernate and it might snow in many places.

Text 18: Elephants
Elephants are the largest animals that live on land. They have thick, gray skin and long noses called trunks that they use like we use our hands. Elephants use their trunks to pick up food, drink water, and even hug other elephants. They also have big ears that help keep them cool by flapping back and forth like fans. Adult elephants can weigh as much as four cars put together!
Elephants are very smart animals that live in family groups led by the oldest female, called the matriarch. They eat plants like grass, leaves, and fruit, and they need to eat for about 16 hours every day! Elephants have excellent memories and can remember other elephants they haven't seen for many years. Baby elephants are called calves, and the whole family helps take care of them.

Text 20: Spiders
Spiders are small animals with eight legs that belong to a group called arachnids. Unlike insects, spiders have two main body parts instead of three, and they don't have wings or antennas. Most spiders can make silk, which they use to build webs to catch their food. Spider silk is incredibly strong – it's actually stronger than steel wire of the same thickness!
Spiders are helpful to people because they eat many insects that can be pests, like mosquitoes and flies. Most spiders are harmless to humans and only bite if they feel threatened. Female spiders lay eggs in silk sacs, and baby spiders are called spiderlings. There are thousands of different types of spiders around the world, from tiny jumping spiders to large tarantulas.

Text 22: Snow
Snow forms when water vapor in clouds freezes into tiny ice crystals instead of turning into raindrops. This happens when the temperature in the clouds is below freezing. The ice crystals stick together as they fall through the cold air, creating snowflakes. Each snowflake has a unique shape with six sides, and no two snowflakes are exactly alike!
Snow is important for many reasons. It provides water for plants and animals when it melts in the spring. Many places depend on melted snow for their drinking water and to water crops. Snow also helps keep the ground warm in winter by acting like a blanket over plants and small animals. Some animals, like polar bears and penguins, need snow and ice to survive in their natural habitats.

Text 23: Giraffes
Giraffes are the tallest animals in the world, with long necks that can be up to 6 feet long! They live in Africa and use their height to eat leaves from tall acacia trees that other animals cannot reach. Giraffes have long, dark tongues that can be 18 inches long, which helps them grab leaves and branches. Their tall height also helps them see predators like lions from far away.
Even though giraffes are very tall, they are gentle animals that live in groups called herds. Baby giraffes are called calves, and they are already 6 feet tall when they are born! Giraffes have unique spotted patterns on their fur, and each giraffe's spots are different, just like human fingerprints. They can run up to 35 miles per hour when they need to escape from danger.

Text 25: Plants
Plants are living things that make their own food using sunlight, water, and air. This process is called photosynthesis, and it happens in the green parts of plants, especially the leaves. Other plants, like pine trees, make cones instead of flowers. Plants can grow almost anywhere – in deserts, underwater, on mountains, and even in the cracks of sidewalks. Text 26: Rocks
Rocks are solid materials made up of minerals that form naturally in the Earth. There are three main types of rocks: igneous rocks form when melted rock cools down, sedimentary rocks form from layers of sand and other materials pressed together over time, and metamorphic rocks form when existing rocks are changed by heat and pressure deep underground.
Rocks are useful to people in many ways. We use granite and marble for countertops and buildings, limestone to make cement, and coal for energy. Some rocks contain valuable metals like gold and silver. Geologists are scientists who study rocks to learn about Earth's history and find useful materials. By examining different rock layers, they can tell what the Earth was like millions of years ago.

Text 27: Ocean
The ocean covers more than 70% of Earth's surface and contains about 97% of all the water on our planet. Ocean water is salty because it has dissolved minerals that have washed into it from rocks and soil over millions of years. The ocean is divided into five main parts: the Pacific, Atlantic, Indian, Arctic, and Southern oceans, but they are all connected as one giant body of water.
The ocean is home to millions of different plants and animals, from tiny plankton to massive blue whales. It provides food for billions of people around the world and helps control Earth's weather and temperature. Ocean currents act like rivers in the sea, carrying warm and cold water around the planet. The ocean also gives us oxygen to breathe – tiny ocean plants called phytoplankton produce much of the oxygen in our atmosphere.

Text 30: Trains
Trains are long vehicles that travel on railroad tracks and can carry many people or large amounts of cargo from one place to another. They are made up of a locomotive that provides the power and several cars that carry passengers or freight. Trains are pulled along steel rails by powerful engines that can run on electricity, diesel fuel, or even steam in older trains.
Trains are an important way to transport people and goods because they can carry much more than trucks or cars. Passenger trains help people travel between cities quickly and comfortably, while freight trains move products like coal, grain, and manufactured goods across long distances. High-speed trains in some countries can travel over 200 miles per hour! Trains are also better for the environment than many other forms of transportation because they can move many people or lots of cargo while using less fuel per person or ton.

Text 32: Sleep
Sleep is a natural state of rest that our bodies and brains need every day to stay healthy and function properly. During sleep, our bodies repair themselves, grow new cells, and strengthen our immune system to fight off illnesses. Our brains use sleep time to organize memories from the day and prepare for learning new things tomorrow.
Children need more sleep than adults because their bodies and brains are still growing and developing. Most school-age children need about 9-11 hours of sleep each night to feel rested and alert during the day. Good sleep helps us concentrate better in school, have more energy for playing, and feel happier overall. Creating a bedtime routine and keeping a regular sleep schedule helps ensure we get the rest we need.

Text 35: Recycling
Recycling is the process of collecting used materials and turning them into new products instead of throwing them away as trash. Common materials that can be recycled include paper, plastic bottles, aluminum cans, and glass jars. When we recycle these materials, they are cleaned, broken down, and made into new items, which helps reduce the amount of waste in landfills and conserves natural resources.
Recycling is important for protecting our environment because it reduces pollution and saves energy. Making new products from recycled materials uses less energy than making them from brand new materials. For example, making paper from recycled paper uses much less water and energy than making paper from trees. Everyone can help by separating recyclable materials from regular trash and putting them in special recycling bins.
