The Secret Garden
A BedrockELA Adaptation
Vocabulary Guide — 2 Words Per Chapter (48 Words Total)

Chapter 1: Nobody's Girl

disagreeable  (adjective)
Unpleasant, bad-tempered, and difficult to get along with.
From the story: Everybody said Mary was the most disagreeable-looking child ever seen — thin, sour, and scowling.
Think about it: Mary is disagreeable because of how she was raised, not because she was born that way. What does this tell you about how our experiences shape who we become?

neglect  (noun/verb)
Failing to care for someone or something properly. Ignoring someone's needs.
From the story: Mary had been neglected her whole life — present but unnoticed, existing in a corner of a house too busy for children.
Think about it: Neglect is not the same as cruelty. Mary's parents did not hurt her — they ignored her. Can ignoring someone be as harmful as being cruel to them?

Chapter 2: Misselthwaite Manor

manor  (noun)
A large country house with lands, often belonging to a wealthy family.
From the story: Misselthwaite Manor was enormous — a hundred rooms, most of them locked and empty, sitting on the edge of the moor like a ship run aground.
Think about it: The manor is huge but empty, just like Mary's life. How does the setting mirror the character?

contrary  (adjective)
Deliberately doing the opposite of what is expected or wanted. Stubbornly difficult.
From the story: Mary was contrary by nature — if you told her to sit, she stood; if you told her to be quiet, she complained.
Think about it: Being contrary is Mary's way of having control. Why might a neglected child become contrary?

Chapter 3: The Moor

moor  (noun)
A large area of open, wild land covered with heather, grass, and rough vegetation. Often windy and exposed.
From the story: The moor stretched in every direction — endless, wild, and open under a sky bigger than any Mary had seen.
Think about it: The moor is the opposite of Mary's stuffy rooms and her controlled life in India. How does a wild, open space begin to change a person who has always been confined?

dormant  (adjective)
Alive but not actively growing. Resting or sleeping, waiting for the right conditions to wake up.
From the story: The heather on the moor was dormant — brown and still in early spring, but promising color when the warm weather came.
Think about it: Dormant does not mean dead. Many things in this story appear dead but are actually dormant. What else might be dormant besides the plants?

Chapter 4: The Locked Garden

curiosity  (noun)
A strong desire to know or learn something. The feeling of wanting to explore and discover.
From the story: Standing before the ivy-covered wall, Mary felt curiosity for the first time — a burning need to know what was behind it.
Think about it: Curiosity is the first positive emotion Mary feels. Why is curiosity so important? What does it mean that Mary is finally interested in something outside herself?

abandoned  (adjective/verb)
Left behind and no longer cared for. Given up on.
From the story: The garden had been abandoned for ten years — locked away because Mr. Craven could not bear to see something his wife had loved.
Think about it: Both the garden and Mary have been abandoned. How are their situations similar? Can something abandoned be brought back to life?

Chapter 5: The Robin

instinct  (noun)
A natural, unlearned way of behaving or knowing. A feeling that guides you without thinking.
From the story: The robin seemed to be guiding Mary by instinct — hopping along the wall, leading her closer to the buried key.
Think about it: Mary follows the robin on instinct, without a plan. Sometimes the best discoveries happen when we stop planning and start following our curiosity. Do you agree?

connection  (noun)
A relationship or bond between people, animals, or things. The feeling of being linked to something.
From the story: Talking to the robin was the closest thing to friendship Mary had ever experienced — her first real connection to another living thing.
Think about it: Mary's first connection is with a bird, not a person. Why might it be easier for someone like Mary to connect with an animal first?

Chapter 6: The Door

threshold  (noun)
The strip of ground at the bottom of a doorway. Also: the point at which something begins or changes.
From the story: When Mary stepped through the hidden door, she crossed a threshold — not just into a garden, but into a completely new phase of her life.
Think about it: A threshold is both a physical and a symbolic thing. What is Mary leaving behind when she steps through this door? What is she stepping into?

dormant (revisited)  (adjective)
You learned this word in Chapter 3. Now Mary sees it in the garden — things that LOOK dead but are actually alive underneath.
From the story: The garden looked dead — brown and tangled. But beneath the soil, green shoots were pushing upward. The garden was dormant, not dead.
Think about it: How is the garden's dormancy a mirror of Mary's own life? She looked 'dead' on the outside — sour, thin, empty — but something alive was waiting inside her too.

Chapter 7: The Garden Awakens

instinct (revisited)  (noun)
Mary works in the garden on instinct — no training, no instructions, just a natural sense of what the plants need.
From the story: Instinct guided her. She could see that the dead vines were choking the living ones, so she pulled them away.
Think about it: Mary has never gardened before, yet she knows what to do. Where does this instinct come from? Can caring about something give you knowledge you never formally learned?

purpose  (noun)
A reason for doing something. A sense of meaning or direction in your life.
From the story: For the first time in her life, something needed Mary — and that gave her a sense of purpose she had never felt before.
Think about it: Purpose changes people. Mary went from having nothing to do to having something that depends on her. How does having purpose transform her daily life?

Chapter 8: Dickon

charm  (noun/verb)
A quality that attracts people or animals. The ability to make others feel comfortable and happy around you.
From the story: Dickon could charm any animal in the world — not through tricks, but through patience, calmness, and genuine kindness.
Think about it: Dickon's charm is not fake. It comes from being honest and calm. How is real charm different from trying to impress people?

judgment  (noun)
An opinion formed about someone or something. The act of deciding whether someone is good or bad.
From the story: There was something about Dickon — honest, steady, completely without judgment — that made it impossible to lie to him.
Think about it: Dickon does not judge Mary. He accepts her exactly as she is. Why does the absence of judgment make it easier for Mary to be honest?

Chapter 9: Seeds and Secrets

nurture  (verb)
To care for and encourage the growth of something. To help something develop.
From the story: Mary and Dickon nurtured the garden together — clearing, planting, and tending with patient hands.
Think about it: Nurture means actively caring for growth. Mary was never nurtured as a child. How does learning to nurture a garden teach her what nurturing feels like?

germinate  (verb)
When a seed begins to grow and put out shoots. The moment life starts.
From the story: Dickon said: 'You put a tiny brown thing in the ground and it turns into a flower. If that's not magic, I don't know what is.'
Think about it: Germination is invisible at first — it happens underground. How is this like the changes happening inside Mary? Are the most important changes always visible?

Chapter 10: The Cry in the Night

investigate  (verb)
To examine or look into something carefully in order to discover the truth.
From the story: Mary heard crying in the night and decided to investigate — she followed the sound through the dark corridors.
Think about it: Mary's decision to investigate shows how much she has changed. The old Mary would have ignored the crying or complained about it. What drives the new Mary to seek the truth?

invalid  (noun)
A person who is ill or disabled, especially one who is confined to bed for a long time.
From the story: Colin had been treated as a permanent invalid — kept in bed, never taken outside, told he would always be sick.
Think about it: Is Colin truly an invalid, or has he been MADE into one by the way he's been treated? Can treating someone as helpless actually make them helpless?

Chapter 11: Colin

tyrant  (noun)
A person who uses their power in a cruel and controlling way. Someone who demands obedience through fear.
From the story: Colin was part tyrant, part prisoner — he ordered servants around like an emperor, but he was also trapped in his dark room.
Think about it: Can someone be both a tyrant and a victim at the same time? Colin controls the servants through tantrums, but he is controlled by fear. Who is really in charge?

platitude  (noun)
A remark or statement that has been used so many times it has no real meaning. Empty, comforting words.
From the story: The doctor spoke in soothing platitudes — saying everything would be fine without actually doing anything to make it true.
Think about it: Why are platitudes harmful? When someone says 'everything will be fine' without meaning it, what message does that actually send?

Chapter 12: Two Contrary Children

contrary (revisited)  (adjective)
You learned this in Chapter 2. Now Mary recognizes her own contrary nature in Colin — two stubborn children who are mirror images of each other.
From the story: Mary and Colin were remarkably alike — spoiled, contrary, and convinced the whole world was against them.
Think about it: Mary can see Colin's flaws clearly because they are her own flaws. Why is it sometimes easier to see problems in others than in ourselves?

radical  (adjective)
Very new and different from what is traditional or ordinary. A complete departure from the norm.
From the story: The idea that Colin might NOT be dying was so radical, so different from everything he had been told, that he did not know what to do with it.
Think about it: Why is a new idea sometimes frightening even when it is good news? What makes it hard to let go of beliefs you have held for a long time?

Chapter 13: The Argument

confrontation  (noun)
A direct, face-to-face conflict. The moment when two people stop avoiding an issue and deal with it openly.
From the story: The confrontation between Mary and Colin was fierce — she refused to pity him, and he was furious that she wouldn't.
Think about it: Why is this confrontation necessary? Could Colin have started healing without someone telling him the truth about his back?

revelation  (noun)
A surprising and important discovery that changes how you see something.
From the story: The revelation that Colin's back was straight — that there was no lump, no hunch, no deformity — shattered ten years of fear in one moment.
Think about it: Colin's revelation is physical (his back is fine) but also psychological (his fear was based on lies). Which revelation is more important?

Chapter 14: Fresh Air

stagnant  (adjective)
Not flowing or moving. Still and often unhealthy. Used for air or water that has been trapped in one place too long.
From the story: Colin's sickroom air was stagnant — stuffy, overheated, and stale. The fresh air from the moor shocked his lungs like cold water.
Think about it: Stagnant air makes you sick. Stagnant life makes you sick too. How has Colin's entire existence been stagnant? What is the 'fresh air' he needs beyond literal air?

gradual  (adjective)
Happening slowly, one small step at a time. Not sudden.
From the story: Mary insisted on gradual change — first the window, then two windows, then the door. She knew that too much too fast would overwhelm Colin.
Think about it: Why does Mary choose gradual change instead of dragging Colin outside immediately? What does this show about how real healing works?

Chapter 15: Colin Sees the Garden

sanctuary  (noun)
A safe, peaceful place. A refuge where you are protected and can heal.
From the story: The secret garden became a sanctuary for all three children — a place apart from the world where they could grow without being watched or judged.
Think about it: How is the garden a sanctuary? What are the children protected FROM inside its walls? What are they free to DO that they couldn't do elsewhere?

resolve  (noun)
A firm determination to do something. An unshakable decision.
From the story: Colin's resolve was absolute: 'I am going to get well, and I am going to stand up, and I am going to walk in this garden.'
Think about it: Where does Colin's resolve come from? Is it the garden, the friendship, or something inside himself that was always there?

Chapter 16: "I Shall Live Forever!"

transformation  (noun)
A complete change. Becoming something fundamentally different from what you were before.
From the story: Colin's transformation was visible in every way — his face flushed with color, his body strengthening, his voice growing deeper and more confident.
Think about it: Three things are transforming simultaneously in this story: Mary, Colin, and the garden. How are their transformations connected? Can one happen without the others?

milestone  (noun)
An important event or achievement that marks progress on a journey.
From the story: Standing for the first time was a milestone — not just for Colin's body but for his belief in himself.
Think about it: What makes a milestone different from just another step? Why do we celebrate milestones? What was YOUR most important milestone?

Chapter 17: The Magic

phenomena  (noun)
Observable events or facts, especially ones that are remarkable or difficult to explain. (Singular: phenomenon.)
From the story: Colin tracked the phenomena of his healing like a scientist — measuring, counting, noting every change.
Think about it: Colin calls his healing a 'scientific experiment.' But can science fully explain what is happening? Is there something beyond data and measurements at work here?

reciprocal  (adjective)
Given and received in return. A two-way exchange where both sides benefit equally.
From the story: The relationship between the children and the garden was reciprocal — they healed the garden, and the garden healed them.
Think about it: What does a reciprocal relationship look like? How is it different from a one-way relationship where only one side gives?

Chapter 18: Ben Weatherstaff

grief  (noun)
Deep sadness caused by loss, especially the death of someone loved.
From the story: Ben said: 'You can't lock away grief. It just grows underground, in the dark, getting wilder and more tangled.'
Think about it: Mr. Craven tried to lock away his grief by locking the garden. Did it work? What happens when we try to avoid grief instead of moving through it?

resilience  (noun)
The ability to recover from difficulty. Bouncing back after being knocked down.
From the story: The roses showed extraordinary resilience — surviving ten years without care, waiting patiently for someone to tend them again.
Think about it: Resilience does not mean being unaffected by hardship. It means surviving it. What characters in this story show resilience? What about the garden itself?

Chapter 19: Walking

determination  (noun)
The quality of being firmly committed to a goal. Refusing to give up no matter what.
From the story: Colin's determination was fierce — he would walk to the moor and back, no matter how much his legs trembled.
Think about it: Determination got Colin from a bed to the moor. What other characters in this story show determination? Is determination a quality you are born with or one you develop?

parallel  (adjective/noun)
Things that happen at the same time in similar ways. Two stories running side by side.
From the story: Mary's transformation and Colin's ran in parallel — both started broken, both were healed by nature, friendship, and purpose.
Think about it: How are Mary and Colin's parallel journeys similar? How are they different? Why did the author choose to tell both stories?

Chapter 20: Dickon's Creatures

harmony  (noun)
A state of peaceful agreement and cooperation. Things working together naturally.
From the story: Dickon lived in harmony with the natural world — he did not try to control it, only to understand and appreciate it.
Think about it: Harmony means working WITH something, not against it. How does Dickon's approach to animals and nature contrast with Frollo's approach to people in Hunchback, or Frollo's approach to Quasimodo?

wisdom  (noun)
The quality of having experience, knowledge, and good judgment. Deep understanding of how the world works.
From the story: Mary told Dickon he was the wisest person she knew. He said: 'I'm not wise. I'm just paying attention.'
Think about it: Dickon says wisdom is just paying attention. Do you agree? Is the wisest person the one who knows the most facts, or the one who notices the most about the world around them?

Chapter 21: The Experiment

hypothesis  (noun)
A proposed explanation that can be tested. An educated guess about how something works.
From the story: Colin's hypothesis was that fresh air, exercise, food, friendship, and belief could heal someone who had been ill for ten years.
Think about it: A hypothesis must be tested. Colin tested his by living it. What were the results? Is his experiment convincing?

holistic  (adjective)
Relating to the whole of something rather than just its parts. Treating the entire person, not just one symptom.
From the story: Colin was not healed by any single thing but by everything together — air, sun, food, friends, garden, and belief. The healing was holistic.
Think about it: Modern medicine often treats symptoms one at a time. Colin's healing addresses his whole self — body, mind, and spirit. Which approach is more effective? Can they work together?

Chapter 22: Stronger Every Day

metamorphosis  (noun)
A dramatic change in form or character. A complete transformation, like a caterpillar becoming a butterfly.
From the story: The metamorphosis was complete — the sour girl and the invalid boy had become healthy, joyful children.
Think about it: A metamorphosis is not just change — it is becoming something entirely new. What did Mary and Colin become that they never could have been without the garden?

anticipation  (noun)
The feeling of excitement about something that is going to happen. Looking forward to a future event.
From the story: The children lived in anticipation of Mr. Craven's return — the moment when the secret would finally be revealed.
Think about it: Why is anticipation sometimes more powerful than the event itself? How does the waiting build tension in the story?

Chapter 23: The Letter

exile (self-imposed)  (noun)
Choosing to stay away from your home or loved ones, often because of guilt or grief.
From the story: Mr. Craven's travels were a self-imposed exile — he was running not from danger but from his own sadness.
Think about it: Mr. Craven chose to leave. How is self-imposed exile different from being forced out? Is it harder to return when you are the one who left?

beckoned  (verb)
Made a gesture or signal to encourage someone to come closer. Called someone toward you.
From the story: Something in Susan Sowerby's letter beckoned Craven home — it pierced his grief and reminded him he was still a father.
Think about it: What words in the letter are powerful enough to reach a man who has been running for ten years? Why does 'your son needs you the way a garden needs the sun' break through when nothing else has?

Chapter 24: In the Garden

reconciliation  (noun)
The restoration of a relationship after a period of separation or conflict. Coming back together.
From the story: The reunion in the garden was a reconciliation — not just between father and son, but between Mr. Craven and his own grief.
Think about it: Reconciliation requires vulnerability. Mr. Craven must face everything he ran from — his son, the garden, his wife's memory. Why is this the hardest and most important thing he has ever done?

renewal  (noun)
The process of being made new again. Starting fresh after a period of decay or loss.
From the story: The garden's renewal was complete — roses covered every wall, bees filled the air, and life had returned to a place that was once sealed and forgotten.
Think about it: The entire story is about renewal — of the garden, of Mary, of Colin, of Mr. Craven. What is the author's ultimate message about the human capacity for renewal?
