THE ODYSSEY
A BedrockELA Adaptation
Based on the epic poem by Homer (c. 8th century BC)
Adapted for 6th Grade Readers

CHAPTER 1
THE FALL OF TROY
For ten long years, the Greeks had fought to capture the city of Troy.
The war had begun because of a woman — Helen, the most beautiful woman in the world, wife of the Greek king Menelaus. She had been taken to Troy by the Trojan prince Paris, and every Greek king and warrior had sailed across the sea to bring her back. Achilles, the greatest fighter alive. Ajax, the mountain of a man. And Odysseus, king of Ithaca — not the strongest, not the fastest, but the cleverest of them all.
It was Odysseus who ended the war.
After ten years of siege, when brute force had failed, Odysseus devised a plan. He ordered the Greeks to build an enormous wooden horse — large enough to hold thirty warriors in its hollow belly. The Greeks left the horse at the gates of Troy and pretended to sail away. The Trojans, believing the war was over, dragged the horse inside their walls as a trophy.
That night, while Troy slept, Odysseus and his men climbed out of the horse, opened the gates, and let the Greek army pour in.
Troy burned.
When the smoke cleared, Odysseus stood on the ruined walls and looked west, toward Ithaca — his small, rocky island kingdom. His wife, Penelope, was waiting there. His son, Telemachus, whom he had left as an infant, was now ten years old.
"We go home," Odysseus told his men. He had twelve ships and over six hundred men.
None of them knew that the journey home would take another ten years. None of them knew that most of them would never see home at all.

CHAPTER 2
THE CICONES
The first stop was a disaster.
Odysseus and his fleet landed at Ismarus, the city of the Cicones, allies of Troy. His men raided the city, took the treasure, and celebrated on the beach.
Odysseus tried to stop them. "Take what we need and leave. We're going home."
His men didn't listen. They feasted, drinking wine and roasting cattle.
At dawn, the Cicones returned with reinforcements. Six men from each ship were killed before they could push off. Seventy-two men, dead in a single morning — not to monsters or gods, but to greed and disobedience.
They sailed south into a storm that blew them far off course.

CHAPTER 3
THE LOTUS EATERS
The storm raged for nine days. When it died, Odysseus found himself off an island with a sweet smell drifting from the interior. He sent three men ashore. They didn't come back.
He found them eating the flowers of the lotus plant, surrounded by people with dreamy eyes. The lotus erased memory — all desire to go home, all ambition, all purpose. The men who ate them forgot who they were.
Odysseus didn't eat. He dragged his men back to the ships by force and ordered the fleet to leave immediately.
The lotus was the temptation to give up — a death not of the body but of the spirit.

CHAPTER 4
THE CYCLOPS
The next island seemed like a gift — wild and fertile, with fat goats and enormous caves. Odysseus took twelve men and a skin of fine wine to explore.
They found a cave filled with cheeses and milk. The owner came home at dusk: Polyphemus, a one-eyed Cyclops, son of Poseidon. He rolled a boulder across the entrance, trapping them inside. Then he grabbed two men, smashed them against the floor, and ate them.
Odysseus couldn't kill the sleeping giant — the boulder was too massive for the men to move. So he made a plan. He offered the Cyclops his powerful wine and told him his name was "Nobody." When Polyphemus passed out drunk, they drove a sharpened stake into his eye.
Other Cyclopes came running. "Who is hurting you?" — "Nobody is hurting me!" They shrugged and left.
In the morning, Odysseus's men escaped by clinging to the undersides of the giant's sheep.

CHAPTER 5
THE CURSE OF POSEIDON
They should have left in silence.
But Odysseus was proud. As his ship pulled away, he shouted: "Cyclops! It was Odysseus, king of Ithaca, who blinded you!"
Polyphemus raised his arms to the sky. "Father Poseidon! Let Odysseus never reach home. And if he does, let him arrive late, alone, with all his companions dead, and let him find trouble in his house."
The sea trembled. Poseidon heard his son's prayer — and answered it.
This was Odysseus's great flaw: he needed the world to know how clever he was. His pride — the Greeks called it hubris — would cost him more than any monster ever could.

CHAPTER 6
AEOLUS AND THE BAG OF WINDS
Aeolus, keeper of the winds, gave Odysseus a leather bag containing all the unfavorable winds. With the bad winds imprisoned, the gentle west wind carried them straight toward Ithaca.
On the tenth day, Odysseus could see home — the rocky cliffs, the smoke from chimneys. He wept with joy. Then he fell asleep.
His men, convinced the bag held gold, untied it. Every wind erupted at once. The storm hurled them backward across the ocean. Ithaca vanished.
They returned to Aeolus and begged for help. "The gods must hate you," he said. "I won't help a man the gods have cursed."

CHAPTER 7
THE LAESTRYGONIANS
Odysseus's fleet entered a beautiful harbor surrounded by cliffs. All eleven remaining ships sailed in — except Odysseus's, which he anchored outside. A precaution that saved his life.
The Laestrygonians were giant cannibals. They attacked from the cliffs, hurling boulders onto the trapped ships. Not a single man from those eleven ships survived.
Odysseus entered the harbor with twelve ships and over five hundred men. He left with one ship and forty-five men.

CHAPTER 8
CIRCE
On the island of Aeaea, the goddess Circe invited Odysseus's men inside her palace, fed them a feast, and turned them to pigs with a touch of her wand.
Odysseus went to confront her alone. The god Hermes gave him a magical herb called moly that blocked her spell. When Circe's wand had no effect, she was stunned.
Odysseus drew his sword and made her swear to restore his men. She did, and became an ally — hosting the Greeks for a year and preparing them for what lay ahead.

CHAPTER 9
THE LAND OF THE DEAD
Circe told Odysseus he must visit the underworld and speak with the prophet Tiresias. At the edge of the world, Odysseus dug a trench, filled it with sacrificial blood, and waited. The dead came — pale, flickering shades.
Tiresias warned him: do not harm the sacred cattle of Helios on the island of Thrinacia. "If you touch those cattle, your ship will be destroyed and your men will die."
But the spirit who shook Odysseus most was his mother, Anticlea. She had died of grief while he was gone. He tried to embrace her — his arms passed through her shadow like smoke.
He understood the full cost of his journey: not just the men he had lost, but the years of his life, spent far from everyone he loved.

CHAPTER 10
THE SIRENS
The Sirens sang a song so beautiful that any man who heard it would steer toward the rocks and die. Odysseus ordered his men to seal their ears with beeswax and bind him to the mast.
The song was not just music — it was knowledge. The Sirens sang of everything and called him by name. Odysseus screamed, thrashed, and begged to be released. His deaf men rowed harder and tied him tighter.
He had heard the Sirens' song and survived. But he would hear it in his dreams for the rest of his life.

CHAPTER 11
SCYLLA AND CHARYBDIS
On one side of a narrow strait was Charybdis — a whirlpool that could swallow a ship. On the other was Scylla — a six-headed monster who would snatch six men from any ship that passed.
"Choose," Circe had said. "Lose six men, or lose everyone."
Odysseus chose Scylla. He told no one. Six heads shot down from the cave. Six men were seized and pulled screaming into the darkness.
Odysseus stood at the stern, looking back. He had made the right choice. But "the right choice" meant nothing when you could still hear your friends screaming.
This, he thought, is what leadership costs.

CHAPTER 12
THE CATTLE OF THE SUN
On the island of Thrinacia, Helios kept his sacred cattle. Odysseus wanted to sail past, but his starving men forced a landing. He made them swear not to touch the cattle.
A month-long storm trapped them. Their food ran out. While Odysseus prayed alone, Eurylochus convinced the men to slaughter the cattle. "Better to die with full bellies."
When Zeus hurled his thunderbolt, the ship split apart. Every man who had eaten the sacred cattle drowned. Odysseus alone survived, clinging to a piece of the mast for nine days, until the current carried him to Calypso's island — where he would be held prisoner for seven years.

CHAPTER 13
THE CATTLE OF THE SUN
Tiresias had warned him. Circe had warned him. Odysseus himself warned his men as the island of Thrinacia appeared on the horizon:
"We must not stop on this island. The cattle that graze there belong to Helios, the sun god. If we harm even one, we will die."
But the men were exhausted and hungry. Eurylochus spoke for the crew: "You ask too much. We need rest. One night on the beach. We won't touch the cattle."
Odysseus relented. One night became a month. Adverse winds trapped them on the island. Their food ran out. Hunger gnawed at them.
Odysseus went inland to pray, and while he was gone, the men broke. Eurylochus made the argument: "We are dying. The cattle are right there. If the gods want to kill us for eating, at least we'll die with full stomachs."
They slaughtered the finest cattle of the sun god.
When Odysseus returned and saw the fire, smelled the meat, he knew they were dead.
Helios demanded justice from Zeus. They sailed from the island with full stomachs and doomed souls. Within hours, Zeus sent a thunderbolt that shattered the ship. Every man was thrown into the sea. Every man drowned.
Except Odysseus.
He clung to the wreckage — the keel and a piece of the mast — and floated alone on the open sea. Every ship, every man, every friend he had led from Troy was gone. The prophecy of Tiresias had come true.
Odysseus was alone.

CHAPTER 14
CALYPSO'S ISLAND
The sea carried him to Ogygia, home of the nymph Calypso — beautiful, immortal, and utterly alone.
Calypso found Odysseus half-dead on her beach and nursed him back to health. She offered him something no mortal had ever been offered: immortality. She would make him a god. He would never grow old, never suffer, never die.
Seven years. Odysseus stayed on Calypso's island for seven years.
But he never accepted her offer. Every day, he walked to the rocky shore and stared east — toward Ithaca, toward Penelope.
"Why?" Calypso asked. "I am more beautiful than your wife. I am offering you eternal life."
Odysseus answered: "You are beautiful. This island is paradise. But Penelope is my home. Ithaca is my home. I would rather live one mortal life with the people I love than live forever in a place where I don't belong."
On Mount Olympus, the goddess Athena pleaded with Zeus to end Odysseus's suffering. Zeus agreed and sent Hermes to Calypso with a command: release Odysseus.
Calypso wept, but she obeyed. She helped Odysseus build a raft and gave him food, water, and directions.
He sailed east. Toward home.

CHAPTER 15
THE RAFT
For seventeen days, Odysseus sailed alone across the open sea, navigating by the stars. On the eighteenth day, he saw land — the mountains of Phaeacia.
And then Poseidon saw him.
The god of the sea had been away and had not noticed Odysseus's departure. Now, returning across the ocean, Poseidon spotted the tiny raft.
"No," Poseidon said. "Not yet."
He raised his hand. The sea obeyed.

CHAPTER 16
POSEIDON'S STORM
The storm was not natural. It was divine rage made physical — waves as tall as mountains, wind that ripped the air itself.
The raft came apart. Odysseus was thrown into the water, dragged under, spun by currents designed to destroy him. After twenty years of surviving everything, he was going to drown within sight of land.
A sea goddess named Ino took pity on him. She gave him her immortal veil. "Wrap this around your body. It will keep you afloat. Swim for the shore."
Odysseus stripped off his heavy clothes and swam. For two days and two nights, he fought currents and waves, his arms burning, his legs numb.
On the third morning, he reached the coast of Phaeacia. But the shore was rocky — sheer cliffs battered by surf. He swam along the coast until he found a river mouth with calm water. He dragged himself onto the bank, crawled into a thicket of olive trees, covered himself with leaves, and collapsed.
He had survived. Again. Barely.

CHAPTER 17
NAUSICAA
He was woken by laughter.
Young women were playing on the riverbank, washing clothes and tossing a ball. At their center was a girl — perhaps sixteen — with the confidence of someone who had never had reason to doubt herself. Princess Nausicaa, daughter of King Alcinous.
Odysseus was a mess — naked, salt-crusted, covered in scratches, with leaves in his hair. But he was Odysseus, and even at his lowest, he knew how to speak.
He broke off an olive branch to cover himself and addressed Nausicaa with the most graceful words he could summon:
"My lady, I do not know if you are a goddess or a mortal. If you are a goddess, you must be Artemis. If you are mortal, then your parents are the most fortunate people alive."
The other girls ran. Nausicaa held her ground.
"You are a stranger. Shipwrecked?"
"Twenty years shipwrecked," Odysseus said. "I am trying to get home."
She gave him clothes, food, and directions to her father's palace.
"Go to my mother first," she advised. "Queen Arete. If she takes your side, my father will help you. She is the real power in this house."
Odysseus smiled — the first real smile in a very long time.

CHAPTER 18
THE PHAEACIAN COURT
The palace of King Alcinous was magnificent. Odysseus entered as a stranger, knelt before Queen Arete, and asked for help returning home.
The Phaeacians held a feast in his honor. A blind poet named Demodocus sang tales of the Trojan War — and when the poet sang of the wooden horse, Odysseus covered his face with his cloak and wept.
Only King Alcinous noticed. He stopped the music.
"Stranger, you weep at the tale of Troy. Who are you?"
Odysseus stood. He pulled back his cloak.
"I am Odysseus, son of Laertes, king of Ithaca. And this is my story."
He told them everything — from Troy to this moment. He spoke for hours, and the Phaeacian court listened in absolute silence.
When he finished, King Alcinous rose and said: "Tomorrow, we take you home."

CHAPTER 19
HOME AT LAST
The Phaeacian ship sailed through the night. By dawn, the rocky shores of Ithaca rose from the sea. They laid Odysseus on the beach while he slept and sailed away.
Odysseus woke on his own shore. Twenty years. The beach looked the same, but he was different.
The goddess Athena appeared and warned him: "Your palace is overrun. More than a hundred suitors have moved in. They eat your food, drink your wine, and each one wants to marry Penelope. They will kill you if they find out who you are."
"Then they won't find out," Odysseus said.
Athena disguised him as an elderly beggar — stooped, wrinkled, unrecognizable.
"Go to Eumaeus, your old swineherd," she said. "He is still loyal."
Odysseus walked up the familiar path from the beach, disguised as a beggar in his own kingdom.
He was home. And the hardest part was still to come.

CHAPTER 20
THE SWINEHERD
Eumaeus didn't recognize the beggar, but he welcomed him anyway — because he was a good man and hospitality was a sacred duty.
Over roast pork and barley bread, the old swineherd told Odysseus everything: the suitors, their greed, Penelope's resistance, Telemachus's danger.
"My master's son is a good young man," Eumaeus said. "But he's outnumbered. These suitors have even plotted to kill him."
Then Telemachus himself appeared at the door — tall, strong, with Odysseus's eyes and Penelope's dark hair. Athena had guided him here.
When Eumaeus left to carry news to Penelope, Athena touched Odysseus. The disguise melted away. Telemachus found himself staring at his father.
"Father?" he whispered.
Odysseus opened his arms.
They held each other and wept. Twenty years of absence compressed into a single embrace in a swineherd's hut.
Then they began to plan.

CHAPTER 21
THE BEGGAR IN THE HALL
Odysseus entered his own palace in the beggar's disguise. He sat on the floor of the great hall while the suitors feasted around him.
They mocked him. Antinous, the cruelest suitor, threw a footstool at him. Odysseus said nothing. He did not flinch.
He watched everything. He noted which suitors were most dangerous. He counted their weapons. He studied the hall like a general surveying a battlefield.
Penelope, watching from the upper balcony, felt something stir when she saw the beggar — not recognition, but a strange pull.
She summoned him to her chamber and asked if he had heard any news of Odysseus.
"Your husband is alive," the beggar said. "He is very close to home."
She didn't recognize him. But she felt the pull again.
"Tomorrow," Penelope said, "I will set a contest. The suitor who can string my husband's great bow and shoot an arrow through twelve axe heads will be my husband."
The beggar smiled. "I believe the contest will end well."

CHAPTER 22
THE BOW
Penelope brought the bow — an enormous weapon of olive wood, horn, and sinew. No one had been able to string it since Odysseus left.
The suitors tried. One by one, they failed. The strongest couldn't bend it. Antinous tried three times, his face red with effort.
"Let the beggar try," Odysseus said quietly.
The suitors laughed. But Telemachus said: "Let him try."
Odysseus took the bow. He turned it in his hands, testing the flex, feeling the grain. Then, in one smooth motion, he strung it. The string sang — a clear, high note that rang through the silent hall.
He nocked an arrow, drew the bow to its full extension, and released. The arrow flew the length of the hall and passed through all twelve axe heads.
Silence.
The beggar's disguise fell away.

CHAPTER 23
THE BATTLE
"Now," Odysseus said, "I try another target."
His second arrow struck Antinous in the throat.
The suitors leaped to their feet, reaching for swords that weren't there — Telemachus had removed their weapons the night before.
Odysseus stood at the threshold, bow in hand. Telemachus stood beside him with a sword. Eumaeus and another loyal servant flanked them with spears.
Four men against more than a hundred. But the four had weapons. The hundred did not.
Odysseus fired arrow after arrow, each finding its mark. The battle lasted hours. The great hall ran with blood.
When it was over, every suitor was dead.
Odysseus stood in the center of his hall, breathing hard. His home was his again.

CHAPTER 24
REUNION
Penelope did not come running.
Twenty years is a long time. She had been deceived before. She would not be deceived again.
She came down the stairs and sat across from the man who claimed to be her husband. She studied him.
"Move his bed," she said to a servant. "Bring the great bed into the hall so this man can sleep."
Odysseus's reaction was immediate.
"Move the bed? No one can move that bed! I built it myself, around the trunk of a living olive tree. The tree is the bedpost. The bed cannot be moved without cutting down the tree. Who has damaged my bed?"
Penelope's face broke open. Tears streamed down her cheeks. She crossed the room and threw her arms around him.
"It is you. Only Odysseus knows about the olive tree."
The bed was their secret — known only to the two of them. No spy, no impostor, no god in disguise could have known it.
They held each other for a long time. Odysseus told her everything — the war, the journey, the monsters and the gods. Penelope told him her story — the suitors, the weaving trick, the years of holding them off with nothing but her mind and her will.
They were two people who had refused to give up — on each other, on the idea that love was worth a twenty-year fight, on the belief that home was more than a place.
Athena held back the dawn, giving them the longest night in the world. And in the olive-tree bed, built by Odysseus's own hands, the hero came home at last.
The Odyssey was over.
THE END
