📖 The Road Through the Forest - Part 1

Dorothy and the Scarecrow walked together along the yellow brick road, and the Scarecrow told Dorothy everything he knew about the world — which was not very much, since he had only been alive for two days.

"I do not mind the walking," the Scarecrow said cheerfully. "My feet do not get tired, since they are stuffed with straw. And the road is interesting, even if I do not understand most of what I see."

"What don't you understand?" Dorothy asked.

"Well, for example — why is the sky blue? And why does water flow downhill instead of up? And what is that bright thing in the sky?"

"That is the sun," Dorothy said, smiling.

"It is very warm," the Scarecrow observed. "I like it. But I am told it is also the thing that sets fire to straw, which makes me nervous."

Dorothy laughed. She was beginning to think the Scarecrow was better company than most people she knew in Kansas. He asked strange questions, but he listened carefully to the answers, and he was always kind and polite.

They passed through Munchkin country, where everything was painted blue. The Munchkin farmers waved at them from their fields. Small blue birds sang in the trees. Dorothy's spirits lifted.

📖 The Road Through the Forest - Part 2

But as the afternoon wore on, the road led them out of the gentle farmland and into a thick, dark forest. The trees grew so close together that their branches tangled overhead, blocking out the sunlight. The cheerful blue fences disappeared, replaced by twisted roots and tangled undergrowth. The bird songs faded. The only sounds were their own footsteps and the creak of branches.

"This forest is very dark," the Scarecrow said. He did not sound frightened — the Scarecrow claimed he was never frightened, since fear required brains and he had none. But he moved a little closer to Dorothy.

"Are there wild animals in this forest?" Dorothy asked.

"I suppose there must be," the Scarecrow said. "But I have never been in a forest before, so I do not know what kinds."

The road was rougher here. Bricks were missing or broken, and roots pushed up through the gaps. The Scarecrow, who was still unsteady on his feet, stumbled every few steps. Each time he fell, Dorothy helped him up, and each time, he thanked her and kept going without complaint.

"Does it hurt when you fall?" Dorothy asked.

"Not at all," the Scarecrow said. "I cannot feel pain. That is one advantage of being stuffed with straw. But I do not like falling. It makes me feel clumsy and foolish, and I would very much like to be neither of those things."

"You are not foolish," Dorothy said firmly.

📖 The Road Through the Forest - Part 3

"That is kind of you to say. But a man without brains cannot be sure of anything, least of all himself."

They walked deeper into the forest. The darkness pressed in around them. Then, through the trees, Dorothy heard a sound — a faint, groaning cry, like someone in terrible pain.

"Did you hear that?" she whispered.

"Yes," the Scarecrow whispered back. "It came from over there."

They pushed through the undergrowth, following the sound. The groaning grew louder. And then, in a small clearing just off the road, they saw him.

A man made entirely of tin.

He stood beside a great tree, his axe raised above his head, frozen mid-swing. His body was dull with rust, and he could not move a single joint. His mouth was rusted shut, but through his clenched tin jaw came that terrible groaning — the sound of someone who had been trapped in the same position for a very, very long time.

"Oh my!" Dorothy cried. "We have to help him!"

✏️ Prefixes and Suffixes

Prefixes come before a word (un-, re-). Suffixes come after (- ly, -ful). They change the meaning.

Examples:

  • un + happy = unhappy (not happy)
  • care + ful = careful (full of care)
  • re + build = rebuild (build again)

🌍 Informational Text: Life on the Prairie: The 1890s

When "The Wonderful Wizard of Oz" was published in 1900, life on the American prairie was vastly different from today. Imagine no electricity, no phones, no cars, no internet. That was everyday life for families like Dorothy's.

Children in the 1890s rarely went to school past 8th grade. They were needed on the farm to help with chores. Girls like Dorothy would cook, clean, sew clothes, tend gardens, feed chickens, and help with canning food for winter. Boys plowed fields, cared for animals, repaired equipment, and helped with harvest.

Entertainment was simple. Families read books by lamplight, played board games, sang songs, and told stories. Going to town was a special event that might happen once a month. There were no movie theaters or theme parks — people made their own fun.

Clothing was practical and homemade. Women and girls wore long dresses and aprons. Everything was sewn by hand or on foot-powered sewing machines. Shoes were expensive and saved for special occasions. Many children went barefoot in summer.

Food came from what you grew or raised. Families ate a lot of bread, potatoes, beans, and whatever vegetables grew in their garden. Meat came from chickens, pigs, or cows raised on the farm. Ice cream and candy were rare treats.

Medicine was basic. There were no antibiotics, no vaccines, no hospitals nearby. People relied on home remedies and hoped for the best. A simple infection could become life-threatening.

Yet despite the hardships, prairie families formed strong communities. Neighbors helped each other during harvest, illness, or disaster. People valued hard work, honesty, and loyalty. These values — the same ones Dorothy demonstrates in Oz — were essential for survival on the prairie.

Understanding this context helps us appreciate why Dorothy is so desperate to return home. Home wasn't just a place — it was Aunt Em and Uncle Henry, the only family she had in a hard but familiar world.
