📖 Quasimodo's Defense - Part 2

The first soldiers to reach the doors found them barred from inside — Quasimodo had wedged heavy oak beams across every entrance. They battered at the wood with a battering ram, and the great doors shuddered but held. Meanwhile, Quasimodo climbed. He hauled stones from the tower restoration — massive blocks of limestone that three men could not lift. Quasimodo carried them alone, one in each arm, climbing the stairs at a run. He reached the gallery, leaned over the railing, and hurled the stones down at the battering ram below. The first stone smashed the ram to splinters. The soldiers scattered. They regrouped and tried again. Quasimodo dropped more stones. When the stones ran out, he poured water — boiling water, heated in the great copper pots from the cathedral kitchen. The soldiers screamed and fell back. Quasimodo was everywhere at once. He raced from tower to tower, from gallery to gallery, raining destruction down on the invaders with the desperate energy of a man protecting the only thing he had ever loved. He was not fighting for Frollo. He was not fighting for the church. He was fighting for Esmeralda — for the girl who had given him water, who had called him good, who had taught him that the world contained kindness as well as cruelty. The soldiers tried to scale the walls. Quasimodo pushed their ladders away. They tried to climb the buttresses. He dropped stones on their hands. They tried to force a side door. He appeared above it instantly, as though the cathedral had told him where to go. For three hours, one man held off fifty soldiers. The people of Paris gathered in the surrounding streets and watched in astonishment. They had thought Quasimodo was a monster — a misshapen creature, barely human, good for nothing but ringing bells. Now they saw something different. They saw courage. They saw love. They saw a man willing to destroy himself to protect someone who had shown him a moment of kindness. The soldiers fell back at last, exhausted and humiliated. The cathedral stood. The doors held. Quasimodo stood on the gallery, bleeding from a dozen cuts, his clothes torn, his chest heaving — but upright. Unbroken. Victorious. Below, in the tower room, Esmeralda pressed her hands against the stone wall and listened to the silence that followed the battle. He had kept his promise.

📚 Informational Text

The Symbolism of the Cathedral

In literature, a symbol is something that represents something else. Notre-Dame Cathedral is the central symbol in Hugo's novel. On one level, it's just a building — stone, wood, glass, and metal. But symbolically, it represents many things: permanence in a changing world, sanctuary and safety, beauty created by human hands, the soul of Paris itself. The cathedral shelters Quasimodo and later Esmeralda. It stands while people come and go, live and die. Hugo wrote the novel partly to save the real Notre-Dame from being destroyed. By making it the heart of his story, he reminded readers that some things — beauty, history, compassion — are worth preserving.
