Today's Words

1. bastion

Definition: A fortified position or stronghold, especially one that projects outward from a wall or defensive line.

"The ruined bastion sat in no-man's-land, exposed to cannon fire from both sides — the most dangerous place to eat breakfast in France."

2. audacity

Definition: Bold, daring behavior that surprises people, sometimes bordering on recklessness.

"The audacity of eating a casual meal under enemy fire made the breakfast in the bastion the most famous meal of the siege."

📖 The Breakfast in the Bastion - Part 2

Halfway through the siege, D'Artagnan needed to tell his friends something important — something he had discovered about Milady and the Cardinal's plans. But spies were everywhere, and every conversation in the camp was overheard by someone. "We need a private place to talk," D'Artagnan said. "Somewhere no one can listen." Athos looked out across the battlefield. Between the king's camp and the walls of La Rochelle stood a ruined bastion — a small stone fort that had been destroyed by cannon fire. It sat in no-man's-land, exposed to fire from both sides. No one went near it. "There," Athos said, pointing to the bastion with a slight smile. "We can talk there." "That is insane," Porthos said. "That bastion is in range of the city's guns. We could be killed." "Which is exactly why no one will be listening," Athos said. "Bring food. We will have breakfast." The idea was absurd. Having a casual breakfast in the middle of a war zone, in a bombed-out fort that could be shelled at any moment — it was the kind of thing only a madman would suggest. Or a musketeer. At dawn, the four friends carried a basket of bread, cheese, ham, and wine across the open ground to the ruined bastion. Soldiers in the camp watched them go with open mouths. Officers shouted at them to come back. They ignored everyone. Inside the bastion, they spread a cloth on a broken wall and laid out their breakfast. Cannonballs whistled overhead. Musket shots cracked from the city walls. Chunks of stone flew from the bastion's crumbling walls. Athos poured wine as calmly as if they were in a Paris tavern. "Now," he said. "What did you want to tell us?" "The Cardinal has sent Milady to England," D'Artagnan said, tearing off a piece of bread. "I overheard his agents talking. She has orders to find Buckingham and —" A cannonball hit the bastion twenty feet away. Stone fragments rained down. Porthos brushed dust off his ham. "Continue," Aramis said, refilling his cup. "— and deliver a message threatening him. If Buckingham does not withdraw his support for La Rochelle, the Cardinal will expose secrets that could ruin him." "Milady is the perfect weapon for this," Athos said grimly. "She is charming enough to get close and ruthless enough to follow through." "We must warn Buckingham," Aramis said. "More than that," D'Artagnan said. "Milady also has orders regarding Constance. The Cardinal knows that Constance helped us with the diamond studs. She is in danger." The mood at the table changed. This was no longer just politics and power games. This was personal. "We must protect Constance," D'Artagnan said. "And we must stop Milady." "Agreed," Athos said. There was steel in his voice — the kind of steel that comes from a man confronting something he has been running from for years. "When the siege allows it, we ride." Another cannonball shattered what remained of the bastion's north wall. The four friends finished their breakfast, packed up the cloth, and walked calmly back across no-man's-land to the camp. The entire army had been watching. When the four friends strolled back into the trenches, unhurried and unharmed, the soldiers erupted in cheers. The breakfast in the bastion became the most famous meal of the siege.

📝 Comprehension Questions

1. Why do the four friends choose to eat breakfast in a bombed-out bastion?

2. The breakfast in the bastion is both terrifying and hilarious — cannonballs flying while they calmly eat cheese and pour wine. Why do you think Dumas wrote this scene as comedy in the middle of a war story? What does humor do for the characters and for the reader?

📚 Informational Text

Food and Drink in 17th Century France

Food in the 1600s was very different from today. Rich people ate elaborate meals with many courses: soup, roasted meat, pies, vegetables, and sweets. They drank wine at almost every meal because water was often unsafe to drink. Spices like cinnamon and nutmeg were expensive and showed wealth. Poor people ate simpler food: bread, cheese, porridge, and occasionally meat. There was no refrigeration, so food spoiled quickly. People preserved meat by salting or smoking it. In The Three Musketeers, the characters often meet in taverns where they eat, drink, and make plans.
