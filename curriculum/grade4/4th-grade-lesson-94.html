📖 The Locked Garden - Part 2

It was during one of these walks that Mary discovered the gardens. Misselthwaite Manor had several gardens, separated by high brick walls and connected by doorways and paths. There was a kitchen garden with rows of brown earth waiting for spring planting. There was a formal garden with neat hedges and stone benches. There was an orchard where bare apple trees stood in rows like soldiers. But one garden had no door. Mary noticed it on her third day of exploring. A long wall of ivy-covered brick, taller than the others, running along the far edge of the grounds. She walked beside it, trailing her fingers along the rough stone, looking for an entrance. There was none. No door, no gate, no gap in the wall. Just solid brick, covered in a thick curtain of dead ivy. "What's behind that wall?" Mary asked the old gardener, Ben Weatherstaff, whom she found digging in the kitchen garden. Ben was a crusty old man with a weather-beaten face and a sharp tongue. He did not seem to like children any more than Mary liked adults, which made them, in a strange way, well-matched. "That's the garden that's been locked up," Ben said. "Ten years now." "Why was it locked?" Ben leaned on his spade. "It belonged to Mrs. Craven — Mr. Craven's wife. She loved that garden more than anything. She made it beautiful — roses everywhere, hundreds of roses. But she had an accident there. She fell from an old tree and was badly hurt, and she died not long after. Mr. Craven was half mad with grief. He locked the garden, buried the key, and swore no one would ever go inside again." "He buried the key?" Mary said. "Aye. Buried it in the earth, so even he couldn't be tempted to go back. He can't bear to look at anything she loved. That's why he travels all the time — he's running from his own house." Mary stared at the wall. Behind it was a garden that had been closed for ten years — untended, unseen, forgotten. A garden full of roses that might be dead or might be alive. A secret place that no one was allowed to enter. Mary had never wanted anything in her life. She had been given everything without asking and had cared about none of it. But standing before that ivy-covered wall, she felt something unfamiliar — a pull, a need, a burning curiosity that was not quite a wish and not quite a plan but something in between. She wanted to find that garden. She wanted to find it more than she had ever wanted anything. "Is the garden dead?" she asked. Ben Weatherstaff shrugged. "Don't know. Haven't been inside in ten years, have I? Could be dead. Could be alive. Roses are tough things. They don't give up easy." Mary looked at the wall one more time. Then she turned and walked back toward the house, her mind racing and her heart — for the first time in her life — beating with something that felt very much like purpose.

📚 Informational Text

Yorkshire Moors: England's Wild Landscape

Misselthwaite Manor sits on the Yorkshire moors—vast, open landscapes covered in heather, grass, and rocky hills. The moors can be beautiful but also harsh and lonely. In winter, they're cold, windy, and gray. In spring and summer, purple heather blooms and birds sing. The weather changes quickly—sunshine can turn to rain in minutes. Few people live on the moors, making them feel isolated and wild. This landscape shapes the story: Mary arrives in winter when everything is dead and gray, matching her own lonely, sour mood. As spring comes and the garden awakens, Mary begins to change too. The moors teach important lessons about resilience—plants and animals survive harsh conditions by being tough and adaptable. The story asks: can Mary learn to bloom like the flowers after a long, cold winter?

Check Your Understanding:

1. What are the Yorkshire moors?

2. How does the landscape change from winter to spring?

3. How does the moor landscape reflect Mary's emotional journey?
