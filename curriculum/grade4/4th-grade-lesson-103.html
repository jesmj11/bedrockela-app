Today's Words

1. tyrant

Definition: A person who uses their power in a cruel and controlling way. Someone who demands obedience through fear.

"Colin was part tyrant, part prisoner — he ordered servants around like an emperor, but he was also trapped in his dark room."

2. platitude

Definition: A remark or statement that has been used so many times it has no real meaning. Empty, comforting words.

"The doctor spoke in soothing platitudes — saying everything would be fine without actually doing anything to make it true."

📖 Colin - Part 2

Mary visited Colin the next night, and the night after that, and every night for a week. She learned his story in pieces. Colin had been born the same night his mother died — she had been injured in the secret garden and never recovered. Mr. Craven, destroyed by grief, could not bear to look at the son who reminded him of everything he had lost. He left the house and traveled the world, running from his own sorrow. Colin was left in the care of servants and a doctor who treated him as a permanent invalid. He was kept in bed. He was never taken outside. He was told, over and over, that he was fragile, that his back was weak, that exertion would kill him. He was wrapped in blankets and fed medicines and surrounded by adults who were so afraid of his father's anger that they let the boy believe he was dying rather than risk doing anything different. The result was a child who was part tyrant, part prisoner. Colin ordered servants around with the fury of a tiny emperor. He threw tantrums that shook the walls. He screamed when he did not get what he wanted, and he got everything he wanted because the servants were terrified of what would happen if he cried and Mr. Craven heard about it. But underneath the tantrums and the self-pity, Colin was desperately lonely. He had never had a friend. He had never played a game. He had never felt grass under his feet or wind on his face. He had spent ten years in a dark room, waiting to die, because the adults around him had told him that was all he could expect. "That's the stupidest thing I've ever heard," Mary said when he told her this. Colin's eyes went wide. "What?" "You're not dying. You're just in bed. There's a difference." "The doctor says —" "The doctor has been telling you what your father's servants want to hear. Has anyone actually LOOKED at your back?" Colin flinched. "I won't let them. I'm afraid of what they'll find." "So you'd rather lie here imagining the worst than actually find out the truth?" Colin opened his mouth, then closed it. No one had ever confronted him so directly. The servants tiptoed. The doctor spoke in soothing, vague platitudes. Only Mary told him the plain, unvarnished truth. "You are the rudest girl I have ever met," Colin said. "And you are the most spoiled boy I have ever met," Mary said. "We're perfect for each other." Despite himself, Colin laughed. It was a weak, rusty sound — the laugh of someone who had almost forgotten how. But it was real. Mary told him about the moor — the wild, open land that had put color in her cheeks. She told him about the wind and the sky and the way the air tasted when you breathed it in after a lifetime of stuffy rooms. She told him about Martha and Ben Weatherstaff and the robin. She did not tell him about the garden. Not yet. That secret was too precious, too fragile. But she was beginning to think that Colin needed the garden as much as the garden needed tending.

📚 Informational Text

Yorkshire Dialect and Social Class

Dickon, Martha, and Ben Weatherstaff speak in Yorkshire dialect—a regional way of speaking English with different vocabulary, grammar, and pronunciation than "standard" English. In the novel, Burnett writes their dialogue phonetically: "th'" for "the," "tha's" for "you are," "mun" for "must," etc. In Victorian England, how you spoke revealed your social class. Upper-class people spoke "properly" with educated accents. Working-class people spoke in regional dialects. Mary, from an upper-class background, initially finds the Yorkshire dialect strange and hard to understand. But she comes to love it because it's warm and genuine, unlike the formal speech of adults in her life. The dialect also connects to nature and the moors—it's the language of people who work the land. When Mary starts understanding and even using Yorkshire words, it shows she's connecting with a more authentic, earthy way of living. Language becomes a bridge between social classes in the story.

Check Your Understanding:

1. What is a dialect?

2. What did a person's way of speaking reveal in Victorian England?

3. What does Mary's acceptance of Yorkshire dialect show about her growth?
