Week 36

Today's Words

1. fixed

Definition: Not changing. Permanent and set in place.

"Every creature in Wonderland was fixed — the Rabbit was always late, the Queen was always angry, the Hatter was always at tea. Only Alice changed."

2. evolve

Definition: To develop gradually over time. To change and grow into something new.

"Alice evolved throughout her journey — from a bored girl on a riverbank to someone who understood the difference between sense and nonsense."

📖 Who Is Alice? - Part 2

The Caterpillar's question would not leave her alone. Who are you? Alice thought about all the different versions of herself she had been in Wonderland. She had been tiny — small enough to swim in her own tears. She had been enormous — big enough to fill an entire house. She had been polite to creatures who were rude to her and rude to creatures who were polite to her. She had been brave and frightened, curious and confused, loud and silent, all in the same afternoon. Which one was the real Alice? "All of them," she decided. "I was all of those things, and I am all of those things. A person is not one size. A person is not one mood. A person is the whole collection — the tall days and the small days and the brave days and the frightened days, all stacked together." This was a very grown-up thought for a ten-year-old, and Alice was a little surprised at herself for thinking it. She thought about the creatures she had met. The White Rabbit, who was always late and always anxious. The Caterpillar, who was always certain and always rude. The Hatter, who was always mad and always stuck at six o'clock. The Queen, who was always angry and always threatening. Each of them was ONE thing. They were fixed, rigid, unchanging. The Rabbit would always be late. The Queen would always be furious. The Hatter would always be at tea. Alice was different. Alice CHANGED. She grew and shrank and learned and questioned and stood up and sat down and made mistakes and corrected them. She was the only character in Wonderland who was not stuck being one thing. "That's what the Caterpillar didn't understand," Alice said. "He asked 'Who are you?' as though the answer should be permanent. But I am not permanent. I am growing. And growing means the answer keeps changing." She thought about the chrysalis — how the Caterpillar would someday become a butterfly. Even HE would change, whether he admitted it or not. "Perhaps," Alice thought, "the real question is not 'Who are you?' but 'Who are you becoming?' And the real answer is: I don't know yet. And that's all right." The wind blew across the riverbank, ruffling her hair. The daisies nodded. A rabbit — an ordinary brown rabbit, not wearing a waistcoat or carrying a watch — hopped across the grass and disappeared into a hole. Alice watched it go and smiled. "Not that hole," she said. "Not today."

📚 Informational Text

Adaptations: Alice Across Media

Alice has been adapted more than almost any other literary work. Stage adaptations began during Carroll's lifetime. Silent films appeared in the early 1900s. Disney's 1951 version remains the most recognizable, combining characters and scenes from both Alice books. Tim Burton's 2010 film aged up Alice and added a hero's journey structure. The Syfy miniseries reimagined Alice in a dystopian Wonderland. Video games, ballets, operas, and even a theme park ride have featured Alice. Each adaptation reflects its era: 1950s Disney emphasized whimsy and musical numbers; 2010 Burton emphasized girl power and visual spectacle; modern versions often explore darker, psychological interpretations. Some adaptations are faithful to Carroll's text; others use his characters as starting points for entirely new stories. The best adaptations capture what makes Alice special: the blend of humor and philosophy, the memorable characters, and the sense that beneath the nonsense lie important truths about growing up and finding yourself.
