📖 The Pool of Tears - Part 2

"Curiouser and curiouser!" cried Alice. She was so surprised that for the moment she quite forgot how to speak proper English. "Now I'm opening out like the largest telescope that ever was! Goodbye, feet!" She was growing. Rapidly. Her head hit the ceiling. Her feet were so far away she could barely see them. She was now more than nine feet tall, and still growing. She grabbed the little golden key from the table — she could reach it easily now — but of course, at this size, the little door was useless. She could not even fit her eye against the keyhole. She was enormous, and the beautiful garden was more impossible than ever. Alice sat down and began to cry again. She cried and cried, great splashing tears that rolled down her cheeks and pooled on the floor. Being nine feet tall meant her tears were proportionally large, and within minutes, she was sitting in a pool of salt water about four inches deep. "I shall be punished for it now, I suppose, by being drowned in my own tears!" she said. "That WILL be a queer thing, to be sure! However, everything is queer today." Just then, she heard the pattering of small feet, and the White Rabbit appeared again, trotting across the hall, muttering to itself. "Oh! The Duchess, the Duchess! Oh! Won't she be savage if I've kept her waiting!" the Rabbit fretted. "If you please, sir —" Alice began, in a low, timid voice. The Rabbit looked up, saw the enormous girl looming above him, gave a terrified squeak, dropped the white kid gloves and the fan it was carrying, and scurried away into the darkness as fast as its legs could carry it. Alice picked up the fan and began fanning herself. She was very hot and confused and enormous, and fanning seemed like the only sensible thing to do. "Dear, dear!" she said. "How queer everything is today! And yesterday things went on just as usual. I wonder if I've been changed in the night? Let me think: was I the same when I got up this morning? I almost think I can remember feeling a little different. But if I'm not the same, the next question is, Who in the world am I? Ah, THAT'S the great puzzle!" She tried to remember things she knew — multiplication tables, geography, poetry — to make sure she was still herself. But everything came out wrong. Four times five was not twelve, and London was not the capital of Paris, and the poems she had memorized came out with all the words changed. "I'm sure those are not the right words," said Alice, and her eyes filled with tears again. "I must be somebody else entirely. But WHO?" She noticed she was getting smaller again. The fan! It was the fan that was shrinking her. She threw it away just in time, or she might have disappeared entirely. She was now about two feet tall and still shrinking — but she slipped on the wet floor and SPLASH! She was up to her chin in salt water. "I wish I hadn't cried so much!" said Alice, paddling about and trying to find her way out. "I shall be punished for it now, I suppose." She was swimming in her own tears. They had become a pool — a salty, ridiculous pool created by her own enormous crying. She was not alone in the pool. A Mouse was swimming near her, paddling with tiny, desperate strokes. "O Mouse!" Alice called out. "Do you know the way out of this pool? I am very tired of swimming." The Mouse looked at her with suspicion but said nothing. "Perhaps it doesn't understand English," Alice thought. "I daresay it's a French mouse." So she tried the first sentence in her French lesson-book: "Où est ma chatte?" This, unfortunately, meant "Where is my cat?" The Mouse gave a great leap out of the water and trembled with fright. "Oh, I beg your pardon!" cried Alice hastily, afraid of hurting the poor animal's feelings. "I quite forgot you wouldn't like cats." "Would YOU like cats if you were my size?" the Mouse demanded, in a voice that was surprisingly deep and indignant for such a small creature. "Perhaps not," Alice said in a soothing tone. "I promise not to mention them again." They swam to the edge of the pool together and climbed out, dripping wet, onto dry ground, where they found a whole crowd of strange creatures that had fallen into the pool: a Duck, a Dodo, an Eaglet, and several other curious animals. Alice looked at the gathered creatures — soaking wet, shivering, and all staring at her as though she were the strangest thing THEY had ever seen — and she wondered, not for the first time, what on earth she had gotten herself into.

📚 Informational Text

Lewis Carroll: The Math Professor Who Loved Nonsense

Lewis Carroll was a pen name for Charles Lutwidge Dodgson, born in 1832. He was a mathematics professor at Oxford University who loved logic puzzles, wordplay, and photography. Carroll was shy around adults but loved entertaining children with fantastic stories. He became friends with the Liddell family and often took their three daughters—Lorina, Alice, and Edith—on boat trips. On July 4, 1862, during one of these river excursions, ten-year-old Alice Liddell asked Carroll to tell them a story. He made up a tale about a girl named Alice who fell down a rabbit hole into a strange world. Alice loved it so much she begged him to write it down. Carroll spent months expanding and illustrating the story, finally publishing it in 1865. It became an instant bestseller. Carroll wrote many other books, but Alice remains his masterpiece—a perfect blend of his mathematical mind and playful imagination.
