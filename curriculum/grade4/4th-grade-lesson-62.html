📖 The Foundling - Part 2

Sixteen years earlier, on a cold March morning, a bundle had been left on the wooden bed that stood just inside the cathedral's main entrance. This bed was called the foundling bed — a place where parents who could not care for their babies left them, hoping the church would take them in. The priests found the bundle at dawn. Inside was a baby — tiny, wailing, and startlingly ugly. His left eye was hidden beneath a massive wart. His body was twisted, with a great hump rising between his shoulder blades. His head was enormous, covered with red bristling hair, and his limbs were bent at strange angles, as though he had been assembled in a hurry by someone who had never seen a baby before. The priests recoiled. Several crossed themselves. One muttered that the child was cursed, that he should be left outside, that no good could come from raising such a creature. But a tall man in black robes stepped forward. His name was Claude Frollo, and he was the Archdeacon of Notre-Dame — the most powerful churchman in Paris. He was young then, barely thirty, with a thin, sharp face and eyes that burned with intelligence. He was brilliant, educated, and feared. He spoke six languages, had memorized more books than most men had touched, and held absolute authority over the cathedral and everyone in it. Frollo looked at the baby for a long time. The priests watched nervously. No one could read Frollo's expression — his face was a locked door that never opened. "I will take the child," Frollo said at last. The priests were stunned. "But, Archdeacon — the deformity —" "The child is an orphan," Frollo said. "The church teaches us to care for orphans. I will raise him." And so he did. He named the baby Quasimodo — a word that meant "half-formed" — and he raised him inside the cathedral walls. Not out of kindness, exactly. Frollo was not a kind man. He took the child because it suited his idea of duty, and because a deformed orphan raised in complete obedience would owe him everything and question nothing. Quasimodo grew up in the towers of Notre-Dame. He learned to walk on stone floors, to climb stone stairs, to sleep in stone rooms. The cathedral was his nursery, his school, his playground, and his prison — all at once. Frollo taught him to read, but only religious texts. He taught him to obey, but not to think for himself. He told Quasimodo that the outside world was cruel and dangerous — that people would mock him, hurt him, and fear him because of his appearance. "You are safe here," Frollo told him. "The cathedral protects you. I protect you. Out there, you would be destroyed." Quasimodo believed every word. He had no reason not to. Frollo was the only person he had ever known. The cathedral was the only world he had ever seen. And the bells — the great, thundering, glorious bells of Notre-Dame — became Quasimodo's voice. He could not speak well. His ears had been damaged by years of ringing the enormous bells, and he had grown nearly deaf. But when he pulled the ropes and the bells swung and the sound poured out over Paris like a river of bronze, Quasimodo felt something that was bigger than words. He felt alive.

📚 Informational Text

Victor Hugo: The Writer Who Saved Notre-Dame

Victor Hugo was born in France in 1802. He grew up to become one of France's most famous writers. When Hugo was young, Notre-Dame Cathedral was falling apart. The French Revolution had damaged it, and people didn't care about fixing old buildings. Hugo loved Notre-Dame and was heartbroken to see it crumbling. So in 1831, he wrote The Hunchback of Notre-Dame to make people fall in love with the cathedral again. His plan worked! After the novel became popular, France spent millions of francs restoring Notre-Dame. Hugo's book literally saved the cathedral. He died in 1885, and over 2 million people attended his funeral — one of the largest funerals in history.
