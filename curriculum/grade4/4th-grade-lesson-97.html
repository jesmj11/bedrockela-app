📖 The Door - Part 2

For three days, Mary carried the key in her pocket and searched for the door. She walked along the wall again and again, pushing aside every curtain of ivy, testing every brick. The door had to be there — hidden behind ten years of growth, buried under dead vines and creeping green. But the ivy was so thick and so tangled that it was like searching for a needle in a haystack made of leaves. On the third day, the wind helped. A strong gust blew across the garden wall, lifting the ivy like a curtain. And behind it, for just a moment, Mary saw something: a round knob, set into the brick, almost entirely hidden by vines. She leaped forward and pushed the ivy aside with both hands. There it was — a door. A small, arched wooden door set into the wall, so covered with ivy that it was invisible from more than two feet away. The wood was gray with age. The hinges were brown with rust. And in the center was a keyhole. Mary pulled the key from her pocket. Her hands were trembling. She put the key in the lock. She turned it. It stuck. She pushed harder. Something grated and scraped inside the old mechanism. Then, with a sound like a sigh, the lock turned. Mary pushed the door open. She stepped through. And stopped breathing. She was standing inside the secret garden. It was the most mysterious place she had ever seen. High walls rose on every side, shutting out the world completely. The ground was covered with brown, tangled growth — dead-looking vines and old stems and the skeletons of plants that had once been cared for and were now wild. Gray branches arched overhead, weaving together into a canopy that filtered the pale winter light into soft, broken patterns on the ground. Climbing roses — hundreds of them — covered the walls and clung to every surface. Their branches were bare and brown, twisted together in great tangled masses that looked like frozen waves. Some had fallen from the wall and lay on the ground in heaps. Others reached upward, stretching toward the light like arms raised in prayer. It looked dead. Everything looked dead — brown and gray and still. But Mary knelt down and scraped the earth away from the base of a rosebush. Just beneath the surface, she found something that made her heart leap. Green. A tiny shoot, pale and tender, pushing up through the dark soil. Alive. "It's not dead," Mary whispered. She scraped more earth away and found another green shoot. And another. "It's not dead. It's just been sleeping." She sat back on her heels and looked around the garden with new eyes. It was not a graveyard. It was a dormitory. Everything here was alive — just waiting. Waiting for someone to come. Waiting for someone to clear away the dead wood and let in the light and give the green things room to grow. "I found you," Mary said to the garden. "And I'm going to bring you back." She did not know it yet, but the garden was going to bring her back, too.

📚 Informational Text

Gardens in Victorian England

Victorians loved gardens. Wealthy estates competed to have the most impressive gardens with exotic plants from around the British Empire. Garden design was considered an art form. Victorian gardens often had different sections: formal flower beds near the house, vegetable and herb gardens for the kitchens, orchards for fruit, and wilder areas with natural landscaping. Garden walls provided shelter from wind and created microclimates where delicate plants could grow. Roses were especially prized—hundreds of varieties were developed during the Victorian era. Gardening was seen as good for both physical and mental health. Doctors sometimes prescribed "garden therapy" for patients with depression or anxiety. This Victorian belief in the healing power of gardens is central to Burnett's story—the secret garden heals Mary, Colin, and even Mr. Craven.
