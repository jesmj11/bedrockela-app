1. chaos

Definition: Complete disorder and confusion. A state where nothing is organized or predictable.

"The Duchess's kitchen was pure chaos — pepper in the air, a baby screaming, a cook throwing things, and a cat grinning from ear to ear."

2. vanish

Definition: To disappear suddenly and completely, as if into thin air.

"The Cheshire Cat vanished slowly, starting with its tail and ending with its grin — which remained floating in the air after the rest was gone."

📖 Pig and Pepper - Part 2

Having gotten herself to a reasonable height — about a foot tall, which was short but manageable — Alice set off through the wood and came upon a little house. Standing outside was a Fish-Footman, dressed in full livery, holding an enormous letter. He was knocking on the door, which was opened by a Frog-Footman, also in full livery. "For the Duchess," said the Fish-Footman, in a solemn voice. "An invitation from the Queen, to play croquet." "From the Queen," the Frog-Footman repeated, in an equally solemn voice. "An invitation for the Duchess, to play croquet." They bowed to each other so deeply that their wigs got tangled together. Alice laughed so hard she had to hide behind a tree so they wouldn't hear her. By the time she composed herself, the Fish-Footman was gone and the Frog-Footman was sitting on the doorstep, staring up at the sky with blank, bulging eyes. "How am I to get in?" Alice asked. "Are you to get in at all?" the Frog-Footman said. "That's the first question, you know." "I shall just go in," Alice said, and she did. The kitchen was full of smoke and chaos. The Duchess sat on a three-legged stool in the middle of the room, holding a baby that was howling at the top of its lungs. A cook was stirring an enormous pot of soup with one hand and throwing pepper into it with the other. Pepper filled the air like fog — everyone was sneezing except the cook and a large cat sitting on the hearth, grinning from ear to ear. "Why does your cat grin like that?" Alice asked politely, sneezing. "It's a Cheshire Cat," the Duchess said, "and that's why." "I didn't know cats could grin," Alice said. "You don't know much," the Duchess said. "And that's a fact." Alice did not like being told she didn't know much, especially by someone sitting in a kitchen full of pepper. But before she could respond, the Duchess thrust the baby at her. "Here! You may hold it if you like. I've got to get ready for croquet with the Queen." And she bustled out of the room. Alice carried the baby outside, which seemed like the safest place for it given the state of the kitchen. But as she walked, the baby began to change. Its nose turned up. Its eyes grew small. It grunted instead of crying. Alice looked down. She was holding a pig. "If you're going to turn into a pig," Alice said, setting it down gently, "I'll have nothing more to do with you." The pig trotted away into the wood, looking, Alice thought, quite content with the arrangement. She was standing alone, considering her next move, when she noticed the Cheshire Cat sitting on the branch of a tree, grinning down at her. "Would you tell me, please, which way I ought to go from here?" Alice asked. "That depends a good deal on where you want to get to," said the Cat. "I don't much care where —" said Alice. "Then it doesn't matter which way you go," said the Cat. "— so long as I get SOMEWHERE," Alice added. "Oh, you're sure to do that," said the Cat, "if you only walk long enough." Alice felt that this could not be denied, but it was not very helpful. She tried another question. "What sort of people live around here?" "In THAT direction," the Cat said, waving its right paw, "lives a Hatter. And in THAT direction," waving the other paw, "lives a March Hare. Visit either you like — they're both mad." "But I don't want to go among mad people," Alice remarked. "Oh, you can't help that," said the Cat. "We're all mad here. I'm mad. You're mad." "How do you know I'm mad?" said Alice. "You must be," said the Cat, "or you wouldn't have come here." And then, slowly, starting from the tip of its tail and ending with the grin, the Cheshire Cat vanished. Alice stared at the spot where it had been. She had seen a cat without a grin. But a grin without a cat? That was the most curious thing she had seen yet. She decided to visit the March Hare. "The Hatter might be more mad," she said to herself. "Besides — it's May. Perhaps the March Hare won't be quite so mad as it was in March." She walked on, and before very long, she came upon the maddest tea party in the history of tea.

📚 Informational Text

Growing and Shrinking: Alice's Identity Crisis

Throughout Wonderland, Alice constantly changes size—growing tall enough to touch the ceiling, shrinking small enough to swim in her own tears. These changes aren't just physical; they represent Alice's confusion about who she is. When she grows huge, she feels powerful but awkward and out of place. When she shrinks, she feels small and helpless. Victorian children, especially girls, often felt this way—expected to be both mature and childlike, seen but not heard, important but powerless. Alice's size changes mirror the confusing experience of growing up: some days you feel big and capable, other days small and lost. The story asks important questions: If your body changes, are you still you? What makes you who you are—your appearance, your memories, your name? When Alice can't remember her lessons or who she is, she's experiencing what all children feel during growth—that scary, exciting feeling of becoming someone new.
