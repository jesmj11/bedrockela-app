2. violation

Definition: Breaking a rule, law, or sacred right. Acting against something that should be protected.

"Attacking the cathedral was a violation of sanctuary — a right older than any law Frollo could claim."

📖 The Siege - Part 2

Frollo kept his word. Two weeks after Esmeralda claimed sanctuary, he sent soldiers to take her. They came at dawn — fifty men in armor, carrying pikes and torches, marching across the square toward the cathedral. Their captain carried a warrant signed by Frollo himself, declaring that the right of sanctuary had been overruled by the authority of the church. It was a lie. No one could overrule sanctuary. It was older than any archdeacon, older than any law. But Frollo had enough power to make people believe the lie, or at least to make them too afraid to challenge it. Quasimodo heard them coming. He had been sleeping lightly for days, his ears pressed against the stone floor, feeling the vibrations of the city below. The marching of fifty soldiers sent tremors through the building like a heartbeat. He was on his feet in an instant. He climbed to the gallery above the main doors and looked down. The square was full of soldiers. More were arriving from the side streets. Torchlight flickered off their armor and their weapons. They formed ranks before the cathedral, and their captain raised his voice. "By order of the Archdeacon of Notre-Dame," the captain shouted, "the criminal Esmeralda is to be surrendered immediately. Anyone who resists will be treated as an enemy of the church." Quasimodo leaned over the gallery railing. His shadow fell across the square like a gargoyle come to life. "She has sanctuary!" he bellowed. His voice — huge, rough, damaged by years of bells — rolled across the square like thunder. "You cannot enter! The law forbids it!" The captain hesitated. The soldiers shifted nervously. They had all grown up hearing the bells of Notre-Dame. The cathedral was sacred to them, even if their orders came from Frollo. But orders were orders. The captain raised his hand. "Break down the doors." The soldiers surged forward. Quasimodo was ready.

📚 Informational Text

Fate vs. Free Will

Fate is the idea that everything is predetermined — that your life is already written and you can't change it. Free will is the belief that you make your own choices and control your own destiny. Medieval people often believed in fate. They thought God had a plan for everyone and trying to fight it was useless. Hugo explores this question throughout his novel. Is Quasimodo's life predetermined by his deformity and Frollo's control? Or does he have the power to choose his own path? When Quasimodo defies Frollo and chooses to protect Esmeralda, he asserts his free will. The novel suggests that even when life seems predetermined, moments of choice — moments of courage — can change everything.
