📖 The Dormouse's Story - Part 2

"Once upon a time," the Dormouse began in a great hurry, "there were three little sisters, and their names were Elsie, Lacie, and Tillie, and they lived at the bottom of a well —" "What did they live on?" Alice asked. She always took a great interest in questions of eating and drinking. "They lived on treacle," said the Dormouse, after thinking a moment. "They couldn't have done that, you know," Alice gently remarked. "They'd have been ill." "So they were," said the Dormouse. "VERY ill." "But why did they live at the bottom of a well?" "It was a treacle-well," the Dormouse said. "There's no such thing!" Alice was getting very cross. "If you can't be civil," said the Dormouse, "you'd better finish the story yourself." "No, please go on!" Alice said, more humbly. "I won't interrupt again." "One, indeed!" said the Dormouse indignantly. But it continued: "These three little sisters — they were learning to draw, you know." "What did they draw?" asked Alice, forgetting her promise not to interrupt. "Treacle," the Dormouse replied, without even thinking. "But I don't understand," Alice said. "Where did they draw the treacle from?" "You can draw WATER out of a water-well," said the Hatter, "so I should think you could draw TREACLE out of a treacle-well — eh, stupid?" "But they were IN the well," Alice said. "Of course they were," said the Dormouse. "Well in." Alice gave up. The story made no sense, the Dormouse kept falling asleep in the middle of sentences, the March Hare was putting butter in the Hatter's watch, and the Hatter was asking riddles with no answers. "Why is a raven like a writing-desk?" she asked again. "Have you guessed the riddle yet?" "No, I give it up," Alice replied. "What's the answer?" "I haven't the slightest idea," said the Hatter. "Nor I," said the March Hare. Alice sighed wearily. "I think you might do something better with the time than waste it asking riddles that have no answers." "If you knew Time as well as I do," said the Hatter, "you wouldn't talk about wasting IT. It's HIM." "I don't know what you mean," Alice said. "Of course you don't!" the Hatter said, tossing his head contemptuously. "I dare say you never even spoke to Time!" "Perhaps not," Alice cautiously replied. "But I know I have to beat time when I learn music." "Ah! That accounts for it," said the Hatter. "HE won't stand beating. Now, if you only kept on good terms with him, he'd do almost anything you liked with the clock. For instance, suppose it were nine o'clock in the morning, just time to begin lessons: you'd only have to whisper a hint to Time, and round goes the clock in a twinkling! Half-past one, time for dinner!" Alice had had enough. The tea party was going in circles — literally and figuratively. The Hatter and the March Hare were moving around the table, taking the next set of cups, because they had quarreled with Time and it was always six o'clock, always tea-time, never any other time at all. "This is the stupidest tea-party I ever was at in all my life!" Alice said, and she got up and walked off into the wood. The last thing she saw was the Hatter and the March Hare trying to stuff the Dormouse into the teapot. "At any rate, I'll never go THERE again!" said Alice as she picked her way through the wood. "It's the stupidest tea-party in the world!" But even as she said it, she noticed a door in one of the trees. A door that led to the long, low hall she had been in before. And on the table — the glass table — was the golden key. This time, Alice knew what to do. She nibbled the mushroom until she was the right size, took the key, opened the little door, and — at last — stepped into the beautiful garden.

📚 Informational Text

The Caterpillar: Wisdom from Unexpected Sources

The Caterpillar sitting on a mushroom smoking a hookah is one of Wonderland's strangest characters. He speaks in short, cryptic questions that frustrate Alice but also make her think. When he asks 'Who are YOU?', Alice realizes she doesn't know how to answer. The Caterpillar is going through his own transformation—eventually he'll become a butterfly. This makes him the perfect character to help Alice understand change. He's not warm or comforting like a traditional wise mentor, but he does give Alice practical help: the mushroom that lets her control her size. In life, wisdom doesn't always come from expected sources or in comfortable ways. Sometimes the most important lessons come from strange, difficult encounters that challenge us. The Caterpillar teaches Alice (and readers) that asking the right questions is often more valuable than giving easy answers. His final words—'Keep your temper'—remind Alice to stay calm even when things are confusing.
