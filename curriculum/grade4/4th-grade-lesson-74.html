📖 The World Below - Part 2

In the hidden alleys and crumbling cellars beneath the streets of Paris, the Court of Miracles was waking up. Esmeralda returned from the pillory to find the Court in its usual state of organized chaos. Children chased rats through the tunnels. Old women stirred pots of thin soup over smoky fires. Clopin held court on his tin throne, settling disputes with equal parts wisdom and sarcasm. And Pierre Gringoire sat in a corner, scribbling poetry that no one had asked for. "You gave water to the hunchback," Clopin said when Esmeralda appeared. It was not a question. News traveled fast in the Court of Miracles. "He was thirsty," Esmeralda said. "He tried to kidnap you last night." "And today he was suffering. Those are two different things." Clopin studied her with his sharp eyes. He was a practical man — survival, not compassion, was his business. But he had long since learned that arguing with Esmeralda about kindness was like arguing with the river about which way to flow. "You are going to get yourself in trouble," he said. "I'm already in trouble," Esmeralda said, smiling. "I live in the Court of Miracles." The Court of Miracles was home to everyone Paris had rejected. The crippled, the scarred, the foreign, the poor — anyone who did not fit the city's idea of a proper citizen ended up here. Some were criminals, it was true. But most were simply people who had nowhere else to go. They had built something remarkable in the shadows: a community. They shared food. They cared for each other's children. They had their own laws and their own justice, rougher than the king's courts but often fairer. In the world above, a beggar could be hanged for stealing bread. In the Court of Miracles, the bread was shared. Esmeralda had lived here since she was a small child. She did not know her parents — her earliest memories were of the Court, of Clopin's gruff kindness, of learning to dance from an old woman who said that dancing was the only prayer that God never ignored. She danced because it made people happy. She danced because it was the one thing she could give the world that the world could not take away from her. She danced because, for a few minutes at a time, the people who watched her forgot about the rigid cruelty of Paris and remembered what joy felt like. But Esmeralda was not naive. She knew that her freedom was fragile. The church and the law considered the Court of Miracles a den of sin. Men like Frollo wanted it destroyed. Every day that Esmeralda danced in the square was a day she risked being arrested, accused, or worse. "Be careful," Clopin told her again. "I am always careful," Esmeralda said. "No, you are not," Clopin said. "You are kind. That is more dangerous." He was right. Kindness, in a city ruled by men like Frollo, was the most dangerous quality a person could have. And Frollo had noticed.

📚 Informational Text

Medieval Dance and Music

Music and dance were everywhere in medieval life. Traveling musicians called minstrels went from town to town playing lutes, drums, and flutes. Dances were performed at festivals, weddings, and markets. The tambourine was especially popular with Roma dancers. Dancing was a way for common people to celebrate and forget their hard lives for a while. However, the church often disapproved of dancing, seeing it as sinful and distracting people from prayer. Women who danced professionally, like Esmeralda in the story, were often viewed with suspicion. People thought dancers were wild and immoral. This prejudice made dancers vulnerable to accusations and arrest.
