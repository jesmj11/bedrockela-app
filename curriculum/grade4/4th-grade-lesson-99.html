📖 Dickon - Part 2

She met Dickon on the moor. Mary had gone out to buy gardening tools with the money Martha had helped her send for. She was sitting on a rock, examining a trowel she had ordered from a catalog, when she heard a sound she had never heard before: a low, sweet piping, like a wooden flute played by someone who had learned music from the birds themselves. She followed the sound and found a boy sitting on a fallen log in the heather. He was about twelve, with a round face covered in freckles, a turned-up nose, and the widest, most cheerful mouth Mary had ever seen. His eyes were the exact color of the moor — a blue-gray that seemed to shift with the light. On his shoulder sat a crow. At his feet lay a young fox, curled up and sleeping. Two squirrels chased each other around his ankles. He was playing a wooden pipe, and every creature within earshot had come to listen. This was Dickon. "Tha' must be Mary," he said when he saw her. His smile was so open and genuine that Mary, who had never been smiled at like that, did not know what to do with her face. She tried to smile back. It felt awkward, like a muscle she had forgotten how to use. "Martha told me about thee," Dickon said. "She says tha's been walkin' on the moor and gettin' color in thy cheeks. That's grand." "I've been gardening," Mary said. She had not meant to tell anyone. The secret had slipped out on its own, as though Dickon's openness had unlocked something inside her. "Gardenin'?" Dickon's eyes lit up. "Where?" Mary hesitated. But there was something about Dickon — something honest and steady and completely without judgment — that made it impossible to lie to him. "I found a garden," she said. "A secret one. It's been locked up for ten years, and nobody knows I've been going in. Everything is tangled and wild, and I don't really know what I'm doing, but there are green things growing, and I think it could be beautiful again." She waited for him to laugh, or to be shocked, or to tell her she was trespassing. Instead, Dickon leaned forward, his eyes shining. "Can I see it?" he asked. They went together. Mary led him through the ivy-hidden door, and Dickon stood in the center of the overgrown garden and turned slowly in a circle, looking at everything — the tangled roses, the bare branches, the patches of green pushing through the earth. "It's alive," Dickon whispered. He knelt down and touched a green shoot with one gentle finger. "It's been takin' care of itself all these years, growin' and dyin' back and growin' again. But it needs help now. It needs us." "Will you help me?" Mary asked. "Aye," Dickon said, as though it were the simplest question in the world. "I'll bring seeds. And tools. And I know which of these branches are dead and which are sleepin'. We'll have this garden bloomin' by summer, tha'll see." He pulled out a small knife and began to test the branches — scratching the bark to see if the wood underneath was green (alive) or brown (dead). Most of the roses were alive. The garden had been waiting, all this time, for someone to come and set it free. Mary watched Dickon work and felt something new: the warm, unfamiliar glow of having a friend who wanted to be there. Not because he was paid, not because he was obligated, but because he cared. "Dickon," she said. "Aye?" "Thank you." He grinned. "Don't thank me. Thank the garden. It's the one doin' the hard work."

📚 Informational Text

The Language of Flowers

Victorians believed that different flowers had specific meanings—a system called "floriography" or the "language of flowers." People sent secret messages through bouquets: red roses meant passionate love, forget-me-nots meant remembrance, yellow roses could mean jealousy or friendship. Entire books were published explaining flower meanings, and people studied them carefully. This tradition appears throughout The Secret Garden. Roses, which dominate the secret garden, symbolized love and beauty but also secrecy ("sub rosa" means "under the rose"—telling secrets in confidence). Crocuses and snowdrops, the first flowers Mary sees pushing through the earth, symbolize hope and new beginnings. The garden's transformation from dead vines to blooming flowers represents the characters' emotional and physical healing. Understanding flower symbolism adds deeper meaning to the story's most important symbol.
