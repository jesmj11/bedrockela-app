📖 Vocabulary

Week 13

Today's Words

1. spectacle

Definition: An impressive or dramatic display that people watch. Also: something or someone being stared at, often in a humiliating way.

"Quasimodo went from being celebrated as a spectacle to being mocked as one — the crowd's love turned to cruelty in an instant."

2. jeering

Definition: Making rude, mocking comments or sounds at someone. Laughing at someone to hurt or embarrass them.

"The cheering became jeering — the crowd threw rotten vegetables and mocked his hunched back and twisted limbs."

📖 The Festival of Fools - Part 2

Quasimodo had watched the festival from his tower window every year for as long as he could remember. He had seen the costumes, the dancing, the torches in the winter darkness. He had heard the music drifting up, faint and sweet, and he had pressed his face against the stone and wished — desperately, painfully — that he could be part of it. This year, something broke inside him. The wanting became too strong. He climbed down from the tower — carefully, quietly, terrified that Frollo would see him. He slipped through a side door and stepped into the square for the first time in his life. The world hit him like a wave. The noise. The colors. The smells — roasted meat, spiced wine, burning torches, the sweet rot of January mud. People everywhere, pressing close, laughing, shouting, their faces painted and their clothes bright with ribbons. It was overwhelming. It was terrifying. It was the most wonderful thing Quasimodo had ever experienced. He moved through the crowd with his head down, his cloak pulled tight around his hunched shoulders. No one noticed him at first. The Festival of Fools was a day for strange costumes and wild disguises — one more odd figure in the crowd was nothing unusual. Then came the contest. Every year, the festival crowned a King of Fools — the person who could make the ugliest face. Contestants stuck their heads through a round frame and contorted their features into the most hideous expressions they could manage. The crowd judged each one with cheers or boos. Someone pushed Quasimodo toward the frame. "You! Try it!" they laughed. Quasimodo did not understand at first. He put his face through the frame. He did not make a face. He simply looked out at the crowd with his one visible eye, his enormous head, his twisted features, his expression of confused hope. The crowd erupted. They thought it was the greatest ugly face they had ever seen. They cheered and stamped their feet and screamed with laughter. They crowned him King of Fools, placed a cardboard crown on his misshapen head, and lifted him onto a wooden throne carried on poles above the crowd. For one shining, impossible moment, Quasimodo was happy. He was being celebrated. People were cheering for him. He raised one trembling hand and waved, and the crowd roared. But the mood shifted. As quickly as the celebration had begun, it turned cruel. Someone realized this was not a costume. This was not an act. The face sticking through the frame was real. "It's the hunchback!" someone shouted. "The bell ringer! The monster from the tower!" The cheering became jeering. People threw rotten vegetables. They pelted him with mud. They mocked his hunched back, his twisted limbs, his enormous head. The crown was knocked from his head and trampled. Quasimodo did not understand what had changed. One moment they had loved him. The next, they hated him. He tried to climb down from the wooden throne, but the crowd pressed in around him, laughing and shoving. He was trapped — a spectacle, a joke, a thing to be pointed at and despised. He covered his face with his hands and made a sound that was not quite a word — a low, broken moan that was swallowed by the noise of the crowd. No one helped him. Almost no one.

📚 Informational Text

Bells and Bell Ringers

Before clocks, radios, or phones, church bells told people the time and warned them of danger. Bell ringers had one of the most important jobs in the city. They rang the bells for morning prayers, noon, evening, and midnight. They also rang bells to warn of fires, floods, or attacks. The largest bell in Notre-Dame is called Emmanuel. It weighs over 13 tons — as much as nine cars! Ringing such heavy bells was exhausting work. Bell ringers pulled on thick ropes with all their strength. The vibrations were so loud that many bell ringers lost their hearing over time. In Hugo's story, Quasimodo loves the bells because they are the only 'voices' he can still hear.

Check Your Understanding:

2. How much does Notre-Dame's largest bell, Emmanuel, weigh?
