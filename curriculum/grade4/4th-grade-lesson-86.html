📖 Betrayal - Part 2

Frollo did not send soldiers again. He had learned that force could not defeat the bell ringer. So he used the weapon he had always been best at: deception. He came to the cathedral alone, at night, through a passage that only he knew about — a narrow tunnel that led from the crypt beneath the altar to a hidden door behind the organ. Frollo had built the tunnel years ago, for exactly this kind of situation. A man who plans for everything always has a secret door. He found Esmeralda alone in the nave. Quasimodo was in the tower, repairing damage from the siege. Djali was asleep in a chapel. Frollo stepped from the shadows. "You must leave," he said. His voice was different tonight — not cold, not commanding. Almost human. "I am trying to help you." Esmeralda backed against a pillar. "Help me? You had me arrested. You sent soldiers to take me." "I was wrong," Frollo said. The words seemed to cost him something. "I see that now. But the situation has changed. The city is turning against you. If you stay in the cathedral, sooner or later the people will demand that sanctuary be lifted. You will be dragged out and punished." "And if I leave?" "I have arranged safe passage. A carriage is waiting at the south door. It will take you out of Paris, to a village where you will be safe. You can start a new life." Esmeralda hesitated. She did not trust Frollo. Every instinct screamed that this was a trap. But she was tired — tired of hiding, tired of fear, tired of being a prisoner in a cathedral that was supposed to be a sanctuary. "Come," Frollo said, and he offered his hand. She did not take his hand. But she followed him toward the south door. It was a trap. On the other side of the door, soldiers were waiting. Not the city guard — Frollo's personal men, loyal only to him, answerable to no law and no authority but his. They seized Esmeralda the moment she stepped through the door. "No!" Esmeralda screamed. "QUASIMODO!" Her voice echoed through the cathedral, bouncing off the stone walls, rising up through the towers. It reached the bell tower where Quasimodo was working. He heard it. Even with his damaged ears, even through hundreds of feet of stone and air, he heard her scream his name. He ran.

📚 Informational Text

Medieval Torture and Interrogation

In medieval times, torture was a legal part of interrogation. Authorities believed that pain would force people to tell the truth. Common torture methods included the rack (stretching the body), the wheel (breaking bones), and the strappado (hanging by bound arms). Accused criminals were tortured until they confessed — even if they were innocent. Many people confessed to crimes they didn't commit just to make the pain stop. Medieval society believed torture was necessary for justice, but we now know it produces false confessions and is morally wrong. Hugo includes torture in his novel to show the cruelty of medieval justice and how power can be abused when one group has total control over another.
