📖 The Ball - Part 2

D'Artagnan found Constance waiting at her house, frantic with worry. When she saw him — filthy, swaying on his feet, but grinning — she nearly wept with relief. "You have them?" she asked. D'Artagnan opened the velvet case. Twelve diamond studs blazed in the morning light. "Every last one," he said. Constance snatched the case and ran. She had to get the studs to the queen before the ball that evening. There was no time for thank-yous, no time for explanations. She simply ran, and D'Artagnan watched her go. Then he collapsed onto a bench and slept for six hours straight. That evening, the ball was the grandest event Paris had seen in years. The palace blazed with a thousand candles. The finest nobles in France filled the great hall in silks and velvets, dancing and laughing and pretending they were not all terrified of the Cardinal. D'Artagnan watched from the gallery above, cleaned up and wearing borrowed clothes. He was not important enough to attend the ball itself, but he had found a spot where he could see the great hall below. The king arrived first. Then the Cardinal, in his red robes, his eyes scanning the room like a hawk. Then the queen entered. She was radiant. Her gown was silver and white, her hair was dressed with pearls, and around her neck — on a ribbon of blue velvet — shone twelve diamond studs, blazing like stars. D'Artagnan held his breath. The Cardinal leaned toward the king and whispered something. The king frowned, then walked to the queen and studied the studs carefully. "They are beautiful," the king said. "All twelve." The Cardinal's face went very still. He counted the studs — once, twice, three times. Twelve. There were supposed to be only ten. His agent, Milady, had stolen two. The queen should have been missing two studs. But she was not. Somehow, impossibly, all twelve studs were there. The Cardinal turned away, and for just a moment, D'Artagnan saw something flicker across his face — not anger, exactly, but a cold, calculating respect. Whoever had foiled his plan had done it perfectly. The queen danced. The king smiled. The court applauded. And high above in the gallery, D'Artagnan leaned against a pillar and breathed. He had done it. A boy from Gascony — no money, no title, no connections — had outrun the most powerful man in France and saved the queen. Later that night, D'Artagnan walked through the quiet streets of Paris, too wired to sleep despite his exhaustion. The moon was full, and the city was silver and black. He found Constance waiting at the corner of her street. She pressed something into his hand — a ring, gold, with a small sapphire. "From the queen," Constance said. "She cannot thank you publicly, but she wants you to know that she will never forget what you did." D'Artagnan looked at the ring. It was the most valuable thing he had ever held. But it was not the ring that moved him. It was the knowledge that he had done something that mattered — something brave, something good, something worthy of the cloak he had not yet earned. "Tell the queen," D'Artagnan said, "that her musketeers are always at her service." "You are not a musketeer yet," Constance said, smiling. "I will be," D'Artagnan said. He walked home through the moonlit streets, and for the first time since leaving Gascony, he felt like he truly belonged in Paris. But the Cardinal was not finished. D'Artagnan had won this round. The next round would be far more dangerous.

📚 Informational Text

Gascon Pride: D'Artagnan's Heritage

D'Artagnan comes from Gascony, a region in southwest France. Gascons had a reputation for being brave, proud, and quick to anger. They were known as excellent fighters who never backed down from a challenge. Many Gascons were poor but noble, meaning they came from good families but had little money. This made them eager to prove themselves and earn honor through courage and skill. Gascons often spoke with a distinctive accent and were sometimes mocked by people from other regions. D'Artagnan's Gascon pride drives many of his actions in the story.

Check Your Understanding:

1. What region of France does d'Artagnan come from?

2. What three traits were Gascons known for?

3. Why were many Gascons eager to prove themselves?
