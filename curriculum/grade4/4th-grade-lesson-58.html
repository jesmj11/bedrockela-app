📖 One for All - Part 2

D'Artagnan found his three friends waiting at the tavern. They were trying to look calm, but the worry in their eyes gave them away. When D'Artagnan walked through the door — alive, free, and carrying a piece of paper — all three of them stood up at once. "Well?" Porthos demanded. "Are we going to prison?" D'Artagnan laid the commission on the table. There was a long silence as the three musketeers read it. A lieutenant's commission. Blank. Ready for any name. "He gave you this?" Athos said, disbelief in his voice. "He said I could fill in any name. Mine, or one of yours." D'Artagnan pushed the document toward Athos. "It should be yours. You are the best of us." Athos pushed it back. "I do not want rank. I have had rank before, and it brought me nothing but grief. Give it to Porthos." Porthos shook his head. "I am too loud and too vain for command. Everyone knows it, including me. Give it to Aramis." Aramis smiled gently. "I am thinking of leaving the musketeers to enter the church. A commission would be wasted on me. It must be yours, D'Artagnan." D'Artagnan looked at his friends — each one refusing the honor, each one insisting it go to someone else. This was who they were. This was what "all for one" truly meant. Not just fighting together, but lifting each other up. "I cannot take this," D'Artagnan said. "Not alone. Everything I have done, I did because of you. Athos taught me to think. Porthos taught me to be strong. Aramis taught me to be clever. Without the three of you, I would still be a boy from Gascony on a yellow horse." "You were never just a boy from Gascony," Athos said. "You were always a musketeer. You simply needed time to prove it." D'Artagnan picked up the quill from the table. His hand trembled slightly. He dipped it in ink and, in his best handwriting, wrote his name on the blank line. D'Artagnan. He looked up at his friends. "I accept this," he said. "Not for myself. For us. For everything we have been through and everything we will face together." Porthos let out a whoop that shook the rafters. Aramis clapped politely. Athos — Athos simply reached across the table and gripped D'Artagnan's hand, and the pressure of his grip said more than any words could. The tavern keeper brought wine. They drank. They told stories — the yellow horse, the three duels, the fight behind the Luxembourg, the ride to England, the breakfast in the bastion. They laughed at the absurdity of it all — four men against the most powerful cardinal in Europe, and somehow, against all odds, still standing. "What now?" Porthos asked, refilling his cup. "Now," Athos said, "D'Artagnan gets his blue cloak." The commissioning ceremony was held the next morning in the courtyard of the musketeers' headquarters. Monsieur de Tréville himself placed the blue cloak on D'Artagnan's shoulders. The silver cross gleamed on his chest. Around him, the company of musketeers stood at attention. D'Artagnan looked down at the blue cloak — the cloak he had dreamed of since he was a boy, the cloak that meant he belonged, that he had earned his place among the finest soldiers in France. Then he looked up at Athos, Porthos, and Aramis, standing together in the front row. Athos nodded. Porthos grinned. Aramis raised an eyebrow that said, about time. D'Artagnan smiled.

📝 Comprehension Questions

1. Why does each musketeer refuse the commission for himself?

2. D'Artagnan says: 'I accept this not for myself, but for us.' How is this different from the boy who arrived in Paris wanting glory for himself? What has the journey from Gascony to musketeer taught D'Artagnan about the difference between personal glory and shared honor?

📚 Informational Text

Taverns and Inns: Meeting Places of Adventure

Taverns and inns were important gathering places in the 1600s. Travelers stopped at inns to rest, eat, and stable their horses. Taverns served food and drink, mostly wine and beer. These places were noisy and crowded, with people from all walks of life. Merchants made deals, soldiers played cards, and locals gossiped about news. Taverns were also places where fights often broke out. In The Three Musketeers, important meetings happen at taverns like the inn where d'Artagnan first meets the musketeers' enemies. Innkeepers often heard valuable information and knew everyone's business.
