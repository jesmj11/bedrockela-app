1. assumption

Definition: Something you believe to be true without proof. A judgment you make before having all the facts.

"The crowd assumed Quasimodo was a monster because of his appearance — they never looked deeper."

2. extraordinary

Definition: Very unusual, remarkable, or exceptional. Far beyond what is ordinary or expected.

"Esmeralda told Quasimodo that the most extraordinary people in the world are often the most ordinary-looking — and the reverse is also true."

📖 The Monster and the Angel - Part 2

Days passed inside the cathedral. Slowly, carefully, Quasimodo and Esmeralda became friends. It was not easy. Quasimodo had never had a friend before. He did not know the rhythms of conversation — when to speak, when to listen, when a silence was comfortable and when it was awkward. He spoke too loudly sometimes (he was nearly deaf and could not gauge his own volume) and too softly other times (he was afraid of saying the wrong thing). He brought her food he thought she would like — bread, cheese, fruit — and left it outside her door, too shy to knock. Esmeralda, for her part, had to learn patience. Quasimodo startled easily. He flinched when she moved too quickly. He could not look her in the eye for more than a few seconds before turning away, certain that she must be disgusted by his face. "You keep looking away," Esmeralda said one morning. They were sitting on the gallery overlooking the nave, eating breakfast. Sunlight streamed through the rose window, casting pools of colored light on the stone floor. "I don't want you to have to look at me," Quasimodo said. Esmeralda set down her bread. "Quasimodo. Look at me." He turned, reluctantly. His one good eye met hers. "I am not afraid of your face," she said. "I never was. Do you know why?" He shook his head. "Because I learned a long time ago that the outside of a person tells you nothing about the inside. I have known beautiful people who were cruel, and plain people who were kind, and people so ordinary-looking that you would pass them on the street without a second glance who turned out to be the most extraordinary people in the world." She paused. "The crowd at the festival looked at you and saw a monster. I looked at you and saw a person who had been alone for too long. I was right. They were wrong." Quasimodo did not know what to say. So he said the only thing he could think of. "You are the first person who ever called my name good." "It IS good. And so are you." Something cracked open inside Quasimodo — not painfully, like the breaking of trust at the pillory, but gently, like a door opening to let in light. He had spent his whole life believing he was a monster. Frollo had told him so. The crowd had confirmed it. The mirror confirmed it every day. But Esmeralda looked at him and saw something else entirely. And if she could see it — this girl who had every reason to hate him, who he had grabbed and frightened, who owed him nothing — then maybe, possibly, it was true. Maybe he was not a monster after all. They spent the days exploring the cathedral together. Esmeralda taught Quasimodo to dance — or tried to. His body was not built for graceful movement, but he shuffled and swayed and once managed a turn that only knocked over two candelabras. Esmeralda laughed, and the sound echoed through the nave, and Quasimodo decided it was the most beautiful sound in the world. Quasimodo taught Esmeralda to ring the bells. He showed her how to grip the rope, how to time the pull, how to lean back and let the weight of the bell carry her upward. The first time Marie's great voice boomed out over Paris, Esmeralda screamed with delight and then laughed so hard she had to sit down. "That's the most powerful thing I've ever felt!" she said. "That's how I feel every day," Quasimodo said. And for the first time, he said it with pride instead of apology. They were an unlikely pair: the dancer and the bell ringer, the girl the city loved and the man the city feared. But inside the cathedral walls, none of that mattered. They were simply two people who had found each other in a world that had been unkind to both of them. They were friends.

📚 Informational Text

Mobs: When Crowds Turn Dangerous

A mob is a large crowd of people who lose their individual judgment and act emotionally as a group. Mobs can form quickly and do terrible things that individuals would never do alone. In a mob, people feel anonymous and powerful. They stop thinking rationally. They feed off each other's emotions — anger, fear, or excitement — until the feeling becomes overwhelming. Mobs can be swayed by a single passionate speaker. Once a mob forms, it's extremely difficult to stop. Hugo shows this multiple times in his novel: the crowd that crowns Quasimodo and then mocks him, the mob that storms the cathedral, the people who cheer for executions. Understanding mob psychology helps explain how good people can participate in terrible acts.
