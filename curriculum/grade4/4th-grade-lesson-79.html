📖 The Cathedral - Part 2

Esmeralda had never been inside Notre-Dame. She had seen it from the outside a thousand times — the great stone towers, the rose window, the gargoyles crouched on every ledge. But she had never stepped through the doors. People like Esmeralda — street dancers, outcasts, people without proper papers or proper names — did not enter cathedrals. Now she was living in one. Quasimodo gave her his room — the small stone cell at the top of the north tower. It was bare and cold, with nothing but a straw mattress and a wooden stool, but it was safe. The thick stone walls muffled every sound from the city below. The narrow window framed a rectangle of sky that changed from blue to gold to black as the hours passed. "It's not much," Quasimodo said apologetically. He stood in the doorway, too shy to enter his own room now that she was in it. "But no one will hurt you here." "It's perfect," Esmeralda said. And she meant it. After the prison cell, after the soldiers, after the terror of the false accusation — this small, quiet room felt like the safest place in the world. Quasimodo showed her the cathedral. He led her through passages and stairways that no one else knew existed — hidden corridors inside the walls, narrow tunnels that connected the towers, secret rooms tucked behind the great organ pipes. He showed her the bells — Marie, the largest, who had a voice like thunder, and the smaller bells with their lighter songs. He showed her how to climb the spiral stairs without getting dizzy and which stones were loose and which were solid. He showed her the gargoyles. "This one is Hugo," Quasimodo said, patting a grotesque stone head with obvious affection. "And that is Laverne. And the angry one on the corner is Victor. They are my friends." Esmeralda looked at the stone creatures — their twisted faces, their frozen grins, their hunched and broken bodies. She understood, suddenly and completely, why Quasimodo loved them. They looked like him. They were the only faces in the world that never flinched from his. "They're wonderful," she said. Quasimodo stared at her. No one had ever called his gargoyles wonderful. He took her to the top of the south tower at sunset. The view stole her breath. All of Paris lay below them — the river Seine winding through the city like a golden ribbon, the rooftops stretching to the horizon, the tiny people moving through the streets like ants. The sky was painted in shades of orange and pink, and the bells of every church in Paris were ringing vespers, and the sound rose up around them like a choir of bronze voices. "You see this every day?" Esmeralda asked. "Every day," Quasimodo said. "But I never had anyone to share it with." They sat on the ledge of the tower and watched the sun go down. Djali settled between them, chewing contentedly on a piece of straw. The city darkened. The stars appeared. "Thank you," Esmeralda said. "For saving me." "You saved me first," Quasimodo said. "At the pillory. With the water." "That was just water." "No," Quasimodo said. "It was everything."

📚 Informational Text

Beauty and Ugliness: Who Decides?

Every culture has ideas about what is beautiful and what is ugly. But these ideas are not universal — they change over time and place. In medieval Europe, pale skin was considered beautiful because it meant you didn't work in the fields. Plumpness was attractive because it meant you had enough to eat. Today's beauty standards are completely different! Hugo's novel challenges the idea that outer beauty reflects inner goodness. Quasimodo is physically deformed, but he is gentle, loyal, and brave. Frollo is handsome and respected, but his soul is twisted by obsession and cruelty. Esmeralda is beautiful, but her beauty makes her a target. The story forces readers to think about what true beauty really means.

Check Your Understanding:

1. How have beauty standards changed over time?

2. What point is Hugo making about outer and inner beauty?

3. Give examples from the story of characters whose appearances don't match their souls.
