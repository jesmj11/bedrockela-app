📖 The Pillory - Part 2

It began with staring. Then whispers. Then the first rotten cabbage. The crowd remembered the Festival of Fools. They remembered the ugly face in the frame, the hunched figure on the wooden throne. They remembered that for one brief moment, they had cheered for this creature — and the memory embarrassed them. People do not like to be reminded of their own cruelty. It makes them crueler. They threw everything they could find. Mud. Stones. Rotten fruit. Old shoes. A dead cat. Each projectile was accompanied by a jeer, a laugh, an insult hurled with the casual cruelty of people who have never considered that the target might have feelings. Quasimodo could not dodge. He could not cover his face. He could not run. The wooden frame held him fast, his neck bent, his arms pinned. All he could do was close his eye and wait. The sun beat down. His throat was parched with thirst. The mud and filth dried on his skin. His shoulders ached from the unnatural position. And still the crowd mocked him. He looked up once, scanning the faces for the one person who might help him. Frollo. His master, his protector, the man who had promised to keep him safe. He found Frollo in the crowd. Their eyes met. Frollo turned away. Something broke inside Quasimodo in that moment. Not his body — his body had endured far worse. Something deeper. The absolute trust he had placed in one person, the unquestioning belief that Frollo would always protect him — it cracked, silently and completely, like ice on a winter pond. Frollo had sent him on the mission. Frollo had not stopped the arrest. Frollo had not come to the trial. And now Frollo stood in the crowd and turned his back. Quasimodo was alone. Truly, completely, devastatingly alone. He bowed his head and let the stones hit him. He did not cry out. He did not beg. He simply endured, the way the cathedral endured wind and rain and time — silently, solidly, without complaint. The crowd grew bored, as crowds do. The throwing slowed. People drifted away to find other entertainment. The sun moved across the sky. And then someone climbed the steps of the pillory.

📚 Informational Text

Deformity and Disability in the Middle Ages

In medieval times, people with physical disabilities or deformities were often treated cruelly. Many believed that a twisted body meant a twisted soul, or that disability was punishment from God. People with deformities were sometimes abandoned as babies, locked away, or displayed as curiosities in traveling shows. Medical knowledge was limited — doctors didn't understand conditions like scoliosis (curved spine) or dwarfism. However, not everyone was cruel. Some monasteries and churches cared for people society rejected. A few disabled people became successful artists, scholars, or craftspeople. Hugo wrote Quasimodo to challenge the idea that physical appearance reflects inner worth. The novel asks: who is truly monstrous — the person who looks different, or the people who treat them cruelly?
