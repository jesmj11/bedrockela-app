2. bystander

Definition: A person who watches something happen without getting involved. Someone who sees wrongdoing but does not act.

"The crowd was full of bystanders — people who knew Esmeralda was innocent but were too afraid to speak."

📖 The Trap - Part 2

The trap was sprung on a Tuesday. Esmeralda was dancing in the square, as she did every afternoon. Djali performed her tricks. The crowd applauded. Coins clinked into the cup at Esmeralda's feet. It was an ordinary day — or so it seemed. Then the soldiers came. Six men in the uniform of the city guard marched into the square and surrounded Esmeralda. Their captain carried a warrant bearing the seal of the Archdeacon of Notre-Dame. "Esmeralda, street performer," the captain read aloud. "You are charged with the practice of witchcraft, the keeping of a demonic familiar, and the corruption of public morals. You will come with us." The crowd gasped. Esmeralda stared at the soldiers in disbelief. "Witchcraft?" she said. "I am a dancer. My goat does tricks. There is no witchcraft here." "The goat communicates with you in an unnatural manner," the captain said stiffly. He was clearly uncomfortable — he had seen Esmeralda dance many times and had never considered it witchcraft. But the warrant bore Frollo's seal, and no one in Paris refused Frollo. "This is madness," Esmeralda said. She looked around at the crowd — the same people who watched her dance every day, who smiled and clapped and tossed coins. "Tell them! You know me! You know I am not a witch!" The crowd was silent. A few people looked away. A few shuffled their feet. No one spoke. This is how power works: not by convincing people that wrong is right, but by making them too afraid to speak the truth. Every person in that crowd knew Esmeralda was innocent. But every person in that crowd knew what happened to people who defied the Archdeacon. Esmeralda was seized. Djali was captured — the little goat bleated and kicked, but the soldiers held her fast. The crowd watched as Esmeralda was led away, and the square that had been full of music and dancing was suddenly, horribly quiet. Pierre Gringoire, who had been watching from behind a cart, ran to the Court of Miracles with the news. Clopin listened grimly. "Frollo," Clopin said. It was not a question. "What do we do?" Gringoire asked. Clopin stared into the fire for a long time. "We cannot fight the church," he said. "We cannot storm a prison. We are beggars and outcasts. The law is not on our side — the law has never been on our side." "Then she is lost?" Gringoire whispered. "Not yet," Clopin said. "There is one place in Paris where even Frollo's power cannot reach. One ancient right that not even the church can violate." "What right?" "Sanctuary." But sanctuary had to be claimed inside the cathedral itself. And Esmeralda was locked in a prison cell, guarded by soldiers loyal to Frollo. To save her, someone would have to break her out. Someone very strong. Someone who knew every stone of Notre-Dame. Someone who had just learned, through one cup of water, that the world was bigger than the tower he lived in.

📚 Informational Text

Obsession: When Love Becomes Dangerous

The word 'obsession' comes from a Latin word meaning 'to besiege' — like an army surrounding a castle. When someone is obsessed, their mind is under siege by a single thought or desire they cannot escape. Obsession is different from love. Love wants the other person to be happy. Obsession wants to possess and control. In Hugo's novel, Frollo is obsessed with Esmeralda. He doesn't love her — he wants to own her. When he can't have her, he wants to destroy her. Hugo shows how obsession can turn a person into a monster, regardless of how respectable they seem on the outside. Understanding obsession helps us see why Frollo is the story's true villain.
