📖 Walking - Part 2

Colin walked more every day. First across the garden. Then around the garden, following the path that wound between the rose beds. Then out the hidden door and along the wall. Then across the lawn. Then — one glorious afternoon in late June — all the way to the edge of the moor and back. "I did it!" he shouted when he returned, red-faced and panting, his legs trembling with effort. "I walked to the moor!" Mary and Dickon cheered. The robin sang. Soot the crow cawed from the garden wall. Even the fox lifted his head and yawned, which was the fox equivalent of a standing ovation. Colin's transformation was astonishing. The boy who had been carried everywhere, who had believed his legs were useless, who had been told he would die — that boy was gone. In his place was someone new: thin still, and pale compared to Dickon, but standing straight, walking strong, and burning with a fierce, joyful energy that would not be contained. He ate enormous meals — not the thin broth and toast the servants had been feeding him, but real food: roast meat, bread, cheese, apples, and great glasses of fresh milk that Dickon brought from the farm. His appetite shocked the cook, who reported to Mrs. Medlock that Master Colin had eaten enough for three boys and asked for more. "Something very strange is happening," Mrs. Medlock said, wringing her hands. "That child has not left his room in ten years, and now the servants say they hear him laughing. LAUGHING. I don't like it." "Perhaps," Martha said carefully, "laughing is good." "It is NOT normal," Mrs. Medlock insisted. "Someone should write to Mr. Craven." Martha wrote to her mother instead. Her mother — a wise woman who had raised fourteen children on the moor and knew more about growing things than any doctor — wrote back with a single line of advice: "Let them be. Children know what they need better than we do." So the adults left them be. And the children grew. Colin grew stronger. His legs filled out. His chest expanded. His voice, which had been thin and whiny, deepened into something that sounded almost like a real boy's voice. He stood straighter every day, and his back — the back he had been so terrified of — was as straight as an arrow. Mary grew too, though her transformation was less visible. The sour face was gone. The scowl was gone. The thin, pinched expression of a child who has never been loved was replaced by something open and warm and alive. She smiled easily now. She laughed out loud. She ran across the moor with her arms spread wide, and when the wind hit her face, she closed her eyes and let it carry her forward. And the garden grew most of all. By July, it was a paradise. Roses covered every wall. The paths were bordered with lavender and sweet peas. The old apple tree was heavy with fruit. Bees and butterflies filled the air. It was, as Dickon said, the most alive place in all of Yorkshire. "We made this," Mary said one evening, standing in the center of the garden as the sun set behind the moor. "We found something dead and we made it alive." "We found each OTHER," Colin said. "That's the part that mattered." He was right. The garden had given them a place to grow. But it was the friendship — the laughter, the arguments, the shared work, the simple act of caring about something together — that had done the real healing. The garden was the soil. The friendship was the seed.

📚 Informational Text

Social Class in The Secret Garden

The Secret Garden depicts a society divided by social class. Mary and Colin are upper-class—they have servants, don't work, and expect to be obeyed. Martha, Dickon, and Ben Weatherstaff are working-class—they have manual jobs, large families in small cottages, and must earn their living. Burnett doesn't ignore these differences, but she challenges class prejudices. The working-class characters are portrayed as more genuine, healthy, and wise than the upper-class ones. Dickon, though poor, is the happiest and healthiest character. Martha's kindness and common sense help Mary more than any upper-class governess did. The garden becomes a space where class distinctions fade—everyone gets dirty, everyone works, everyone shares the secret. This was mildly radical for 1911. Burnett suggests that happiness and worth don't come from wealth or status but from connection to nature, meaningful work, and loving relationships. The upper-class children must learn from the working-class characters to become whole, healthy people.
