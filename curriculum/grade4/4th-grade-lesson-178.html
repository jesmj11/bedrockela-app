1. reflect

Definition: To think deeply and carefully about something, especially past experiences. To look back with thought.

"Alice reflected on Wonderland and realized she missed the creatures — not the nonsense, but the aliveness of a world where nothing was boring."

2. authentic

Definition: Real, genuine, true to itself. Not fake or pretending.

"The creatures of Wonderland were maddening but authentic — they never pretended to be something they weren't."

📖 Alice Remembers - Part 2

That evening, Alice sat in her room and tried to write down everything she remembered about Wonderland. She wrote about the fall — the long, slow tumble through the earth, past cupboards and bookshelves and empty marmalade jars. She wrote about the pool of tears and the Caucus Race and the Caterpillar on his mushroom. She wrote about the mad tea party where it was always six o'clock and the Dormouse lived in a teapot. She wrote about the Queen's croquet ground, where the rules changed with every swing and the penalty for everything was beheading. She wrote about the creatures she had met, and as she wrote, she realized something: she missed them. Not the Queen. Definitely not the Queen. But the others — the Hatter with his riddles, the Cheshire Cat with his vanishing grin, the Mock Turtle with his melancholy songs, even the bossy Caterpillar with his infuriating question. They were maddening and absurd and completely impossible, but they had been, in their strange way, alive. More alive than the people Alice saw every day in the real world, who followed rules without thinking and answered questions without listening and never, ever admitted that they were confused. The creatures of Wonderland were confused all the time. They were also rude, illogical, and frequently wrong. But they were never boring, and they were never pretending to be something they were not. "Except the Queen," Alice thought. "The Queen was pretending to be powerful. But she was only a card." Alice set down her pen and looked at what she had written. It was messy and rambling and full of crossed-out words, but it was hers. Her record of Wonderland. Her proof that it had happened — if not in the world, then in her mind, which was perhaps the more important place. She thought about the question that had followed her through the whole adventure: Who are you? She picked up her pen and wrote, at the bottom of the last page: "I am Alice. I am the girl who fell down a rabbit hole and found a world where nothing made sense, and instead of pretending it did, I said so. I am the girl who was small and became large and became small again, and learned that my size does not determine my worth. I am the girl who stood before a Queen and told the truth. I am still growing. I am still changing. I do not know who I will be tomorrow. But today, I am Alice, and that is enough." She closed the notebook, turned off the lamp, and went to sleep. She did not dream of Wonderland. She dreamed of gardens and rivers and a cat who grinned, and when she woke up, she was smiling.

📚 Informational Text

Coming of Age: Alice's Journey

Though Alice doesn't age during her adventure, the story is fundamentally about growing up. At the start, Alice is passive—things happen to her and she reacts. By the end, she's taking action—defying the Queen, making her own decisions, and waking herself up. She begins confused about who she is and ends with clearer self-knowledge. She starts polite even to rude characters and ends standing up for herself. She begins wanting adults to tell her what to do and ends trusting her own judgment. This character arc mirrors real coming-of-age experiences: leaving childhood's certainties, navigating confusing social rules, questioning authority, and ultimately finding your own voice. The size changes represent the awkwardness of growing—sometimes feeling too big for your life, sometimes too small. The strange creatures represent the confusing adults you encounter. Wonderland itself represents the transition between childhood and adulthood—neither one nor the other, but a confusing space between. Alice's journey teaches that growing up requires courage, curiosity, and the willingness to question things that don't make sense.
