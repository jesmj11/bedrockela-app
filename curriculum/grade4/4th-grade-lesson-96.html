Today's Words

1. instinct

Definition: A natural, unlearned way of behaving or knowing. A feeling that guides you without thinking.

"The robin seemed to be guiding Mary by instinct — hopping along the wall, leading her closer to the buried key."

2. connection

Definition: A relationship or bond between people, animals, or things. The feeling of being linked to something.

"Talking to the robin was the closest thing to friendship Mary had ever experienced — her first real connection to another living thing."

📖 The Robin - Part 2

Mary became obsessed with the locked garden. She walked along the wall every day, searching for a way in. She pushed at the ivy, she tested the bricks, she even tried climbing — but the wall was too high and too smooth, and the ivy was too slippery in the late-winter damp. Then the robin appeared. He was a small bird with a bright red breast and sharp black eyes that glittered with intelligence. He perched on the wall above Mary's head and chirped at her — not a frightened chirp, but a friendly one, as though he were introducing himself. "Hello," Mary said. The robin chirped again and hopped along the top of the wall. He seemed to want her to follow. Mary followed. The robin hopped and flew in short bursts, always staying just ahead of her, leading her along the wall. He landed on a branch. He chirped. He flew to another spot and chirped again. He was showing her something. "What are you trying to tell me?" Mary asked. Ben Weatherstaff appeared around the corner, pushing a wheelbarrow. He saw Mary talking to the robin and stopped. "He's taken to thee," Ben said, surprised. "That robin don't talk to just anyone. He's been livin' in that garden — the locked one — for years. Flies over the wall whenever he likes. He's the only creature that's been inside since the day it was locked." "He lives in the garden?" Mary said. She looked at the robin with new eyes. He was her connection to the secret place — the only living thing that had seen what was behind the wall. "He was born there," Ben said. "In one of the old rose trees, I reckon. He flies out to visit me sometimes, but he always goes back." The robin chirped once more, then flew up and over the wall, disappearing into the locked garden. Mary watched him go. "I wish I could follow you," she whispered. Over the next few days, the robin came to see Mary every time she walked near the wall. He sat on branches and fenceposts and sang to her, and Mary found herself talking to him — telling him about India, about the ship, about the empty manor and the howling wind. The robin listened with his head tilted, as though every word were fascinating. It was the closest thing to friendship Mary had ever experienced. One afternoon, the robin was scratching in a patch of freshly turned earth near the wall. Mary watched him dig, his little claws flicking dirt in every direction. Then she saw something glint in the soil. She knelt down and brushed the dirt away with her fingers. It was a key. An old iron key, rusted but solid, half-buried in the earth. It had been underground for years — perhaps ten years. Mary's heart hammered. She picked up the key and held it in her palm. It was heavy and cold. "Is this the key to the garden?" she whispered to the robin. The robin chirped. Mary closed her fingers around the key and held it tight. She had the key. Now she needed the door.

📚 Informational Text

Victorian Manor Houses and Servants

Misselthwaite Manor represents the grand country estates of wealthy Victorian families. These houses had dozens of rooms—ballrooms, libraries, drawing rooms, dining halls—and employed many servants to maintain them. There was a strict hierarchy among servants: the butler and housekeeper were at the top, then came lady's maids and valets, then footmen and housemaids, then kitchen staff. Servants had specific duties and rarely spoke to the family unless spoken to first. Children of the house might not even know all the servants' names. The "upstairs" family lived separate lives from the "downstairs" servants. By the early 1900s, this system was beginning to fade as more people moved to cities for factory jobs and fewer wanted to be servants. The Secret Garden captures this changing world—the huge manor is mostly empty, with many rooms locked and unused.

Check Your Understanding:

1. What was the hierarchy among Victorian servants?

2. How did the "upstairs" and "downstairs" worlds differ?

3. Why was the servant system beginning to fade by the 1910s?
