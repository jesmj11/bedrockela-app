📖 Vocabulary

Week 33

Today's Words

1. arbitrary

Definition: Based on random choice rather than reason. Rules that change depending on someone's mood.

"The Queen's croquet rules were entirely arbitrary — she changed them whenever she was losing and punished anyone who noticed."

2. rigged

Definition: Arranged dishonestly to produce a particular result. A game that is set up so one person always wins.

"The croquet game was rigged — the Queen always won because the rules changed to suit her, and anyone who scored against her lost their head."

📖 The Queen's Croquet Ground - Part 2

Alice had never seen such a curious croquet ground in her life. The ground was all ridges and furrows. The croquet balls were live hedgehogs. The mallets were live flamingos. And the hoops were soldiers who had to bend over and stand on their hands and feet to form arches. Alice's flamingo kept twisting its neck around to look at her with a puzzled expression, as if to say: "What exactly do you expect me to do?" The hedgehog, meanwhile, uncurled itself and crawled away every time Alice tried to hit it. And the soldiers kept standing up and walking to different parts of the ground, so the hoops were never in the same place twice. "It's very hard to play," Alice said, "when nothing stays where you put it." The Queen did not seem to find it hard at all. She played with terrifying intensity, slamming her flamingo at hedgehogs with the force of a battering ram. Whether she actually hit anything or whether the animals simply scurried out of her way in terror was hard to say. But the Queen won every point, because the Queen always won every point, because anyone who suggested otherwise would lose their head. "Off with his head!" the Queen shouted at a soldier who had sneezed during her swing. "Off with her head!" she screamed at a courtier who had looked in the wrong direction. "Off with EVERYBODY'S head!" she roared, apparently just for the exercise. Alice noticed that no one's head was actually removed. The King quietly pardoned everyone the moment the Queen's back was turned. It was, Alice thought, rather like a game of pretend played by someone who did not know it was pretend. "How are you getting on?" the Cheshire Cat asked, appearing suddenly as a floating grin above the croquet ground. "I don't think they play at all fairly," Alice complained. "And they all quarrel so dreadfully you can't hear yourself think — and they don't seem to have any rules. Or if they have, nobody follows them." "How do you like the Queen?" said the Cat in a low voice. "Not at all," said Alice. "She's so extremely —" At that moment, the Queen appeared behind her, and Alice quickly finished: "— likely to win, that it's hardly worth finishing the game." The Queen smiled and passed on. The Cheshire Cat watched her go, its grin widening. "Who ARE you talking to?" the King asked, peering at the floating cat head with obvious distaste. "It's a friend of mine — a Cheshire Cat," Alice said. "Allow me to introduce it." "I don't like the look of it at all," the King said. "It must be removed." "Off with his head!" the Queen called, without even looking. "How do you cut off a head," the executioner argued, "when there is no body to cut it from?" This created a magnificent argument. The King said anything that had a head could be beheaded. The executioner said you couldn't cut off a head without a body underneath it. The Queen said that if something wasn't done about it in less than no time, she'd have EVERYBODY beheaded, including the executioner. The Cat solved the problem by slowly vanishing, beginning with the tip of its tail and ending with the grin, which remained for some time after the rest of it had gone. Alice decided it was time to leave the croquet ground. She slipped away while the Queen was busy threatening someone else and wandered through the garden, feeling tired and confused and more than a little homesick. Wonderland, she was beginning to realize, was not just absurd. It was unfair. The Queen made rules that no one could follow, then punished people for breaking them. The game was rigged. The logic was backwards. And the only way to survive was to agree with whatever the most powerful person said, whether it made sense or not. "I don't like this at all," Alice said to herself. "Rules should make sense, and punishments should fit the crime, and people should be allowed to disagree." She had not yet realized that this idea — this simple, brave, radical idea — was the most important thing she would discover in all of Wonderland.

📚 Informational Text

The Mad Tea Party: Breaking Social Rules

The Mad Hatter's tea party is Wonderland's most chaotic scene—a perfect parody of Victorian tea parties, which were highly formal social events with strict rules about proper behavior. At the Mad Tea Party, every rule is broken: participants are rude, they ask riddles with no answers, time has stopped at six o'clock (always tea time), they constantly switch seats, and the Dormouse falls asleep in the teapot. Victorian readers would have found this both shocking and hilarious. Tea parties represented civilization, order, and proper manners—everything Wonderland rejects. The phrase 'mad as a hatter' was already common in Carroll's time (hatters really did go mad from mercury poisoning used in hat-making), so readers expected the Hatter to be strange. But Carroll made him a specific kind of mad—obsessed with time and trapped in an eternal tea party. The scene satirizes how silly and arbitrary social rules can be when you really think about them.
