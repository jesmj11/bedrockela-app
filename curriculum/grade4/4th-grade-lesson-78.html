Today's Words

1. sanctuary

Definition: A safe place where someone is protected from harm. In medieval times, a person who reached a church could claim sanctuary and be legally untouchable.

"Quasimodo carried Esmeralda into the cathedral and shouted the word that would protect her: SANCTUARY!"

2. defied

Definition: Openly refused to obey someone in authority. Stood against a powerful person or rule.

"The hunchback had defied Frollo — for the first time in his life, he made a choice his master did not make for him."

📖 Sanctuary! - Part 2

Quasimodo had been watching from the tower. He had seen the soldiers surround Esmeralda. He had seen her led away, her arms gripped by guards, her face pale with shock. He had watched the crowd do nothing — the same crowd that loved her dancing, that smiled at her goat, that tossed coins at her feet. When she needed them, they vanished. Something burned inside Quasimodo. Not the dull, trained obedience that Frollo had cultivated. Something newer. Something that had roots in a cup of water and a voice that said drink. He knew what Frollo had done. He understood, now, that the order to capture Esmeralda had been the beginning of this. Frollo had used Quasimodo as a weapon, and when the weapon was caught, Frollo had turned his back. For the first time in his life, Quasimodo made a decision that Frollo had not made for him. He would save her. He waited until nightfall. The city darkened. The prison where Esmeralda was held was a stone building near the river — old, crumbling, and guarded by two soldiers who were already half-asleep. Quasimodo descended from the cathedral like a creature from a dream. He scaled the prison wall in darkness, his massive fingers finding holds in the crumbling mortar that no ordinary climber could have used. He reached a barred window on the second floor, gripped the iron bars, and pulled. The bars bent. Then broke. Quasimodo was stronger than iron when he needed to be. He squeezed through the window and dropped into the cell. Esmeralda was there, sitting on the stone floor with Djali in her lap. She looked up and saw his silhouette — the enormous head, the hunched shoulders, the one eye gleaming in the darkness. She did not scream. She recognized him. "Quasimodo," she whispered. "Come with me," he said. "Quickly." He lifted her — gently, so gently that she barely felt the transition from floor to arms. Djali scrambled up his back and clung to his shoulder. Then Quasimodo climbed. Up the prison wall. Across the rooftops of Paris. Through the night, moving with impossible speed and silence, leaping gaps between buildings, scaling walls that should have been unclimbable. He carried Esmeralda the way the wind carries a leaf — easily, naturally, as though she weighed nothing at all. The guards did not hear them. The city did not see them. They were shadows among shadows, moving across the moonlit rooftops toward the one place in Paris where no authority could touch them. Notre-Dame. Quasimodo reached the cathedral and scaled its west face, climbing the stone as easily as a staircase. He reached the gallery above the great doors and stepped out onto the balcony that overlooked the square. He raised Esmeralda above his head with both arms — this small, brave girl who had given him water when the world gave him stones — and he shouted one word into the night. "SANCTUARY!" His voice — the voice of a man who had spent his life in silence — echoed across the rooftops of Paris. It bounced off walls, rolled down streets, shook the windows of houses where people lay sleeping. "SANCTUARY! SANCTUARY!" It was an ancient right, older than the king, older than the church, older than Paris itself. Anyone who claimed sanctuary inside a cathedral was protected. No soldier, no judge, no cardinal, no king could touch them. The cathedral was holy ground, and within its walls, the law of man gave way to the law of God. Esmeralda was safe. Frollo, watching from his study window, gripped the edge of his desk until his knuckles turned white. The hunchback had defied him.

📚 Informational Text

Loyalty: Choosing Who You Serve

Loyalty means staying faithful to a person, cause, or institution even when it's difficult. In medieval times, loyalty was everything. Knights swore loyalty to their lords. Servants were loyal to their masters. Breaking an oath of loyalty was one of the worst things a person could do. But Hugo's novel asks a harder question: what do you do when the person you're loyal to is wrong? Quasimodo has been loyal to Frollo his entire life. Frollo raised him, protected him, and gave him a home. But when Frollo commands Quasimodo to do something evil, Quasimodo faces an impossible choice. Sometimes true loyalty means disobeying orders when those orders are cruel.

Check Your Understanding:

1. Why was loyalty so important in medieval times?

2. What difficult choice does Quasimodo face in the story?

3. Can disobeying an order sometimes be the loyal thing to do?
