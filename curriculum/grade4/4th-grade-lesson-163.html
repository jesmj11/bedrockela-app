Today's Words

1. parody

Definition: An imitation of something that exaggerates its features for comic effect. A funny version of something serious.

"The Mock Turtle's school subjects are parodies of real subjects — 'Reeling and Writhing' instead of Reading and Writing, 'Uglification' instead of Multiplication."

2. melancholy

Definition: A deep, persistent sadness. A feeling of thoughtful sorrow.

"The Mock Turtle was melancholy — weeping constantly about a past that may never have existed."

📖 The Mock Turtle's Story - Part 2

The Duchess found Alice in the garden and seized her arm with uncomfortable familiarity. "You're thinking about something, my dear," the Duchess said, "and that makes you forget to talk. I can't tell you just now what the moral of that is, but I shall remember it in a bit." "Perhaps it hasn't got one," Alice ventured. "Tut, tut, child!" said the Duchess. "EVERYTHING'S got a moral, if only you can find it." And she squeezed Alice's arm harder. Alice was rescued by the Queen, who appeared and screamed at the Duchess until the Duchess fled. Then the Queen grabbed Alice and marched her to a sunny spot on the shore, where two creatures sat on a rock: a Gryphon (a creature with the head and wings of an eagle and the body of a lion) and a Mock Turtle. The Mock Turtle was a sad creature. He had the body of a turtle and the head and tail of a calf, and he was weeping — great, salty tears that rolled down his cheeks and dripped into the sea. "What's the matter with him?" Alice asked the Gryphon. "It's all his fancy, that," the Gryphon whispered. "He hasn't got no sorrow, you know. Come on!" They went to the Mock Turtle, who looked at them with large, mournful eyes. "This here young lady," said the Gryphon, "she wants to know your history, she do." "I'll tell it her," said the Mock Turtle in a deep, hollow tone. "Sit down, both of you, and don't speak a word till I've finished." They sat. The Mock Turtle sighed deeply and began. "Once," he said, "I was a real Turtle." A long pause. "When we were little," the Mock Turtle went on, more calmly, "we went to school in the sea. The master was an old Turtle — we used to call him Tortoise." "Why did you call him Tortoise, if he wasn't one?" Alice asked. "We called him Tortoise because he taught us," said the Mock Turtle angrily. "Really you are very dull!" Alice bit her lip. The wordplay was maddening. Everything in Wonderland was a pun, a riddle, or a trick of language. "We had the best of educations," the Mock Turtle continued. "We studied Reeling and Writhing, of course, to begin with, and then the different branches of Arithmetic — Ambition, Distraction, Uglification, and Derision." "I never heard of 'Uglification,'" Alice ventured. "You know what to BEAUTIFY is, I suppose?" the Gryphon said. "Yes," Alice said. "Well, then," the Gryphon continued, "if you don't know what to UGLIFY is, you ARE a simpleton." Alice decided not to ask any more questions about the curriculum. The Mock Turtle's school seemed to operate on the same logic as everything else in Wonderland: it SOUNDED right if you didn't think about it too hard, but the moment you examined it closely, it fell apart. And yet — was it so different from Alice's own schooling? She had memorized multiplication tables without understanding them, recited poems without feeling them, learned geography without ever seeing the places on the map. Was the Mock Turtle's school really more absurd, or was it just more honest about its absurdity? Alice filed this uncomfortable thought away and listened as the Mock Turtle described his eleven hours of lessons a day. "Eleven hours a day?" Alice said. "That's a great deal." "Ten hours the first day," said the Mock Turtle, "nine the next, and so on." "What a curious plan!" Alice exclaimed. "That's the reason they're called lessons," the Gryphon remarked. "Because they lessen from day to day." Alice sat on the rock by the sea and listened to the Mock Turtle cry about a school that might never have existed, taught by a Tortoise who was not a tortoise, in subjects that were not subjects, and she thought: this is what happens when you stop asking whether things make sense and just accept them. She was not going to do that.

📚 Informational Text

The Queen of Hearts: Tyranny and Terror

The Queen of Hearts is Wonderland's villain—a tyrant who shouts 'Off with their heads!' at anyone who displeases her. She represents arbitrary authority: power without justice, rules without reason, and punishment without mercy. Victorian children knew authority figures like this—adults whose word was law, who punished for tiny infractions, and who never had to explain themselves. The Queen's croquet game uses living flamingos as mallets and hedgehogs as balls—a perfect example of trying to impose order on chaos (the game is impossible to play). What's interesting is that the Queen's threats are mostly empty—the King pardons everyone she sentences to death. She's all bluster and rage without real power. This deflates the terror: beneath her fury, she's just a playing card. Alice's growing courage throughout the story builds to her final confrontation with the Queen, where Alice declares 'You're nothing but a pack of cards!' This is the climax of Alice's journey—she's learned to stand up to unfair authority.

2. Why is the croquet game impossible to play?
