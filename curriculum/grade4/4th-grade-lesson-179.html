📖 The Girl Who Asked Questions - Part 2

The next morning, Alice went downstairs to breakfast and found the world exactly as she had left it. The toast was in the toast rack. The tea was in the pot. Her sister was reading the same book — the one without pictures or conversations. The cat, Dinah, was washing her face by the fire. Everything was ordinary and predictable and precisely as it should be. Alice sat down and poured her tea, and she thought about how strange it was to be back in a world where tea was just tea and cats were just cats and nothing turned into anything else without warning. "Alice, you're very quiet this morning," her sister said. "I'm thinking," Alice said. "About what?" "About questions." Her sister smiled. "What kind of questions?" "The kind that don't have answers. Or the kind that have MANY answers. Or the kind that change depending on who's asking." Her sister put down her book. This was, she thought, not entirely the sort of thing one expected to hear over breakfast. "For instance," Alice said, buttering her toast with great deliberation, "why is a raven like a writing-desk?" "I haven't the faintest idea," her sister said. "Neither has anyone else," Alice said. "But isn't it wonderful that the question exists?" She ate her toast. She drank her tea. She fed Dinah a small piece of fish. And then she went outside, into the garden, into the ordinary, sensible, beautiful world. The morning was bright. The sky was very blue. Birds sang in the apple tree. The flowers in the garden were red and white and yellow, and none of them needed painting. There were no soldiers standing on their heads, no flamingos being used as mallets, and no one was shouting "Off with her head!" It was, by any reasonable standard, a much better garden than the Queen's. Alice walked through it slowly, touching the flowers, listening to the birds, feeling the sun on her face. She was still Alice — the same girl who had sat on the riverbank yesterday, bored and sleepy and waiting for something to happen. But she was also the Alice who had fallen through the earth and swum in her own tears and talked to a caterpillar about identity and stood before a queen and refused to be silent. She was the Alice who had learned that curiosity is the bravest thing a person can have, and that asking questions is more powerful than knowing answers, and that the most important words in any language are: "Why?" and "Who says so?" and "I don't think that's right." She was the girl who had gone to Wonderland and come back with the most important souvenir of all: the knowledge that she did not have to accept the world as it was presented to her. She could question it. She could challenge it. She could look at a Queen and see a playing card. "I wonder," Alice said aloud, to no one in particular, "what would happen if I went looking for another rabbit hole." She did not go looking. Not today. Today, the ordinary world was enough. But she kept her eyes open. Because Alice had learned the most important lesson of all: the extraordinary is always there, hiding just beneath the ordinary, waiting for someone curious enough to notice. All you have to do is ask the right question. All you have to do is follow the rabbit. All you have to be is Alice.

📚 Informational Text

Why Nonsense Matters

Alice is a nonsense story—events don't follow logical cause and effect, rules change randomly, and conversations often make no sense. But nonsense isn't the same as meaningless. Nonsense literature serves important purposes: it questions assumptions about how the world should work, it celebrates imagination and creativity, it provides relief from strict rules and logic, and it gives readers permission to think differently. For Victorian children living in a rigid, rule-bound society, nonsense was liberating—a space where anything could happen. For modern readers, nonsense offers similar freedom from expectations and conventional thinking. Nonsense also develops important cognitive skills: following absurd logic requires mental flexibility, understanding wordplay builds linguistic intelligence, and recognizing satire develops critical thinking. By showing a world where normal rules don't apply, nonsense helps us see our own world's rules more clearly—some are necessary, but some are arbitrary and could be changed. Alice teaches that taking nonsense seriously can reveal profound truths about sense.
