Today's Words

1. hypocrisy

Definition: Pretending to have beliefs or standards that you do not actually follow. Saying one thing and doing another.

"Frollo's greatest hypocrisy was claiming to serve justice while building a tower of lies to destroy an innocent girl."

2. fabricated

Definition: Made up. Invented and presented as true even though it is false.

"Frollo fabricated the witchcraft charges against Esmeralda — every piece of evidence was a lie."

Frollo's Fury

📖 Frollo's Fury - Part 2

Frollo could not stop thinking about the cup of water. He had watched from the edge of the crowd — seen Esmeralda climb the pillory steps, seen her kneel beside Quasimodo, seen her offer water to the creature he had spent sixteen years controlling. And he had seen something that terrified him: the look on Quasimodo's face. Gratitude. Wonder. The first flicker of independent thought. For sixteen years, Frollo had kept Quasimodo dependent, obedient, and isolated. He had told Quasimodo the world was cruel, and he had made sure Quasimodo experienced enough cruelty to believe it. He had positioned himself as the only source of safety, the only protection against a hostile world. One cup of water had unraveled all of it. Esmeralda had shown Quasimodo kindness — real kindness, freely given, with no strings attached. And in doing so, she had proven that Frollo's version of the world was a lie. The world was not only cruel. People were not only monsters. And Frollo was not the only person capable of caring. This could not be allowed. Frollo paced his study, his shadow flickering on the stone walls. He told himself his anger was righteous — that Esmeralda was a threat to order, to morality, to the authority of the church. He told himself he was protecting Paris. But the truth was simpler and darker: Frollo was afraid. Afraid of losing control over Quasimodo. Afraid of a girl whose kindness made his power seem small. Afraid that the people of Paris might discover that compassion could exist outside the cathedral walls. He decided that Esmeralda must be destroyed. Not physically — Frollo was too careful for that. He would destroy her reputation, her freedom, her place in the world. He would use the machinery of law and authority to crush her, and he would do it so skillfully that no one would see his hand behind it. He began gathering evidence. He sent spies to the Court of Miracles. He collected testimony from people who owed him favors — merchants, priests, guards who would say whatever Frollo told them to say. He built a case piece by piece, a tower of lies that looked, from the outside, like justice. The charge would be witchcraft. It was the perfect accusation — impossible to disprove, terrifying to the public, and entirely within Frollo's authority to prosecute. Esmeralda's dancing would be called enchantment. Her trained goat would be called a demon's familiar. Her kindness to Quasimodo would be called an attempt to bewitch the church's property. It was monstrous. It was brilliant. And there was no one in Paris with the power to stop it. Except, perhaps, a hunchback in a bell tower who was beginning to think for himself.

📚 Informational Text

The Power of the Church in Medieval Times

In the Middle Ages, the Catholic Church was one of the most powerful institutions in Europe. It owned vast amounts of land, collected taxes called tithes, and had its own courts and laws. Church leaders like bishops and archdeacons were as powerful as nobles. A corrupt church official could destroy lives without fear of punishment. The church controlled education — almost all schools were run by priests. It also controlled what people read: books had to be copied by hand by monks, and the church decided which books were acceptable. People who questioned the church could be arrested for heresy (disagreeing with church teachings). This power is important in Hugo's story, where Frollo uses his church position to harm innocent people.
