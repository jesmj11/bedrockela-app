📖 Justice - Part 2

The truth came out quickly, the way truth always does once the hand that held it down is removed. Frollo's private papers were found in his study — letters, orders, and documents that revealed everything. The false charges against Esmeralda. The bribery of witnesses. The manipulation of the courts. The illegal attempt to violate sanctuary. Every scheme, every lie, every abuse of power was laid out in Frollo's own meticulous handwriting. The people of Paris were horrified. They had trusted Frollo. They had obeyed him. They had let him wield the authority of the church like a weapon, and they had been too afraid to question him. Captain Phoebus — the soldier who had rescued Esmeralda on the night of the capture — led the investigation. He was honest, methodical, and genuinely disturbed by what he found. "Esmeralda is innocent," Phoebus announced publicly, standing in the square before Notre-Dame. "The charges of witchcraft were fabricated by the Archdeacon for personal reasons. She is free." The crowd cheered. Esmeralda stood on the steps of the cathedral and watched them cheer, and she felt — not joy, exactly. Something more complicated. Relief, certainly. Vindication. But also a weary sadness, because these were the same people who had watched her be dragged away and said nothing. Kindness after the fact is better than no kindness at all. But it is not the same as kindness when it counts. Pierre Gringoire wrote a poem about the whole affair. It was not a very good poem, but it was heartfelt, and people liked it well enough. The Court of Miracles celebrated with a feast that lasted three days. Clopin raised a cup of stolen wine and said, "To Esmeralda — who survived the worst Paris could throw at her and came out dancing." Djali did a backflip. The crowd went wild. But Esmeralda's first thought, when the cheering died and the feasting ended, was not for herself. "Where is Quasimodo?" she asked. No one had seen him since the night on the rooftop. He had disappeared back into the cathedral, as he always did, retreating to the one place he knew. Esmeralda climbed the tower stairs. She found him sitting by the great bell Marie, his hand resting on the bronze as though it were the flank of a sleeping animal. He was staring out the window at the city below. His face — his battered, bruised, misshapen face — was quiet. "It's over," Esmeralda said. Quasimodo nodded. "Frollo is gone. The charges are dropped. You are free." "Free," Quasimodo repeated. He said the word carefully, as though tasting it for the first time. "I have never been free." "You are now." He looked at her. "Where will you go?" "Back to the Court of Miracles," Esmeralda said. "It is my home. Where will YOU go?" Quasimodo looked around the tower — the bells, the gargoyles, the narrow window with its view of all of Paris. This was his home, too. The only one he had ever known. "I will stay here," he said. "But not because I am afraid to leave. Because this is where I belong. The bells need me." He paused. "And I need them." Esmeralda smiled. "Then I will visit you every day." "Every day?" "Every single day." Quasimodo's face did something it had almost never done before. It smiled.

📚 Informational Text

Heroism: Courage in Impossible Situations

A hero is someone who shows great courage, especially in the face of danger. But heroism isn't just about fighting dragons or winning battles. Sometimes heroism is protecting someone weaker than yourself. Sometimes it's standing up to authority when authority is wrong. Sometimes it's choosing compassion when everyone else chooses cruelty. Quasimodo is an unlikely hero. He's been taught to hide, to obey, to believe he's worthless. But when the moment comes, he acts with extraordinary courage. He defies Frollo, claims sanctuary for Esmeralda, and defends the cathedral against an army. His heroism is powerful because he had to overcome so much fear and self-doubt to find it. True heroes aren't fearless — they're afraid and act anyway.
