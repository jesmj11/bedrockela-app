Today's Words

1. discernment

Definition: The ability to judge well. The skill of seeing differences that others miss, especially between what is true and what is false.

"Alice developed discernment — the ability to tell harmless nonsense from dangerous nonsense, silly rules from unjust ones."

2. defend

Definition: To protect something from attack. To argue in support of something you believe in.

"Alice realized that sense, fairness, and logic are not automatic — they have to be defended by people willing to ask 'Why?'"

📖 Sense and Nonsense - Part 2

Alice's sister came back with tea and biscuits and found Alice sitting on the riverbank with a very thoughtful expression. "You look serious," her sister said. "Are you still thinking about your dream?" "I'm thinking about nonsense," Alice said. "Nonsense?" Her sister raised an eyebrow. "In my dream, everything was nonsense. The rules didn't make sense. The conversations didn't make sense. The trial didn't make sense. And at first, I was very frustrated, because I kept trying to make sense of things that HAD no sense." "And now?" "Now I think nonsense is more complicated than I thought." Alice bit into a biscuit and chewed thoughtfully. "Some nonsense is just silliness — like the Dormouse's story about living at the bottom of a well. That kind of nonsense is fun. It makes you laugh." "And the other kind?" "The other kind is dangerous," Alice said. "The Queen's kind. When someone in charge makes rules that don't make sense and punishes you for not following them — that's nonsense too. But it's not funny. It's cruel." Her sister set down her teacup. She was looking at Alice with an expression of quiet surprise — the look of someone who has just realized that the child in front of them is not quite a child anymore. "The trick," Alice continued, "is knowing which kind of nonsense you're dealing with. The Hatter's nonsense is harmless. The Queen's nonsense is not. And the only way to tell the difference is to ask yourself: is this nonsense hurting anyone?" "That's very wise, Alice." "I learned it in Wonderland," Alice said. "Though I don't think Wonderland meant to teach me." They sat together on the riverbank, sipping tea and watching the river flow. The world was quiet and sensible and beautifully boring, and Alice appreciated it in a way she had not appreciated it that morning. Because now she knew what the alternative looked like. She had been to a world without sense, without fairness, without logic. And she had learned that these things — sense, fairness, logic — were not automatic. They had to be built, and maintained, and defended. Every time someone asked "Why?", they were defending sense against nonsense. Every time someone said "That's not fair," they were building something. Every time a girl looked at a Queen and said "You're nothing but a pack of cards," the world got a little more honest. Alice finished her biscuit and reached for another. "I think I should like to go back to Wonderland someday," she said. "Whatever for?" her sister asked. "Because there's still so much nonsense to sort out," Alice said. "And I'm rather good at it."

📚 Informational Text

The Illustrators: Bringing Wonderland to Life

Carroll himself illustrated the original handwritten manuscript he gave to Alice Liddell, but when he published the book, he hired professional illustrator John Tenniel. Tenniel's illustrations became iconic—most people picture his Alice (with long blonde hair, blue dress, and white apron) when they think of the character. Tenniel's detailed engravings set the visual standard for Wonderland for decades. Later illustrators brought different styles: Arthur Rackham's dreamy watercolors, Salvador Dalí's surrealist interpretations, and Ralph Steadman's grotesque, energetic drawings each showed Wonderland through different artistic lenses. Disney's simplified, animated characters introduced Alice to audiences who never read the book. Modern illustrators continue reimagining Wonderland in graphic novels, picture books, and digital art. The text is so open to interpretation that each illustrator can create their own version of what Wonderland looks like. This visual flexibility is part of why Alice endures—it can be cute, creepy, psychedelic, elegant, or absurd depending on the artist.
