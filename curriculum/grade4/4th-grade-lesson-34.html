📖 All for One - Part 2

That evening, the four friends sat together on the steps of the musketeers' barracks as the sun set over Paris. The sky was painted in shades of gold and crimson, and the rooftops of the city glowed in the fading light. It was one of those rare, perfect moments when the world seems to pause and allow you to feel exactly where you are. D'Artagnan wore his blue cloak. It was heavier than he had expected — not just the weight of the fabric, but the weight of everything it represented. The battles he had fought. The friends he had made. The boy he had been and the man he was becoming. "I keep thinking about the day I arrived in Paris," D'Artagnan said. "On that ridiculous yellow horse, with my patched clothes and my father's old sword. I was so sure I was ready for anything." "You were not ready for anything," Athos said. "You crashed into me, broke Porthos's belt, and stepped on Aramis's foot. All in one morning." "And challenged all three of us to duels," Porthos added, chuckling. "I have never met anyone so determined to get himself killed." "Or so determined to make friends," Aramis said. D'Artagnan laughed. It was true. He had stumbled into the greatest friendships of his life by literally running into people. There was something perfectly D'Artagnan about that — graceless, reckless, and somehow exactly right. "My father told me three things before I left Gascony," D'Artagnan said. "Never run from a fight. Never tolerate an insult. And never betray a friend." "Your father was a wise man," Athos said. "He was. But he forgot to mention the most important thing." "What is that?" D'Artagnan looked at his three friends — Athos, who had taught him that true strength is quiet; Porthos, who had taught him that true joy is generous; Aramis, who had taught him that true wisdom is gentle. "He forgot to tell me that the friends you find along the way are worth more than anything you set out to find." The sun slipped below the rooftops. The first stars appeared. Paris settled into its evening rhythms — the clatter of carriages, the distant music from a tavern, the laughter of soldiers heading home. Porthos stood and stretched his enormous frame. "Well," he said, "I suppose we should decide what to do next. We have defeated the Cardinal's plot, saved the queen, survived a siege, and D'Artagnan has his cloak. What adventure is left?" "All of them," Athos said. Aramis closed his book and stood. "France is full of trouble. The Cardinal will not rest. There will be new plots, new villains, new dangers." "Good," Porthos said. "I was getting bored." D'Artagnan stood last. He looked out over the city — this impossible, beautiful, treacherous city that had tried to chew him up and spit him out on his very first day. He thought about the boy who had ridden in on a yellow horse, full of fire and foolishness, with nothing but a sword and a dream. That boy was gone. In his place stood a musketeer. But the fire was still there. It would always be there. He turned to his friends. "Whatever comes next," he said, "we face it together." Athos drew his sword. The blade caught the last light of the sunset and gleamed. "All for one," Athos said. Porthos drew his. "And one for all." Aramis drew his. "Always." D'Artagnan drew his father's old sword — nicked, worn, and still sharp — and raised it to meet the other three. Four blades touched in the fading light, steel ringing against steel, a sound like a promise. All for one. And one for all. Below them, Paris glittered in the darkness, full of danger and possibility. And four friends — the finest swordsmen in France — were ready for whatever came next.

📚 Informational Text

Paris in the 1600s: A City of Contrast

Paris in the 1600s was a city of grand palaces and muddy streets. The royal family lived in beautiful buildings with gardens and fountains. Rich nobles wore silk and velvet clothes and attended fancy parties. But most people were poor. They lived in crowded neighborhoods with narrow streets. There was no running water or electricity. People threw their garbage into the streets, which made the city smell terrible. Horse-drawn carriages clattered over cobblestone roads. Despite the poverty, Paris was growing into Europe's center of art, fashion, and culture.

Check Your Understanding:

1. How did rich and poor people's lives differ in Paris?

2. Why did the city smell bad?

3. What was Paris becoming known for in Europe?
