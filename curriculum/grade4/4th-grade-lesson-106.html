Week 22

Today's Words

1. confrontation

Definition: A direct, face-to-face conflict. The moment when two people stop avoiding an issue and deal with it openly.

"The confrontation between Mary and Colin was fierce — she refused to pity him, and he was furious that she wouldn't."

2. revelation

Definition: A surprising and important discovery that changes how you see something.

"The revelation that Colin's back was straight — that there was no lump, no hunch, no deformity — shattered ten years of fear in one moment."

📖 The Argument - Part 2

The tantrum came two days later. Mary had been spending the morning in the garden with Dickon, and when she came to visit Colin that afternoon, he was in a rage. His face was blotchy with tears, his pillows were on the floor, and a glass of water had been thrown against the wall. "Where were you?" Colin demanded. "I sent for you this morning and you weren't here! You were supposed to come!" "I was busy," Mary said. "Busy? BUSY? I am ill! I could be DYING! And you were BUSY?" In the old days — the India days — Mary would have either screamed back or walked away. But the moor and the garden had changed her. She had learned, from pulling weeds and clearing dead wood, that sometimes the thing that looked most frightening was the thing that needed the most attention. She sat down in a chair and looked at Colin calmly. "You are not dying," she said. "I AM! The lump on my back —" "Let me see it." Colin went white. "No!" "Why not? You've been terrified of this lump for ten years. You've let it ruin your life. But you've never once LOOKED at it. What if it's nothing?" "It's not nothing! The servants say —" "The servants say what they think will keep you quiet. They're afraid of you, Colin. Not because you're powerful, but because your tantrums make their lives miserable. So they agree with everything you say, including the idea that you're dying, because it's easier than arguing with you." Colin stared at her, his chest heaving. "You are the most horrible person I have ever met," he whispered. "I know," Mary said. "But I'm also the only person who tells you the truth. Now — let me see your back." Something in Mary's voice — not gentle, not harsh, but absolutely certain — broke through Colin's wall. He turned over, slowly, and let Mary lift his nightshirt. His spine was straight. There was no hump. There was no lump. There was nothing but the thin, pale back of a boy who had never used his muscles, never stood in the sun, never done anything more strenuous than ring a bell for his servants. "Colin," Mary said. "There is nothing wrong with your back." "You're lying." "Turn around and look in the mirror yourself." He did. He twisted and craned and looked, and his face went through a series of expressions — disbelief, confusion, anger, and finally, something that cracked open like an egg: hope. "There's nothing there," he said. "No," Mary said. "There never was." Colin sat on his bed for a long time without speaking. Ten years of fear — ten years of believing he was doomed, that his body was broken, that death was coming — evaporated in one moment of truth. "I've wasted so much time," he said quietly. "Then stop wasting it," Mary said. "I know a place that can help."

📚 Informational Text

Grief and How People Cope

Mr. Craven's ten-year grief over his wife's death drives much of the story's conflict. When she died giving birth to Colin, he locked her garden, traveled constantly to avoid home, and emotionally abandoned his son because Colin reminded him of his loss. Different characters show different responses to grief: Mr. Craven escapes, Colin becomes an invalid, the servants whisper and worry, and Ben Weatherstaff tends the dead garden in secret. The novel suggests that avoiding grief makes it worse—Mr. Craven's travels don't heal him, they just delay healing. Healthy grief involves feeling the pain, remembering the lost person with love rather than agony, and eventually opening your heart to new life and relationships. The garden becomes a metaphor for grief: locked and abandoned, it seems dead, but life persists underneath. When the garden is reopened and tended, it blooms again—just as hearts can heal when people face their grief with courage and support from others.
