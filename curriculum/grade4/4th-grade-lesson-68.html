Today's Words

1. authority

Definition: The power to give orders and make decisions. The right to control others.

"Frollo held absolute authority over the cathedral and everyone in it — he decided what was sin and what was virtue."

2. manipulation

Definition: Controlling someone through cleverness and deception rather than force. Making people do what you want without them realizing it.

"Frollo's manipulation of Quasimodo was masterful — not with shouting, but with quiet, relentless control that made Quasimodo believe the world hated him."

📖 The Judge - Part 2

Claude Frollo sat in his study in the cathedral, surrounded by books and silence. The study was high in the south tower — a cold, narrow room with a single window that looked out over Paris. The walls were lined with shelves holding ancient texts in Latin, Greek, Hebrew, and Arabic. A single candle burned on the desk, casting long shadows that made the room feel like a cave. Frollo was reading, as he always was. But tonight, his mind was not on his books. He had seen the festival from his window. He had seen Quasimodo leave the cathedral — disobeying his explicit orders to stay inside. He had seen the crowning, the mockery, and the girl who had helped the hunchback down from the platform. Esmeralda. Frollo knew who she was. He knew everything that happened in Paris — the church's network of information was vast, and Frollo sat at its center like a spider in a web. He knew Esmeralda was a street dancer. He knew she lived among the outcasts. He knew the people loved her. And that was the problem. Frollo believed in order. He believed in rules, in hierarchy, in the absolute authority of the church — which meant, in practice, the absolute authority of Claude Frollo. He had spent his life building power: over the cathedral, over the priests, over the city's courts of law. He controlled who was punished and who was pardoned. He decided what was sin and what was virtue. People like Esmeralda — free, joyful, kind without permission, loved without authority — were a threat to everything Frollo stood for. If the people could find goodness in a street dancer, what did they need the church for? If they could find compassion in the gutter, what did they need Frollo for? He could not allow it. But Frollo was not a fool. He did not act in anger. He planned. He waited. He found the precise point of leverage and applied pressure with surgical care. Tonight, the leverage was Quasimodo. "You left the cathedral," Frollo said when Quasimodo returned to his tower room, muddy and trembling. Quasimodo hung his head. "I only wanted to see the festival." "I told you the world outside was dangerous. Was I wrong?" Quasimodo thought of the crowd — the cheering that became jeering, the love that became hatred in an instant. "No," he whispered. "You were right." "They mocked you," Frollo said. His voice was soft, almost gentle. This was how Frollo worked — not with shouting, but with quiet, relentless control. "They threw things at you. They laughed at your face, your body, your very existence. This is what the world thinks of you, Quasimodo. This is what people are." Quasimodo said nothing. A tear rolled down his misshapen cheek. "But I took you in," Frollo continued. "I raised you. I protected you. I am the only one who has ever cared for you. Without me, you would have died on that foundling bed. Remember that." "I remember," Quasimodo said. "Good. Now — I need you to do something for me." Quasimodo looked up. He would do anything for Frollo. Frollo was his master, his father, his entire world. Whatever Frollo asked, Quasimodo would do. He did not yet know that obedience and love are not the same thing.

📚 Informational Text

Sanctuary: The Law of the Church

In medieval Europe, churches offered sanctuary — a safe place where criminals could hide from arrest. If someone being chased by guards ran into a church and grabbed the altar, they were protected by church law. Even the king's soldiers could not drag them out. Sanctuary could last for weeks or even months while the church negotiated with authorities. This law existed because the church believed forgiveness was more important than revenge. However, sanctuary had limits. If you left the church grounds, you could be arrested immediately. Some criminals lived in churches for years, afraid to step outside. Notre-Dame's sanctuary plays a crucial role in Hugo's story.
