1. quadrille

Definition: A formal dance for four couples, popular in the 18th and 19th centuries, with set movements and patterns.

"The Lobster Quadrille was a ridiculous version of a formal dance — with lobsters as partners and somersaults in the sea."

2. absurdity

Definition: The quality of being wildly unreasonable or ridiculous. Complete nonsense.

"Alice finally laughed at the absurdity of everything — the dance, the teachers, the rules, and herself for trying so hard to make sense of it all."

📖 The Lobster Quadrille - Part 2

"Shall we try the Lobster Quadrille?" the Gryphon suggested. "Or would you rather the Mock Turtle sang you a song?" "Oh, a song, please, if the Mock Turtle would be so kind," Alice replied. She was curious what a Mock Turtle's song would sound like. The Mock Turtle sang in a slow, melancholy voice. It was a song about soup — beautiful soup, rich and green, waiting in a hot tureen. He sang it with such deep feeling that Alice felt moved, though she was not entirely sure why a song about soup should be emotional. "Thank you," Alice said when he finished. "It's a very interesting song." "Shall I sing you the second verse?" the Mock Turtle asked eagerly. "I'd rather see the Lobster Quadrille first," said the Gryphon. "Go on, old fellow, show her." The Mock Turtle sighed and began to describe the dance, but the Gryphon interrupted: "The thing is, you advance twice —" "Each with a lobster as a partner!" cried the Mock Turtle. "Of course," said the Gryphon. "Advance twice, set to partners —" "— change lobsters, and retire in same order," continued the Mock Turtle. "Then, you know," the Gryphon went on, "you throw the —" "The lobsters!" shouted the Mock Turtle, bouncing up and down. "— as far out to sea as you can —" "Swim after them!" screamed the Mock Turtle, turning somersaults. "Turn a somersault in the sea!" cried the Gryphon. "Change lobsters again!" yelled the Mock Turtle. "Back to land again, and that's all the first figure," said the Gryphon, dropping his voice. Alice watched, bewildered, as the Mock Turtle and the Gryphon demonstrated the dance — hopping and spinning and waving their limbs in patterns that seemed to follow no logic whatsoever. They crashed into each other, stepped on each other's feet, and nearly fell into the sea twice. "It must be a very pretty dance," Alice said, politely. "Would you like to try it?" the Mock Turtle asked. "Very much indeed," said Alice, which was not entirely true but seemed like the right thing to say. They tried to teach her the steps, but the steps kept changing. Every time Alice thought she understood the pattern, the Gryphon would add a new move, or the Mock Turtle would insist that she had done it wrong, even though he could not agree with the Gryphon on what "right" was. Alice tried her best. She hopped when they said hop. She spun when they said spin. She threw imaginary lobsters into an imaginary sea. And somewhere in the middle of this ridiculous exercise, she started to laugh. Not a polite laugh. Not a careful, proper English girl's laugh. A real laugh — deep and helpless, the kind that makes your sides ache and your eyes stream. Because the dance was absurd, and the teachers were absurd, and Wonderland was absurd, and Alice was absurd for trying so hard to make sense of a place that had no sense to make. The Gryphon and the Mock Turtle stopped dancing and stared at her. "What's so funny?" the Mock Turtle asked, offended. "Everything," Alice said. "Absolutely everything." And she meant it as the highest compliment, though neither of them understood. Just then, a cry came from the distance: "The trial's beginning!" "Come on!" the Gryphon cried, seizing Alice by the hand and dragging her away at a run, without waiting for the end of the song or the dance or the Mock Turtle's tears. "What trial?" Alice asked, panting. "Come on!" was all the Gryphon said.

📚 Informational Text

The Trial: Justice Without Sense

The trial scene is Carroll's sharpest satire of the British legal system. Everything about the trial is absurd: the evidence makes no sense, witnesses contradict themselves, the jury writes down random things, and the King and Queen make up rules as they go along. 'Sentence first—verdict afterwards!' the Queen declares, reversing the entire purpose of a trial. Yet beneath the absurdity is a serious point: legal systems can be confusing, unfair, and intimidating, especially to children who have no power within them. Victorian trials were often theatrical performances where lawyers showed off their cleverness more than seeking truth or justice. Carroll, who studied law, knew this well. By making the trial completely nonsensical, he exposes how real trials can also fail to make sense or deliver justice. Alice's growing frustration mirrors how anyone feels when facing a system that claims to be logical but operates arbitrarily. Her final act of defiance—standing up to the court despite their threats—shows she's ready to wake up and return home.
