Today's Words

1. tragedy

Definition: A very sad event, especially one involving death or suffering. In literature, a story where things end badly despite the hero's best efforts.

"They arrived to find tragedy — Milady had struck, and no amount of riding could undo what had been done."

2. vengeance

Definition: The desire to punish someone who has hurt you or someone you love. Revenge.

"Something harder and more determined appeared in D'Artagnan's eyes — vengeance, cold and clear."

📖 Too Late - Part 2

The convent was quiet. Too quiet. D'Artagnan burst through the door and found the Mother Superior in the front hall, her face streaked with tears. "Where is Constance?" D'Artagnan demanded. The old woman could not speak. She simply pointed down the corridor. D'Artagnan ran. He threw open door after door until he found a small, plain room at the end of the hall. Constance was there, lying on a narrow bed. Her eyes were closed. Her face was pale and still. On the table beside her sat a cup of wine — half empty. D'Artagnan fell to his knees beside the bed. "Constance," he said. "Constance, please." She opened her eyes — barely. They were clouded and unfocused, but she recognized him. A faint smile crossed her lips. "You came," she whispered. "I came. I'm here. Everything is going to be fine." "The woman," Constance murmured. "The beautiful woman. She was so kind. She brought me wine..." Her voice faded. D'Artagnan looked at the cup. Poison. Milady had come as a friend, shared a drink, and left death behind her. It was exactly how Athos had described her: charm used as a weapon, kindness as a disguise. Athos, Porthos, and Aramis arrived at the doorway. One look at D'Artagnan's face told them everything. "No," Porthos said, his voice cracking. Aramis knelt beside the bed and took Constance's hand. He prayed quietly, his lips moving in words too soft to hear. Athos stood in the doorway, his face carved from stone. He had known this was possible. He had warned them about Milady, told them what she was capable of. But knowing did not make it easier. Nothing made this easier. Constance passed away quietly, with D'Artagnan holding her hand and Aramis praying beside her. She was brave to the end — she did not cry out, did not complain, did not curse the woman who had poisoned her. She simply closed her eyes and let go. D'Artagnan did not move for a long time. The room was very still. The candle flickered. Outside, night had fallen, and the convent garden was silver with moonlight. When he finally stood, something had changed in his eyes. The boyish eagerness was gone. The reckless grin was gone. What remained was something harder, colder, and more determined than anything his friends had seen in him before. "Where is Milady?" D'Artagnan asked. "She left the convent this morning," the Mother Superior said through her tears. "She rode north." D'Artagnan turned to his friends. "We find her," he said. "We end this." Athos nodded. His face was grim, but behind the grimness was something like relief — the relief of a man who had been running from a reckoning for years and had finally decided to face it. "Yes," Athos said. "We end this."

📚 Informational Text

Medicine in the 1600s

Medical care in the 1600s was primitive and often dangerous. Doctors did not understand germs or how diseases spread. They believed illness came from an imbalance of four 'humors' (blood, phlegm, yellow bile, black bile). Common treatments included bloodletting (cutting someone to let blood out), purging (giving medicines to make people vomit), and using leeches. Many medicines were made from herbs, some helpful and some poisonous. Surgery was performed without anesthesia or antibiotics. A simple wound could easily become infected and kill someone. In The Three Musketeers, injuries from sword fights and gunshots were often fatal.

Check Your Understanding:

2. What were three common medical treatments?

3. Why were sword wounds so dangerous?
