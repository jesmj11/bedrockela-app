📖 The Cry in the Night - Part 2

That night, Mary heard the crying again. She had heard it before — a faint, muffled sound drifting through the long corridors of Misselthwaite Manor in the dark hours after midnight. The first time, she thought it was the wind. The second time, she thought it was a ghost. Mrs. Medlock, when asked, said there was no crying and that Mary must have been dreaming. But Mary was not dreaming. She was wide awake, lying in her bed, listening to a sound that was unmistakably human: a child crying, somewhere deep inside the house. Mary Lennox had many faults — she was stubborn, impatient, and frequently rude — but she was not a coward, and she was unbearably curious. She got out of bed, wrapped a shawl around her shoulders, and slipped into the corridor. The house was dark and cold. Candles guttered in their holders, casting trembling circles of light on the walls. Mary followed the sound of crying down one hallway, then another, then up a flight of stairs she had never climbed before. The corridors grew narrower. The candles grew fewer. Cobwebs hung from the ceiling. She turned a corner and saw a sliver of light under a door. The crying was coming from behind it. Mary pushed the door open. The room was large and dim, lit by a single lamp. Heavy curtains covered the windows, blocking every trace of outside air. A fire burned low in the grate, making the room stuffy and hot. And in the center of the room, in an enormous four-poster bed piled with pillows and blankets, lay a boy. He was thin — thinner even than Mary had been when she arrived. His face was pale, almost white, with dark shadows under his eyes. His hair was dark and damp with sweat. He looked as though he had not been outside in years — as though sunlight were a rumor he had heard about but never experienced. He was crying. When he saw Mary in the doorway, he stopped. They stared at each other across the dim room — two pale, thin, unhappy children who had been hidden away in different parts of the same enormous house. "Who are you?" the boy demanded. His voice was imperious — the voice of someone accustomed to being obeyed. "Are you a ghost?" "No," Mary said. "Are you?" "I am Colin Craven," the boy said. "My father owns this house. Who are you?" "I am Mary Lennox. Your cousin, I suppose. I live here now." "No one told me there was a girl living here," Colin said, offended. "No one told me there was a boy living here, either," Mary shot back. "Why are you hidden in this room? Why were you crying?" Colin's face crumpled. "Because I am going to die," he said. "Everyone says so. I have a lump on my back that is going to grow into a hunch, and I shall become a hunchback, and then I shall die. So I stay in this room and wait." Mary looked at him — at his pale face, his wasted limbs, his eyes full of fear and self-pity — and she felt two things at once: a pang of sympathy and a flash of recognition. She was looking at herself. Not physically — Colin was a boy, and she was a girl — but emotionally. She recognized the same loneliness, the same anger, the same wall built out of misery and neglect. The difference was that Mary had started to tear her wall down. "How do you know you're going to die?" she asked. "Everyone says so," Colin repeated. "Everyone said I was the most disagreeable child ever seen," Mary said. "They were right, but that doesn't mean things can't change." Colin stared at her. No one had ever spoken to him like this — bluntly, without pity, without fear of upsetting him. "You're very strange," he said. "So are you," Mary said. They looked at each other. And something — small, fragile, and completely unexpected — sparked between them.

📚 Informational Text

Disability and Society in Victorian Times

Colin's fear of becoming a "hunchback" reflects Victorian attitudes toward disability. People with physical disabilities were often hidden away, either in institutions or kept in private rooms at home. Wealthy families felt ashamed if they had a disabled child. The term "invalid" (meaning sick or disabled person) literally means "not valid"—suggesting the person wasn't fully human. Many disabled people were capable of living full lives but weren't given the chance because society assumed they couldn't. Colin has internalized these beliefs—he thinks that if he becomes disabled, his life will be worthless. Dickon and Mary's acceptance of Colin, combined with the garden's healing effects, help Colin realize that his worth isn't determined by his body. The novel challenges Victorian prejudices by showing Colin becoming healthy not through medical treatment but through friendship, nature, and believing in himself. This was a radical message in its time.
