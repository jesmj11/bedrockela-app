📖 Colin Sees the Garden - Part 2

They brought him on a morning in late April, when the air was warm and the sky was the soft blue of a robin's egg. Dickon pushed the wheelchair — a wicker chair on wheels that had been stored in the attic, dusty and forgotten. Mary walked ahead, leading the way through the grounds. Colin sat wrapped in blankets, his thin face turned up to the sky like a flower turning toward the sun. "Where are we going?" Colin asked for the tenth time. "You'll see," Mary said. They reached the ivy-covered wall. Mary pushed aside the curtain of green and revealed the hidden door. She pulled the key from her pocket and turned it in the lock. "Close your eyes," Mary said. Colin closed them. Dickon pushed the wheelchair through the door. The wheels crunched softly on the gravel path. Birds were singing. The air smelled of earth and roses and something sweet that Colin could not identify. "Open them," Mary said. Colin opened his eyes. The garden had been transformed. Where Mary had first found only brown tangles and dead-looking vines, there was now an explosion of green. Shoots and leaves pushed up from every bed. The rosebushes — pruned and cleared of dead wood by Mary and Dickon — were covered with new growth, their branches thick with buds that would soon burst into color. Crocuses and daffodils blazed in patches of purple and yellow and white. Ivy climbed the walls in fresh, bright green. A carpet of tiny blue flowers covered the ground beneath the oldest tree. And above it all, the branches of the great trees had leafed out, creating a canopy of new green that filtered the sunlight into a thousand shifting, dancing patterns. Colin could not speak. He sat in his wheelchair and looked at the garden, and tears streamed down his face — not tears of sadness, but tears of something so overwhelming that he did not have a word for it. "This is the secret," Mary said. "This is the garden that was locked for ten years. Dickon and I have been bringing it back to life." "It's the most beautiful thing I've ever seen," Colin whispered. Dickon knelt beside the wheelchair. "See that?" he said, pointing to a rosebush covered in fat pink buds. "That'll be bloomin' in a fortnight. And those over there — those are the old climbing roses. They'll cover that whole wall in red by June." Colin reached out and touched a leaf. It was soft and cool and alive beneath his fingers. "I want to come here every day," Colin said. "You will," Mary said. "I want to help. I want to plant things and dig in the dirt and watch them grow." "You will," Dickon said. Colin looked at the garden — at the green shoots and the opening buds and the sunlight falling through the branches — and he said something that neither Mary nor Dickon expected. "I am going to get well," he said. His voice was not frightened or uncertain. It was calm and absolute. "I am going to get well, and I am going to stand up, and I am going to walk in this garden. I know it." The robin, watching from a branch above, sang one bright, clear note into the spring air.

📚 Informational Text

Animal Characters in The Secret Garden

Dickon's connection with animals is central to his character. He talks to the robin, raises a fox cub and crow, and understands the moorland creatures. This reflects old English and Celtic beliefs about people who have a special gift with animals—sometimes called "animal whisperers" or "beast speakers." In folk tales, such people are pure of heart and in harmony with nature. Dickon represents an idealized version of rural, working-class life—healthy, happy, connected to the earth, and uncorrupted by wealth or social climbing. His animals are symbols: the robin represents guidance and secrets revealed, the lamb represents innocence and spring, the fox represents wildness tamed by gentleness, the crow represents intelligence. When the animals accept Mary and Colin, it shows that they're becoming worthy—learning gentleness, patience, and respect for life. Nature becomes both teacher and judge in the story.
