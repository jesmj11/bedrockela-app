📖 The Letter - Part 2

Archibald Craven was in a valley in the Austrian Alps when the letter found him. He had been traveling for months — as he always did, moving from country to country, never staying anywhere long enough to feel at home. He visited beautiful places: the lakes of Italy, the mountains of Switzerland, the gardens of southern France. But beauty did not touch him. He moved through the world like a ghost, seeing everything and feeling nothing. He was a tall man, slightly stooped, with a face that might have been handsome if grief had not carved permanent lines around his eyes and mouth. His hair was gray at the temples, though he was not old. His eyes were dark and distant. He carried his sadness like a stone in his chest — always there, always heavy, impossible to set down. He thought about his wife every day. He thought about the garden she had loved. He thought about the son he had left behind — the son he could not look at without seeing the woman he had lost. He knew this was wrong. He knew he was failing as a father. But the grief was stronger than the knowledge, and it held him in place like chains. The letter was from Martha's mother, Susan Sowerby. She was a woman Craven had never met, but her name was known throughout the district as someone of uncommon sense and kindness. The letter was brief. "Dear Mr. Craven," it read. "I think you should come home. Something is happening at Misselthwaite that you ought to see for yourself. Your son needs you. Not the way he has needed you before — not as a patient needs a doctor. He needs you the way a garden needs the sun: to finish becoming what it was always meant to be. Come home, sir. Come home." Craven read the letter three times. Then he did something he had not done in years. He turned toward home. He did not know why. The letter said almost nothing. But something in the words — your son needs you the way a garden needs the sun — pierced the armor of his grief and touched the part of him that was still, despite everything, a father. He traveled fast. Train to Paris. Train to Calais. Ship across the Channel. Train to Yorkshire. Carriage from the station. The journey took three days, and for the first time in ten years, Craven was moving toward Misselthwaite instead of away from it. The manor appeared through the trees on an August evening. It looked the same — the gray stone, the empty windows, the moor stretching behind it like a purple sea. Nothing had changed. And yet, as Craven stepped out of the carriage and stood in the drive, he heard something he did not expect. Laughter. Coming from behind the garden wall.

📚 Informational Text

Symbolism: What the Garden Represents

The secret garden is the story's central symbol, representing multiple layered meanings. On one level, it's Mrs. Craven's memory—locking it was Mr. Craven's attempt to freeze time and avoid grief. On another level, it represents the children's inner lives—locked, neglected, seemingly dead but actually dormant and waiting to bloom. It represents healing—the process of recovery from emotional and physical wounds. It represents the natural world's power to restore and renew life. It represents secrets that can be either harmful (when locked) or healing (when opened and shared). It represents childhood itself—a magical time that adults lock away when they grow up but that can be recovered. The garden's transformation from dead to living mirrors every character's personal transformation. By the end, different readers might emphasize different meanings, which is the mark of a powerful symbol—it contains multiple truths simultaneously.
