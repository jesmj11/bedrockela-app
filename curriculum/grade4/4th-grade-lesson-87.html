Week 18

Today's Words

1. confrontation

Definition: A direct, face-to-face conflict between opposing people. The moment when tensions finally come to a head.

"The rooftop confrontation between Quasimodo and Frollo was sixteen years in the making — the foundling finally facing the man who had raised and controlled him."

2. reckoning

Definition: A time when someone must face the consequences of their actions. A moment of judgment and truth.

"Frollo's reckoning came on the rooftop — the place where he had kept Quasimodo imprisoned for sixteen years became the place where his power finally ended."

📖 The Rooftop - Part 2

Quasimodo exploded from the south tower door like a boulder launched from a catapult. He hit the first soldier before the man could draw his sword, sending him sprawling across the cobblestones. The second soldier swung a pike at his head; Quasimodo caught it in one hand and snapped it like a twig. But there were too many. They swarmed over him, and for all his strength, Quasimodo could not fight ten men at once. They pinned his arms. They forced him to his knees. And they dragged Esmeralda away, toward a waiting carriage. Then Quasimodo heard a sound that changed everything: Frollo's voice, cold and calm, giving orders from the doorway of the cathedral. The man who had raised him. The man who had told him the world was cruel. The man who had used him, manipulated him, and lied to him for sixteen years. Quasimodo stopped struggling. He looked at Frollo, and for the first time, he saw the man clearly — not as a master, not as a father, not as a protector, but as what he truly was: a man so consumed by his need for control that he would destroy an innocent girl rather than admit he was wrong. "You are the monster," Quasimodo said. Frollo's face went white. Quasimodo surged to his feet with a roar that shook the square. Soldiers flew in every direction. He grabbed Esmeralda, threw her over his shoulder, and ran — not into the cathedral this time, but UP. Up the exterior wall of Notre-Dame, scaling the stone face with his bare hands, climbing faster than any human should have been able to climb, powered by rage and love and the absolute certainty that he would not let Frollo win. Frollo followed. The Archdeacon was not strong, but he was driven by a fury that burned away caution and fear. He climbed the interior stairs, higher and higher, his black robes streaming behind him, his breath ragged, his eyes burning. They met on the rooftop. Paris spread below them in every direction — a sea of dark rooftops under a silver moon. The wind howled across the stone, and the gargoyles stared with their frozen grins. It was the highest point in the city, and there was nowhere left to climb. Frollo stepped onto the rooftop and faced Quasimodo across a gap of twenty feet. Esmeralda stood behind the bell ringer, pressed against the base of a tower. "Give her to me," Frollo said. "No." "She is a criminal. She is a witch. She has corrupted you." "She showed me kindness," Quasimodo said. "You showed me a cage." "I saved your life!" "You used my life." Frollo lunged. Not at Quasimodo — at Esmeralda, trying to reach past the hunchback to seize the girl. Quasimodo stepped into his path, and Frollo struck him across the face with a fury born of sixteen years of absolute authority being challenged for the first time. Quasimodo did not hit back. He simply stood. Immovable. A wall of flesh and bone and will. Frollo struck again. And again. Each blow landed on Quasimodo's hunched shoulders, his massive arms, his bowed head. Each blow accomplished nothing. "You cannot hurt me," Quasimodo said quietly. "You have hurt me every day for sixteen years, and I survived. You cannot do worse than what you have already done." Frollo staggered backward. His foot found the edge of the rooftop. The stone crumbled beneath him. He grabbed for the railing, the ledge, anything — and caught nothing but air. He fell. The square below was very far away. Quasimodo closed his eye and did not watch. When he opened it again, the rooftop was empty except for Esmeralda, the wind, and the gargoyles, who stared out at the city with their eternal, knowing grins.

📚 Informational Text

The Seine River: Paris's Lifeline

The Seine River flows through the center of Paris, dividing the city into the Left Bank and Right Bank. In medieval times, the river was essential for transportation, trade, and daily life. Boats carried goods up and down the river. Fishermen caught fish to sell in the markets. People drew water from the river for drinking, cooking, and washing (though this often made them sick). Notre-Dame sits on an island in the Seine, called Île de la Cité. This island was the original center of Paris. The river features prominently in Hugo's novel — it's where characters travel, hide, and sometimes meet their fate. Understanding the river's importance helps us see why the cathedral's location was so significant.
