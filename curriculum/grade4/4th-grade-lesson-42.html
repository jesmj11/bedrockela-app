📖 D'Artagnan Alone - Part 2

London was nothing like Paris. The streets were wider but grayer, and the people spoke a language D'Artagnan barely understood. He had learned a few words of English from Aramis, who spoke four languages, but it was not enough to ask for directions to a duke's palace. He showed Constance's letter to a merchant near the docks. The man studied the seal, raised his eyebrows, and pointed D'Artagnan toward a grand house near the river. The Duke of Buckingham's residence was magnificent — marble columns, gleaming windows, and guards in red uniforms at every door. D'Artagnan marched up to the front gate, covered in road dust and sea salt, his clothes torn, his face unshaven, and his eyes wild with exhaustion. "I must see the Duke of Buckingham," he said. "Immediately." The guards looked at this ragged young Frenchman and very nearly laughed. But D'Artagnan produced the letter with the queen's seal, and the laughter died instantly. Within minutes, he was standing in a private room before one of the most powerful men in England. The Duke of Buckingham was tall, elegant, and impeccably dressed. He had dark eyes and a sharp intelligence that D'Artagnan recognized immediately — this was a man who understood power. "You come from the queen?" Buckingham said, reading the letter. His expression changed as he understood the danger. "The diamond studs. The Cardinal means to expose her." "The ball is in seven days," D'Artagnan said. "If the queen cannot wear the studs, the king will know she gave them away. You must return them." Buckingham moved immediately. He unlocked a velvet case and produced the diamond studs — twelve brilliant stones set in a ribbon of blue silk. They were dazzling, even in the dim light of the room. Then he stopped. His face went pale. "Two are missing," Buckingham said. "Missing?" Buckingham examined the ribbon closely. "The clasps have been cut. Someone has stolen two of the studs." His jaw tightened. "Milady de Winter. She was here last week — a guest at a banquet. She must have taken them. She is an agent of the Cardinal." D'Artagnan felt the floor shift under him. Two missing studs. The Cardinal's agent had been one step ahead of them. "How long to replace them?" D'Artagnan asked. Buckingham was already calling for his jeweler. "Two days," he said. "My jeweler is the finest in London. He can make copies that even the queen herself could not tell from the originals." "Two days," D'Artagnan repeated. That left five days to cross the Channel and ride back to Paris. It would be close — desperately close. For two days, D'Artagnan paced the halls of Buckingham's residence like a caged animal. He ate without tasting. He slept in snatches. He checked the jeweler's progress every few hours, watching the old man set tiny diamonds into gold with steady, ancient hands. On the morning of the third day, the jeweler finished. Twelve diamond studs lay on blue velvet — ten originals and two copies so perfect that even Buckingham could not tell them apart. "Thank you," D'Artagnan said, tucking the velvet case inside his coat. "I will never forget this." Buckingham provided a fast horse, a bag of gold, and a letter of passage that would get D'Artagnan through English ports without delay. At the dock, he gripped D'Artagnan's hand. "Tell the queen that Buckingham is her servant," the duke said. "And tell her that her friendship is the greatest treasure I possess — worth more than any diamonds." D'Artagnan nodded, mounted his horse, and rode for the coast. The Channel crossing was rough — gray waves and howling wind — but D'Artagnan barely noticed. All he could think about was the road ahead. Five days to Paris. The Cardinal's agents would be watching every mile. But D'Artagnan had the studs, a fast horse, and a fire in his chest that no Cardinal could extinguish. He rode.

📚 Informational Text

The Royal Court: Where Power and Intrigue Meet

The royal court was where the king, queen, and nobles gathered. It was held in beautiful palaces with paintings, tapestries, and gold decorations everywhere. Court life involved fancy balls, elaborate meals, and careful etiquette (rules of polite behavior). But the court was also dangerous. People schemed and plotted to gain the king's favor. Spies listened to conversations. Rivals spread rumors to destroy each other's reputations. One wrong word could mean disgrace or exile. In The Three Musketeers, much of the drama happens at court, where political games are just as dangerous as sword fights.

Check Your Understanding:

1. What kinds of events happened at the royal court?

2. Why was the court considered dangerous?

3. What could happen if someone made a mistake at court?
