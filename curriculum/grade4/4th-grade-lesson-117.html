Today's Words

1. metamorphosis

Definition: A dramatic change in form or character. A complete transformation, like a caterpillar becoming a butterfly.

"The metamorphosis was complete — the sour girl and the invalid boy had become healthy, joyful children."

2. anticipation

Definition: The feeling of excitement about something that is going to happen. Looking forward to a future event.

"The children lived in anticipation of Mr. Craven's return — the moment when the secret would finally be revealed."

📖 Stronger Every Day - Part 2

By August, the secret was becoming very difficult to keep. Colin ate so much food that the cook began ordering extra supplies. He outgrew his clothes. His face, which the servants remembered as pale and thin, was now round and pink. His voice, which had been a whiny whisper, now carried through the hallways with startling volume. The servants whispered among themselves. Something extraordinary was happening to Master Colin, and no one could explain it. The doctor came, examined Colin, and left shaking his head in bewilderment. "There is nothing wrong with this child," the doctor announced. "His spine is straight. His lungs are clear. His muscles are developing normally. I cannot account for it." "Fresh air," Mary muttered under her breath. Colin still pretended to be ill when the servants were around. He slumped in his wheelchair and spoke in a weak voice and refused to eat much at meals, then sneaked to the garden and devoured the enormous lunches Dickon brought from home. "This is ridiculous," Mary said. "You're pretending to be sick inside the house and running around like a wild animal outside it." "I'm preserving the surprise," Colin said. "When my father comes home, I want to walk up to him on my own two feet. If the servants write and tell him I'm better, he'll think it's another one of their stories. He has to see it for himself." Mary understood. Words would not be enough for Mr. Craven. He had spent ten years believing his son was dying. Only seeing the truth — standing in front of him, alive and strong — would break through the wall of grief. So they kept the secret a little longer. Colin walked in the garden. Mary tended the roses. Dickon played his pipe and the animals gathered and the flowers bloomed and the summer deepened into something golden and warm. One evening, as the sun was setting and the garden was bathed in amber light, the three children sat under the apple tree and talked about what would happen when the secret was finally revealed. "My father will cry," Colin said. He said it matter-of-factly, without self-pity. "He's been sad for ten years. When he sees me, and when he sees this garden — my mother's garden — he'll cry." "That's all right," Mary said. "Sometimes crying is how healing starts." "Like rain," Dickon said. "The moor needs rain to grow. Maybe people do too." They sat in the fading light, three children in a garden that had brought them all back to life, and waited for the moment when the last door would finally open.

📚 Informational Text

The Power of Friendship

Friendship is the engine of transformation in The Secret Garden. Mary arrives friendless and believes she doesn't need friends. Her first friendship with the robin opens her heart to connection. Martha's sisterly warmth gives Mary her first experience of affection. Dickon's genuine kindness and shared love of nature creates Mary's first real friendship with a peer. Colin and Mary's friendship is the most complex—they bond over being lonely, angry, and misunderstood, then help each other grow beyond those limitations. The friends give each other what adults couldn't provide: acceptance, shared purpose, encouragement, and belief in each other's potential. They also hold each other accountable—Mary calls Colin on his tantrums, Colin challenges Mary's bossiness. This is healthy friendship: supporting each other's best selves while refusing to enable each other's worst behaviors. The novel suggests that peer relationships can be as important as family relationships in shaping who we become.

Check Your Understanding:

1. How does Mary's capacity for friendship develop?

2. What makes Mary and Colin's friendship particularly powerful?

3. What does the novel suggest about the importance of peer relationships?
