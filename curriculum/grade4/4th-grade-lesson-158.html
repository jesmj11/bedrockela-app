Today's Words

1. contradiction

Definition: A combination of statements or ideas that are opposite or inconsistent with each other.

"The Hatter said: 'I see what I eat' is NOT the same as 'I eat what I see.' What seemed like a contradiction turned out to be a real logical difference."

2. perpetual

Definition: Never ending or changing. Continuing forever without stopping.

"The tea party was perpetual — stuck at six o'clock forever because the Hatter had quarreled with Time."

📖 A Mad Tea-Party - Part 2

There was a table set out under a tree in front of the March Hare's house, and the March Hare and the Hatter were having tea at it. A Dormouse was sitting between them, fast asleep, and the other two were using it as a cushion, resting their elbows on it and talking over its head. "Very uncomfortable for the Dormouse," thought Alice. "Only, as it's asleep, I suppose it doesn't mind." The table was enormous — long enough for a dozen guests — but the three were all crowded together at one corner of it. "No room! No room!" they cried out when they saw Alice coming. "There's PLENTY of room!" said Alice indignantly, and she sat down in a large armchair at the head of the table. "Have some wine," the March Hare offered in an encouraging tone. Alice looked all over the table, but there was nothing on it but tea. "I don't SEE any wine," she said. "There isn't any," said the March Hare. "Then it wasn't very civil of you to offer it," said Alice angrily. "It wasn't very civil of you to sit down without being invited," said the March Hare. The Hatter opened his eyes very wide. He was a peculiar little man with a large head and wild eyes, wearing an enormous top hat with a price tag still stuck in the band. He leaned across the table and said: "Why is a raven like a writing-desk?" "A riddle!" said Alice. "I believe I can guess that." "Do you mean that you think you can find out the answer to it?" asked the March Hare. "Exactly so," said Alice. "Then you should say what you mean," the March Hare went on. "I do," Alice hastily replied. "At least — at least I mean what I say — that's the same thing, you know." "Not the same thing a bit!" said the Hatter. "You might just as well say that 'I see what I eat' is the same thing as 'I eat what I see'!" "You might just as well say," added the March Hare, "that 'I like what I get' is the same thing as 'I get what I like'!" Alice had no answer for this. The logic was absurd, and yet — she could not find the flaw. Saying what you mean and meaning what you say SOUNDED like the same thing, but perhaps they were not. Language, in Wonderland, was slippery. "What day of the month is it?" the Hatter asked, looking at his watch. "The fourth," said Alice. "Two days wrong!" the Hatter sighed. "I told you butter wouldn't suit the works!" he added, looking angrily at the March Hare. "It was the BEST butter," the March Hare meekly replied. "Yes, but some crumbs must have got in as well," the Hatter grumbled. Alice looked at the watch. It was a watch that told the day of the month but not the hour. "What a funny watch!" she said. "Why should it?" the Hatter said. "Does YOUR watch tell you what year it is?" "Of course not," Alice replied. "But that's because it stays the same year for such a long time together." "Which is just the case with MINE," said the Hatter. Alice was dreadfully puzzled. The Hatter's words seemed to make sense, but they didn't — or perhaps they did, but in a way her brain couldn't quite follow. It was like trying to catch water in your hands. "The Dormouse is asleep again," the Hatter observed, and poured a little hot tea onto its nose. The Dormouse shook its head impatiently and said, without opening its eyes, "Of course, of course. Just what I was going to remark myself." "Tell us a story!" the March Hare demanded. "Yes, please do!" Alice pleaded. "And be quick about it," added the Hatter, "or you'll be asleep before it's done."

📚 Informational Text

The Pool of Tears: Emotions in Wonderland

When Alice grows huge and cries, her tears create a pool so large that when she shrinks, she nearly drowns in it. This absurd image has a deeper meaning: sometimes our emotions can overwhelm us, especially when we're young and experiencing big feelings for the first time. Alice is lonely, confused, and frightened—natural responses to finding herself in a strange place where nothing makes sense. But she also keeps trying to solve problems and move forward. Victorian children were taught to suppress emotions and maintain composure at all times. Crying in public was considered shameful, especially for girls who were expected to be cheerful and pleasant. By showing Alice crying enough to create an ocean, Carroll acknowledges that children have real, powerful emotions that can't always be controlled. The pool of tears becomes both a problem (Alice almost drowns) and a solution (it carries her to new adventures). Emotions can be difficult, but they're also part of being human.
