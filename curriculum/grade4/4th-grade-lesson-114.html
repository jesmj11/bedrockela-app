📖 Dickon's Creatures - Part 2

Dickon brought the garden to life in ways that went beyond plants. His animals came with him every day — not because he asked them to, but because they wanted to be where he was. The crow, Soot, perched on the garden wall and cawed warnings whenever anyone approached. The fox, Captain, curled up in the sunniest spot and slept with one eye open. Two squirrels chased each other through the branches. A lamb that Dickon had raised from a baby followed him everywhere, bleating softly. And the robin, of course. The robin who had started it all — who had led Mary to the key, who had watched over the garden through ten lonely years. He sang from the highest branch of the apple tree, and his song was the garden's heartbeat. Colin was fascinated by Dickon's gift with animals. "How do you DO it?" he asked, watching a squirrel eat a nut from Dickon's open palm. "How do you make them trust you?" "I don't make them do anything," Dickon said. "I just sit still and wait. Animals can tell if you're safe. They read you — your body, your breath, your heartbeat. If you're calm and quiet and honest, they'll come to you. If you're scared or angry or pretending, they'll know." "Is it the same with people?" Colin asked. Dickon thought about this. "Aye," he said. "More or less. People are just animals who've forgotten how to listen." Mary watched Dickon with the animals and thought about how different he was from everyone else she had known. In India, the servants had been afraid of her. At Misselthwaite, the servants were afraid of Colin. The adults in their lives were afraid of everything — of illness, of grief, of breaking rules, of making mistakes. Dickon was not afraid of anything, because Dickon did not try to control anything. He let the animals come and go as they pleased. He let the plants grow in their own time. He let the wind blow and the rain fall and the seasons change without complaint. He lived in the world as it was, not as he wished it to be. "You're the wisest person I know," Mary told him one afternoon. Dickon looked surprised. "I'm not wise," he said. "I'm just paying attention." "That IS wisdom," Mary said. Colin, who was doing his daily walk around the garden — fifteen laps now, up from three the week before — called out from across the path. "When I'm a scientist, I'm going to study why being outside makes people well. There must be a scientific explanation for it." "Maybe," Dickon said. "Or maybe some things just work, and you don't need to know why." "That," Colin said, "is the most unscientific thing I have ever heard." "And yet," Mary said, gesturing at the garden, at the blooming roses and the singing birds and the healthy boy walking on his own two legs, "it worked." Colin opened his mouth to argue, then closed it and laughed. He was getting better at laughing. He was getting better at everything.

📚 Informational Text

Magic and Miracles in the Garden

Colin names the garden's power "Magic," but what does he mean? The novel uses "Magic" to describe several overlapping ideas: the natural growth cycle (seeds sprouting, flowers blooming), the power of positive belief, the healing effect of nature, the strength of friendship, and spiritual forces beyond human understanding. Burnett was influenced by Christian Science, New Thought, and Eastern philosophies that emphasized the mind's power over the body and a life force connecting all living things. The "Magic" isn't supernatural in the fantasy sense—it's the wonder and power of life itself. When Colin conducts his "scientific experiments" with Magic, he's trying to understand and harness natural laws. Modern readers might call it a combination of psychology (positive thinking), biology (fresh air and exercise), and ecology (connection to living systems). The novel suggests there's something sacred about growth, healing, and transformation—something that deserves reverence even if we don't fully understand it.
