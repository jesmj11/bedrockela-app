Today's Words

1. ordinary

Definition: Normal, usual, with no special or distinctive features. Everyday and unremarkable.

"The riverbank was perfectly ordinary — grass, daisies, a book. Everything was exactly as it had been. And yet Alice felt different."

2. perspective

Definition: A particular way of seeing or thinking about something. Your point of view shaped by your experiences.

"Alice's perspective had shifted. The riverbank was the same, but she saw it with eyes that had been to Wonderland."

📖 Waking Up - Part 2

Alice opened her eyes. She was lying on the riverbank, with her head in her sister's lap. Dead leaves were fluttering down from the trees and landing on her face — that was what the cards had been. Just leaves. "Wake up, Alice dear!" said her sister. "What a long sleep you've had!" "Oh, I've had such a curious dream!" said Alice, and she began to tell her sister all about it — the White Rabbit, the pool of tears, the Caucus Race, the Caterpillar, the Duchess, the Cheshire Cat, the mad tea party, the Queen's croquet, and the trial. She told it all, tumbling over her words in her excitement to get every detail out. Her sister listened with a patient smile. The sun was warm. The river sparkled. The afternoon was golden and peaceful and completely, perfectly ordinary. "It was just a dream," Alice's sister said gently. "I know," Alice said. She sat up and brushed the leaves from her dress. "But it was a very REAL sort of dream." She looked around at the riverbank — the grass, the daisies, the book her sister was reading. Everything was exactly as it had been before she fell asleep. Nothing had changed. And yet Alice felt different. She felt as though she had traveled a great distance and come back, not to a different place but to the same place seen with different eyes. The riverbank was the same. The daisies were the same. But Alice was not. She had been to a world where nothing made sense, where the rules changed every five minutes, where the most powerful people were the least reasonable, and where a simple question — "Why?" — was the most dangerous thing you could say. She had been small and large and confused and frightened and brave. She had been told she was nobody and told herself she was somebody. She had stood before a Queen and said: you are only a pack of cards. "Was it really just a dream?" Alice asked. "What else would it be?" her sister said. Alice considered this. Dreams are strange things. They feel real while you are in them. They teach you things you did not know you were learning. And sometimes, when you wake up, the person who opens their eyes is not quite the same person who closed them. "I don't know what else it would be," Alice said. "But I know it meant something." Her sister smiled and went back to her book. Alice lay on the grass and watched the clouds move across the sky, and she thought about Wonderland — not as a place she had visited, but as a question she was still answering. Who are you? She was Alice. That much she knew. But who Alice WAS — that was a question she would spend the rest of her life exploring, and the answer would keep changing, and that was exactly as it should be.

📚 Informational Text

Alice as a Feminist Hero

Although written in 1865, Alice can be read as a surprisingly feminist text. Alice is curious, brave, independent, and willing to challenge authority—qualities Victorian girls were actively discouraged from displaying. She doesn't wait to be rescued; she solves her own problems. She argues with powerful figures like the Queen and Caterpillar. She questions nonsensical rules rather than blindly obeying. She trusts her own judgment even when everyone tells her she's wrong. At the end, she literally shouts down the entire court and wakes herself up—she escapes through her own power. This was radical for a children's book in 1865, when most female characters were passive, obedient, and waiting to be saved by men. Carroll's real-life friend Alice Liddell was bright and spirited, and he captured those qualities in his fictional Alice. The book suggests that girls can be smart, brave, questioning, and strong. This message resonated with early feminists and continues to inspire readers today who see Alice as a girl who refuses to be controlled.
