📖 Vocabulary

Week 14

Today's Words

1. obedience

Definition: Doing what you are told without questioning. Following orders because you believe the person giving them has the right to command you.

"Quasimodo obeyed Frollo's command to capture Esmeralda, even though something inside him whispered it was wrong."

2. conscience

Definition: The inner sense of right and wrong. The small voice inside you that tells you when something is not right.

"Something inside Quasimodo — a small, new feeling like a seed breaking through soil — whispered that capturing Esmeralda was wrong."

📖 Frollo's Command - Part 2

"There is a girl," Frollo said. "A dancer. She performs in the square. You saw her today." Quasimodo's heart thumped. The girl who had wiped the mud from his face. The girl who had called his name good. "She is dangerous," Frollo said. "She spreads ideas that threaten the order of the city. She lives among criminals and outcasts. She has bewitched the people with her dancing, making them forget their duty to God and to the law." Quasimodo frowned. The girl had not seemed dangerous. She had seemed kind. "I need you to bring her to me," Frollo said. "Tonight, under cover of darkness. No one must see you. Can you do this?" Quasimodo hesitated. Something inside him — something small and new, like a seed that had just broken through the soil — whispered that this was wrong. The girl had helped him. She had shown him kindness when no one else would. How could she be dangerous? But Frollo was waiting. And Frollo was never wrong. "Yes, Master," Quasimodo said. That night, Quasimodo descended from the cathedral in darkness. He moved through the streets like a shadow — quick, silent, invisible. Despite his size and his hunched body, he could climb walls, leap between rooftops, and drop from ledges that would break an ordinary man's bones. The cathedral had trained him. He was as agile as a cat and as strong as three men. He found Esmeralda walking home through a narrow street near the river. She was alone except for Djali, her goat. The little bells on her ankles chimed softly in the cold air. Quasimodo stepped out of the shadows. Esmeralda saw him and stopped. She recognized his silhouette — the hunched back, the enormous head. "Quasimodo?" she said. "From the festival?" He did not answer. He lunged forward and seized her arm. Esmeralda screamed. The sound echoed through the empty street. Quasimodo was confused — he had expected her to come quietly. He did not know how to be gentle; no one had ever taught him. He held her arm too tightly, and she twisted and kicked, and Djali rammed his legs with her tiny horns. Then hoofbeats thundered down the street. A horse and rider appeared from the darkness — a soldier in the uniform of the King's Guard, with a drawn sword and a commanding voice. "Release her!" the soldier shouted. Quasimodo looked up and saw steel flashing in the torchlight. The soldier swung. Quasimodo let go of Esmeralda and staggered backward. More soldiers appeared. They surrounded him, and though Quasimodo was enormously strong, he did not fight. He did not know how to fight people. He only knew how to obey. The soldiers seized him and threw him to the ground. The young captain — his name was Phoebus — helped Esmeralda to her feet. "Are you hurt?" he asked. "No," Esmeralda said, shaking. "Who sent him? Why would he do this?" No one asked Quasimodo that question. No one asked Quasimodo anything. He was dragged away in chains, and Frollo's name was never mentioned. Frollo, watching from a window high above, pulled back into the shadows and said nothing.

📚 Informational Text

The Roma People: Europe's Outcasts

The Roma (sometimes called Gypsies, though many consider this term offensive) have lived in Europe for over 600 years. They originally came from India and traveled in caravans, working as musicians, dancers, metalworkers, and traders. Because they moved from place to place and had different customs, many Europeans feared and mistrusted them. Laws banned Roma from entering cities. They were accused of witchcraft and theft, often unfairly. In Hugo's novel, Esmeralda is Roma. Hugo shows how prejudice treats an entire group of people as criminals just because they are different. Understanding this history helps us see why the Roma characters in the story face such cruelty.
