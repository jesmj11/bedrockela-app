📖 The Rules of Wonderland - Part 2

Alice made a list in her head of all the rules she had encountered in Wonderland. Rule 1: Drink things labeled "DRINK ME." Eat things labeled "EAT ME." (This was how you changed size, which was how you got through doors, which was how you got anywhere at all. The first rule of Wonderland was: accept what you are given and see what happens.) Rule 2: Everyone is mad. The Cheshire Cat had said so, and as far as Alice could tell, the Cat was right. Madness in Wonderland was not an exception — it was the standard. The sane were the strangers. Rule 3: Time can be broken. The Hatter had quarreled with Time, and Time had stopped at six o'clock forever. In Wonderland, time was not a fact but a relationship. If you offended it, it could punish you. If you pleased it, it could do anything you liked. Rule 4: Words do not mean what you think they mean. They mean what the speaker WANTS them to mean, which is different for everyone, which is why conversations in Wonderland always went in circles. Rule 5: The Queen is always right, even when she is wrong. Especially when she is wrong. Rule 6: Sentence first, verdict afterwards. Alice examined this list and frowned. These were not really rules. They were the ABSENCE of rules — a world where nothing was stable, nothing was fair, and nothing could be relied upon. And yet, Wonderland had one rule that was real, even if no one said it out loud: Rule 7: The person who asks questions is more powerful than the person who demands answers. The Queen demanded. The Hatter puzzled. The Caterpillar interrogated. The Duchess moralized. Everyone in Wonderland TOLD Alice things. But Alice was the only one who ASKED. "Who ARE you?" the Caterpillar had demanded. It was the most important question in Wonderland, and the Caterpillar had asked it as a challenge — as if Alice should be ashamed of not knowing. But Alice was not ashamed. She was ten years old. She was still becoming who she would be. Not knowing who you are is not a failure. It is a beginning. "I am Alice," she said to the empty riverbank. "I am the girl who asks questions. And that is enough." The river flowed on. The sun moved across the sky. And Alice sat on the bank and felt, for the first time since waking up, that she understood something important — not about Wonderland, but about herself.

📚 Informational Text

Mathematical Concepts Hidden in Wonderland

As a mathematician, Carroll embedded mathematical ideas throughout Alice. Alice's size changes explore proportion and scale—if you double in size, do you double in strength? In mass? The concept of negative numbers (controversial in Carroll's time) appears when Alice tries to subtract from zero. The Mock Turtle's lessons parody Victorian education but also reference real mathematical terms. The Caucus Race (where everyone wins and all must have prizes) satirizes certain voting systems and game theories. The Hatter's riddle 'Why is a raven like a writing desk?' has no answer—it's a mathematical proof by contradiction, showing that some questions have no solutions. Time stopping at six o'clock plays with the idea of infinity and eternal loops. The Cheshire Cat's gradual disappearance explores set theory—what's the smallest part of something that can still represent the whole? Carroll wasn't trying to teach math directly; he was playing with mathematical concepts in imaginative ways that make readers think.
