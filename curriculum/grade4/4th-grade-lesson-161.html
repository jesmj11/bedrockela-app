📖 The Queen's Garden - Part 2

The garden was even lovelier than Alice had imagined from peeping through the keyhole. Bright flowers bloomed in perfect beds. Fountains sparkled in the sunshine. The air smelled of roses. But something was wrong. Near the entrance, three gardeners were clustered around a rose tree. They were shaped like playing cards — flat, rectangular, with arms and legs and painted faces. These particular cards were the Two, the Five, and the Seven of Spades, and they were painting the roses. They were painting white roses red. "Would you tell me," Alice said, very shyly, "why you are painting those roses?" Five and Seven said nothing, but looked at Two. Two began, in a low voice, "Why, the fact is, you see, Miss, this here ought to have been a RED rose-tree, and we put a white one in by mistake. And if the Queen was to find it out, we should all have our heads cut off, you know." "The Queen?" Alice said. "The Queen of Hearts," Two whispered. "She ordered red roses. We planted white ones. If she sees them —" He drew his finger across his neck in a gesture that needed no explanation. "That seems very excessive," Alice said. "That's the Queen," Five said gloomily. At that moment, a great procession entered the garden. First came ten soldiers carrying clubs — they were all shaped like playing cards, just like the gardeners. Then came ten courtiers covered in diamonds. Then the royal children — ten of them, decorated with hearts. Then came the guests — mostly kings and queens from other decks of cards. Then came the Knave of Hearts, carrying the King's crown on a red velvet cushion. And last of all came the KING AND QUEEN OF HEARTS themselves. The Queen was a small, furious woman with a red face and a voice that could shatter glass. Her eyes darted over the garden like searchlights, looking for something — anything — to be angry about. She stopped in front of Alice. "Who IS this?" the Queen demanded, pointing. "My name is Alice, so please your Majesty," Alice said politely. She added to herself: "Why, they're only a pack of cards, after all. I needn't be afraid of them." "And who are THESE?" the Queen said, pointing at the three gardeners, who were lying flat on their faces on the ground. "How should I know?" said Alice, surprised at her own courage. "It's no business of MINE." The Queen turned crimson with fury. "Off with her head! Off —" "Nonsense!" said Alice, very loudly and decidedly. The Queen was silent. Everyone was silent. Nobody in Wonderland had ever said "Nonsense" to the Queen before. It was such a novel experience that the Queen did not seem to know how to react. She stood there, mouth open, for a full five seconds. Then the King tugged her sleeve and murmured, "Consider, my dear: she is only a child!" The Queen turned away, and Alice breathed. "Can you play croquet?" the Queen shouted at Alice. "Yes!" shouted Alice. "Come on, then!" the Queen roared, and the entire court swept away toward the croquet ground, leaving Alice to follow in a daze. "They're only cards," she told herself again. But her heart was pounding all the same.

📚 Informational Text

The Cheshire Cat: Grinning Madness

The Cheshire Cat is perhaps Wonderland's most iconic character—famous for his enormous grin and his ability to vanish and reappear at will, sometimes leaving only his smile behind. He represents the idea that in a mad world, perhaps being mad is the most sensible approach. When Alice asks for directions, the Cat points out that where you go doesn't matter if you don't know where you want to end up. This sounds like nonsense but is actually profound wisdom: if you have no destination in mind, any path will do. The Cat is also the only character who seems genuinely friendly to Alice without being threatening or rude. He explains things patiently, unlike most Wonderland creatures. His disappearing act—especially leaving just the grin—plays with philosophy: can a smile exist without a face? This relates to bigger questions about what things really are versus what we perceive. The Cheshire Cat shows that madness and wisdom aren't always opposites.
