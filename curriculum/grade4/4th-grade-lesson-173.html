Today's Words

1. moral

Definition: A lesson about right and wrong learned from a story or experience. The deeper meaning behind events.

"The Duchess found a moral in everything — but her morals were forced and false. Alice learned that real morals can't be crammed into everything."

2. conviction

Definition: A firmly held belief or opinion. The strong feeling that something is true.

"Alice spoke with quiet conviction when she said 'I don't think that's right.' Not loudly, not angrily — just certainly."

📖 The Duchess's Morals - Part 2

Alice's sister had gone inside for tea, leaving Alice alone on the riverbank with her thoughts. And what thoughts they were. She found herself going back through Wonderland piece by piece, examining it the way she might examine a puzzle box — turning it over, pressing its edges, looking for the hidden mechanism that made it work. She thought about the Duchess, who found a moral in everything. "Be what you would seem to be," the Duchess had said. Or, put more simply: "Never imagine yourself not to be otherwise than what it might appear to others that what you were or might have been was not otherwise than what you had been would have appeared to them to be otherwise." Alice had not understood this at the time. Now, turning it over in her mind, she decided it still made no sense — but the Duchess's impulse was not entirely wrong. She WANTED things to have meaning. She just couldn't find it clearly. "Perhaps," Alice thought, "the problem isn't that Wonderland has no meaning. The problem is that everyone in Wonderland INSISTS on the wrong meaning — or insists that their nonsense IS meaning — instead of admitting when something doesn't make sense." The Duchess found morals in everything, whether they belonged there or not. The Hatter asked riddles with no answers and blamed Alice for not guessing them. The Queen declared sentences before verdicts and called it justice. Everyone in Wonderland was absolutely certain about things they had no right to be certain about. Alice had been the uncertain one. She had asked questions. She had said "I don't understand." She had admitted when she was confused. And that, Alice was beginning to realize, was not weakness. It was honesty. The world — the real world, not Wonderland — was full of people who talked like the Duchess: confidently, loudly, in sentences that sounded important but meant nothing. It was full of people who made rules like the Queen: arbitrary, unfair, enforced by fear instead of reason. It was full of Hatters, asking questions they couldn't answer, and Mock Turtles, mourning things that never existed. But it was also full of people like Alice. People who asked why. People who challenged nonsense. People who stood up in the middle of a crooked court and said: this is wrong. Those people were rare. And they were important. More important than any Queen. Alice picked a daisy and pulled its petals off, one by one. "He loves me. He loves me not." She stopped. "That's nonsense too," she said. "A flower doesn't know anything about love." She laughed. Then she pulled the last petal anyway, because some nonsense is worth keeping.

📚 Informational Text

The Real Alice: Alice Liddell

Alice Pleasance Liddell was born in 1852 to a wealthy, prominent family. Her father was dean of Christ Church College at Oxford, where Lewis Carroll taught. Alice had two sisters—Lorina (older) and Edith (younger)—who also appear in the story as the Lory and the Eaglet. The real Alice was known for being spirited, curious, and strong-willed. Carroll's friendship with the Liddell family ended mysteriously when Alice was about 11—historians still debate why, but it may have involved Carroll's growing attachment to Alice being seen as inappropriate. Alice grew up to marry a cricket player named Reginald Hargreaves and had three sons. She lived a long life—dying in 1934 at age 82—long enough to see her childhood immortalized in one of the world's most famous books. In 1928, she sold Carroll's original handwritten manuscript of Alice to pay bills. She was reportedly somewhat ambivalent about her fame, noting that being 'Alice' overshadowed her real identity.
