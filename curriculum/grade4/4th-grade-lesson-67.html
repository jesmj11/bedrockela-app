Today's Words

1. outcast

Definition: A person who has been rejected by society or a group. Someone who does not belong to the accepted community.

"The Court of Miracles was home to everyone Paris had rejected — the outcasts who had nowhere else to go."

2. vouch

Definition: To say that someone can be trusted. To take responsibility for another person.

"Esmeralda vouched for the poet Gringoire, saving his life by taking responsibility for him even though she did not know him."

📖 The Poet - Part 2

Pierre Gringoire was having the worst day of his life, which was saying something, because Pierre Gringoire had a great many bad days. He was a poet — thin, hungry, and perpetually confused. He had written a play for the Festival of Fools, a grand theatrical masterpiece that he was certain would make him famous. Instead, the crowd had ignored it completely. They had shouted over his actors, thrown things at the stage, and eventually wandered off to watch Quasimodo's crowning, leaving Gringoire alone with his unfinished play and his broken dreams. Now it was evening, the festival was over, and Gringoire was wandering the dark streets of Paris with no money, no dinner, and no idea where he was going. "I am a genius," he told himself. "The world simply does not appreciate me yet." This was the kind of thing Gringoire told himself often. It was rarely true, but it kept him going. He wandered into a part of Paris he did not recognize — narrow streets, broken lanterns, shadows that moved in ways shadows should not. He was so lost in self-pity that he did not notice the figures closing in around him until it was too late. A hand seized his collar. A torch flared in his face. He found himself surrounded by the strangest collection of people he had ever seen: men and women in ragged clothes, some missing limbs, some scarred, some painted, all of them staring at him with suspicious eyes. "Who are you?" a deep voice demanded. "I am Pierre Gringoire!" he squeaked. "A poet! A man of letters! A — please don't hurt me." He had stumbled into the Court of Miracles. The Court of Miracles was the hidden heart of Paris's underworld — the place where the city's outcasts lived. Here, beggars who pretended to be blind during the day could see perfectly well. Cripples who dragged themselves through the streets on their bellies stood up and walked. It was called the Court of Miracles because the "miracles" were that everyone's disabilities vanished the moment they stepped through the hidden gates. But not all of them were faking. Many were genuinely poor, genuinely desperate, genuinely cast out by a society that had no room for people who did not fit. They had built their own community in the shadows — with their own laws, their own leaders, and their own code of honor. Their leader was a large, shrewd man called Clopin, who wore a crown made of tin and ruled the Court with rough justice and a sharp tongue. "A poet," Clopin said, circling Gringoire. "We do not like outsiders. Outsiders bring trouble. The punishment for trespassing in the Court of Miracles is... severe." Gringoire went very pale. "However," Clopin continued, "we are not barbarians. If someone among us will vouch for you — take responsibility for you — you may live." The outcasts looked at each other. No one stepped forward. Gringoire's life was ticking away like sand in an hourglass. Then Esmeralda appeared. "I will vouch for him," she said calmly. Djali the goat bleated in agreement. Clopin raised an eyebrow. "You know this man?" "No," Esmeralda said. "But he has done nothing wrong except get lost. And getting lost is not a crime." There was a murmur through the crowd. Clopin considered this, then shrugged. "Very well. The poet lives. But he is your responsibility, Esmeralda. If he causes trouble, it falls on you." Gringoire collapsed with relief. "Thank you," he gasped. "Thank you, thank you. I am forever in your debt." "You can repay me by not being useless," Esmeralda said. Gringoire was not at all sure he could keep that promise. But he was alive, and that was enough for now.

📚 Informational Text

Medieval Justice: Cruel and Public

In medieval times, punishments were harsh and public. If you stole bread, you might lose a hand. If you lied in court, your tongue could be cut out. The pillory was a common punishment: a wooden frame that locked a person's head and hands in place while crowds threw rotten food and rocks at them. Public punishment served two purposes: it punished the criminal and warned others not to break the law. Trials were often unfair. Poor people had no lawyers. Torture was used to force confessions. If a judge believed you were guilty, you were guilty — there was no appeal. The system was based on fear, not fairness.
