📖 "I Shall Live Forever!" - Part 2

Colin came to the garden every day, and every day he grew stronger. It did not happen all at once. The first few days, he could only sit in his wheelchair and watch while Mary and Dickon worked. But watching was enough. The garden gave him something his dark room never had: a reason to pay attention. There was always something new — a bud that had opened overnight, a butterfly landing on a leaf, a worm pushing through the soil. The world was alive, ferociously alive, and Colin drank it in like water. Then he began to participate. Small things at first — passing tools to Dickon, sorting seeds into packets, holding a watering can while Mary filled it. His hands trembled. His arms tired quickly. But every day, they tired a little less. "Dig here," Dickon said one morning, handing Colin a trowel and pointing to a patch of soft earth. "Not too deep. Just enough to make a hole for the seed." Colin took the trowel. His hand shook. He drove the blade into the soil and scooped out a small hole. It was the first time he had dug in the earth in his life. "Now drop the seed in," Dickon said. Colin dropped a seed into the hole and covered it with soil, pressing gently with his palm. He looked up at Dickon and Mary, and his face was shining. "I planted something," he said. "I made something alive." "No," Dickon said, grinning. "Tha' gave it a chance to become alive. The seed does the rest." One afternoon, after weeks of coming to the garden, Colin gripped the arms of his wheelchair and pushed himself to his feet. The world swayed. His legs, unused for ten years, trembled like young tree branches in a wind. Mary and Dickon stood on either side of him, ready to catch him, but they did not touch him. This was something Colin had to do himself. He stood. He swayed. He stood. "I'm standing," he said. His voice was barely a whisper. "I'm standing up." "Aye," Dickon said softly. "Tha' is." Colin raised his face to the sky. The sun fell on his skin. The wind moved through his hair. The garden rustled and whispered around him. "I shall live forever and ever and ever!" Colin shouted. His voice rang out across the garden, startling the birds and making the robin chirp in protest from his branch. "I shall live to be a hundred! I shall live to be a THOUSAND!" Mary laughed. Dickon laughed. Even the fox, lying in a patch of sunlight, lifted his head and looked amused. Colin stood in the garden for three full minutes before his legs gave out and he sank back into the wheelchair. But his face was transformed — flushed with color, bright with joy, alive in a way it had never been before. "Tomorrow I'll stand for five minutes," he said. "And the day after, ten. And then I'll walk. I WILL walk." "Of course tha' will," Dickon said. "Tha's growin', just like the garden." And he was right. Colin was growing — not just physically, but in every way that mattered. His fear was shrinking. His anger was fading. His world, which had been the size of a dark room, was expanding with every breath of fresh air, every seed planted, every minute spent in the sun. The garden was healing him. Or perhaps he was healing himself. It was hard to tell the difference, and it did not much matter. What mattered was that it was working.

📚 Informational Text

Secrets: Harmful and Healing

The Secret Garden explores different kinds of secrets. Some secrets in the story are harmful: Mr. Craven locking the garden and refusing to speak of his wife keeps everyone trapped in grief. Colin being hidden away like a shameful secret makes him believe he's defective. These secrets come from fear, shame, and avoiding pain. But other secrets are healing: Mary, Dickon, and Colin keeping the garden work secret creates excitement and shared purpose. The secret gives them agency—it's something they control in lives where adults make all the decisions. This positive secret builds friendship and confidence. The novel suggests that secrets become harmful when they isolate people, but they can be positive when they create connection and purpose. Eventually, the good secret (the restored garden) breaks open the bad secrets (the locked garden, Colin's isolation), allowing truth and healing to emerge. This is one of the story's key messages: some things must be kept private for a time, but truth ultimately needs to come into the light.
