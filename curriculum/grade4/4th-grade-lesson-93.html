Today's Words

1. moor

Definition: A large area of open, wild land covered with heather, grass, and rough vegetation. Often windy and exposed.

"The moor stretched in every direction — endless, wild, and open under a sky bigger than any Mary had seen."

2. dormant

Definition: Alive but not actively growing. Resting or sleeping, waiting for the right conditions to wake up.

"The heather on the moor was dormant — brown and still in early spring, but promising color when the warm weather came."

📖 The Moor - Part 2

Martha would not stop talking about the moor. "Tha' should go outside," she said, every single morning, with the relentless cheerfulness of someone who has never been told to be quiet. "The fresh air'll do thee good. Tha' looks like tha's never been outside in tha' life." "I've been outside plenty," Mary snapped. "I lived in India." "Aye, but India's hot and dusty. Yorkshire's different. The moor's got air that'll put color in thy cheeks and fill up thy lungs proper. My brother Dickon — he's out on the moor every day, and he's the healthiest lad in the village." Martha talked about Dickon constantly. He was twelve years old, one of fourteen children in a cottage so small that Mary could not imagine how they all fit inside. According to Martha, Dickon could charm any animal in the world — foxes ate from his hands, birds landed on his shoulders, and rabbits followed him like dogs. Mary did not believe a word of it. "You're making that up," Mary said. "I am not," Martha said, offended. "Wait till tha' meets him. Tha'll see." Eventually, Mary went outside. Not because Martha told her to, but because the house was so large and so empty and so desperately boring that Mary thought she might scream if she spent one more hour staring at the walls. She stepped through a side door and stopped. The moor. It stretched in every direction — an endless sweep of wild, open land, rolling and rising under a sky that was bigger than any sky Mary had seen in India. The ground was brown and purple with heather, dormant now in early spring but promising color. The wind blew steadily, carrying scents Mary could not name: wet earth, something sharp and green, and underneath it all, a cold, clean freshness that made her gasp. She walked. She did not know where she was going, and she did not care. She just walked, her thin legs pushing through the heather, the wind whipping her hair, the sky enormous above her. She walked until her cheeks burned and her lungs ached and her legs were tired in a way they had never been tired before. It felt terrible. It felt wonderful. When she came back inside, Martha looked at her and smiled. "There's a bit of pink in thy cheeks," she said. "The moor's workin' on thee already." Mary looked in the mirror. Martha was right. Her thin, sallow face had the faintest flush of color. Her eyes, usually dull and flat, had a brightness she did not recognize. "It's just the wind," Mary said. But she went out again the next day. And the day after that. And every day that week. And with each walk, something inside Mary Lennox — something that had been sleeping for ten years under the weight of loneliness and neglect — began, very slowly, to wake up.

📚 Informational Text

The British Raj: England's Rule of India

Mary Lennox begins her story in India, where her parents lived as part of the British Raj—Britain's colonial rule over India that lasted from 1858 to 1947. British families went to India for business, government work, or military service. They lived privileged lives with many servants, often staying separate from Indian communities. The climate was very different from England—hot, humid, with monsoon rains and tropical diseases like cholera (the disease that kills Mary's parents). British children in India were often sent back to England for school, separating them from their families for years. This colonial system was deeply unfair—Britain took India's resources while treating Indian people as inferior. Understanding this historical context helps explain why Mary was so spoiled and why she knew so little about taking care of herself.
