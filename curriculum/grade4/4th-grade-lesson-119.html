📖 In the Garden - Part 2

Archibald Craven walked toward the sound. His feet carried him without thinking — past the kitchen garden, past the formal hedges, past the orchard with its heavy apple trees, to the wall. The wall he had not approached in ten years. The wall behind which lay the garden his wife had loved, the garden where she had been hurt, the garden he had locked and buried the key to and sworn he would never see again. The ivy still covered the wall. But the door — the hidden door — was slightly ajar. Craven pushed it open and stepped inside. The garden hit him like a wave. Color. Everywhere, color. Roses climbed the walls in cascades of red and pink and white and gold. The flower beds blazed with late summer blooms — dahlias and zinnias and sweet peas in every shade imaginable. The paths were neat and clean, bordered by lavender that hummed with bees. The old apple tree — the tree his wife had loved most — was heavy with fruit, its branches spreading wide like open arms. And underneath that tree, three children sat on the grass, laughing. A girl with bright eyes and rosy cheeks. A boy with freckles and a crow on his shoulder. And a third child — a boy — who was standing up from the blanket and walking toward him. Walking. On his own two feet. Straight-backed. Strong. Alive. "Father," Colin said. Craven could not move. His legs had turned to stone. His eyes were fixed on the boy walking toward him — the boy he had last seen as a pale, wasted figure in a dark room, a child he had been too broken to love, a son he had abandoned to grief and servants and the slow decay of isolation. That boy was gone. In his place stood someone new — someone tall and straight and burning with life, someone who looked like his mother around the eyes and like his father around the jaw, someone who was, unmistakably and miraculously, well. "Colin?" Craven whispered. Colin walked the last few steps and stood before his father. They were almost the same height now — Colin had grown enormously in the months since the garden opened. "I'm well, Father," Colin said. "I'm completely well. I can walk and run and dig in the garden and eat real food and I'm not going to die. I never was." Craven's face broke. The wall he had built — ten years of grief, ten years of running, ten years of not allowing himself to feel — crumbled like the garden wall crumbles when the ivy pushes through. He pulled his son into his arms and held him, and he wept. Not the quiet, controlled tears of a man trying to maintain his composure, but the deep, shaking sobs of someone who has been holding his breath for a decade and has finally, finally let it out. "I'm sorry," Craven said into his son's hair. "I'm so sorry. I should have been here. I should never have left." "It's all right," Colin said. "You're here now." Mary and Dickon watched from the apple tree. Mary's eyes were wet, but she was smiling. Dickon played a soft, low note on his pipe, and the robin sang along. Craven looked around the garden — his wife's garden, alive and blooming and more beautiful than it had ever been. He touched the roses she had planted. He stood under the tree where she used to read. He breathed the air that smelled of lavender and earth and growing things. "She would have loved this," he said. "She does," Ben Weatherstaff said. The old gardener had appeared at the garden door, his hat in his hands, his eyes red. "Beggin' your pardon, sir, but some things don't die just because the person is gone. She's in every rose in this garden. She always was." Craven nodded. He could not speak. Colin took his father's hand and led him down the garden paths, showing him everything — the beds Mary had cleared, the seeds Dickon had planted, the roses that had survived ten years of neglect because they were, as Ben said, stubborn things that didn't give up easy. "This is Mary," Colin said. "She found the garden. She found me. She saved us both." Craven looked at Mary — the thin, sour girl who had arrived at Misselthwaite six months ago, disagreeable and unwanted. She did not look thin or sour anymore. She looked like a child who had been planted in good soil and given room to grow. "Thank you," he said. "Don't thank me," Mary said. "Thank the garden." That evening, for the first time in ten years, the Craven family ate dinner together at the great table in the dining room. Colin sat in a chair, not a wheelchair. Mary sat beside him. Dickon had gone home to his mother and his fourteen siblings, but his presence lingered in the wildflowers he had left on the table. Mrs. Medlock stood in the doorway and stared. She could not reconcile what she was seeing with what she knew — the invalid boy walking, the sour girl laughing, the absent father sitting at the head of his own table with tears drying on his cheeks and something in his eyes that looked very much like peace. "I don't understand it," Mrs. Medlock whispered to Martha. "Tha' doesn't need to understand it," Martha said. "Tha' just needs to let it happen." After dinner, Craven walked back to the garden alone. The sun had set, and the garden was silver with moonlight. The roses glowed white. The air was still and warm and sweet. He sat on the bench under the apple tree — the bench where his wife used to sit — and he let the garden hold him. He did not lock the door when he left. He would never lock it again.

📚 Informational Text

The Role of Servants in the Story

The servants in The Secret Garden play crucial roles despite being lower-status characters. Martha is Mary's first friend and teaches her basic self-care skills. Mrs. Medlock, though strict, protects the household routines. Ben Weatherstaff, though gruff, secretly tended Mrs. Craven's roses and eventually helps the children. The servants know about Colin's existence (an open secret) and about the garden's importance. They enable the children's secret work by choosing not to report it to Mr. Craven. This reflects a complex reality: servants in great houses knew family secrets and had significant power through their knowledge and discretion. They could expose or protect, report or remain silent. The novel treats working-class characters with respect and agency. Martha's wisdom often surpasses that of her social superiors. Ben's gruff tenderness reveals depth of feeling. The story suggests that character and worth aren't determined by social position—a revolutionary idea for its time.
