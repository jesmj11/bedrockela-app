📖 Misselthwaite Manor - Part 2

The carriage that met Mary at the train station rattled through miles of darkness before reaching the house. It was nighttime, and all Mary could see through the window was black — black sky, black land, and a wild, moaning wind that sounded like the earth itself was grieving. "Is it always so dark here?" Mary asked the woman sitting beside her, Mrs. Medlock, the housekeeper. "It's the moor," Mrs. Medlock said. She was a stout, sharp-eyed woman who did not seem particularly fond of children. "Miles and miles of wild land with nothing on it but heather and gorse and sheep. You'll get used to it." "I don't want to get used to it," Mary said. "Nobody asked what you wanted," Mrs. Medlock said, and that was the end of the conversation. Misselthwaite Manor was enormous — a great stone house with a hundred rooms, most of them locked and empty. It sat on the edge of the moor like a ship run aground, gray and cold and silent. The hallways were long and dim, lit by flickering candles that made shadows jump and dance on the walls. Mary was led to her rooms — a bedroom and a sitting room, clean but plain, with a fire burning in the grate and heavy curtains drawn against the wind. It was the most cheerless place she had ever seen. "Your uncle, Mr. Craven, doesn't want to be disturbed," Mrs. Medlock told her at the door. "He's not well. He travels most of the time and doesn't see anyone when he's home. You are to stay in your rooms, play by yourself, and keep out of the way. Do you understand?" "Am I to have nothing to do?" Mary asked. "You may go outside if you wish. There are gardens, though most of them are nothing special this time of year. But you are not to go poking about the house. There are rooms that are locked, and they are locked for a reason." Mrs. Medlock left. Mary stood alone in her room and listened to the wind howl across the moor. She was ten years old, orphaned, unwanted, and trapped in a strange house where her own uncle did not care to meet her. She should have cried. Any normal child would have. But Mary Lennox did not cry. She crawled into bed, pulled the covers over her head, and scowled at the darkness. The next morning, she was woken by a cheerful voice and the clatter of a coal scuttle. "Good mornin'!" said a girl about fourteen years old, with round cheeks, bright eyes, and the broadest Yorkshire accent Mary had ever heard. "I'm Martha. I'm to be your servant. Did tha' sleep well?" "No," Mary said. "The wind kept me awake. And this room is ugly." Martha laughed — a real laugh, warm and unconcerned. "Aye, the wind's summat fierce on the moor. But tha'll get used to it. And the room's not so bad once the fire's goin'. Come on, then — tha' breakfast is ready." Mary stared at Martha. She had never met a servant who laughed at her. In India, the servants bowed and whispered and never contradicted her. Martha seemed entirely unimpressed by Mary's scowl, her bad temper, and her general air of misery. "I don't like you," Mary said. "That's all right," Martha said cheerfully. "Tha'll like me eventually. Everyone does." Mary was not sure about that. But she got out of bed, because the fire was warm and the porridge smelled good, and because — though she would never have admitted it — Martha's voice was the most human sound she had heard in weeks.

📚 Informational Text

Frances Hodgson Burnett: From Poor to Famous

Frances Hodgson Burnett was born in England in 1849. When she was a teenager, her family became poor after her father died. They moved to America to start over. Frances began writing stories to help support her family, selling her first story when she was just 17. She eventually became one of the most successful authors of her time. The Secret Garden, published in 1911, is now considered her masterpiece, though her earlier book Little Lord Fauntleroy was more popular during her lifetime. Burnett loved gardens—she had beautiful gardens at her homes in England and America. Much of The Secret Garden was inspired by her own experiences with gardening and her belief that nature could heal people's hearts and minds. She died in 1924, but her stories continue to inspire readers more than a century later.
