Week 34

Today's Words

1. testimony

Definition: A formal statement, especially one given in a court of law. Evidence given by a witness.

"The witnesses' testimony at the trial was useless — the Hatter couldn't remember when he started his tea, and the jury wrote down dates that made no sense."

2. verdict

Definition: The decision reached by a jury at the end of a trial. The final judgment of guilty or not guilty.

"The King wanted the verdict immediately, even though no real evidence had been presented."

📖 Who Stole the Tarts? - Part 2

The King and Queen of Hearts were seated on their thrones when Alice arrived at the court, with a great crowd assembled around them — all sorts of birds and beasts, as well as the whole pack of cards. The Knave of Hearts stood before them, in chains, looking miserable. And near the King was the White Rabbit, with a trumpet in one hand and a scroll of parchment in the other. In the very middle of the court was a table with a large dish of tarts upon it. They looked so good that Alice felt quite hungry just looking at them. "I wish they'd get the trial done," she thought, "and hand round the refreshments!" The White Rabbit blew three blasts on the trumpet and read from the scroll: "The Queen of Hearts, she made some tarts, all on a summer day. The Knave of Hearts, he stole those tarts, and took them quite away!" "Consider your verdict," the King said to the jury. "Not yet, not yet!" the Rabbit hastily interrupted. "There's a great deal to come before that!" The jury was writing busily on their slates. Alice noticed, with surprise, that they were writing down their own names, as though they might forget who they were before the trial was over. One of them did not know how to spell his name and had to ask his neighbor. "Call the first witness," the King said. The first witness was the Hatter. He came in with a teacup in one hand and a piece of bread-and-butter in the other. "I beg pardon, your Majesty," he began, "for bringing these in: but I hadn't quite finished my tea when I was sent for." "You ought to have finished," the King said. "When did you begin?" The Hatter looked at the March Hare, who had followed him into the court, arm-in-arm with the Dormouse. "Fourteenth of March, I THINK it was," he said. "Fifteenth," said the March Hare. "Sixteenth," said the Dormouse. "Write that down," the King said to the jury, and the jury eagerly wrote down all three dates on their slates and then added them up and reduced the answer to shillings and pence. "Take off your hat," the King said to the Hatter. "It isn't mine," said the Hatter. "STOLEN!" exclaimed the King, turning to the jury, who instantly made a memorandum of the fact. "I keep them to sell," the Hatter explained hastily. "I've none of my own. I'm a hatter." Alice watched the trial with growing dismay. It was the most disorganized, unfair, and ridiculous proceeding she had ever witnessed. The witnesses contradicted each other. The jury wrote down whatever they wanted. The King made rulings based on nothing. And the Queen sat on her throne shouting "Off with his head!" at regular intervals, whether it was relevant or not. "This isn't a trial," Alice thought. "It's a farce." But she said nothing. Not yet.

📚 Informational Text

Dreams vs. Reality in Alice

The entire adventure is a dream—Alice wakes up on the riverbank at the end, and everything returns to normal. But was it 'just' a dream? Dreams can teach us real things about ourselves and the world. Alice's dream allows her to explore fears, practice courage, question authority, and think about identity—all important work for a growing child. Carroll never tells us whether dreams are less real than waking life. In fact, he suggests they might be equally important. The Red King in Through the Looking-Glass (Carroll's sequel) asks: what if Alice is the dream character in someone else's dream? This mind-bending question challenges our assumptions about reality. Many cultures throughout history have seen dreams as meaningful messages or alternate realities, not just random brain activity. Alice's dream is certainly meaningful: it's a journey of self-discovery disguised as nonsense. The story asks readers: when you wake from a powerful dream, are you the same person you were before you fell asleep?
