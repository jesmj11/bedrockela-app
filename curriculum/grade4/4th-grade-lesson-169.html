2. certainty

"Everyone in Wonderland was full of certainty about things that were completely wrong. Alice was the only one who admitted uncertainty — and that made her the most honest person there."

📖 Growing Taller - Part 2

Alice was growing, and she could not stop. She was already taller than the King. Taller than the Queen. Taller than the soldiers, the courtiers, the witnesses, and the jury combined. Her head was approaching the ceiling. Her feet were pushing the furniture aside. She was becoming, once again, enormous — but this time, she was not frightened. "You're doing it again," she told herself. "Growing when you should be sitting still. But perhaps growing is exactly what I should be doing." Because it was not just her body that was growing. It was her mind. Her voice. Her willingness to say what she thought, even when the most powerful person in the room was screaming at her. She had arrived in Wonderland small, confused, and obedient. She had drunk when told to drink, eaten when told to eat, run when told to run. She had answered questions politely, followed rules that made no sense, and kept her opinions to herself because the creatures around her were louder and more certain than she was. But certainty, Alice was learning, was not the same as being right. The Queen was the most certain person in Wonderland, and she was almost always wrong. The Caterpillar had been certain. The Hatter had been certain. Everyone in Wonderland was absolutely, unshakably certain about things that were completely and obviously absurd. Alice was the only one who had been uncertain. She was the only one who had asked questions, challenged answers, and admitted when she did not understand. She had thought this made her weak. It did not. It made her the only honest person in Wonderland. "Off with her head!" the Queen screamed again. Alice looked down at the Queen of Hearts — this small, red-faced, furious woman who ruled through fear and shouting and the constant threat of beheading. The Queen who made rules no one could follow, played games no one could win, and held trials where the verdict came before the evidence. Alice had been afraid of the Queen. She was not afraid anymore. "You can't cut off my head," Alice said calmly. "You can't even reach it." The Queen sputtered. "And even if you could," Alice continued, "it wouldn't change the fact that this trial is unfair, this evidence is meaningless, and the Knave of Hearts may very well be innocent." The court was utterly silent. No one had ever spoken to the Queen this way. No one had ever stood in the middle of Wonderland's most powerful court and said: this is wrong. Alice looked at the pack of cards assembled before her — soldiers and courtiers and Kings and Queens and Knaves — and she felt something settle inside her. Not anger. Not fear. Something simpler and stronger than both. Clarity.

📚 Informational Text

Victorian Childhood: Reality vs. Fantasy

Alice's Wonderland adventures are the complete opposite of a Victorian girl's actual life. Real Victorian children, especially from wealthy families, lived highly structured, controlled lives. Girls wore restrictive dresses with corsets, tight shoes, and lots of uncomfortable layers. They studied needlework, music, French, and manners rather than subjects like math or science. They were expected to be quiet, obedient, and decorative. Outdoor play was limited and supervised. Many children saw their parents only briefly each day—nannies and governesses raised them. Rules governed everything from how to sit to how to speak to which emotions could be shown. Wonderland inverts all this: Alice runs free, argues with adults, experiences intense emotions, and encounters a world where rules are nonsensical or nonexistent. For Victorian readers, especially girls, Alice represented a fantasy of freedom. Modern readers might not relate to all the specific Victorian restrictions, but the desire to escape rules and adult control is universal to childhood.
