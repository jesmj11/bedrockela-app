📖 Ben Weatherstaff - Part 2

Their secret lasted until June. Ben Weatherstaff had been watching for weeks. The old gardener was sharp-eyed and curious, and he had noticed things: the sound of children's laughter drifting over the garden wall, the freshly turned earth visible through a gap in the ivy, and the robin making frequent trips over the locked wall with a purposeful air. One morning, Ben propped a ladder against the outside of the garden wall and climbed to the top. He peered over — and what he saw took his breath away. The garden was alive. Roses cascaded down the walls in great waterfalls of color. The flower beds were bursting with life. The paths had been cleared and weeded. The old trees were pruned and healthy. And in the middle of it all, three children sat on the grass eating apples — a girl with pink cheeks, a boy with freckles and a crow on his shoulder, and a thin, pale boy who was standing up from a blanket and walking — actually walking — across the garden on his own two legs. Ben Weatherstaff nearly fell off his ladder. "Colin Craven!" he shouted from the top of the wall. "Is that THEE?" The children froze. Their secret was discovered. Colin turned and looked up at Ben. He was standing straight, his chin raised, his face flushed with health. He looked nothing like the wasted invalid the servants whispered about. "Yes," Colin said. "It's me. And I can walk." Ben Weatherstaff stared. Then his weathered face crumpled, and tears — real, honest, uncontrollable tears — ran down his cheeks and disappeared into his gray beard. "Thy mother's garden," he whispered. "It's alive. She'd have been so glad." He climbed down the ladder, came around to the hidden door, and stepped inside the garden. He walked through the paths slowly, touching the roses, looking at the cleared beds, shaking his head again and again. "I used to tend this garden for Mrs. Craven," Ben said. "She was the sweetest woman who ever lived. She loved every flower, every bush, every inch of this place. When she died, and Mr. Craven locked the door, I thought the garden would die too." "It almost did," Mary said. "But the roses held on. They were waiting." "Aye," Ben said. "Roses are stubborn things. They don't give up easy." He looked at Colin. "Neither do Cravens, it seems." Colin straightened even taller. "I am going to walk across this entire garden," he announced. "And then I am going to walk across the moor. And then I am going to walk into the house and down the stairs and into the dining room and eat my supper at the table like a normal person. And when my father comes home, I am going to walk straight up to him." Ben Weatherstaff wiped his eyes. "Tha' mother would be proud," he said. He joined their secret. Every day, he came to the garden and worked alongside the children — the old gardener and the three young ones, tending the roses that Mrs. Craven had planted, bringing back to life the place that Mr. Craven had tried to kill with a locked door and a buried key. "You can't lock away grief," Ben told Mary one afternoon, as they pruned a climbing rose together. "You can't bury it in the ground like a key. It just grows underground, in the dark, getting wilder and more tangled. The only way through grief is to let things grow again." Mary thought about Mr. Craven — traveling the world, running from this garden, running from his own son. She thought about Colin, locked in a dark room, told he was dying, abandoned by a father who was too broken to love him. "Someone should tell Mr. Craven," Mary said. "Someone should tell him what's happening here." "Aye," Ben said. "Someone should."

📚 Informational Text

Nature as Healer and Teacher

The Secret Garden presents nature as the ultimate healer and teacher. The garden teaches patience—plants grow in their own time, not on human schedules. It teaches resilience—flowers bloom after harsh winters. It teaches renewal—dead-looking branches sprout new life. It teaches care—living things need tending and love to thrive. It teaches wonder—miracles happen when seeds sprout and buds open. This wasn't just Victorian romanticism—modern science confirms that exposure to nature improves mental and physical health. Being outdoors reduces stress, boosts immune function, improves mood, and increases physical fitness. The Japanese practice "forest bathing" (spending time in nature for health) has been studied extensively. Gardening therapy is used to help people with depression, PTSD, and physical rehabilitation. Burnett was ahead of her time in recognizing nature's healing power. The novel asks: in our modern world of screens and indoor living, are we missing something essential that the garden provides?
