📖 The Bell Ringer - Part 2

Quasimodo's daily life followed the same pattern, year after year. He woke before dawn in his small room in the north tower — a bare stone cell with a straw mattress, a wooden stool, and a window that looked out over all of Paris. He ate his breakfast alone: bread, water, whatever the priests left at the bottom of the tower stairs. Then he climbed. The cathedral had hundreds of stairs. Quasimodo knew every one of them — every cracked step, every worn groove, every place where the stone was slippery with damp. He moved through the cathedral the way a bird moves through the sky: naturally, effortlessly, as though the building had been made for him and him alone. His job was the bells. Notre-Dame had ten bells of different sizes, and each one had a name. The great bell — the largest, the one whose voice could shake the towers — was called Marie. Quasimodo loved Marie best. When he pulled her rope and she swung and her voice boomed out across the rooftops, it was the closest thing to joy he knew. The smaller bells had lighter voices — some bright and clear, some deep and mellow. Quasimodo played them the way a musician plays an instrument, timing the pulls so the sounds wove together into something that was almost music. The people of Paris heard the bells every day and thought nothing of it. They did not know that the strange, misshapen figure in the tower was composing symphonies above their heads. When he was not ringing the bells, Quasimodo wandered the cathedral. He knew every inch of it: every chapel, every column, every hidden passage and secret stairway. He knew where the light fell at different hours of the day — which windows caught the morning sun and which glowed at sunset. He knew where the pigeons nested and where the rats hid and where the stone was warm in summer and cold in winter. But his favorite companions were the gargoyles. They perched on every ledge and rooftop — stone creatures with twisted faces, gaping mouths, and wild eyes. They were ugly. Grotesque. Monstrous. And Quasimodo loved them, because they looked like him. He talked to them. He gave them names. He told them about his day — which bells he had rung, what he had seen from the tower, what Frollo had said during their evening lesson. The gargoyles never answered, of course. But they never flinched from him, either. They never looked away in disgust. They simply stared out at the city with their frozen grins, and Quasimodo found comfort in their silence. From the top of the tower, he could see everything. The river Seine, curving through the city like a silver ribbon. The rooftops of Paris, a jumble of red and gray stretching to the horizon. The tiny people in the streets below, going about their lives — shopping, arguing, laughing, holding hands. Quasimodo watched them for hours. He wanted, with a hunger that never faded, to be one of them. To walk through the market. To sit in a tavern. To have someone look at him and smile instead of scream. But Frollo had told him the world below was dangerous. Frollo had told him he was too ugly, too different, too broken for ordinary life. And Quasimodo believed him, because Frollo was the only person who had ever spoken to him at all. So he stayed in the tower. He rang the bells. He talked to the gargoyles. And he watched the world from above, like a ghost haunting a building he could never leave. Until the day of the Festival of Fools.

📚 Informational Text

Gothic Architecture: Building to the Sky

Notre-Dame is built in a style called Gothic architecture. Gothic cathedrals have several special features: pointed arches, tall towers, huge stained-glass windows, and flying buttresses (stone supports on the outside that keep the walls from collapsing). Medieval builders wanted their churches to reach toward heaven, so they built them as tall as possible. The inside of a Gothic cathedral is designed to make you feel small and amazed. Light streams through colorful windows, creating patterns on the floor. The ceilings soar so high above you that your voice echoes. Building a Gothic cathedral required hundreds of workers and took decades — sometimes over a century — to complete.

Check Your Understanding:

2. Why did medieval builders make cathedrals so tall?

3. How long did Gothic cathedrals typically take to build?
