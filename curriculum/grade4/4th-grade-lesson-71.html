2. prejudice

Definition: Judging someone before you know them, usually based on how they look or where they come from.

"The judge's prejudice against Quasimodo's appearance made a fair trial impossible — he had decided guilt before hearing a word."

📖 The Capture - Part 2

The trial was swift and merciless. Quasimodo was brought before a judge the next morning — not Frollo, who had arranged for a different magistrate to handle the case so that his own involvement would remain hidden. The judge was old, half-deaf, and in a foul mood. He squinted at Quasimodo and saw what everyone saw: a misshapen creature, barely human, who had attacked a girl in the street. "What do you have to say for yourself?" the judge demanded. Quasimodo tried to explain. But he could barely hear the questions — years of ringing the great bells had damaged his ears — and his voice was rough and halting. The words came out wrong. He stuttered. He mumbled. The judge grew impatient. "Speak clearly!" the judge shouted. Quasimodo tried harder. But fear and confusion made everything worse. He could not explain why he had grabbed Esmeralda without revealing Frollo's order, and some deep loyalty — some trained, unthinking obedience — kept him from naming his master. The judge did not care about explanations. He saw a monster who had attacked a girl, and he sentenced Quasimodo to public punishment: one hour in the pillory, in the square before Notre-Dame. The pillory was a wooden platform in the center of the square, with a heavy wooden frame that locked around the prisoner's wrists and neck. The prisoner stood hunched and trapped, unable to move, unable to protect themselves, fully exposed to whatever the crowd chose to throw. It was designed to humiliate. And for someone like Quasimodo — already humiliated, already mocked, already wounded by the festival — it was the cruelest punishment imaginable. They dragged him to the pillory at noon, when the square was fullest. They locked his wrists and neck into the wooden frame, and they left him there. The crowd gathered.

📚 Informational Text

Gargoyles: Monsters with a Purpose

Gargoyles are strange, often frightening stone creatures that stick out from the sides of Gothic buildings. They look like dragons, demons, or twisted animals. But gargoyles weren't just decoration — they were actually waterspouts. Rain would flow through the gargoyle's mouth and shoot away from the building, protecting the walls from water damage. Medieval people also believed gargoyles scared away evil spirits. The word 'gargoyle' comes from an old French word meaning 'throat' or 'to gargle,' because of the gurgling sound water makes as it flows through them. In Hugo's story, gargoyles are Quasimodo's companions — silent, misunderstood, and serving a purpose most people don't appreciate.

Check Your Understanding:

1. What practical purpose did gargoyles serve?

2. Where does the word 'gargoyle' come from?

3. What did medieval people believe about gargoyles and evil spirits?
