Today's Words

1. intercepted

Definition: Stopped or caught something or someone before they reached their destination.

"The Cardinal's agents would try to intercept anyone riding for England — blocking roads, setting ambushes, stopping the studs from reaching the queen."

2. sacrifice

Definition: Giving up something valuable for the sake of someone or something more important.

"'If we are attacked, whoever is still free must keep riding. The mission comes first.' It was a sacrifice they all agreed to make."

📖 The Cardinal's Plot - Part 2

D'Artagnan went straight to his friends. He found them in their usual tavern, Athos brooding over a cup of wine, Porthos admiring his reflection in a spoon, and Aramis composing a letter that was certainly not about theology. "I need your help," D'Artagnan said. "All three of you." He told them everything — the queen, the diamond studs, the Cardinal's trap, the ball in twelve days, the need to reach London and back before time ran out. When he finished, there was a long silence. "This is extremely dangerous," Athos said. "Yes," D'Artagnan agreed. "The roads will be full of the Cardinal's agents," Aramis added. "He has spies everywhere. If he knows about the studs — and clearly he does — he will do everything in his power to stop anyone from reaching Buckingham." "Yes," D'Artagnan agreed. "We could be arrested," Porthos said. "Imprisoned. Executed, even. We would be acting against the most powerful man in France." "Yes," D'Artagnan agreed. There was another silence. Then Porthos drained his cup, slammed it on the table, and stood up. "When do we leave?" he asked. Athos allowed himself one of his rare smiles. "In the morning. We ride at dawn." "I'll pack lightly," Aramis said. "One book of poetry should suffice." They planned through the night. The route to the coast was long — through the towns of Chantilly, Beauvais, Amiens, and finally Calais, where they would cross the Channel to England. The Cardinal's agents would be waiting at every stop. "We ride together as far as we can," Athos said. "But if we are attacked, we cannot all be captured. Whoever is still free must keep riding. The mission comes first." "The mission comes first," the others echoed. It was a hard thing to agree to. It meant that if one of them fell, the others would have to leave him behind. It went against everything they believed — all for one, one for all. But the queen's safety depended on the diamond studs reaching Paris before the ball. There was no room for failure. D'Artagnan gathered supplies: fresh horses, money, weapons, and a letter from Constance that would identify him to the Duke of Buckingham. He barely slept. Every time he closed his eyes, he saw the road ahead — long, dark, and full of enemies. At dawn, the four friends mounted their horses in the courtyard of D'Artagnan's lodgings. The morning air was cold, and mist hung in the streets. Paris was still asleep. Athos pulled his cloak tight and looked at D'Artagnan. "Whatever happens on this road," he said, "remember: you ride for the queen, and you ride with friends. That is more than most men can say." D'Artagnan nodded. His throat was too tight to speak. "All for one," Porthos said. "One for all," they answered. They spurred their horses and rode out of Paris into the pale morning light, four friends against the most powerful man in France, with twelve days to change the course of history.

📚 Informational Text

Becoming a Musketeer: Training and Requirements

Becoming a musketeer was not easy. Young men usually came from noble families or had connections to someone important. They started as cadets, training in sword fighting, horseback riding, and shooting. Cadets also had to learn good manners and how to behave at court. The training was intense and could last several years. Only the best fighters and most loyal soldiers were accepted into the musketeers. Once accepted, a musketeer received a uniform, weapons, and a small salary. More importantly, they gained honor, respect, and the friendship of fellow musketeers.
