📖 Seeds and Secrets - Part 2

Dickon brought seeds the next day — packets of them, each one labeled in his careful handwriting. Mignonette. Poppies. Larkspur. Sweet peas. And a special packet of English wildflower seeds that Dickon had gathered himself from the moor. "These'll grow anywhere," Dickon said, shaking the wildflower seeds into Mary's palm. "They don't need fancy soil or careful tendin'. They just need a bit of earth and a bit of sun and they'll do the rest." They planted together, side by side, on their hands and knees in the dark soil. Dickon showed Mary how to make a hole with her finger, drop the seed in, and cover it gently — not packed too tight, because seeds need room to breathe, just like people. "How do you know so much about plants?" Mary asked. "Same way I know about animals," Dickon said. "I watch. I listen. Plants tell you what they need if you pay attention. A droopy leaf means too little water. A yellow leaf means too much. A branch that bends toward the light is askin' for more sun." "Plants don't talk," Mary said. "Not with words," Dickon agreed. "But they talk all the same." They worked through the morning, clearing and planting and pruning. The robin watched from a branch overhead, chirping encouragement. Dickon's crow — a huge, glossy bird named Soot — perched on the wall and supervised with an air of great importance. By noon, they had cleared three entire beds, planted rows of seeds, and pruned a dozen rosebushes. The garden looked different already — not beautiful, not yet, but cared for. There was a shape emerging from the wildness, like a painting slowly being uncovered beneath layers of dust. "We have to keep this secret," Mary said. "If Mr. Craven finds out, he'll lock it again. He can't bear to think about this place." "I won't tell a soul," Dickon promised. "A secret garden ought to stay secret. That's part of what makes it magic." "Do you believe in magic?" Mary asked. Dickon considered this. "I believe in seeds," he said. "I believe that you put a tiny brown thing in the ground and it turns into a flower. If that's not magic, I don't know what is." Mary looked at the rows of freshly planted earth and thought about the green shoots already pushing toward the light. Seeds turning into flowers. Dead-looking gardens coming alive. A sour, thin girl beginning to change. If that was not magic, she did not know what was, either.

📚 Informational Text

Health and Medicine in 1910

Colin Craven's illness reflects early 1900s medical understanding—and misunderstanding. Doctors knew less than they do today, and many treatments were ineffective or even harmful. "Hysteria" and psychosomatic illness (physical symptoms caused by emotional distress) were common diagnoses, especially for wealthy children. Many Victorian children were kept indoors because parents feared fresh air and exercise would make them sick—exactly the opposite of what we know today! Colin's doctor father has convinced himself that Colin has a curved spine and will die young, just as Colin's mother did in childbirth. This is likely a psychological projection—Mr. Craven's grief and guilt have been transferred onto his son. The novel suggests that Colin's real illness is emotional neglect and lack of fresh air, exercise, and hope. When he goes outside, eats well, and has friends, he gets stronger. This was revolutionary thinking in 1911—the idea that emotional health affects physical health.

Check Your Understanding:

1. Why did Victorian parents often keep children indoors?

2. What is psychosomatic illness?

3. What revolutionary idea does the novel suggest about health?
