"Alice impulsively drank from the bottle in the Rabbit's house without reading the label — and grew so large she filled the entire room."

2. predicament

Definition: A difficult, unpleasant, or embarrassing situation that is hard to get out of.

"Being stuck inside a house with her arm out the window and her foot up the chimney was quite a predicament."

📖 The Rabbit Sends a Little Bill - Part 2

Alice had barely caught her breath when the White Rabbit came trotting back, peering anxiously in every direction and muttering, "The Duchess! The Duchess! Oh my dear paws! Oh my fur and whiskers! She'll have me executed, as sure as ferrets are ferrets!" He spotted Alice and cried, "Why, Mary Ann, what ARE you doing here? Run home this moment, and fetch me a pair of gloves and a fan! Quick, now!" Alice was so startled that she ran off at once in the direction he pointed, without even stopping to tell him she was not Mary Ann. "He took me for his housemaid," she said as she ran. "How surprised he'll be when he finds out who I really am! But I'd better take him his gloves and fan — at least, I'll try." She found a neat little house with "W. RABBIT" on a brass plate by the door. She went in and hurried upstairs, where she found a pair of white kid gloves and a fan. She also found a little bottle, and though it did not have a "DRINK ME" label, she drank from it anyway. "I know SOMETHING interesting is sure to happen," she said, "whenever I eat or drink anything in this place." Something interesting did happen. She began to grow. And grow. And grow. Within a minute, she filled the entire room. One arm was stuck out the window. One foot was jammed up the chimney. Her head was pressed against the ceiling. "I can't get any bigger than this," she said hopefully. But she could. She kept growing until her elbow was pressing out one window and her knee was breaking through another. "It was MUCH pleasanter at home," Alice thought, "when one wasn't always growing larger and smaller, and being ordered about by mice and rabbits. I almost wish I hadn't gone down that rabbit hole — and yet — and yet — it's rather curious, you know, this sort of life." The Rabbit arrived outside and tried to get in, but the door was blocked by Alice's enormous body. She heard it calling, "Bill! Bill! Come here! There's an arm sticking out the window!" Bill turned out to be a small lizard. The other animals decided to send him down the chimney, which seemed to Alice like a very bad plan for Bill. She gave a little kick, and the next thing she heard was a chorus of voices crying, "There goes Bill!" as the poor lizard went sailing through the air. Eventually, the creatures outside began throwing pebbles through the window at Alice. The pebbles, when they hit the floor, turned into small cakes. "If I eat one of these," Alice reasoned, "it will probably change my size. Since I can't possibly get any LARGER, it must make me smaller." She was right. She shrank quickly and ran out of the house before the Rabbit and his friends could catch her, diving into a thick wood where the trees came up to about her current height — which was not very high at all. "The first thing I've got to do," said Alice to herself, as she wandered among the trees, "is to grow to my right size again; and the second thing is to find my way into that lovely garden. I think that will be the best plan." It was an excellent plan. The only difficulty was that she had not the smallest idea how to carry it out. She was wandering through the wood, looking up at the great mushrooms that grew like tables above her head, when she noticed a large blue caterpillar sitting on top of one of them, calmly smoking a long hookah and taking not the smallest notice of her or of anything else.

📚 Informational Text

Logic and Nonsense: Two Sides of Lewis Carroll

Lewis Carroll was a brilliant mathematician who loved logic puzzles, precise thinking, and rules. Yet he also created Wonderland, a place where logic breaks down and rules make no sense. How can someone love both? Carroll understood that playing with logic requires understanding it deeply. Many jokes in Alice work by taking logical rules and pushing them to absurd extremes. When Alice grows and shrinks, Carroll is exploring mathematical concepts of proportion and size. When the Cheshire Cat disappears leaving only its grin, he's questioning what makes something real. The Mad Hatter's tea party parodies the strict rules of Victorian social behavior. Carroll wasn't rejecting logic—he was having fun with it, showing that imagination and reason aren't opposites but can work together. This combination makes Alice unique: it's simultaneously silly and smart, playful and profound. Readers can enjoy it purely for fun, or dig deeper into its clever wordplay and logical puzzles.

Check Your Understanding:

1. What was Carroll's profession that involved logic?

2. How does the Mad Hatter's tea party relate to Victorian society?

3. Can logic and nonsense work together? How does Carroll show this?
