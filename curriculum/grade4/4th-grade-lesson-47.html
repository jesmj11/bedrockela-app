📖 Athos's Secret - Part 2

The truth came out one night when Athos had drunk more wine than usual. The four friends were alone in Athos's rooms. A storm raged outside, rain hammering the windows and wind howling through the streets. Porthos was asleep in a chair, snoring. Aramis was reading by candlelight. D'Artagnan sat on the floor, cleaning his sword. "I was married once," Athos said suddenly. D'Artagnan looked up. Athos never talked about his past. In all the months they had been friends, Athos had shared nothing about who he was before Paris — no family, no home, no history. He was like a man who had appeared fully formed, with nothing behind him but shadows. "She was beautiful," Athos continued, his voice flat and distant, as though he were talking about someone else's life. "The most beautiful woman I had ever seen. I was young — younger than you, D'Artagnan — and I was a fool. I fell in love with her face and her voice and her smile, and I did not look deeper." Aramis closed his book. Even Porthos stopped snoring, though whether he was awake or simply holding his breath was unclear. "I married her," Athos said. "I gave her my name, my home, my trust — everything. And then one day, by accident, I discovered the truth. She was a criminal. She had committed terrible crimes — theft, deception, cruelty. She had been branded on the shoulder as punishment — a mark that she had hidden from everyone, including me." D'Artagnan felt cold. "What did you do?" "What honor demanded," Athos said. "I cast her out. She was dead to me. I left everything behind — my lands, my title, my name — and came to Paris as a simple musketeer. I buried my past and swore I would never speak of it again." "And the woman?" D'Artagnan asked, though he already knew the answer. His stomach was tight. "The woman," Athos said slowly, "is Milady de Winter." The room was very quiet. Rain streamed down the windows. "She survived," Athos said. "She changed her name, invented a new history, and found a new patron — the Cardinal. She is cleverer than any of us. She is more ruthless than any of us. And she has no conscience. None." "She is working for the Cardinal now," D'Artagnan said. "Yes. And she will not stop until she has destroyed everything and everyone she considers an enemy. That includes the queen, Buckingham, and anyone who has ever stood in her way." Athos's eyes met D'Artagnan's. "That now includes you." D'Artagnan understood the weight of what Athos had shared. This was not just a warning. It was a confession — the deepest, most painful secret of a man who kept everything locked inside. "Thank you for telling me," D'Artagnan said. Athos nodded once. Then he poured himself more wine and said nothing else for the rest of the night. But something had shifted between them. Athos had trusted D'Artagnan with his most guarded secret. And D'Artagnan understood, perhaps for the first time, that the greatest wounds a man carries are not always the ones you can see.

📚 Informational Text

Letter Writing: Communication in the 1600s

In the 1600s, there was no email, phones, or text messaging. People communicated through letters written with quill pens dipped in ink. Letters were folded, sealed with wax, and stamped with a personal seal. Sending a letter was slow and expensive. A messenger might take days or weeks to deliver a letter, depending on the distance. Important letters were often sent with trusted servants. People had to be careful what they wrote because letters could be intercepted and read by enemies. In The Three Musketeers, secret letters play a crucial role in the plot.
