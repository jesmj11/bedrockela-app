📖 Three Duels Before Noon - Part 2

Paris was enormous. D'Artagnan had never seen so many people in one place. The streets were packed with carriages, horses, merchants, soldiers, beggars, and nobles in silk and lace. Buildings rose three and four stories high, and the noise was overwhelming — the clatter of hooves on cobblestone, the shouts of vendors, the barking of dogs, and underneath it all, a constant hum of life that never stopped. D'Artagnan sold the yellow horse for three gold coins — which was, honestly, more than it was worth — and found a tiny room above a shop that he could afford for a week. Then he put on his least-patched clothes, strapped on his father's sword, and went to find Monsieur de Tréville. The headquarters of the King's Musketeers was a grand building near the palace. The courtyard was full of musketeers — tall, confident men in blue cloaks with silver crosses on the chest. They practiced swordplay, polished their boots, and told stories of their adventures. D'Artagnan watched them with burning envy. These were the finest soldiers in France. And he was a nobody from Gascony with no letter of introduction. But D'Artagnan was not the sort of person who let small problems like having nothing stop him. He walked straight up to the front door and asked to see the captain. To his shock, Monsieur de Tréville agreed to see him. The captain was a serious man with sharp eyes and a military bearing. He listened as D'Artagnan explained who he was — the son of an old comrade, come to Paris to serve the king. "I remember your father," Tréville said. "He was a good man and a fine swordsman. I would have welcomed his letter, but I believe your story. I can offer you a place in the guards — not the musketeers, mind you, but the guards. Earn your place, and perhaps one day you will wear the blue cloak." D'Artagnan's heart soared. It was not everything he wanted, but it was a start. He was leaving Tréville's office, practically floating with excitement, when he glanced out a window and saw something that turned his blood to ice. The man with the scar. Walking calmly through the street below, as though he had every right to exist in the same city as D'Artagnan. The man who had humiliated him in Meung, who had stolen his letter, who had laughed at his horse and his sword and his pride. D'Artagnan did not think. He bolted down the stairs, crashed through the door, and sprinted into the street after the man — and in his haste, he slammed directly into a musketeer who was walking up the steps. The musketeer was tall, dark-haired, and carried himself with quiet dignity. He winced sharply when D'Artagnan hit his shoulder. "Forgive me!" D'Artagnan gasped. "I am in a hurry —" "You hit my shoulder," the musketeer said through clenched teeth. His face was pale with pain. "That shoulder was wounded in a duel last week, and you have just made it worse." "I am sorry, truly —" "Sorry is not enough," the musketeer said coldly. "I will expect you at noon, behind the Luxembourg Palace. Bring your sword." A duel. D'Artagnan had been challenged to a duel by a musketeer. He had been in Paris less than a day. "I will be there," D'Artagnan said, because he had promised his father he would never run from a fight. He continued chasing the man with the scar, but the man had vanished into the crowd. Cursing, D'Artagnan turned a corner too fast and ran straight into another musketeer — a large, broad-shouldered man in an enormous plumed hat who was showing off his new sword belt to a friend. D'Artagnan's foot caught the edge of the belt, and the elaborate gold clasp snapped clean off. "My belt!" the big musketeer roared. "Do you know how much that cost?" "I am terribly sorry —" "One o'clock," the big musketeer snarled. "Behind the Luxembourg. And prepare to pay for that belt with your blood." Two duels. D'Artagnan staggered away, his head spinning. He was so flustered that he walked directly into a third musketeer — a slim, handsome man with gentle eyes who was reading a book of poetry. D'Artagnan stepped on the man's foot. "Ow," said the musketeer mildly. "I am so sorry," D'Artagnan said. "Two o'clock," the man said, still polite but firm. "Behind the Luxembourg. I trust you know the place by now." Three duels with three musketeers in one morning. D'Artagnan walked back to his tiny room and sat on his bed and stared at the wall. He was going to die in Paris on his very first day.

📚 Informational Text

Sword Fighting: The Art of Dueling

In the 1600s, sword fighting was an important skill for soldiers and noblemen. The most common sword was called a rapier—a long, thin blade perfect for quick attacks. Musketeers trained for hours every day to master fencing. A duel was a formal fight between two people to settle an argument or defend their honor. Duels followed strict rules: opponents would bow, agree on the rules, and fight until one person surrendered or was wounded. Though dueling was often illegal, it continued because honor was so important to people of that time. Good swordsmen were respected and feared.
