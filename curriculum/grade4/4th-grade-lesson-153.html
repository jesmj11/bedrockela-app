Today's Words

1. absurd

Definition: Wildly unreasonable or illogical. So silly that it is almost funny.

"The Caucus Race was completely absurd — everyone ran in random directions, everyone won, and everyone got prizes."

2. ceremony

Definition: A formal event with specific rituals and traditions. An official, often serious, occasion.

"The Dodo presented Alice with her own thimble with great ceremony, as if awarding her the most important prize in the world."

📖 A Caucus Race - Part 2

The first question was, of course, how to get dry. The Mouse, who seemed to be a person of authority among the assembled creatures, cleared its throat importantly and said: "Ahem. I shall tell you a very dry story. That should do the trick." "A dry story?" Alice asked. "The driest I know," the Mouse said. And it began to recite a long, complicated passage about English history — William the Conqueror, the Archbishop of Canterbury, something about earls and bishops — in a voice so flat and so dull that within two minutes, everyone was more bored than wet. "I beg your pardon," said the Dodo, a large, round bird with a serious expression and an air of great self-importance, "but I believe there is a better way to get dry. I propose a Caucus Race." "What IS a Caucus Race?" Alice asked. The Dodo waved a wing vaguely. "The best way to explain it," the Dodo said, "is to do it." It marked out a rough circle on the ground, placed the participants around it — Alice, the Mouse, the Duck, the Eaglet, and several others — and said, "Run." "But when do we start?" Alice asked. "You already have," the Dodo said. They ran. There was no starting signal, no set direction, and no particular speed. Everyone ran in whatever direction they liked, some fast and some slow, some in circles and some in zigzags. The Dodo walked. The Mouse scurried. Alice jogged. The Duck waddled. The Eaglet hopped. After about half an hour, the Dodo called out, "The race is over!" Everyone stopped, panting. "But who won?" Alice asked. The Dodo thought about this for a long time. Then it said, "EVERYBODY has won, and ALL must have prizes." "But who is to give the prizes?" a chorus of voices asked. "She will," the Dodo said, pointing at Alice. Alice reached into her pocket and found a box of sweets. She handed them around — there was exactly one for each creature, which was fortunate. But there was nothing left for Alice. "She must have a prize herself, you know," the Mouse said. "Of course," the Dodo agreed. "What else have you got in your pocket?" Alice pulled out a thimble. "Hand it over here," the Dodo said. Then, with great ceremony, the Dodo presented the thimble back to Alice, saying: "We beg your acceptance of this elegant thimble." Everyone cheered. Alice thought the whole thing was very ridiculous, but it seemed rude to say so, so she bowed as solemnly as she could and accepted her own thimble with as much dignity as the situation allowed. "This is the most absurd thing I have ever been part of," Alice thought. But then she thought: "Actually, I rather enjoyed it." And that was the strange thing about Wonderland. The rules made no sense. The logic was upside down. And yet, there was a kind of joy in the nonsense — a freedom in letting go of the way things were supposed to work and simply running in circles until everyone won. Alice was beginning to understand that Wonderland operated on its own logic. It was not the logic she had learned in the schoolroom. But it was logic, of a sort. You just had to squint at it sideways.

📚 Informational Text

The Golden Age of Children's Literature

Before the Victorian era, most books for children were educational and moralistic—they taught lessons about obedience and proper behavior. But in the mid-1800s, a revolution began. Authors started writing books meant purely to entertain children and spark their imaginations. Alice's Adventures in Wonderland was a groundbreaking part of this movement. Carroll didn't try to teach moral lessons—he created a world of pure fun, nonsense, and imagination. Other authors of this 'Golden Age' included Robert Louis Stevenson (Treasure Island), Louisa May Alcott (Little Women), Mark Twain (Tom Sawyer), and Rudyard Kipling (The Jungle Book). These writers changed how society viewed childhood—no longer just a time for strict discipline and preparation for adulthood, but a unique and precious period deserving of wonder, play, and magic. Alice helped lead this transformation by showing that children's books could be wild, funny, and delightfully absurd.
