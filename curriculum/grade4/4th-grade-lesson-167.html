📖 Vocabulary

Week 34

Today's Words

1. evidence

Definition: Facts or information used to prove whether something is true. The basis for believing something.

"Alice was called to give evidence, but she had none — and she said so honestly, which was more than anyone else in the courtroom had done."

2. authority

Definition: The power to give orders and make decisions. The right to be obeyed.

"The Queen's authority was based on fear alone — she screamed 'Off with her head!' but Alice was growing too tall to be afraid."

📖 Alice's Evidence - Part 2

The next witness was the Duchess's cook, who carried a pepper-box and made everyone in the court sneeze. She refused to give her evidence, the King got flustered, and the Queen got furious. Then the White Rabbit read the next name on his list. "Alice!" Alice jumped up so quickly that she knocked over the jury box with the edge of her skirt, spilling the jurors all over the floor. She scrambled to pick them up and put them back, though she accidentally put one in upside down. "What do you know about this business?" the King asked Alice. "Nothing," said Alice. "Nothing WHATEVER?" persisted the King. "Nothing whatever," said Alice. "That's very important," the King told the jury. "UN-important, your Majesty means, of course," the White Rabbit said hastily. "Unimportant, of course, I meant," the King said. "Important — unimportant — unimportant — important —" as if he were trying out which word sounded best. Some of the jury wrote down "important" and some wrote down "unimportant." Alice could see them from where she stood. "It doesn't matter a bit," she thought. "Let the jury consider their verdict," the King said, for about the twentieth time that day. "No, no!" said the Queen. "Sentence first — verdict afterwards." "Stuff and nonsense!" said Alice loudly. "The idea of having the sentence first!" "Hold your tongue!" said the Queen, turning purple. "I won't!" said Alice. The court gasped. The jury dropped their slates. The Knave of Hearts stared. Even the Dormouse woke up. "OFF WITH HER HEAD!" the Queen shrieked. Alice felt something happening. She was growing. Right there in the courtroom, she was getting taller and taller, and as she grew, something else was growing too — something inside her that had been building since the moment she fell down the rabbit hole. Her courage.

📚 Informational Text

Wordplay and Language Games

Carroll was obsessed with language and filled Alice with puns, riddles, and verbal tricks. The Mock Turtle's 'lessons' include Reeling and Writhing (Reading and Writing) and Ambition, Distraction, Uglification, and Derision (Ambition, Distraction, Multiplication, and Division). These jokes work by changing sounds slightly to create new, absurd meanings. Carroll also plays with idioms and literal interpretations: the Cheshire Cat's grin exists without a cat, Alice swims in her own tears, and the Queen wants to remove everyone's heads (literally!). This wordplay serves multiple purposes: it's funny, it shows how arbitrary language rules can be, and it makes readers think about how words work. Children learning language often make similar mistakes—taking idioms literally or mishearing words. By celebrating these 'errors,' Carroll validates children's experiences with language. He also challenges readers to stay alert and think carefully about meaning. In Wonderland, you can't take anything at face value—words are as slippery and changeable as everything else.
