📖 Two Contrary Children - Part 2

Mary and Colin were remarkably alike, and they hated it. "You're exactly like me," Mary told him one night, sitting cross-legged on the end of his enormous bed. "Spoiled, contrary, and convinced the whole world is against you." "I am nothing like you," Colin said hotly. "I am an invalid." "You are a boy in a bed who has never been told no. That's not the same as being an invalid." "How dare you —" "I was exactly the same," Mary said, cutting him off. "In India, no one ever told me no. I was horrible. I was the worst child anyone had ever seen. And then I came here, and Martha wouldn't bow to me, and the moor wouldn't care about my tantrums, and the rain fell whether I liked it or not. And do you know what happened?" "What?" Colin asked, despite himself. "I got better. Not because anyone fixed me. Because I started DOING things instead of lying around feeling sorry for myself." Colin was furious. He was also, somewhere beneath the fury, intrigued. Mary was the first person who had ever treated him as an equal rather than as a patient. She did not speak gently or carefully or with the nervous deference of someone afraid of making him cry. She spoke to him the way she spoke to everyone: bluntly. "You don't understand," Colin said. "You can walk. You can go outside. I can't." "Have you tried?" "Of course not. It would kill me." "Who told you that?" "Everyone." "Everyone told me I was disagreeable, and they were right. But being disagreeable didn't mean I was stuck that way forever. Maybe being told you can't walk doesn't mean you actually can't." Colin stared at her. The idea was so radical, so different from everything he had ever been told, that he did not know what to do with it. It sat in his mind like a seed in dark soil — not growing yet, but present. "I hate you," Colin said. "Good," Mary said. "Hating me is the most energy you've used in ten years. Tomorrow, we'll try walking." "I am NOT walking." "We'll see," Mary said, and she smiled a small, contrary smile that looked remarkably like the smile she had first learned in the secret garden, when she discovered that dead things could come alive.

📚 Informational Text

Orphans in Literature and Society

Mary Lennox joins a long tradition of orphan protagonists in children's literature: Oliver Twist, Anne of Green Gables, Harry Potter, and many others. Why are orphans such popular main characters? First, orphans face challenges that test their character—they must be resourceful, brave, and independent. Second, orphans can go on adventures without parents saying "no, it's too dangerous." Third, orphans search for belonging, which is a universal human need. In Victorian times, many real children were orphaned—diseases like cholera, tuberculosis, and complications from childbirth killed many parents. Some orphans went to orphanages (often grim places), some to workhouses (even worse), and lucky ones went to relatives. Mary is fortunate that her uncle takes her in, even though he largely ignores her. Her orphan status forces her to learn self-reliance and to create her own family through friendship with Dickon, Colin, Martha, and Ben.
