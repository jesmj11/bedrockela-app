📖 Nobody's Girl - Part 2

When Mary Lennox was sent to Misselthwaite Manor to live with her uncle, everybody said she was the most disagreeable-looking child ever seen. It was true. She had a thin, sour face, thin yellowish hair, and a thin, pinched body. She scowled more than she smiled. She whined more than she spoke. And she had a temper that could curdle milk at twenty paces. But it was not entirely her fault. Mary had been born in India, where her father was a British officer and her mother was a beautiful woman who cared more about parties than about her daughter. From the moment Mary was born, she had been handed to servants — an Ayah who dressed her, fed her, and gave her everything she wanted so that she would not cry and bother her mother. Mary had never been told no. She had never been hugged. She had never been played with, read to, sung to, or taught anything at all. She had been kept, the way one keeps a piece of furniture — present but unnoticed, existing in a corner of a house that was too busy for children. The result was a child who knew how to give orders and throw tantrums but did not know how to button her own shoes. A child who had never had a friend, because she had never learned how to be one. A child who was, in the simplest and saddest sense of the word, alone. Then the cholera came. It swept through the district like a terrible wind, killing swiftly and without mercy. In one awful night, it emptied Mary's house. The servants fled. Her father died. Her mother died. And Mary, who had been left in her nursery and forgotten — as she had always been forgotten — was found the next morning by soldiers, sitting on the floor, hungry and bewildered, in a house full of silence. "There's a child here," one of the soldiers said, surprised. "No one told us there was a child." No one had told them because no one had thought of her. Mary was passed from one family to another — British officers' wives who did not want her but felt obligated to take her in. She overheard them talking about her when they thought she was not listening. "She's a plain, unpleasant little thing," one woman said. "No wonder her mother never bothered with her." Mary heard every word. She did not cry. She had learned long ago that crying accomplished nothing. Instead, she scowled harder, spoke less, and built a wall around herself so thick and so high that nothing — no kindness, no cruelty, no pity — could get through. Eventually, it was decided that Mary would be sent to England to live with her uncle, Archibald Craven, at his estate called Misselthwaite Manor. No one asked Mary what she wanted. No one considered that a ten-year-old girl who had just lost everything might need comfort. They simply put her on a ship and sent her away. The ship crossed the ocean. The train crossed England. And Mary pressed her thin face against the window and watched a country she had never seen slide past in shades of gray and green and rain, and she felt nothing at all. She was nobody's girl. She always had been.

📚 Informational Text

Victorian England: Life in the Early 1900s

The Secret Garden is set in Yorkshire, England, around 1910—during the final years of the Victorian era. This was a time of great change. The Industrial Revolution had transformed Britain into the world's most powerful nation, but it also created huge differences between the rich and the poor. Wealthy families like the Cravens lived in enormous manor houses with dozens of servants. Children of wealthy families were often raised by governesses and nannies rather than their parents. Discipline was strict, and children were expected to be "seen and not heard." This was also a time before modern medicine—many children died from diseases we can easily cure today. Understanding this world helps us see why Mary begins the story so lonely and unhappy, and how the garden becomes her path to healing.
