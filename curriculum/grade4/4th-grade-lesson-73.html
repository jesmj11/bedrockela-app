📖 Vocabulary

Week 15

Today's Words

1. mercy

Definition: Compassion or forgiveness shown to someone you could punish or ignore. Choosing kindness when cruelty would be easier.

"Esmeralda's cup of water was an act of mercy — she helped the person who had hurt her, not because he deserved it, but because he needed it."

2. gratitude

Definition: A deep feeling of thankfulness. Appreciation for kindness received, especially when unexpected.

"The tear that rolled from Quasimodo's eye was not from pain but from gratitude — the overwhelming realization that kindness existed."

📖 A Cup of Water - Part 2

Esmeralda climbed the steps alone. The remaining crowd fell silent. They recognized her — the dancer from the square, the girl Quasimodo had tried to capture. She was the last person anyone expected to see here. She was the last person anyone expected to show mercy. She carried a cup of water. She walked across the platform to where Quasimodo hung in the pillory, his face swollen, his clothes filthy, his one visible eye glazed with pain and thirst. She knelt beside him and raised the cup to his cracked lips. "Drink," she said. Quasimodo stared at her. He recognized her. She was the girl from the night before — the one he had grabbed, the one he had frightened, the one whose arm he had bruised with his enormous hand. He had attacked her. And now she was giving him water. He did not understand. "Drink," Esmeralda said again, gently. Quasimodo drank. The water was cool and clean, and it ran down his parched throat like mercy itself. A tear rolled from his eye — not from the pain of the pillory, not from the humiliation of the crowd, but from something he had never felt before. Gratitude. And with it, the shattering realization that kindness existed in the world, and that it could come from the person you least expected — the person you had wronged. The crowd watched in complete silence. Some felt ashamed. Some felt confused. A few — the ones who had thrown stones — felt the first stirrings of something that might have been conscience, though they would not have called it that. Esmeralda lowered the cup. She looked at Quasimodo with those warm brown eyes, and she did not flinch from his face, did not recoil from his deformity, did not look away. She looked at him the way you look at a person. "Why?" Quasimodo whispered. His voice was so broken that only she could hear it. "I hurt you." "Because you were thirsty," Esmeralda said simply. "And because cruelty should be answered with kindness, not more cruelty." She stood up, walked down the steps, and disappeared into the crowd. Quasimodo watched her go. The water was still cool on his lips. The sun was still hot on his back. The wood of the pillory still cut into his wrists. But something had changed. Something enormous and quiet and irreversible. For sixteen years, Quasimodo had believed that Frollo was the only person in the world who cared about him. In the space of one cup of water, that belief had been replaced by something truer: that real kindness does not come from authority or obligation. It comes from the heart. And the girl who danced in the square — the girl he had wronged — had more heart than anyone he had ever known. When the guards came to release him from the pillory, Quasimodo climbed back up to his tower in silence. He sat in his room and looked out over Paris, and for the first time, the view looked different. The world was not only cruel. It was also kind. He just had to learn which was which.

📚 Informational Text

The Court of Miracles: Paris's Hidden City

The Court of Miracles was a real place in medieval Paris — a neighborhood where outcasts, criminals, and the poor lived together outside the law. It was called the 'Court of Miracles' because beggars who pretended to be blind, lame, or sick during the day would miraculously 'heal' when they returned home at night. The area was a maze of narrow alleys where city guards rarely dared to enter. It had its own rules, its own king, and its own sense of justice. Thieves and con artists mixed with genuinely poor families who had nowhere else to go. Hugo's novel shows the Court of Miracles as both dangerous and compassionate — a community of people rejected by the world above.
