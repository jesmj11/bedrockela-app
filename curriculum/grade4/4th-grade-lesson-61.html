Today's Words

1. cathedral

Definition: A large, important church, often the main church of a city. Cathedrals are usually grand and ancient buildings.

"Notre-Dame was more than a building — it was the heart of Paris, the place where kings were crowned and the city gathered in times of trouble."

2. gargoyle

Definition: A carved stone creature, often ugly or monstrous, placed on the outside of a building to drain rainwater. Gargoyles also serve as decoration.

"The gargoyles perched on every ledge and rooftop, staring down at the city with frozen expressions that seemed to be laughing at a joke no one else could hear."

📖 The Bells of Notre-Dame - Part 2

On the sixth of January, in the year 1482, the people of Paris woke to the sound of bells. Not just any bells. These were the bells of Notre-Dame — the great cathedral that rose from the heart of the city like a mountain of stone. Its twin towers stood taller than anything else in Paris, and its bells could be heard for miles in every direction. When they rang, the whole city listened. When they fell silent, something felt missing from the air. Today, the bells rang with special joy. It was the Feast of Epiphany AND the Festival of Fools — two celebrations crammed into one glorious day. The streets of Paris were already filling with people: merchants closing their shops, children running barefoot through the mud, nobles in fur-trimmed cloaks stepping carefully around puddles, and beggars rattling their cups at every corner. The Festival of Fools was the one day of the year when the ordinary rules of Paris were turned upside down. The poor pretended to be rich. The serious pretended to be silly. Students dressed as bishops. Donkeys wore crowns. It was a day for laughter, for mischief, and for the kind of joyful chaos that makes adults nervous and children ecstatic. The center of it all was the square in front of Notre-Dame — the great plaza where the cathedral's shadow fell like a sundial across the cobblestones. A wooden stage had been built for performances. Vendors sold roasted nuts and hot cider. Acrobats tumbled. Musicians played pipes and drums. And everywhere, people laughed and shouted and pushed for a better view. But towering above the noise and the color and the madness, silent and immense, stood Notre-Dame itself. The cathedral was more than a building. It was the heart of Paris — the place where kings were crowned, where the city gathered in times of trouble, where the poor could always find shelter and the lost could always find a door. Its stone walls had stood for three hundred years. Its stained glass windows turned sunlight into rivers of red and blue and gold. Its gargoyles — those strange, twisted stone creatures perched on every ledge and rooftop — stared down at the city with frozen expressions that seemed to be laughing at a joke no one else could hear. And somewhere inside that cathedral, high above the festival, high above the city, high above everything, a young man watched. He watched through a narrow window in the north tower, pressing his face against the cold stone. He could see the crowds below — tiny figures moving like ants across the square. He could hear the music, faint and far away, drifting up through the January air. He could smell the roasted nuts and the smoke from the bonfires. But he could not go down. He had never been to the festival. He had never walked through the square. He had never tasted roasted nuts or danced to a pipe and drum or been part of the joyful, chaotic, beautiful mess of ordinary human life. His name was Quasimodo, and the cathedral was the only world he had ever known.

📚 Informational Text

Medieval Paris: The City of Notre-Dame

In the Middle Ages, Paris was very different from the modern city we know today. The streets were narrow and dirty. People threw their garbage and waste right into the streets! There were no cars — instead, people walked or rode horses. Notre-Dame Cathedral stood at the center of the city on an island in the Seine River. The cathedral took nearly 200 years to build, from 1163 to 1345. It was the tallest building in Paris and could be seen from miles away. On Sunday mornings, the bells would ring so loudly that everyone in the city could hear them. The cathedral was not just a church — it was a gathering place, a shelter, and a symbol of the city itself.
