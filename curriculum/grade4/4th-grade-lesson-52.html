📖 The Prisoner - Part 2

The warnings arrived too late. Milady reached Buckingham. She delivered the Cardinal's threats. But when Buckingham refused to back down — when he told her that he would never abandon his principles for fear of a cardinal or a spy — Milady's mission changed. What happened next reached D'Artagnan as a series of fragmented reports, carried across the Channel by slow ships and slower messengers. Milady had been captured by English authorities who had been warned about her by one of Aramis's messages. She was imprisoned in a country house under guard. For a time, it seemed the danger had passed. But Milady was not the kind of person who stayed captured. She studied her guards the way a chess player studies a board. She found the one who was young, impressionable, and lonely. She used her voice — soft, sad, filled with invented sorrow — to win his sympathy. She told him she was an innocent woman, wrongly imprisoned, separated from her family by cruel men. It was all lies. Every word, every tear, every trembling hand was calculated. Milady was performing the role of her life, and her audience of one believed every moment of it. The young guard helped her escape. By the time his commander discovered what had happened, Milady was gone — vanished into the English countryside like smoke. When this news reached the camp at La Rochelle, D'Artagnan felt the ground shift beneath him. Milady was free. She was angry. She was heading back to France. And she had unfinished business with everyone who had tried to stop her. "She will come for Constance," D'Artagnan said. He knew it in his bones. Milady would strike at the person D'Artagnan cared about most — not out of strategy, but out of revenge. That was how Milady operated. She did not simply defeat her enemies. She destroyed the things they loved. "We must find Constance before Milady does," D'Artagnan said. "We must get her somewhere safe." "The siege —" Porthos began. "The siege can wait," D'Artagnan said, and the fierceness in his voice surprised even him. "Constance cannot." Athos nodded. "He is right. This is no longer about politics or queens or cardinals. This is about protecting an innocent person from a monster." They went to Monsieur de Tréville and requested leave from the siege. Tréville, who understood loyalty better than most, granted it. "Go," the captain said. "Do what you must. But be careful. The Cardinal has eyes everywhere." The four friends gathered their weapons, saddled their horses, and rode north — away from the siege, away from the war, toward a danger that was more personal and more terrible than anything they had faced on the battlefield.

📚 Informational Text

Athos, Porthos, and Aramis: Fact and Fiction

The Three Musketeers were based on real people too, though Dumas changed their names and personalities. The real musketeers were Armand de Sillègue d'Athos d'Autevielle, Isaac de Porthau, and Henri d'Aramitz. These three men really were friends and served as musketeers together. However, Dumas invented most of their adventures and personalities. The real Athos was not a mysterious nobleman in hiding. The real Porthos was not obsessed with wealth and strength. And the real Aramis did not dream of becoming a priest. Dumas took their names and created memorable characters for his story.

3. Which parts of the musketeers did Dumas invent?
