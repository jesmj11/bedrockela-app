📜 BETRAYAL (Part 1)

The betrayal came from within. A man named Roger of Doncaster had joined the band six months earlier. He was clever and capable, and he had quickly made himself useful. Robin trusted him. That was Robin's nature — he saw the best in people, even when the best wasn't there. Roger was a spy. The Sheriff had planted him in the band, paying his family handsomely in exchange for information. For months, Roger had been reporting everything — the location of the camp, the patrol routes, the identities of Robin's supporters in the villages. The Sheriff used this information to plan a devastating strike. Not at Robin directly — at his support network. In a single night, the Sheriff's men arrested twenty people who had been helping the outlaws: farmers who hid weapons, women who passed messages, a blacksmith who repaired their arrows. The arrests shattered the band's connection to the outside world. Worse, they shattered Robin's faith. When the truth about Roger came out — discovered by Marian, who had always been wary of the newcomer — Robin was devastated. "I brought him into our family," Robin said. "I vouched for him." "You trusted someone," Marian said gently. "That's not a weakness. It's who you are." "It's who I was," Robin said, and his voice was older than his years. Roger escaped into the night before Robin's men could catch him. The people who were arrested were eventually released — the Sheriff had no real evidence — but the damage was done. The invisible web that connected Robin Hood to the people of Nottinghamshire was torn, and it could not be easily mended.

📝 Grammar: Misplaced and Dangling Modifiers

Learn:

Modifiers should be placed next to what they modify. Misplaced: 'She told a story to the king with great skill' (the king doesn't have the skill—she does). Dangling: 'Walking through the palace, the stories came to mind' (stories can't walk). Correct: 'Walking through the palace, she thought of stories.'

Example:

"Misplaced: 'The merchant found treasure in the cave that was priceless' (the cave or the treasure?). Better: 'The merchant found priceless treasure in the cave.' Dangling: 'After telling the story, the king was impressed.' Better: 'After she told the story, the king was impressed.'"
