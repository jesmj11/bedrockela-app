📜 FRIAR TUCK (Part 1)

Robin had heard rumors of a friar living near Fountain Abbey — a holy man who was also, supposedly, the best swordsman in the county. Robin had to see for himself. He found the friar sitting by a stream, roasting a chicken. Friar Tuck was not what Robin expected. He was round and red-faced, with a bald head ringed by a fringe of brown hair. He wore the brown robes of his order, but beneath them were the shoulders and arms of a fighter. A broadsword hung at his side — unusual equipment for a man of God. "Good friar," Robin said politely, "I need to cross this stream, and I don't wish to get my feet wet. Would you be so kind as to carry me across on your back?" The friar looked Robin up and down. "Very well," he said mildly. He hoisted Robin onto his broad back, waded into the stream — and halfway across, dumped him into the water. Robin came up sputtering. "What was that for?" "You looked like you needed a bath," the friar said. Robin demanded the friar carry him back. The friar agreed — and dumped him again. This went on three times until both men were soaked, furious, and laughing so hard they could barely stand. Then they fought. Swords clashed, water sprayed, and for an hour they battled back and forth across the stream, neither able to gain the upper hand. Friar Tuck was faster than his round body suggested, and he fought with a joyful ferocity that delighted Robin even as it bruised him. "Enough!" Robin finally called. "You're the finest fighting man I've met since Little John. Come join us in the greenwood. We need a chaplain — and apparently a swimming instructor." The friar considered this. He looked at his lonely campfire, his half-eaten chicken, and the empty forest around him. He had been living alone for years, cast out by the Church for his temper and his unwillingness to bow to corrupt bishops. "Will there be regular meals?" he asked. "The best venison in England." "I'm your man." And so Friar Tuck joined the band — bringing with him his sword, his appetite, and three dogs that followed him everywhere.

📰 Informational Text

Medieval Monasteries and Friars

In medieval England, monasteries were centers of learning, medicine, and hospitality. Monks and friars took vows of poverty, chastity, and obedience. Friars, unlike monks, traveled and preached among common people rather than staying in cloistered monasteries.

However, not all religious men were holy. Some abbots and bishops grew wealthy and corrupt, forgetting their vows. Friars who spoke out against this corruption were sometimes cast out of the church. These "rogue friars" often had more in common with outlaws than with church officials. Friar Tuck represents this type: a genuine man of faith who couldn't stomach the church's hypocrisy.
