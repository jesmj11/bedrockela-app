📜 MAID MARIAN (Part 1)

Robin Hood had a secret that none of the ballad singers ever got quite right. Before he became an outlaw — before the deer, the foresters, and the flight into the greenwood — Robin of Locksley had been in love. Her name was Marian Fitzwalter, and she was the daughter of a minor nobleman whose estate bordered the forest. They had grown up together, climbing trees and shooting at targets in the meadow behind her father's house. She was better than Robin with a short bow, though he would never admit it publicly. When Robin was declared an outlaw, Marian's father forbade her from seeing him. He was afraid — not of Robin, but of the Sheriff, who punished anyone suspected of helping the outlaw. For two years, Robin and Marian saw each other only in stolen moments — a meeting at the forest's edge at dawn, a message carried by a trusted friend, a glimpse across a crowded market square. But Marian was not the kind of woman who waited quietly. She had been feeding information to Robin for months. She knew which roads the tax collectors would travel. She knew when the Sheriff planned his raids. She had a network of servants, farmers, and village women who passed messages through the countryside like a human telegraph. One autumn afternoon, Robin was walking in the forest when he encountered a slim young knight in full armor — visor down, sword drawn. The knight challenged Robin to a fight. Robin accepted, because Robin always accepted. They fought with swords, and Robin was startled by how good the knight was. Quick, precise, and relentlessly aggressive. Robin couldn't find an opening. Every attack was parried. Every feint was read. After fifteen minutes, Robin finally landed a blow that knocked the knight's helmet off. A cascade of dark hair tumbled out. Marian glared at him, breathing hard, a thin cut on her cheek from where Robin's blade had grazed her. "You've gotten slower," she said. Robin stared. Then he laughed until he couldn't breathe. From that day forward, Marian was part of the band — not as a decoration or a damsel, but as a fighter, a strategist, and Robin's equal. She could outshoot half the men, outthink most of them, and out-argue all of them, including Robin. The Merry Men learned quickly: when Robin Hood wanted to know what to do, he asked Marian Fitzwalter.

📝 Language: Multiple Meaning Words

What is it?

Some words have more than one meaning. You must use context to determine which meaning is being used. Example: 'bow' can mean a weapon for shooting arrows or to bend forward as a sign of respect.

Example:

"The word 'fair' can mean beautiful ('a fair maiden'), just and honest ('a fair trial'), or a gathering with games and rides ('the county fair')."

📰 Informational Text

Women in Medieval England

Medieval women were expected to be obedient daughters, then obedient wives. They couldn't own property, make contracts, or hold most positions of power. Education was rare for women except in noble families. A woman's value was often measured by her father's wealth or her ability to produce sons.

But reality was more complex than the rules. Some women managed estates while husbands were away at war. Others ran businesses, practiced medicine, or influenced politics indirectly. Maid Marian represents an idealized version of this: a woman who refuses to be limited by society's expectations and proves herself equal to any man.
