📜 THE LEGEND (Part 1)

They buried Robin Hood where his arrow fell — at the edge of Sherwood Forest, beneath an ancient oak tree, with the green canopy overhead and the open meadow stretching away to the south. Little John dug the grave himself. Marian laid Robin's bow beside him. The Merry Men gathered for the last time. Little John. Friar Tuck. Will Scarlet. Allan-a-Dale. And dozens of others — the men and women who had lived and fought and laughed together in the greenwood for more than twenty years. Allan-a-Dale played his lute. No one spoke. The music said everything. Marian stood at the grave for a long time after the others had gone. She touched the fresh earth with her hand and whispered something that no one else heard. Then she walked into the forest and disappeared among the trees. In the weeks that followed, the band scattered. There was no one who could replace Robin Hood — not because no one was brave enough or skilled enough, but because Robin had been the one thing that held them all together. He was the idea that had made the greenwood more than just a hiding place for outlaws. Little John went north. Some say he lived to be very old. Friar Tuck returned to the church — a small, poor church in a farming village, where he served the people Robin had always fought for. Will Scarlet traveled to the continent, still wearing his scarlet. Allan-a-Dale wandered England, singing songs about Robin Hood at every fair and market and manor house that would have him. And the songs survived. Long after the oaks of Sherwood Forest had grown old and fallen and been replaced by new trees, the story of Robin Hood lived on. Farmers told it to their children. Minstrels sang it in great halls and village squares. Scholars wrote it down. And every person who heard the tale understood the same simple truth that Robin had lived by: The strong should protect the weak. The rich owe a debt to the poor. Courage means standing up for what is right, even when the powerful say it is wrong. And no law written by a tyrant is more just than the law written in a good person's heart. Robin Hood is buried at the edge of Sherwood Forest, beneath the greenwood tree, with his great bow beside him and an arrow's flight between him and the world. But the legend never rests. The legend walks the roads of England still, looking for injustice, ready to fight, and always — always — wearing Lincoln green. THE END

📝 Language: Homophones

What is it?

Homophones are words that sound the same but have different meanings and spellings. Example: their/there/they're, to/too/two, bow (weapon)/bow (bend). Context determines which spelling is correct.

Example:

"Their arrows flew through the air. Robin waited there in the forest. They're brave outlaws. / I want to help. You do, too. The two men fought."

📰 Informational Text

How Legends Survive

The Robin Hood legend has survived for over 700 years through ballads, plays, and stories. Why? Because it addresses timeless themes: rich vs. poor, justice vs. law, freedom vs. security, individual vs. authority. Every generation reinterprets the legend for its own time.

Historians debate whether Robin Hood was real. Some evidence suggests several real outlaws might have inspired the legend. But historical accuracy misses the point: Robin Hood is real as an idea. He represents the hope that someone will stand up to injustice, that the powerful can be challenged, and that one person with courage can make a difference.
