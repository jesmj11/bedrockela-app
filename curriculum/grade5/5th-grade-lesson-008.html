These words appear in today's chapter. As you read, notice how they're used in context!

1. persona

Part of Speech: noun

Definition: a role or character adopted by a person, especially one that is different from their true self From the text: Robin adopts the persona of a butcher so convincingly that the Sheriff invites him to dinner. How does Robin's persona as the butcher differ from his real personality? 2. GREEDY (adjective) — having an intense and selfish desire for something, especially wealth

In context: "The Sheriff, who was greedy above all things, grew very interested."

2. irony

Part of Speech: noun

Definition: a situation where the outcome is opposite to what was expected; a form of humor From the text: The Sheriff is robbed by the very man he invited to dinner. What is ironic about the Sheriff's feast? 4. ESCORT (noun/verb) — a person or group accompanying someone for protection; to accompany

In context: "Robin led the Sheriff and a small escort deep into Sherwood Forest."

📜 THE SHERIFF'S FEAST (Part 1)

Robin loved a good disguise. It was his favorite weapon — more useful than any bow or sword. He could be a beggar, a merchant, a potter, or a monk. He changed his voice, his walk, his entire manner. The Sheriff had posted Robin's description in every village in Nottinghamshire, but descriptions were useless against a man who could become anyone. One market day, Robin dressed as a butcher — a broad-faced, cheerful fellow with a blood-stained apron and a cart full of meat. He drove into Nottingham, set up a stall, and began selling at prices so low that every housewife in the city came running. "Best beef in England! Half price today only!" The other butchers were furious. The customers were delighted. And the Sheriff's wife, hearing about the bargains, sent a servant to buy their entire dinner from the generous newcomer. The butcher was so charming, so funny, and so generous that the Sheriff himself invited him to dinner at Nottingham Castle. Robin accepted with a deep bow. He sat at the Sheriff's own table, ate the Sheriff's food, drank the Sheriff's wine, and told the Sheriff outrageous stories about his "cattle ranch" deep in Sherwood Forest. The Sheriff, who was greedy above all things, grew very interested. "You say you have hundreds of head of cattle? In the forest?" "Five hundred at least, my lord. Fine beasts, every one. I'd sell the whole herd for a fair price." "Show me," the Sheriff said, his eyes gleaming with greed. Robin led the Sheriff and a small escort deep into Sherwood Forest — deeper and deeper, until the trees closed overhead and the paths disappeared. Then Robin put his fingers to his lips and blew three sharp blasts on his hunting horn. Green-clad men appeared from behind every tree. "Welcome to my cattle ranch, my lord Sheriff," Robin said, dropping the butcher's accent. "I'm Robin Hood. And you are my guest." The Sheriff's face went white. But Robin was true to his code — he didn't harm the Sheriff. He simply took every coin the man was carrying, fed him a fine dinner of roast venison, and sent him home on a donkey, with the laughter of the Merry Men ringing in his ears. The Sheriff did not find it funny.

📰 Informational Text

Medieval Disguises

In an age before photographs or widespread literacy, disguises were remarkably effective. Most people couldn't read wanted posters, and descriptions were vague: "tall," "dark-haired," "bearded." Changing clothes, posture, and accent could make someone unrecognizable.

Successful disguises required more than costume—they required acting. Robin Hood was a master of this. He could mimic the speech patterns, mannerisms, and attitudes of different social classes. A beggar walked differently than a merchant. A butcher talked differently than a knight. By observing carefully and practicing, Robin could become anyone.
