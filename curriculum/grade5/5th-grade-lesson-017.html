📜 THE KING'S PARDON (Part 1)

King Richard threw back his hood and revealed himself. The Merry Men dropped to their knees. Even Little John bowed — and Little John bowed to no one. "Rise," the King said. "I didn't come here to be worshiped. I came to see the truth." The truth, as Richard explained, was complicated. While he had been away, his brother Prince John and corrupt officials like the Sheriff had bled the country dry. The common people were suffering. And the one man who had stood against the injustice — Robin Hood — was a wanted criminal. "I've heard two stories about you, Robin Hood," the King said, settling by the fire. "The Sheriff says you're a murderer, a thief, and an enemy of the crown. The common people say you're the only honest man in Nottinghamshire. Which is it?" "A bit of both, Your Majesty," Robin said honestly. "I've stolen. I've fought. I killed a man the day I became an outlaw, and I've never stopped regretting it. But everything I've taken has gone to people who needed it more than those I took it from." The King listened. He questioned Little John, Friar Tuck, Allan-a-Dale, and Marian. He heard about the poor knight, the forced wedding, the starving villages fed with the Bishop's gold. He heard about the Sheriff's cruelty, the unjust taxes, the families driven from their homes. When he had heard enough, Richard stood. "Robin of Locksley, I pardon you and every member of your band. Your crimes are forgiven. The Sheriff of Nottingham will answer for his own." A cheer went up that shook the ancient oaks of Sherwood Forest. "But," the King continued, and the cheering stopped. "I need men I can trust at court. Robin, I want you to come to London. Serve me as you've served these people. Bring your skills, your courage, and your loyalty to the crown." Robin looked at his men. He looked at Marian. He looked at the green canopy of Sherwood Forest — the only home he had known for years. "Yes, Your Majesty," he said. He didn't know it then, but it was the beginning of the end.

📰 Informational Text

Medieval Pardons

Kings had the power to pardon criminals—even those guilty of serious crimes. Pardons were sometimes granted as rewards for service, sometimes sold for money, and sometimes given to win popularity. A royal pardon could restore citizenship, property, and legal rights.

But pardons came with expectations. A pardoned outlaw owed the king loyalty and service. Refusing could be seen as treason. Robin's pardon puts him in an impossible position: accept and lose his freedom, or refuse and insult the king. The story shows that even well-intentioned authority can trap those it claims to help.
