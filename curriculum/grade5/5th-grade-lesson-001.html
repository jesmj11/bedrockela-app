📜 ROBIN BECOMES AN OUTLAW (Part 1)

In the green heart of England, in the days when King Henry ruled the land, there lived a young man named Robin of Locksley. He was eighteen years old, strong and quick, with sharp eyes and a laugh that could be heard across a meadow. He was the finest archer in all of Nottinghamshire — and everyone knew it, including Robin himself. That was the problem. One spring morning, Robin set out through Sherwood Forest on his way to Nottingham, where the Sheriff was holding an archery contest. The prize was a fine barrel of ale and a purse of silver, and Robin intended to win both. He walked with a longbow over his shoulder, whistling, feeling like the world belonged to him. At a crossroads he met a group of the King's foresters — rough men who patrolled the royal forest and enforced the harsh laws that protected the King's deer. In those days, killing one of the King's deer was a crime punishable by hanging, even if your family was starving. The foresters were hated by common people, and they knew it, and they didn't care. "Where are you going, boy?" the chief forester called out. He was a big man with a red face and mean eyes. "To Nottingham, to win the shooting match," Robin said cheerfully. The foresters laughed. "You? You're just a beardless boy. I'll wager you couldn't hit a deer at a hundred paces." Robin's temper flared — a weakness that would follow him all his life. "I'll bet you twenty marks I can hit any target you name." The forester pointed to a herd of the King's deer grazing in a clearing a hundred yards away. "Hit the finest buck in that herd, and I'll pay you twenty marks." Robin nocked an arrow, drew his great bow, and let fly. The arrow sang through the air and struck the buck cleanly. The animal fell without a sound. The foresters' laughter died. "You fool," the chief forester snarled. "You've killed one of the King's deer. That's a hanging offense." Robin realized what he had done. In his pride and temper, he had committed a crime that could cost him his life. The foresters reached for their weapons. Robin ran. An arrow whistled past his ear. He plunged into the thick woods, crashing through brambles and leaping over fallen logs. Behind him, the foresters shouted and cursed. Another arrow hissed by, close enough to clip a leaf beside his face. Robin turned, drew his own bow, and fired back. His arrow found its mark — one of the foresters fell. Robin stared in horror at what he had done. Then he disappeared into the deepest part of Sherwood Forest, where the oaks grew so thick that sunlight barely reached the ground. By nightfall, Robin of Locksley was a wanted man. A price was on his head. He could never go home again. He was eighteen years old, and his old life was over.

📝 Grammar: Sentence Structure: Simple, Compound, Complex, Compound-Complex

Learn:

Understanding the four sentence types is essential for sophisticated writing. Simple sentences have one independent clause. Compound sentences join two independent clauses with a coordinating conjunction. Complex sentences combine an independent clause with one or more dependent clauses. Compound-complex sentences have multiple independent clauses AND at least one dependent clause.

Example:

"Simple: 'The king listened.' Compound: 'The king listened, and Scheherazade spoke.' Complex: 'When Scheherazade spoke, the king listened.' Compound-Complex: 'When Scheherazade spoke, the king listened, and the night passed quickly.'"
