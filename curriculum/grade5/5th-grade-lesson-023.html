📜 RETURN TO SHERWOOD (Part 1)

They left London before dawn, without permission and without farewell. Robin, Marian, Little John, Friar Tuck, Will Scarlet, Allan-a-Dale, and a dozen others rode north through the spring rain, shedding their fine clothes mile by mile until they were back in Lincoln green. When they reached the edge of Sherwood Forest, Robin stopped his horse and breathed deep. The smell of oak and moss and wet earth filled his lungs. A thrush sang from a high branch. Somewhere deep in the trees, a deer moved through the undergrowth. He was home. Word spread quickly. Within a week, the band was back to full strength. Men who had tried to return to normal life drifted back to the greenwood — farmers who couldn't pay their taxes, soldiers who couldn't find work, young men who wanted something more than servitude and silence. Robin's return was not welcome everywhere. The new Sheriff of Nottingham — the old one had been removed by King Richard, but his replacement was no better — immediately sent word to London that the outlaw had broken his pardon. Robin didn't care. He was done with pardons and politics and the suffocating world of power. He picked up his bow, walked into the forest, and began again. Marian walked beside him. "No regrets?" she asked. "Not one," he said. And for a while, the old days returned. The laughter in the greenwood, the adventures on the road, the songs around the fire at night. Allan-a-Dale composed new ballads. Friar Tuck blessed the venison. Little John challenged every stranger to a fight on the bridge, and usually won. But the world was changing. Robin was no longer eighteen. And time, which spares no one, was beginning to make itself felt.

📝 Grammar: Noun Clauses and Their Functions

Learn:

A noun clause acts as a noun in a sentence, often starting with that, what, who, whoever, whether, if. They can be subjects, objects, or complements: 'What she said convinced him' (subject). 'He knew that she was clever' (object). 'The question is whether he will change' (complement).

Example:

"Subject: 'Whoever tells the best story wins.' Object: 'The king realized that he had been wrong.' Complement: 'The problem was that he couldn't trust anyone.'"
