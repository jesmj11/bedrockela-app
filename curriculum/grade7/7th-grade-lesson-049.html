📜 Matthew (Part 1)

Matthew

She came home for the summer with the Avery Scholarship and both teaching certificates and a heart full of plans for Redmond College in the autumn, and the first thing she did was look for Matthew.

He was in the field. He looked older than when she'd left — she noticed this and dismissed it, because she did not want to think about what it meant. He was tired in a way that went past ordinary tiredness. But he smiled when he saw her, the full uncomplicated smile, and she put her arm through his and walked with him back to the house telling him everything.

He listened to all of it with his whole attention. He asked questions — careful, good questions — about the scholarship and the college and what she would study. He said he was proud of her, which he almost never said directly, and when he said it she felt it land in her chest and stay there.

That night she woke to the sound of something in the kitchen.

She found Marilla sitting at the table with a telegram in her hand.

The bank had failed. The Cuthbert savings — everything they had — were gone.

She sat down across from Marilla.

📜 Matthew (Part 2)

"The farm," Marilla said. Her voice was very steady, which meant it was costing her something. "We'll manage the farm. Matthew can — "

In the morning they found Matthew in the yard.

He had died in the night. His heart, which had never been particularly strong, had given out. He was found by the fence, peaceful, as if he had simply stopped.

Anne sat with the fact of it and could not make it real.

She had come home with her whole future planned and now she stood in the kitchen of Green Gables while Matthew was still and the farm was still and Marilla was trying very hard not to break apart, and the world had reorganized itself entirely overnight.

She did not cry immediately. She went through the motions of the day — the neighbors coming, the arrangements, the food that appeared on the table that she could not eat. She sat with Marilla in the evenings and they didn't talk very much.

One afternoon she went out to the field where he had been.

She stood there for a long time.

📜 Matthew (Part 3)

Matthew had never once looked at her and seen something lacking. He had never wished she were different, or a boy, or less strange, or less talkative, or less red-haired. He had just — seen her. The way a person sometimes, rarely, sees you completely.

She had been seen, fully, exactly once in her life before Green Gables, and that had been by Matthew.

She went back inside and told Marilla she was not going to Redmond.

"I'm staying," she said. "I'm not leaving you with the farm and your eyes the way they are. I'm going to teach at the Avonlea school. It's arranged. I spoke to the trustees this morning."

Marilla looked at her.

"Anne —"

"It's decided," Anne said. "Don't argue."

She had Matthew's voice when she said it. They both heard it.

🗣️ Language: Author's Purpose and Rhetorical Strategies

Learn:

Authors choose words to achieve specific purposes: inform, persuade, entertain, inspire. Rhetorical strategies include appeals to emotion (pathos), logic (logos), and credibility (ethos). Word choice, sentence structure, and figurative language all serve the author's purpose.

Example:

"Scheherazade uses narrative (storytelling) to persuade the king to change—this is a rhetorical strategy. She appeals to his emotions through compelling characters (pathos) and to his logic through cautionary tales (logos). Her credibility comes from her vast knowledge (ethos)."
