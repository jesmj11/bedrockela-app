📜 Teaching

Teaching

Anne taught at Avonlea school for two years, and was good at it.

She was not immediately good at it. She was, at first, inconsistent — too lenient with students whose stories interested her, too demanding of students who seemed like they should be doing better. She didn't know how to manage a classroom that didn't want to be managed, which was most classrooms most of the time. She made mistakes and learned from them slowly and made different mistakes.

But she understood something about children that not every teacher understood: that they were small people with full inner lives, and that what happened in those lives mattered more to them than anything happening at the front of the classroom. She learned to read a room — to see who was unhappy today and make allowance for it without excusing everything, to see who was bored and needed a different kind of challenge.

She was the teacher who let children read ahead if they finished early, and the teacher who made them go back and do it again if they finished by not doing it properly. She was the teacher who noticed which students were struggling and found a different way to explain things, and which were struggling because something was wrong at home and needed a different kind of attention.

📜 Teaching (Part 2)

She also kept writing. She had put it aside for the months immediately after Matthew died — there was too much else, and the words felt thin against the weight of things. But they came back, eventually. She wrote in the mornings before school, in a notebook she kept in her desk drawer. Essays, mostly. Observations. Stories she started and didn't finish. Descriptions of things: the way light hit the apple trees in November, the particular quality of silence in a schoolroom before the students arrived.

She sent two pieces to a newspaper in Charlottetown. Both were accepted.

She showed Marilla the payment she received — a small sum, but real money.

Marilla looked at the amount. "It's a start," she said.

"It is," Anne agreed.

📜 Teaching (Part 3)

"Your father would have been —" Marilla stopped. She had a policy about using that language, because it was the kind of thing that got said carelessly and meant nothing. She corrected herself. "Matthew would have been very proud."

"I know," Anne said. "I could tell."

She put the money in the farm account and went back to her desk.

The words that came now were different from the words that had come at Queen's — less eager, less trying. She had suffered some things she would not have chosen to suffer, and the suffering was in the words now, grounding them. They had weight.

She was twenty years old and she was beginning, slowly, to understand what she was actually good at.

📝 Grammar Skill: Noun Clauses and Their Functions

A noun clause acts as a noun in a sentence, often starting with that, what, who, whoever, whether, if. They can be subjects, objects, or complements: 'What she said convinced him' (subject). 'He knew that she was clever' (object). 'The question is whether he will change' (complement).

Example:

Subject: 'Whoever tells the best story wins.' Object: 'The king realized that he had been wrong.' Complement: 'The problem was that he couldn't trust anyone.'

📰 Cultural Context

Arabian Nights reflects the rich cultural exchange of the medieval Islamic world, where stories from India, Persia, Arabia, and beyond blended together through trade routes and oral tradition.

The frame story structure—stories within stories—mirrors how professional storytellers in coffeehouses would spin tales over many nights, stopping at cliffhangers to ensure audiences returned.
