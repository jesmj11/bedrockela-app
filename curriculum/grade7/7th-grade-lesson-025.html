📚 Reading Comprehension

Passage from "What Stories Leave Behind"

What Stories Leave Behind

In the morning — the first morning in a thousand and one days that Scheherazade had woken without the particular weight of not knowing what the morning would bring — she sat at the window of her chamber and looked at the city. It was ordinary. Markets opening. Children going to school. Women at the wells. Men driving carts to the river. The ordinary life of a city that had been afraid for three years and was not yet sure it could stop being afraid, not sure the news was real. Dunyazad came and sat beside her. 'It's done,' Dunyazad said. 'It's done,' Scheherazade agreed. They sat in silence for a while, watching the city. The city did not know yet. The news would take days to spread fully — the announcement, the gradual understanding that the practice was finished, the slow relaxation of a fear that had been carried so long it had started to feel like weather. 'Do you think it will hold?' Dunyazad asked. 'His change.' Scheherazade thought about this honestly, because she was a person who tried to be honest. 'I think change is not a destination,' she said. 'It is a practice. He will need to keep choosing it. There will be days when it is harder than others. There will be moments when the old habits surface.' She paused. 'But I think he is a man who is paying attention now. And a man who is paying attention can correct himself when he goes wrong.' 'You changed him with stories,' Dunyazad said. 'No,' Scheherazade said. 'He changed himself. I gave him something to think about. He did the thinking. I gave him evidence he hadn't considered. He considered it.' She looked at the city below. 'Stories can't change anyone. They can only show a person something they hadn't seen. Whether they change is always their own choice.' She thought about a thousand and one nights of tales — the sailor and his voyages, the fisherman and the genie, Aladdin and his lamp, Ali Baba and his cave, Morgiana with her clear seeing and her dancer's dagger, the young king with his marble legs, the prince who kept finding the mechanism to descend. Each one had been chosen. Each one had been placed where it was for a reason. What she had done was not magic. It was simply the deliberate use of the one tool she had, applied with everything she knew. 'The city will be different now,' Dunyazad said. 'The city will be different now,' Scheherazade agreed. She stood. She had things to do — children to see, a father to send a letter to, a conversation with the king that she suspected would continue for many years if they were both fortunate. There was a library she intended to expand. There were scholars she intended to bring to the palace. She had lived, after all. She had more time than she had spent the last three years planning for. She intended to use it. 'What will you do now?' Dunyazad asked. Scheherazade smiled — the real one, the one she had kept mostly to herself for a thousand nights because there had not been room for it. 'Everything,' she said. 'I've been waiting long enough.' She walked out into the morning. The stories she had told would travel — carried by people who heard them, changed slightly by each retelling, taking root in different soils and growing differently in each place. That was what stories did. They outlasted the teller. They went where the teller could not go. They changed minds the teller would never meet. A thousand and one nights. A thousand and one chances. She had used every one.

1. Scheherazade insists she did not change the king — he changed himself. Why does this distinction matter to her?

2. The final paragraph describes stories traveling beyond the teller, changing with each retelling, taking root in different soils. What is Scheherazade's — and the author's — final claim about what stories do?
