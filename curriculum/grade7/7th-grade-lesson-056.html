📜 Redmond (Part 1)

Redmond

Redmond College was everything Queen's had been and more — larger, more rigorous, more alive with the particular energy of people who have chosen a serious thing and are doing it together.

Anne arrived in September with her scholarship and her two teaching years and the writing she'd done in the mornings and the things the teaching had taught her about how people worked. She was different from the girl who had left for Queen's four years ago. She knew this and was glad of it.

📜 Redmond (Part 2)

She studied English literature with a passion that had not diminished over the years but had deepened. She had a professor named Dr. Harmon Andrews who had read everything and had opinions about all of it and pushed his students to have opinions of their own — not to parrot back what the critics said but to read and think and feel and know what they actually believed. This was harder than it sounded. It required reading something and asking yourself, honestly, what you thought, and then being able to say why. Anne found it was the most interesting thing she'd ever been asked to do.

She wrote for the Redmond literary magazine. Her pieces were received well — well enough that the editor, a small intense girl named Stella Maynard, told her the magazine was better with her in it. Anne received this with the mixture of pleasure and discomfort that praise always carried for her, as if she hadn't quite earned the right to enjoy it yet.

Gilbert was at Redmond too, studying medicine in the science college on the other side of the common. They were friends now — genuinely friends, the way the best friendships come out of the hardest old enmities. They studied together sometimes, walked together sometimes, argued about books and science and whether feeling or reason should govern a life, which was the argument she would not have been able to have without the six years of competition behind it.

📜 Redmond (Part 3)

She liked him. She had known this for a while and was done pretending otherwise. She had not said so. She was not sure what it amounted to, or what she would do about it, or whether he felt anything similar. She had enough to think about without adding that.

She wrote to Marilla every Sunday. She wrote to Diana every other Sunday. She got letters back that kept Green Gables real and present even at this distance — the farm, the neighbors, the seasons turning. She carried it with her, Green Gables, the way you carry a home you've chosen. Not as a weight but as an anchor.

She was becoming something. She wasn't sure exactly what. But she could feel it happening, and it did not frighten her anymore.

📝 Grammar: Misplaced and Dangling Modifiers

Learn:

Modifiers should be placed next to what they modify. Misplaced: 'She told a story to the king with great skill' (the king doesn't have the skill—she does). Dangling: 'Walking through the palace, the stories came to mind' (stories can't walk). Correct: 'Walking through the palace, she thought of stories.'

Example:

"Misplaced: 'The merchant found treasure in the cave that was priceless' (the cave or the treasure?). Better: 'The merchant found priceless treasure in the cave.' Dangling: 'After telling the story, the king was impressed.' Better: 'After she told the story, the king was impressed.'"
