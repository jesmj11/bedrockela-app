📜 The Rivalry

The Rivalry

Anne refused to speak to Gilbert Blythe for the rest of the school year, all of the summer, and well into the following autumn. She was consistent about this in a way she was not always consistent about other things.

Gilbert, for his part, tried several more times to apologize. He came to it from different angles — direct, joking, indirect, through intermediaries. Anne acknowledged none of these attempts. She walked past him in the school yard as if he were a fence post. She answered his comments in class as if he were a general member of the air.

What she could not do — what annoyed her considerably — was stop competing with him academically.

Gilbert Blythe was clever. He was the kind of clever that had always meant something in Anne's world: quick, disciplined, confident in his intelligence. He was used to being the best in the class. Anne was also used to being the best in the class — or would have been, if there had been a class to be best in before Avonlea. Their teacher, Mr. Phillips, had never encountered two students who cared this much about being better than each other at spelling.

Anne won the spelling prize. Gilbert won the arithmetic medal. Anne won the essay competition. Gilbert won the geography award. They circled each other at the top of the class list like two stars caught in each other's orbit, unable to move apart.

Anne told herself she didn't care about Gilbert specifically — she just cared about being excellent. This was mostly true. But she did notice when he did well, and she did feel something particular when she edged him out by a single mark. She was not sure what to call that feeling. It was nothing like friendship.

📜 The Rivalry (Part 2)

Diana watched all of this with patient amusement.

"He likes you," she said.

"He called me carrots," Anne said.

"He teases the people he likes. All boys do."

"That," Anne said, "is an excuse. If he likes me he should not tease me. He should say so directly and accept the consequences."

"That's not how boys work."

"Then boys should work better."

📜 The Rivalry (Part 3)

Diana had no answer for this.

The competition continued. Anne pushed herself harder than she would have without Gilbert there. She wrote better essays, studied more carefully, read more widely. She would not have said he made her better. But she was better that year than she'd been the year before, and he was one of the reasons, and somewhere under the stubborn grudge she almost knew it.

She won the final class award at the end of the term — the one that went to the overall best student, by points across all subjects. She stood to receive it and, without quite meaning to, looked across the room at Gilbert.

He was already looking at her. He smiled — not mockingly, but the real smile, the one she'd seen him give his friends.

She looked away. She did not smile back.

But the look had happened.

📝 Grammar Skill: Active and Passive Voice: Strategic Use

Active voice emphasizes the doer: 'Scheherazade told stories.' Passive voice emphasizes the action or receiver: 'Stories were told by Scheherazade.' Passive voice is useful when the doer is unknown, unimportant, or when you want to shift focus. Overusing passive voice weakens writing.

Example:

Active: 'The king executed the women.' Passive: 'The women were executed' (focuses on victims, not perpetrator). Sometimes passive voice is more appropriate for the meaning you want to convey.

📰 Cultural Context

Arabian Nights reflects the rich cultural exchange of the medieval Islamic world, where stories from India, Persia, Arabia, and beyond blended together through trade routes and oral tradition.

The frame story structure—stories within stories—mirrors how professional storytellers in coffeehouses would spin tales over many nights, stopping at cliffhangers to ensure audiences returned.
