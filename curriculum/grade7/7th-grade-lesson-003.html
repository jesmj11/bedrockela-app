📜 Ali Baba and the Forty Thieves — Part One (Part 2)

In a city at the foot of a mountain, Scheherazade began, there lived two brothers. Their names were Cassim and Ali Baba, and they had started life with the same nothing and arrived at very different somethings. Cassim had married a wealthy woman and lived in a large house with servants and fine clothes and the comfortable certainty that came with money. He was not precisely unkind — he simply had the habit, common to people who have not struggled for a long time, of having forgotten what struggling was like. Ali Baba had married for love, which had turned out to be a somewhat mixed financial strategy. He was a woodcutter. He woke early, worked hard, cut trees on the mountain, sold the wood in the market, and came home in the evening to a small house and a wife who was genuinely glad to see him. He was not rich, but he had the kind of contentment that Cassim, for all his wealth, did not quite have. One morning Ali Baba was cutting wood on the mountain when he heard the sound of hoofbeats — many of them, coming fast. He climbed the nearest large tree and hid himself among the branches. Forty men rode into the clearing below. They were hard-faced and well-armed, and they moved with the easy confidence of men accustomed to taking what they wanted. Their leader — a large man with a black beard and cold eyes — rode to the face of the mountain and spoke words that Ali Baba, from his tree, could just barely hear. 'Open Sesame.' The rock face opened. The forty men rode inside. The rock closed behind them. Ali Baba gripped his branch and did not breathe. After a long time — long enough that his legs were cramped and his heart had slowed from its first gallop — the rock opened again, and the forty men rode out. Their horses were now laden with saddlebags that clinked with unmistakable weight. Gold, Ali Baba thought. Or treasure of some kind. Whatever was in the cave was considerable. The leader spoke again: 'Close Sesame.' The rock sealed itself, and the forty men rode away. Ali Baba climbed down from his tree. He stood before the rock face for a long time. He was an honest man. He had been an honest man his whole life, in the particular way of people for whom honesty is not a policy but simply the shape of how they move through the world. He did not take things that weren't his. He did not lie. He worked for what he had. He was also, at this moment, a man standing before a door to unimaginable treasure, with the key in his memory, and no one watching. He was in the tree, he told himself. He didn't steal the treasure. He simply found the door. 'Open Sesame,' he said. The rock opened. Inside was a cave so vast he could not see the far wall. It was stacked from floor to ceiling with bags of gold, bolts of silk and velvet, chests of jewels, silver plate, and things whose value he could not even estimate. Generations of theft, gathered here in the dark, waiting. Ali Baba took what would fit on his donkeys — not all of it, not even a fraction of it. He took what seemed like enough. He brought it home and showed his wife, who sat down suddenly and had to be given water. 'We must bury it,' he said. 'Carefully. And we must not speak of it.' His wife agreed. But she also wanted to know exactly how much they had, because she was a practical woman who liked to know where she stood. She went to borrow a measuring cup from Cassim's wife next door. Cassim's wife lent the cup. But she was a curious woman, and she pressed a small piece of wax to the bottom of the cup before she gave it — just to see what it had been used to measure. When the cup came back, there was a single gold coin stuck to the wax. Scheherazade paused. The sky was lightening. The king, she noted, had not moved in the last hour. 'Tomorrow night,' she said, 'I will tell you what Cassim's wife did with what she found.'

📝 Grammar: Restrictive vs. Non-Restrictive Clauses and Commas

Learn:

Restrictive clauses are essential to the meaning and need no commas: 'The girl who told the stories survived.' Non-restrictive clauses add extra information and use commas: 'Scheherazade, who had read everything, told stories.' The difference changes meaning: 'Students who studied passed' vs. 'Students, who studied, passed.'

Example:

"Restrictive: 'The tales that changed the king came from many cultures.' Non-restrictive: 'Arabian Nights, which contains hundreds of tales, influenced world literature.'"
