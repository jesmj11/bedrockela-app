📜 The Haunted Wood

The Haunted Wood

Anne had one significant weakness that she was somewhat ashamed of: she was afraid of the dark, and specifically of a stand of woods between Green Gables and the Barry farm that she had, in a moment of literary inspiration, named the Haunted Wood.

The naming had been a mistake. Once you named a place the Haunted Wood, the imagination supplied the rest without being asked.

She had begun seeing things there. Not real things — she knew they weren't real things, which was almost worse. A pale shape among the trees. Something at the edge of the path that retreated when she looked directly at it. She stopped going through the wood after dark.

Then she told Diana about the hauntings, because she told Diana everything.

And then Diana told three other people, because Diana's gift for keeping secrets was not quite equal to her other gifts.

And then the haunting of the wood became a neighborhood legend, and Avonlea children started daring each other to walk through it at night, which they did, and reported various atmospheric experiences, none of which were supernatural.

Marilla found out, as Marilla found out everything.

📜 The Haunted Wood (Part 2)

"You've been telling ghost stories," she said.

"It started as imagination," Anne said. "It got out of hand."

"You terrified half the children in the neighborhood."

"I may have also terrified myself," Anne said, with honesty.

Marilla told her to stop going around with her head in the clouds and to remember that imaginary things had real effects on real people.

This stayed with Anne. She had always thought of her imagination as purely a gift — something that made dull things interesting and difficult things bearable. She had not entirely considered that it could mislead her, or affect others in ways she didn't intend.

She walked through the Haunted Wood in the daytime and looked at it carefully. It was birch and maple, the ordinary lovely woods of Prince Edward Island in autumn, the light coming through the leaves in pale gold columns. There was nothing there. There had never been anything there.

"I did that myself," she told Diana, on the walk home. "I made myself afraid of trees."

📜 The Haunted Wood (Part 3)

"We all did," Diana said fairly. "We all wanted there to be something there."

"Why?"

Diana thought about it. "I suppose," she said, "because something being there would have been more interesting than just trees."

Anne thought about this for the rest of the walk home. It was a truth about herself she had not quite faced: that she sometimes preferred the more dramatic version of things, even when the dramatic version caused trouble. She liked the story better than the facts, and this was not always a virtue.

She told Marilla she had sorted out the Haunted Wood.

"Good," Marilla said. "Try not to invent any new ones."

Anne thought she probably would, eventually. But she would try to be more careful about who she told.

📝 Grammar Skill: Parallel Structure in Lists and Series

Parallel structure uses the same grammatical form for items in a series, creating rhythm and clarity. All items should be the same part of speech or phrase type: 'She was intelligent, determined, and resourceful' (all adjectives). Not parallel: 'She was intelligent, determined, and had resourcefulness.'

Example:

Parallel: 'The king lost his faith, abandoned his principles, and destroyed his compassion.' Not parallel: 'The king lost his faith, his principles were abandoned, and destroying his compassion.'

📰 Cultural Context

Arabian Nights reflects the rich cultural exchange of the medieval Islamic world, where stories from India, Persia, Arabia, and beyond blended together through trade routes and oral tradition.

The frame story structure—stories within stories—mirrors how professional storytellers in coffeehouses would spin tales over many nights, stopping at cliffhangers to ensure audiences returned.
