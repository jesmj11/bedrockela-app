📜 Queen's (Part 1)

Queen's

Charlottetown was nothing like Avonlea, and everything like what Anne had always imagined a city should be.

It had streets full of people and shops she could look at for hours and a harbor she could see from the dormitory window of Queen's Academy if she leaned at a certain angle. It had professors who treated the students as capable of serious thought, which was new and exhilarating. It had a library larger than anything Anne had seen in her life.

It also had a great deal of loneliness, which she had not anticipated.

She had Diana's letters, which arrived with faithful regularity and were long and detailed and warm. She had Matthew's short notes that always included some piece of news from the farm — the cow had calved, the apple trees were looking good this year, the weather had been unusually fine — and underneath the news, something else, the way Matthew communicated: I am thinking of you. She had Marilla's letters, which were practical and contained instructions she hadn't asked for and ended, every single time, with "we hope you are eating properly."

She was not particularly eating properly. She was busy. She was studying in a way she had never had to study before — not the effortless absorption she'd managed at Avonlea school, but the grinding, organized, daily work of really difficult material. Mathematics especially was a struggle. Latin she loved but found the grammar tortuous.

📜 Queen's (Part 2)

Gilbert Blythe was also at Queen's.

He had a group of friends and he was easy in it, in the way some people are naturally easy in company. He said good morning to her once, in the corridor outside the lecture hall. She said good morning back before she'd thought about it, and then felt odd about it for the rest of the day.

She made a friend at Queen's — a girl named Priscilla Grant, who was from the country like Anne and equally overwhelmed by the city and equally determined not to show it. They studied together and walked along the harbor on Sunday afternoons and were, in the particular way of people who are thrown together and discover a natural understanding, immediately close.

"You're always looking at that boy," Priscilla said one afternoon.

"I'm not," Anne said.

"You are. The tall one."

📜 Queen's (Part 3)

"I'm watching the competition," Anne said. "He's very good. He's my main rival."

"That's not what it looks like," Priscilla said.

Anne said it absolutely was what it looked like, and changed the subject.

But she thought about it later. What did it look like?

She had been watching him for two years. She told herself it was competitive vigilance. She had not, she realized, entirely convinced herself.

🗣️ Language: Etymology: Word Histories and Evolution

Learn:

Words have histories—they come from other languages, change meanings over time, and carry their origins with them. 'Sinbad' comes from Arabic. 'Scheherazade' is Persian. 'Genie' comes from Arabic 'jinn.' Knowing etymology deepens understanding and makes words memorable.

Example:

"'Assassin' comes from Arabic 'hashashiyyin' (users of hashish), from a medieval group. 'Admiral' from Arabic 'amir al-' (commander of). Many English words have Arabic origins through trade and cultural exchange."
