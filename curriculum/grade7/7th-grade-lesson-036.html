📜 School (Part 1)

School

Anne went to school in Avonlea and immediately made two things: a best friend, and an enemy.

The best friend was Diana Barry, who lived nearby and had dark hair and rosy cheeks and a sweet disposition that was almost the opposite of Anne's, which made them, somehow, perfectly complementary. Anne decided within five minutes that Diana was a kindred spirit, which was her term for people who understood the world the same way she did. Diana was not entirely sure what a kindred spirit was, but she was quite certain she liked Anne.

The enemy was Gilbert Blythe, who was handsome and clever and used to being the most interesting person in the room.

On Anne's first day, he reached across the aisle and tugged one of her braids and said "Carrots."

The class laughed. Anne went white, then red. Red hair had been a source of suffering all her life — she minded it more than almost anything, which was illogical, she knew, but feelings frequently are.

She picked up her slate and brought it down on Gilbert's head with a crack that silenced the entire room.

📜 School (Part 2)

The teacher kept her after school and made her stand in the corner for the afternoon. Anne stood with her back perfectly straight and did not cry.

Gilbert tried to apologize after school. Anne walked past him without looking at him.

"I said sorry," he called.

"Some things," Anne said, without turning around, "cannot be apologized for."

She meant it. The grudge was complete and she had every intention of keeping it.

She was, she knew, being unreasonable. She didn't care. The red hair was the particular wound that had never healed, and Gilbert Blythe had poked it deliberately.

School itself, once she had established this enmity, she found mostly wonderful. She loved literature with a love that was almost embarrassing. She loved history except for dates. She did not love mathematics except when it became a problem she could think her way through. She threw herself into every lesson with the kind of whole-hearted attention that made some teachers delighted and others exhausted.

📜 School (Part 3)

And she made Diana her devoted companion. They walked to school together and home again and spent the long summer afternoons in a raspberry cane field they had claimed as their own, reading to each other from books they passed back and forth, inventing elaborate games and stories that neither of them ever wrote down.

"You're my best friend," Anne told Diana solemnly, one afternoon. "I've never had a best friend before. I used to pretend I had one — a girl in the reflection in the glass. Her name was Katie Maurice. But she couldn't answer back, which was a limitation."

Diana promised, equally solemnly, to be Anne's best friend for as long as she lived.

Anne was so happy she couldn't sleep that night. She lay in her room with the east window open and thought: I have a friend. A real one. I have a home and a friend.

It seemed almost too much to be true, and she was afraid to trust it.

But in the morning Diana was there at the gate with her schoolbag, exactly as promised.

And Anne walked to school beside her, and for once the red hair didn't matter.

📝 Grammar: Verbals: Participles, Gerunds, and Infinitives

Learn:

Verbals are verb forms used as other parts of speech. Participles (-ing or -ed forms) act as adjectives: 'The sleeping king.' Gerunds (-ing forms) act as nouns: 'Storytelling saved her life.' Infinitives (to + verb) act as nouns, adjectives, or adverbs: 'To survive was her goal.'

Example:

"Participle: 'Scheherazade, speaking carefully, chose her words.' Gerund: 'Listening became the king's obsession.' Infinitive: 'To trust again would require courage.'"
