📜 The Avery Scholarship (Part 1)

The Avery Scholarship

There were prizes at Queen's, and the greatest of them was the Avery Scholarship — a full year's study in English at Redmond College in Nova Scotia, awarded to the top student in English and composition.

Anne had not known about the Avery when she came to Queen's. She learned about it in the second month, from a notice board, and stood reading the description of it for much longer than it required.

Redmond College. A full year. English and composition, which were the things she was best at, the things she actually loved rather than just worked at. Nova Scotia — farther from home than she'd ever been, farther than she'd imagined going.

She wanted it with a fierceness that surprised her.

📜 The Avery Scholarship (Part 2)

She began writing with more intention than she had before. She had always written easily — too easily, she realized, looking back. She had relied on the natural outpouring of a mind that ran to language, and this had carried her through so far, but it was not the same as really working at a piece. Really working meant revising, which she had always resisted, because revisions meant admitting that the first version wasn't right.

Her teacher, a professor named Dorinda Mitchell who was severe and encouraging in almost equal measure, read her essays and said: "You have a genuine talent. You are also lazy with it. Fix that."

Anne fixed it. She revised. She wrote drafts and threw them away and wrote better ones. She began to hear the difference between the version she wanted to write and the version she needed to write to actually achieve what she was after.

She wrote the competition piece in early spring. It was about Green Gables — not by its name, but by its quality: the particular light of an east-facing window, the smell of apple orchards, the feeling of arriving at a place and knowing, without being told, that it was home.

She handed it in and tried not to think about it.

📜 The Avery Scholarship (Part 3)

The results were announced in May. Professor Mitchell read the list of awards beginning with the smaller prizes and working upward. Anne's name appeared twice in the lower prizes. When she got to the Avery, she paused — she was that sort of professor, she liked a moment — and then said: "Miss Anne Shirley."

Anne heard it. She did not immediately react, because she was not sure she had heard correctly. Then the girl beside her touched her arm.

She had heard correctly.

She sat very still for a moment. Then she thought: Matthew. She had to tell Matthew.

📝 Grammar: Appositives and Appositive Phrases

Learn:

An appositive renames or explains a noun right beside it, usually set off with commas: 'Scheherazade, the vizier's daughter, had a plan.' Appositives add detail efficiently without needing separate sentences. Essential appositives (no commas) are rare: 'My sister Maria' (if you have multiple sisters).

Example:

"Nonessential: 'The king, a broken man, listened to the tale.' Essential: 'The character Ali Baba discovered treasure.' (if there are other characters to distinguish from)"
