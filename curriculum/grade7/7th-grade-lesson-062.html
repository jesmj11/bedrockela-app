📜 Queequeg (Part 1)

Queequeg

The harpooner arrived at midnight.

I was half asleep in the big bed when I heard the door open. I saw, in the candlelight, a figure that was unlike anything I had prepared myself for: a large man, tattooed from crown to ankle in patterns I could not read, with a shaved head and a face that had been marked in ways that made his expressions difficult to interpret at first. He was carrying, of all things, a shrunken human head, and a harpoon that he leaned against the wall with the casual ease of someone setting down an umbrella.

He saw me in the bed. He made a sound I could not interpret.

I made a sound back. I may have been somewhat alarmed.

📜 Queequeg (Part 2)

He lunged. I yelled. Peter Coffin appeared in the doorway and sorted things out, and after some conversation it became clear that we were both reasonable people who had merely startled each other, and that the bed was large enough for two, and that there was no alternative anyway.

His name was Queequeg. He was from an island called Rokovoko in the South Sea — a place so remote it appeared on no map, because the best things never do, he told me later, or at least I chose to believe he was telling me something like that, because my Fijian was poor and his English was limited, but we communicated well enough through tone and gesture and the patient goodwill that develops between people who have decided to trust each other.

By morning I had decided I liked him considerably.

By the end of the second day I had decided he was the finest man I had met in years. This was not despite his being foreign and tattooed and the owner of a shrunken head. It was simply about who he was: calm, competent, unexpectedly gentle, with a quality of attention — he listened to what you actually said rather than what he expected you to say — that was rare in any man of any background.

He smoked his pipe. He carved his idol. He sharpened his harpoon. He offered me half of everything he had without being asked, which was the custom of his people, and I was grateful for it and tried to offer half of everything I had in return.

📜 Queequeg (Part 3)

He agreed to sail with me. He had been looking for a ship himself, and there was an understanding between us already that where one went, the other would go.

On Sunday we went to church together. Father Mapple stood in a pulpit shaped like the bow of a ship and preached on Jonah — the man who ran from what he was meant to do and was swallowed by a whale and spent three days in darkness before being given a second chance. Father Mapple's voice was the voice of a man who had been to sea himself and knew the difference between a sermon about the ocean and the ocean itself.

I thought about Jonah for a long time afterward. The man who tried to escape what he was meant to do. The whale that was not a punishment but a passage.

I signed on to the Pequod the following morning. Queequeg signed on beside me.

We did not know, either of us, what we were sailing into. We were young enough to prefer not knowing.

🗣️ Language: Context Clues: Multiple Strategies Combined

Learn:

Expert readers use multiple context clue types simultaneously: definition/restatement (author defines it), synonym/antonym (similar/opposite word nearby), example (specific instances given), inference (reasoning from situation). Look for signal words: 'or,' 'such as,' 'unlike,' 'however.'

Example:

"From Arabian Nights: 'The king was consumed by a rage that was also grief—unlike simple anger, this sorrow-fury combination destroyed his judgment.' Multiple clues: definition ('sorrow-fury'), contrast ('unlike simple anger'), and effect (destroyed judgment)."
