📜 What Marilla Said (Part 1)

What Marilla Said

The weeks after Matthew died were the hardest of Anne's life, and she had had some hard weeks.

It was not simply grief, though there was grief — a clean, uncomplicated grief for the man who had driven to Bright River station to collect a boy and come home with her instead, and had never seemed to mind. It was also the whole shape of the future rearranging itself. Redmond was gone. The scholarship would be held, she'd been told — she could apply again — but the timing was off now and she could not leave Marilla.

She taught herself not to think about Redmond. She was not always successful.

Marilla's eyesight was failing. The doctor had told her so in the spring, and the crisis at the bank and Matthew's death had accelerated something that was already happening. She needed rest from close work, from reading, from anything requiring focus. She needed, the doctor said, to not spend the next year alone.

So Anne stayed.

She set up her classroom at Avonlea school and learned to be a teacher in the way she learned most things — by doing it badly at first and then better, by paying attention to what worked and discarding what didn't. She discovered she loved it with a love that was different from anything she'd expected. Children were strange and various and endlessly interesting, and teaching required her to understand not just what she knew but how to make someone else understand it, which was harder and more rewarding.

One evening in September Marilla called her into the sitting room.

📜 What Marilla Said (Part 2)

"Sit down," she said.

Anne sat. Marilla stood at the window with her back to the room. This was unusual. Marilla generally faced the things she had to say.

"When you came," Marilla said, "I didn't want you. I don't think I told you that plainly. I wanted to send you back."

"I know," Anne said.

"I was wrong to. Though I couldn't have known that then." She paused. "I have not always been — I am not an easy person, Anne. I know that. I have not always said the things I should have said."

Anne waited.

"I want you to know," Marilla said, "that whatever I have not said — I have felt. All of it." A pause. "You have been the best thing that happened to this house."

She still didn't turn around.

📜 What Marilla Said (Part 3)

Anne looked at her back — the straight, slightly rigid back of a woman who said hard things looking the other way — and felt something give way in her chest, quietly.

"I know," she said. "I've always known."

"I wasn't sure."

"I know you," Anne said. "I've learned to know you. The way you say things without saying them."

Marilla turned around then. Her face was composed, but her eyes were the eyes of someone who has just handed something over.

"Good," she said. "Then I don't need to say more."

"No," Anne said. "You don't."

They sat together as the evening came in through the windows, and Anne thought: this is home. Not because it's perfect — because I know the people in it. Because they know me.

📝 Grammar: Subjunctive Mood for Hypotheticals and Wishes

Learn:

The subjunctive mood expresses wishes, suggestions, or hypothetical situations. It often uses 'were' instead of 'was': 'If I were the king...' Common in formal writing after verbs like wish, suggest, recommend: 'I wish she were here.' 'I suggest that he study harder' (not 'studies').

Example:

"Subjunctive: 'If Scheherazade were to fail, she would die.' 'The king wished that his wife were faithful.' Indicative (not subjunctive): 'Scheherazade was clever.'"
