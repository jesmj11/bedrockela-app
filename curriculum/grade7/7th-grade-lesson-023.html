📜 The Ebony Horse (Part 2)

A mechanical horse carved from ebony was presented to a great king as a gift. It was the most remarkable object any of them had ever seen — a horse that could fly, that responded to hidden mechanisms in the saddle, that could carry a rider to any destination in the world. The king's son asked to ride it. The inventor who had built the horse had not yet finished explaining how it worked. The prince had not yet heard the part about how to make it descend. He only knew how to make it rise. He rose. He rose very, very high. He spent a long time in the sky above the city, discovering through experimentation what the inventor had not yet told him: there was a second mechanism, a pin on the right shoulder of the horse, that controlled the descent. He found it eventually. He landed in a foreign kingdom, in the garden of a palace, in the middle of the night. In the garden was a princess. Her name was Shams al-Nahar — Morning Sun. She was reading in the garden by lamplight because she was a person who read when she could not sleep, which was often. She was not frightened by the prince falling out of the sky on a mechanical horse, which told him something about her. He said hello. She said hello. They talked for the rest of the night. He flew home in the morning. He came back the next night. He came back the night after that. This went on for some time — a secret courtship conducted in the hours of darkness, between two people who both understood that what they were doing was not conventional and both found they did not care. Eventually the princess's father found out. This created several difficulties involving imprisonment and disguise and the mechanical horse changing hands more than once and the prince having to rescue the princess from a foreign king who had ideas about what to do with a princess found in a flying horse. The prince prevailed. They married. The inventor was rewarded. But what Scheherazade chose to emphasize was not the adventure. 'He came back,' she said. 'Every night, he found the mechanism to descend, which was harder than the mechanism to rise. He chose, deliberately, the harder thing — to return to the earth, to the conversation, to the person.' She paused. 'Most people find rising easy. The descent is the discipline.' The king looked at her. He had been doing this more often — looking at her as if recalibrating something. 'You mean something by that,' he said. 'I mean several things,' she said. 'I leave you to choose which one applies.'

🤔 Reading Comprehension

2. Scheherazade says 'most people find rising easy; the descent is the discipline.' She leaves the king to decide which meaning applies to him. What meanings might she intend?

📝 Grammar: Noun Clauses and Their Functions

Learn:

A noun clause acts as a noun in a sentence, often starting with that, what, who, whoever, whether, if. They can be subjects, objects, or complements: 'What she said convinced him' (subject). 'He knew that she was clever' (object). 'The question is whether he will change' (complement).

Example:

"Subject: 'Whoever tells the best story wins.' Object: 'The king realized that he had been wrong.' Complement: 'The problem was that he couldn't trust anyone.'"
