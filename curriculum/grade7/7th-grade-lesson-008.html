📜 Sinbad the Sailor — The First Voyage (Part 2)

There was once, in the city of Baghdad, a poor porter named Sinbad who spent his days carrying loads for other people and his evenings wondering why some men were born to wealth and others to labor. One afternoon, exhausted from the heat and the weight of the day, he sat down to rest outside a grand house from which came the sound of music and the smell of food that he would never taste. He muttered, to himself, about the injustice of the world. A servant came out and told him the master of the house wished to speak with him. The master was a man of middle age with an easy manner and a face weathered by years in places far from Baghdad. His name, he said, was Sinbad. Sinbad the Sailor. The porter said, with some confusion, that this was also his name. The sailor laughed. 'Then sit down, namesake, and eat, and I will tell you how I came to this house. Because I was not born to it. I was born exactly where you are sitting — in the dust, wondering why.' This is what Scheherazade told the king, and this is what Sinbad the Sailor told his namesake: He had inherited a modest sum from his father and spent it, as young men often do, on the pleasures of a city. When it was gone, he had nothing left but his wits and a berth on a merchant ship heading east. The ship found an island, green and lovely, rising from the blue sea. The crew went ashore to cook and rest. They built fires on the beach. They were settling in with great contentment when the island moved. It was not an island. It was a whale — the oldest, largest whale in the ocean, so ancient that soil had accumulated on its back and plants had grown there for centuries, so vast that it had passed for land to every ship that had ever stopped upon it. Now, disturbed by the fires, it dove. Sinbad clung to a piece of driftwood and watched his ship sail away without him. He floated for a day and a night, through cold water and dark, telling himself at every moment that he was not dead yet, that not-dead-yet was enough to work with. He washed up on a real island. On the island he found the king's horses being watered, and the king's grooms, and eventually the king himself — a generous man who gave Sinbad a position in his court and treated him well. He might have stayed. It was a comfortable life, and he had earned a comfortable life. But one day a merchant ship stopped at the island. And on the deck of the ship, Sinbad recognized his own goods — the cargo he had loaded in Baghdad, which he had been convinced was lost. The captain had kept everything safe and sought the owner. He returned it all to Sinbad, every item accounted for. Sinbad sold it for a good price and sailed home to Baghdad considerably richer than he had left. 'The first lesson the sea taught me,' Sinbad the Sailor told his namesake, 'was that the world is larger and stranger than anything you can imagine from one city. The second was that honesty has a long reach — it comes back to you when you least expect it.' Scheherazade paused. Dawn was an hour away. 'Tomorrow,' she said, 'I will tell you about Sinbad's second voyage, which was stranger still.'

📝 Grammar: Parallel Structure in Lists and Series

Learn:

Parallel structure uses the same grammatical form for items in a series, creating rhythm and clarity. All items should be the same part of speech or phrase type: 'She was intelligent, determined, and resourceful' (all adjectives). Not parallel: 'She was intelligent, determined, and had resourcefulness.'

Example:

"Parallel: 'The king lost his faith, abandoned his principles, and destroyed his compassion.' Not parallel: 'The king lost his faith, his principles were abandoned, and destroying his compassion.'"
