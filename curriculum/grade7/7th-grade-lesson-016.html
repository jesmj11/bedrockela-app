📜 Aladdin — What He Built and Nearly Lost (Part 2)

The palace Aladdin built rose overnight. He had resisted using the lamp for a long time — longer than anyone who knew him as a boy would have predicted. He had courted the princess slowly, earned the Sultan's cautious respect through patience and genuine gifts, and spent two years simply proving that he was more than the street boy he had been. But the Sultan finally said he needed a palace, a home worthy of a princess, before the wedding could be arranged. Aladdin looked at the empty lot across from the Sultan's own palace and told the genie what he wanted: not the grandest palace in the world, but one that was made with craft and beauty, where every room had something to recommend it, where the garden was designed by someone who understood gardens, where the kitchens were large enough to feed the whole city on feast days. The genie built it by morning. Aladdin and the princess were married. He was, by all accounts, a good husband — attentive, honest, willing to listen. The people of the city loved him because he fed the poor from his kitchens and was visible in the streets, not hidden behind palace walls. He had not forgotten where he came from. This was the best thing about him. The sorcerer had not forgotten him either. The sorcerer had survived his own rage, recovered from his humiliation, and spent the intervening years planning. He had determined that the lamp was still in play — the palace, the marriage, all of it screamed the genie's work. He came back to the city in disguise and waited. His opportunity came when Aladdin was away on a hunt. He moved through the city buying old lamps, calling in the street: new lamps for old. A merchant's game, ordinary enough. He came to the palace. The princess did not know the story of the lamp — Aladdin had not told her, which was his single significant error of judgment. She saw an old lamp sitting in the prince's room. She thought it was, well, an old lamp. She traded it for a new one. The sorcerer had what he had always wanted. That night he used it. The palace vanished. The princess vanished with it — transported to the sorcerer's home in a distant country, where he proceeded to make her life extremely unpleasant while waiting for Aladdin. Aladdin came home from the hunt to an empty lot and a Sultan demanding his daughter back. He still had the ring. He asked the ring-genie to take him to the princess. It could not undo the magic of the greater lamp, but it could transport him. He appeared in the room where the princess was kept and they had, very briefly, a reunion that was also a planning session. The princess hated the sorcerer. The princess was also, it turned out, someone who had spent the last three days thinking carefully about the situation while Aladdin was presumably finding his feet. 'There is wine he likes,' she said. 'He has it every evening.' She needed nothing more than a small packet of powder, pressed into the wine, to complete the plan. The sorcerer drank his wine that evening and did not wake up. Aladdin retrieved the lamp. He brought his wife and his palace home. The king asked Scheherazade what Aladdin had learned. 'That power without trust is a door you leave open for someone else to walk through,' she said. 'He didn't tell the princess about the lamp because he was afraid she would think less of him — would see the magic instead of the man. But his silence nearly cost him everything. The truth told earlier would have been the better armor.'

📝 Grammar: Subordination and Coordination for Sentence Variety

Learn:

Coordination (using and, but, or, so) joins equal ideas. Subordination (using because, although, when, if) shows one idea is dependent on another. Varying these creates sophisticated writing: 'She told stories because the king needed to hear them' (subordination) vs. 'She told stories, and the king listened' (coordination).

Example:

"Coordination: 'The tale ended, but another began.' Subordination: 'Although the tale ended, another began immediately.' Notice how subordination creates a different emphasis."
