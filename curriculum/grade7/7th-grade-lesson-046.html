📜 The Scholarship (Part 1)

The Scholarship

The results of the Queen's entrance examination came back in July, and Anne stood first in the whole of Prince Edward Island.

She read the notice three times, because she couldn't entirely believe it. She had worked for it — she had known she'd worked for it — but the whole island. She had beaten every student in every school in every county, and her name was at the top of the list, and Gilbert Blythe's name was directly below hers, tied with another student for second.

She ran to tell Matthew. Matthew beamed at her with such uncomplicated delight that she had to look away. Then she ran to the fence between their property and the Barrys' to call for Diana, who was allowed to speak with her again — had been allowed since the winter, when Diana's little sister had croup and the doctor was hours away and Anne had administered the right medicine in the right amount and saved the girl's life, and Mrs. Barry had admitted that she had perhaps been mistaken about Anne's character.

Diana shrieked. She told her mother. Her mother sent over a cake.

📜 The Scholarship (Part 2)

Marilla read the notice when Anne brought it home and set it on the table.

"Well," she said. "You've done it."

"We've done it," Anne said. "You let me stay. You made me work. You didn't let me give up when the Latin was terrible."

Marilla said she'd only done what was sensible.

But she kept the notice. Anne didn't know this for a long time — didn't find it until much later, when she was older and sorting through Marilla's things. She found the clipping carefully folded in the back of Marilla's Bible, alongside a lock of red hair that had been kept since the day they'd had to cut it off.

📜 The Scholarship (Part 3)

For now, though, there was just this summer afternoon and the notice on the table and the cake from the Barrys and Matthew's beam and Marilla's careful practicality, and Anne felt something she had no adequate word for — the sensation of a thing you hoped for so hard it frightened you coming completely, absolutely true.

She went to her room and stood at the east window and looked at the apple orchards for a long time.

Then she went back downstairs and helped make dinner and was, for once, almost completely in the present moment, not imagining anything at all.

The real thing, she was discovering, was sometimes better than what you imagined.

📝 Grammar: Subordination and Coordination for Sentence Variety

Learn:

Coordination (using and, but, or, so) joins equal ideas. Subordination (using because, although, when, if) shows one idea is dependent on another. Varying these creates sophisticated writing: 'She told stories because the king needed to hear them' (subordination) vs. 'She told stories, and the king listened' (coordination).

Example:

"Coordination: 'The tale ended, but another began.' Subordination: 'Although the tale ended, another began immediately.' Notice how subordination creates a different emphasis."
