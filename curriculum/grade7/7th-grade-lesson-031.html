📜 The Wrong Child

The Wrong Child

Matthew Cuthbert was not a man who liked surprises. He liked his farm, his horse, his sister Marilla, and the quiet of Prince Edward Island in the early evening. He did not like strangers, conversation, or unexpected developments. So when he arrived at the Bright River station to collect the orphan boy he and Marilla had arranged to adopt — a boy who would help on the farm — and found instead a girl sitting alone on a bench, his first instinct was to turn around and go home.

She was about eleven years old, with a thin face covered in freckles and two long braids of red hair. She wore a dress that was too short and too tight, and she had a battered carpet bag at her feet that looked like everything she owned in the world was inside it. She was sitting very straight, as if posture might compensate for everything else, and she was looking at the world around her with enormous, interested eyes.

She saw him looking and stood up immediately.

"I suppose you are Mr. Matthew Cuthbert?" she said. "I'm very glad you're here, because I was beginning to be afraid no one was coming for me, and I was imagining all the things that might have happened — perhaps you had been thrown from your buggy and were lying injured in a ditch somewhere, or perhaps there had been a miscommunication and you'd come yesterday instead and I'd been left here entirely by mistake. I've been imagining for the past hour."

📜 The Wrong Child (Part 2)

Matthew said, carefully, that no such thing had occurred.

"I'm relieved," she said. "I've been told I imagine too much. But I find it better than not imagining at all, don't you? If you don't imagine things, you just sit there with things as they are, and things as they are can be rather dull."

Matthew, who had no experience with children and especially not with children who talked like this, said that he supposed so.

He could not bring himself to tell her, on the drive to Green Gables, that there had been a mistake. She was so obviously happy — happy with the trees, the sky, the road, the horse, the smell of apple blossoms. She had never been in a place this beautiful, she told him. She had never had anyone come to meet her anywhere before.

"I know I'm not what you wanted," she said, at one point, as if she could read the thought he hadn't voiced. "I know you wanted a boy. People always want boys when they want help. But I'll try very hard to be useful, and I have a very strong imagination, which I think could be an asset in some situations."

📜 The Wrong Child (Part 3)

Matthew was not accustomed to children speaking frankly about their own situations. He found, to his considerable surprise, that he rather liked it.

He said nothing about the mistake. He brought her home. He told Marilla she'd have to deal with it.

Marilla was not pleased.

📝 Grammar Skill: Sentence Structure: Simple, Compound, Complex, Compound-Complex

Understanding the four sentence types is essential for sophisticated writing. Simple sentences have one independent clause. Compound sentences join two independent clauses with a coordinating conjunction. Complex sentences combine an independent clause with one or more dependent clauses. Compound-complex sentences have multiple independent clauses AND at least one dependent clause.

Example:

Simple: 'The king listened.' Compound: 'The king listened, and Scheherazade spoke.' Complex: 'When Scheherazade spoke, the king listened.' Compound-Complex: 'When Scheherazade spoke, the king listened, and the night passed quickly.'

📰 Cultural Context

Arabian Nights reflects the rich cultural exchange of the medieval Islamic world, where stories from India, Persia, Arabia, and beyond blended together through trade routes and oral tradition.

The frame story structure—stories within stories—mirrors how professional storytellers in coffeehouses would spin tales over many nights, stopping at cliffhangers to ensure audiences returned.
