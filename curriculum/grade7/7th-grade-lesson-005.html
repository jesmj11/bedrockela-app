Ali Baba and the Forty Thieves — Part Two

Cassim's wife showed her husband the gold coin, and Cassim went immediately to find his brother. He was not pleased. He was not the sort of man who received news of another's good fortune with generosity, especially when that person was his less-successful brother. He demanded to know where the gold had come from, and when Ali Baba, after some hesitation, told him the whole story — the mountain, the cave, the forty thieves, the magic words — Cassim's eyes lit with a fire that had nothing to do with sharing. 'I will go tomorrow,' he said. Ali Baba warned him to be careful, to take only a little, to come home before the thieves returned. Cassim heard none of this. He was already calculating. In the morning he rode to the mountain with ten mules and went to the cave. He said 'Open Sesame' and the rock opened and he went inside. He was not prepared for what he found — the scale of it, the sheer overwhelming abundance. He walked through it in a daze, touching things, counting things, trying to decide what to take first, then changing his mind, then changing it again. Time passed. More time than he had intended. When he finally decided he had loaded enough and went to the door to leave, he could not remember the magic words. He said 'Open Wheat.' He said 'Open Barley.' He said the names of every grain he knew, every seed, every plant, because for some reason his mind had organized 'Sesame' alongside other things you grew in a garden, and it wouldn't come. He paced. He sweated. He could hear, distantly, the sound of hoofbeats. The forty thieves found him when they returned. We need not say more than that they were not merciful men. When Cassim did not come home, his wife wept and then, being a practical woman in a difficult situation, went to Ali Baba. Ali Baba went to the cave and found what was left of his brother and brought him home with great sorrow and proper ceremony, and buried him respectfully. And then the problem of the thieves. They would know, the captain of the thieves understood, that someone else had discovered the cave. He sent a spy into the city to find out who. The spy was clever and diligent, and he traced the story back through a chain of small details to Ali Baba's house. He marked the door with a chalk mark so the captain could find it. But Ali Baba had a servant — a woman named Morgiana, who was the most observant and resourceful person in the household. She noticed the chalk mark that evening. She did not know what it meant, but she knew that an unmarked mark on a door was never good news. She took a piece of chalk and put the same mark on every door in the street. The captain's men came in the night and stood in the street, looking at forty identical marks, and did not know which house to enter. Scheherazade smiled at the memory of telling this part. The king had leaned forward, she noticed. He was not a man who leaned forward at things. Morgiana, she continued, was not done. When the captain tried again with a different disguise — an oil merchant, with large jars supposedly full of oil — Morgiana discovered the jars contained not oil but armed men, each one waiting for the signal to climb out and kill the household in their beds. Morgiana built up the kitchen fire. She heated the real oil until it was scalding. And then, very quietly, she dealt with the situation. The captain escaped. But his forty men did not. 'The rest,' Scheherazade told the king, 'I will tell you tomorrow — how the captain returned in a final disguise, and how Morgiana recognized him, and what she did.'

📰 The Historical Arabian Nights: Origins and Cultural Context

The collection we know as "One Thousand and One Nights" or "Arabian Nights" didn't originate in a single place or time—it's a compilation of stories gathered over centuries from across the Islamic world, from India to North Africa.

The frame story of Scheherazade and King Shahryar first appeared in a Persian book called "Hezār Afsān" (A Thousand Tales) sometime before the 10th century. As the collection traveled westward into the Arab world, it absorbed stories from many cultures: Indian fables, Persian romances, Egyptian folklore, and stories from Baghdad during the Islamic Golden Age.

The first European translation, by Antoine Galland in the early 1700s, introduced these tales to Western audiences—but Galland made significant changes, adding some stories (like Aladdin and Ali Baba) that weren't in the original Arabic manuscripts. This raises an interesting question about authenticity: what makes a story "part of" Arabian Nights?

The tales reflect the cosmopolitan culture of the medieval Islamic world, where trade routes connected China, India, Africa, and Europe. Sinbad's voyages mirror real merchant expeditions. The genies and magic carpets represent the mythological imagination of multiple cultures blending together.

What's remarkable is how these stories functioned in their original context—they were oral tales, told in coffeehouses and homes, passed down through generations. Professional storytellers would spin them out over many nights, stopping at crucial moments (just as Scheherazade does) to ensure their audience returned. The structure of the frame story literally mimics the social function of storytelling in that culture: creating suspense, building community, passing time, and transmitting wisdom.

2. How did the original function of these stories (oral performance in coffeehouses) influence their structure?

3. What does the passage suggest about cultural exchange in the medieval Islamic world?
