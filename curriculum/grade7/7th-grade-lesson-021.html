📜 The Tale of Judar (Part 2)

There was a young man named Judar, Scheherazade said, who had two brothers and a mother and very little else, and who went on a journey that would have broken most people. His brothers were not good people. They took what Judar earned, invented stories to excuse themselves, borrowed money they had no intention of repaying, and when Judar tried to hold them accountable, they went to their mother and wept, and their mother believed them because she was a woman who loved her sons and found it easier to doubt Judar than to doubt the other two. Judar left. He became, through a sequence of events involving a Moroccan magician and an enchanted treasure and a fish made of gold that swam in the sea, immensely wealthy. He went home. He divided his wealth with his brothers. He asked nothing in return except honesty going forward. His brothers thanked him, took the money, and immediately began planning how to get the rest. They poisoned him at a feast. It was, Scheherazade noted, not a surprise to anyone who had been paying attention. The pattern was visible from the first page. The only person who didn't see it — or wouldn't see it — was Judar, because he had decided, at some point, that loving people meant trusting them regardless of evidence, and he could not separate love from trust. 'Is this a story about being foolish?' the king asked. 'It is a story about the difference between love and willful blindness,' Scheherazade said. 'Judar loved his brothers. That was real and it was good. But love does not require you to ignore what is plainly in front of you. He saw who they were and decided to love them anyway, which he had every right to do. But he also gave them power over him, over and over, which was the choice that destroyed him.' She was quiet for a moment. 'You can choose to love people who have betrayed you. But wisdom asks you to also choose carefully where you place your trust. These are not the same decision.' The king was quiet for longer than usual. 'You have been telling me stories,' he said, 'about people who act from rage without thinking, and about people who trust without discernment, and about people who see clearly and survive because of it.' 'Yes,' Scheherazade said. 'Why?' She looked at him directly. 'Because I think you are a man who knows what he lost, but has confused it with something larger. One person betrayed you. You have punished the world.' She paused. 'I do not say this to accuse you. I say it because you are not a foolish man, and a man who is not foolish should eventually be able to see the distance between what happened and what he has done in response.' The room was very quiet. 'That is not a story,' the king said. 'No,' Scheherazade agreed. 'That is what I think.'

📝 Grammar: Subjunctive Mood for Hypotheticals and Wishes

Learn:

The subjunctive mood expresses wishes, suggestions, or hypothetical situations. It often uses 'were' instead of 'was': 'If I were the king...' Common in formal writing after verbs like wish, suggest, recommend: 'I wish she were here.' 'I suggest that he study harder' (not 'studies').

Example:

"Subjunctive: 'If Scheherazade were to fail, she would die.' 'The king wished that his wife were faithful.' Indicative (not subjunctive): 'Scheherazade was clever.'"
