📜 The Fisherman and the Genie (Part 2)

There was a fisherman, Scheherazade told the king, who was poor in the way that good men sometimes are — through no fault of their own but simply the particular arrangement of the world that gives some people everything and other people the daily work of getting by. He cast his net four times each day. The first three casts of this particular day brought him a dead donkey, a jar of sand, and an armful of broken pottery. He was considering the theology of a universe that gave a hardworking man broken pottery when the fourth cast brought up a copper jar, sealed with lead, stamped with the seal of Solomon. He knew what this might mean. Everyone knew the stories. But he was practical about it — he broke the seal and opened the jar, because a sealed jar found in a net is worth opening regardless of what's inside. There was smoke. Then there was a genie — vast and terrible, older than the fisherman had words for. The genie said that it had been imprisoned in the jar for eighteen hundred years. For the first four hundred years, it had planned to give whoever freed it every treasure in the world. For the second four hundred years, it had planned to grant three wishes. For the next four hundred years, it had planned to grant one wish. After that it had grown so enraged by confinement that it had decided to kill whoever freed it, by whatever painful means came to mind. The fisherman was having, in sum, a very bad fishing day. He thought quickly. 'I believe,' he told the genie, 'that you were really in that jar. But I'm afraid I can't believe you're as large as you appear. That jar is quite small.' The genie found this insulting. It said of course it had been in the jar. The fisherman said that seemed impossible given its current size. The genie said it could fit back in the jar very easily and proceeded, to demonstrate the point, to do exactly that. The fisherman sealed the jar. 'Now,' he said to the sealed jar, 'we can discuss terms.' The genie, from inside the jar, made various threats. The fisherman waited. Eventually the genie agreed to cooperate — specifically, to lead the fisherman to a lake where fish of extraordinary color lived, which would make his fortune, in exchange for his freedom. The fisherman thought carefully about this offer, evaluated the genie's incentives, decided that a genie who had just been outwitted would prefer to repair its reputation through legitimate cooperation rather than risk further humiliation, and opened the jar. The genie kept its word. The fisherman became prosperous. 'The genie's mistake,' Scheherazade told the king, 'was assuming its power meant it didn't need to think. It was stronger, older, and more terrible than the fisherman by every measure. And yet the fisherman survived because he used what he had — his wits — while the genie had stopped needing to.' She paused. 'Power that goes unexamined becomes stupidity,' she said quietly. 'The genie sat in its rage for a thousand years and did not notice it had become predictable.' The king said nothing for a long time. Then: 'Tell me another.'

🗣️ Language: Etymology: Word Histories and Evolution

Learn:

Words have histories—they come from other languages, change meanings over time, and carry their origins with them. 'Sinbad' comes from Arabic. 'Scheherazade' is Persian. 'Genie' comes from Arabic 'jinn.' Knowing etymology deepens understanding and makes words memorable.

Example:

"'Assassin' comes from Arabic 'hashashiyyin' (users of hashish), from a medieval group. 'Admiral' from Arabic 'amir al-' (commander of). Many English words have Arabic origins through trade and cultural exchange."
