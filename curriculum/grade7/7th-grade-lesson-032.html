📜 Marilla Makes Up Her Mind (Part 1)

Marilla Makes Up Her Mind

Marilla Cuthbert was a woman of sharp edges and practical views. She believed in plain food, plain speech, and plain dress, and she was deeply suspicious of anything that seemed designed to make life more pleasant than it needed to be. She was not unkind — she would have been shocked to be called unkind — but she expressed care through action rather than words, and she had no patience for sentiment.

When Matthew brought home a girl instead of a boy, Marilla's first response was brisk and practical: the girl would have to go back.

She said this to the child directly, because she did not believe in softening things unnecessarily.

"You'll have to be returned to the orphanage," she told Anne — for the girl had told them her name, and given the full version of it with the e spelled out, because she felt the e made a considerable difference. "We asked for a boy. We need help on the farm. You're not what we arranged for."

Anne went very still. She had the quality, when something hurt her, of becoming entirely motionless, as if the stillness might contain it.

"I understand," she said. "I'm used to not being wanted."

She said it quietly, without self-pity. It was worse, somehow, than crying would have been.

📜 Marilla Makes Up Her Mind (Part 2)

Marilla went to bed that night intending to send Anne back in the morning. She woke up still intending it. But she found, as the day went on, that the intention was harder to carry out than she'd expected.

Anne was strange — there was no question of that. She had a habit of going off into long, flowing descriptions of things she found beautiful. She named a lane full of white cherry trees "the White Way of Delight" and a pond "the Lake of Shining Waters" and when Marilla pointed out these were not their real names, Anne said yes, but the real names weren't good enough for the places, and the places deserved better names. This was not logic Marilla could entirely argue with, though she tried.

She also prayed differently than Marilla expected. When asked to say grace at dinner, Anne said she would try her best, and produced a prayer that began "Dear God, I'm so thankful for this beautiful world and for the cherry blossoms and for being here instead of the orphanage and also for the fact that my hair is not black, because I think that would be worse somehow."

Marilla said that was not a proper grace.

Anne said she'd always thought grace should be honest, and she was honest about what she was grateful for.

Something shifted in Marilla, slightly. Not enough to name. But enough to pause.

She wrote to the orphanage to ask about the circumstances of the mistake. She received a letter that told her of three other families who had turned Anne back over the years. Not because she was bad, the letter said. She was bright and eager and full of ideas. It was just — she was a lot. And most people wanted children who were less.

Marilla read this letter twice. Then she folded it and put it in her pocket and went to find Anne.

📜 Marilla Makes Up Her Mind (Part 3)

"You'll stay," she said. "On trial. We'll see how it goes."

Anne looked at her with a combination of hope and disbelief that was painful to look at directly.

"On trial," she repeated. "That's — I'll be so good, Mrs. Cuthbert. Or I'll try to be. I may not always succeed because I have a very bad temper and sometimes I say things I shouldn't. But I'll try."

"I know you'll try," Marilla said. "That's all anyone can do."

She went back to her work and thought no more about it.

Or so she told herself.

🗣️ Language: Context Clues: Multiple Strategies Combined

Learn:

Expert readers use multiple context clue types simultaneously: definition/restatement (author defines it), synonym/antonym (similar/opposite word nearby), example (specific instances given), inference (reasoning from situation). Look for signal words: 'or,' 'such as,' 'unlike,' 'however.'

Example:

"From Arabian Nights: 'The king was consumed by a rage that was also grief—unlike simple anger, this sorrow-fury combination destroyed his judgment.' Multiple clues: definition ('sorrow-fury'), contrast ('unlike simple anger'), and effect (destroyed judgment)."
