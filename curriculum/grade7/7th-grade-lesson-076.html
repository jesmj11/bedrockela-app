📜 Pip (Part 1)

Pip

Pip fell out of a boat.

It was during a chase — one of the ordinary whale chases, not Moby Dick, just a sperm whale that had been spotted and brought the boats down into the water in the usual rush. Pip was in Stubb's boat as the extra hand, and when the whale surged and the boat lurched, Pip went over the side.

This happens sometimes. The protocol is clear: the harpooner cuts the line if a man is overboard, because a man is worth more than a whale. Stubb's harpooner cut the line. The whale was lost. They circled back for Pip.

Stubb was furious. He told Pip that next time he would not cut the line. That whales were worth more per pound than Pip, which was a cruel joke, and Pip should think about that before falling out of the boat again.

Pip fell out again.

📜 Pip (Part 2)

This time Stubb did not cut the line immediately. He ran out a hundred yards before turning back. It was a minute, perhaps two. Not long by any ordinary measure.

It was long enough.

When they pulled Pip out of the water he was alive. He was not, quite, the same person who had gone in. He had been alone in the open ocean — vast and moving and nothing but water in every direction. He was very small and the ocean was very large and the time alone in it had done something to his mind that could not be undone.

He was not mad, exactly. He was elsewhere — occasionally present, occasionally in some other place, moving between the two states with a freedom that could look, at a distance, like cheerfulness. He still played his tambourine sometimes. He still laughed. But the laughs were at things no one else could see.

Ahab found him on the deck one evening and sat with him.

This surprised everyone. Ahab had not been soft with anyone since the voyage began. But he sat with Pip and talked to him quietly and held his hand, and his face during this had a quality I had not seen there before: not obsession, not intensity. Something more like grief.

📜 Pip (Part 3)

He told Pip to stay close to him. That he would keep him safe.

Pip looked at him with the clear eyes of someone who has no performance left. 'The sea is big,' he said.

'Yes,' Ahab said.

'Bigger than us,' Pip said.

Ahab did not answer. He looked at the ocean.

I think Pip said what Ahab already knew. The difference was that Pip could live with it, and Ahab could not.

📝 Grammar: Subordination and Coordination for Sentence Variety

Learn:

Coordination (using and, but, or, so) joins equal ideas. Subordination (using because, although, when, if) shows one idea is dependent on another. Varying these creates sophisticated writing: 'She told stories because the king needed to hear them' (subordination) vs. 'She told stories, and the king listened' (coordination).

Example:

"Coordination: 'The tale ended, but another began.' Subordination: 'Although the tale ended, another began immediately.' Notice how subordination creates a different emphasis."
