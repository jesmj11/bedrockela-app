📜 The Island of the Wak-Wak (Part 2)

There was an expedition, Scheherazade said, to the Island of the Wak-Wak — a place at the edge of the mapped world where the trees bore fruit that was human in shape and that cried out when the wind moved through them. It was a place of wonders but also of dangers, and the man who led the expedition — a warrior named Sayf al-Muluk — went there not for the island itself but for someone who was there. He went for a woman from his own country who had been taken there against her will, because she was too remarkable to be kept close and the king who had taken her had brought her to the island where she could not be reached. Sayf traveled to the end of the world. He fought his way through each obstacle — genies and enchantments and trials of courage and trials of patience, which were harder than the trials of courage. He arrived at the island. He fought his way to where the woman was kept. And she was not what he expected. He had carried, all this way, an image of her — someone who needed saving, someone waiting, someone for whom his arrival would be the resolution of the story. But she was not waiting. She had been busy. She had learned the language of the island, had made herself useful and then trusted, had mapped the palace and identified the weak points, had been laying the groundwork for her own escape for three years. He arrived and she said, practically: 'Good, there are two of us now. Here is what I know. Here is the plan.' 'I came to rescue you,' he said. 'I know,' she said. 'And I'm glad you came. But I have been working on this myself, and what I have and what you have together is better than either alone. Shall we proceed?' They proceeded. They escaped. They went home together. 'He set out to rescue someone,' Scheherazade said. 'He arrived to find a partner. The rescue he had planned turned into a collaboration he had not imagined, and was better for it.' She looked at the king. 'We make stories about what we expect,' she said. 'And then the real thing arrives and it is different from the story, and we can either refuse the real thing because it doesn't match the story, or we can put the story down and pay attention to what is actually in front of us.' She had been watching the king put stories down, slowly, night by night. She could not say what he was building in their place. But something was being built. She had four nights left.

🗣️ Language: Tone and Mood: Language Creates Atmosphere

Learn:

Tone is the author's attitude toward the subject (humorous, serious, ironic, respectful). Mood is the feeling created for the reader (suspenseful, hopeful, melancholy). Authors create tone and mood through word choice, sentence structure, imagery, and pacing.

Example:

"Serious tone: 'The king's decree devastated the kingdom.' Ironic tone: 'The king's "justice" brought only misery.' The quotation marks create irony. Suspenseful mood: 'The dawn approached. Would the king demand she continue or order her death?'"
