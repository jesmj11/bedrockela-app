📜 The Creature's Demand

The moral complexity of the creature's proposal forced Victor Frankenstein to confront questions about justice, responsibility, and the rights of artificial beings that no human being had ever been required to consider.

"Listen to me, Frankenstein," the creature said as they continued their confrontation among the Alpine glaciers. "You accuse me of murder, and yet you would, with a satisfied conscience, destroy your own creation. Oh, praise the eternal justice of man!"

The creature's argument was structured with logical precision that demonstrated his intellectual development while also revealing the depth of his understanding about the moral responsibilities that Victor had abandoned.

"I am malicious because I am miserable," the creature continued. "Am I not shunned and hated by all mankind? You, my creator, would tear me to pieces and triumph; remember that, and tell me why I should pity man more than he pities me?"

The philosophical framework that the creature used to justify his demand was based on natural rights theory that he had learned through his literary education, combined with emotional arguments that appealed to basic human sympathy.

"You must create a female for me," the creature insisted, "with whom I can live in the interchange of those sympathies necessary for my being. This you alone can do, and I demand it of you as a duty which you owe not only to me, but to all humanity."

The promise that accompanied the creature's demand was designed to address Victor's concerns about creating another potentially dangerous being while also appealing to his desire to protect his remaining family members from further violence.

"My companion and I," the creature explained, "will retire to the vast wilds of South America, where our mode of life will be peaceful and innocent. My food does not consist of that of man; I do not destroy the lamb and the kid to glut my appetite; acorns and berries afford me sufficient nourishment."

But the creature's description of his proposed exile also revealed his understanding that human society would never accept him regardless of his moral development or peaceful intentions.

"The picture I present to you is peaceful and human," the creature said, "and you must feel that you could deny it only in the wantonness of power and cruelty. Pitiless as you have been towards me, I now see compassion in your eyes; let me seize the favorable moment and persuade you to promise what I so ardently desire."

📜 The Creature's Demand (Part 2)

The emotional appeal that concluded the creature's argument was calculated to activate Victor's remaining capacity for moral reasoning while also warning him about the consequences of continued refusal.

"If you consent," the creature said, "neither you nor any other human being shall ever see us again; I will go to the vast wilds of South America. My food does not consist of that of man; I do not destroy the lamb and the kid to glut my appetite; acorns and berries afford me sufficient nourishment."

But the threat that accompanied this promise revealed the creature's complete understanding of his power over Victor's psychological welfare and family safety.

"But if you refuse," the creature continued, "I will glut the maw of death, until it be satiated with the blood of your remaining friends. I shall be with you on your wedding night!"

Victor's recognition that the creature possessed both the intellectual capability to plan systematic revenge and the physical power to implement it against any target he chose forced him to understand that refusal might result in the destruction of everyone he loved.

"The being finished speaking," Victor told Captain Walton, "and fixed his looks upon me in expectation of a reply. But I was bewildered, perplexed, and unable to arrange my ideas sufficiently to understand the full extent of his proposition."

The impossible choice that Victor faced was between creating a second potentially dangerous artificial being or accepting responsibility for whatever violence his refusal might provoke against innocent people who had no knowledge of his scientific experiments.

"On the one hand," Victor reasoned, "creating a female companion for my creature might result in a race of artificial beings that could threaten human civilization. On the other hand, refusing his demand would certainly result in continued murder of people whose only crime was being loved by the man who had created and abandoned him."

*Victor must choose between humanity's safety and his conscience.*

📝 Language: Idioms, Colloquialisms, and Cultural Expressions

Idioms are expressions whose meanings aren't literal: 'Break the ice' doesn't mean breaking actual ice. Understanding cultural expressions helps comprehension and shows how language reflects culture. Some expressions translate across cultures; others don't.

Example:

'The king lost his head' (lost control, not literally lost his head). 'She kept him in the dark' (kept him uninformed). Cultural expression: 'A thousand and one nights' suggests something vast, uncountable—the title itself is an idiom.
