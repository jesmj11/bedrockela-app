📜 The Carew Murder (Part 1)

The crime that transformed Edward Hyde from a mysteriously disturbing individual into London's most wanted criminal occurred on a fog-shrouded October night when Sir Danvers Carew made the fatal mistake of encountering the wrong person in the wrong place at the wrong time.

The only witness to the murder was a housemaid named Lucy Morrison, whose testimony to the police provided shocking evidence of violence that exceeded anything in London's criminal records.

"I was preparing to close the curtains in the upstairs sitting room," Morrison testified, "when I noticed two gentlemen meeting on the street below. One was an elderly man I recognized as Sir Danvers Carew, who lived several streets away and was well-known in the neighborhood for his charitable work with orphaned children."

Sir Danvers Carew was indeed one of London's most respected citizens—a member of Parliament whose humanitarian initiatives had earned him recognition throughout the British Empire. His murder would shock London society and create enormous pressure on police to identify and capture his killer quickly.

"The other gentleman was much smaller and younger," Morrison continued with obvious distress at recalling the events she had witnessed. "I had never seen him before, but there was something about his appearance and manner that made me continue watching their encounter."

"What specifically drew your attention to the smaller man?" asked Inspector Bradshaw, who was conducting the investigation with the urgency that major crimes against prominent citizens required.

Morrison struggled to articulate what had made her so uneasy about the stranger's presence.

"He moved differently than normal people, sir. Like an animal that's pretending to be human but can't quite manage to conceal its true nature. And even from two stories above, I could sense that he was... wrong, somehow. Wrong in ways that made my skin crawl."

📜 The Carew Murder (Part 2)

"Did you hear any of their conversation?" Inspector Bradshaw asked.

"Sir Danvers appeared to be asking for directions," Morrison replied. "Very polite, as he always was with everyone he met. The smaller man seemed to be providing helpful information at first. But then something changed."

Morrison's account of what happened next would become one of the most disturbing crime descriptions in London police files.

"Without any warning or provocation that I could observe, the small man attacked Sir Danvers with a heavy walking stick. Not just struck him once or twice, but beat him systematically and brutally, as if he was taking pleasure in causing pain and destruction."

The attack, according to Morrison's testimony, continued far beyond what would have been necessary to disable or kill the victim. Hyde had beaten Carew with sustained fury that suggested the violence was motivated by enjoyment rather than any practical criminal purpose.

"The most horrible part," Morrison continued, "was that the attacker seemed to be experiencing intense pleasure during the assault. He was laughing, sir. Laughing with genuine joy as he destroyed a man who had done nothing except ask for directions."

When police arrived at the crime scene, they found evidence that confirmed Morrison's account and suggested that London was dealing with a killer whose motivations exceeded normal criminal understanding.

Sir Danvers Carew's body had been battered beyond recognition. The walking stick used as the murder weapon had been broken in half from the force of the attack, with the remaining portion left beside the victim in an apparent gesture of contempt.

📜 The Carew Murder (Part 3)

Most disturbing of all, the killer had taken nothing from his victim—not money, not jewelry, not even the valuable watch that Sir Danvers had been carrying when he left his home for his evening walk.

"This wasn't robbery," Inspector Bradshaw concluded after examining the evidence. "This was murder committed for the pleasure of killing. We're dealing with someone who enjoys violence for its own sake."

The investigation that followed revealed that the killer had been identified by multiple witnesses as the same Edward Hyde who had been the subject of Gabriel Utterson's private inquiries.

But by the time police arrived at Hyde's building to arrest him, the mysterious figure had disappeared completely, leaving behind only a laboratory filled with chemical apparatus and a half-burned letter addressed to Dr. Henry Jekyll.

*Hyde had graduated from mere evil to actual murder.*

📝 Grammar: Verbals: Participles, Gerunds, and Infinitives

Learn:

Verbals are verb forms used as other parts of speech. Participles (-ing or -ed forms) act as adjectives: 'The sleeping king.' Gerunds (-ing forms) act as nouns: 'Storytelling saved her life.' Infinitives (to + verb) act as nouns, adjectives, or adverbs: 'To survive was her goal.'

Example:

"Participle: 'Scheherazade, speaking carefully, chose her words.' Gerund: 'Listening became the king's obsession.' Infinitive: 'To trust again would require courage.'"
