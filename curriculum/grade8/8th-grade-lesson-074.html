📜 The Sailor's Revenge

The introduction of James Vane into Dorian Gray's story brought with it the possibility that past actions might finally demand payment, regardless of supernatural protection or social position.

James Vane had spent eighteen years preparing for the moment when he would encounter the man responsible for his sister Sibyl's suicide—eighteen years of physical training, investigation, and patient determination to achieve justice that the law had failed to provide.

"I know he's still alive," James told his friend Tom Sturges as they discussed his continuing search for the aristocrat who had destroyed Sibyl's life. "Men like that don't just disappear. They're protected by wealth and social position, but they're not invisible."

The working-class determination that drove James's pursuit was fundamentally different from the aesthetic philosophy that governed Dorian's approach to life—where Dorian sought pleasure and beauty, James sought justice and responsibility.

"My sister died because some rich man thought her feelings were less important than his entertainment," James continued with the bitter knowledge that came from understanding how class differences affected the value placed on individual lives.

But James's investigation was complicated by the apparent impossibility of his quarry's continued existence—every description of "Prince Charming" suggested a young man in his twenties, while eighteen years had passed since Sibyl's death.

"It doesn't make sense," James admitted to Tom. "The man who seduced my sister should be nearly forty now, but everyone who's seen him recently describes someone who looks twenty-five at most."

The breakthrough that would bring James closer to his target came through his patient investigation of London's theater community, where rumors about Dorian Gray's influence had persisted for decades despite his careful cultivation of respectable appearances.

"I remember him," confirmed Mrs. Hedley, an elderly actress who had worked with Sibyl during her final performances. "He was the most beautiful young man any of us had ever seen, but there was something wrong about him. Something cold and cruel that made you uncomfortable despite his perfect manners."

📜 The Sailor's Revenge (Part 2)

The physical description that Mrs. Hedley provided matched perfectly with more recent accounts of Dorian's appearance—suggesting either that he possessed some supernatural method of preserving youth or that James was pursuing the wrong person entirely.

"Golden hair, blue eyes, features like a Greek statue," Mrs. Hedley continued. "But the eyes were empty of any genuine feeling, even when he was charming people or expressing concern. He looked at my poor Sibyl like she was a beautiful object rather than a human being."

James's decision to continue his pursuit despite the apparent impossibility of the situation was based on his understanding that justice mattered more than logical explanation—if Dorian Gray had somehow preserved his youth through unnatural means, that only made him more dangerous to other innocent people.

"I don't care how he's managed to stay young," James declared with the determination that had sustained him through eighteen years of searching. "I know he's responsible for Sibyl's death, and I'm going to make sure he pays for what he did to her."

The plan that James developed for confronting his sister's destroyer was simple and direct—he would track Dorian to some location where social protection was unavailable, then demand both confession and payment for the suffering he had caused.

*The past had a way of demanding payment.*

📝 Language: Idioms, Colloquialisms, and Cultural Expressions

Idioms are expressions whose meanings aren't literal: 'Break the ice' doesn't mean breaking actual ice. Understanding cultural expressions helps comprehension and shows how language reflects culture. Some expressions translate across cultures; others don't.

Example:

'The king lost his head' (lost control, not literally lost his head). 'She kept him in the dark' (kept him uninformed). Cultural expression: 'A thousand and one nights' suggests something vast, uncountable—the title itself is an idiom.
