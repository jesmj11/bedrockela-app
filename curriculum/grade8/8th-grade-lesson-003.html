📜 University Obsession (Part 1)

The transformation of Victor Frankenstein from promising student to dangerous obsessive began during his first encounter with Professor Waldman at the university, where modern scientific methods would provide him with capabilities that his alchemical studies had only suggested were possible.

"Young man," Professor Waldman said during Victor's initial interview about his academic interests, "I understand that you have been studying the works of medieval alchemists whose theories have been superseded by contemporary scientific understanding."

Victor had expected dismissal of his previous interests, similar to his father's reaction to his fascination with alchemical texts. But Professor Waldman's response revealed how modern chemistry had achieved many of the goals that ancient writers had pursued through less systematic methods.

"The alchemists," Waldman continued, "were correct in believing that dedicated scientific investigation could unlock fundamental secrets of natural processes. Their error was not in their ambitions, but in their primitive experimental techniques and their failure to develop mathematical frameworks adequate for understanding the phenomena they observed."

The lecture that followed opened Victor's mind to possibilities that exceeded anything he had imagined during his adolescent experiments with electrical forces and chemical transformations.

"Modern chemistry," Professor Waldman explained, "has indeed achieved transmutation of elements, artificial creation of compounds that do not exist in nature, and systematic control over biological processes that our predecessors could only dream of influencing."

But most significantly for Victor's future development, Waldman described contemporary research into the fundamental nature of life itself—investigations that suggested the boundary between living and non-living matter was less absolute than conventional understanding assumed.

"The vital principle," Waldman said, "appears to be a natural force that operates according to discoverable laws, rather than a mysterious essence that exceeds human comprehension or scientific investigation."

📜 University Obsession (Part 2)

Victor left that first meeting with Professor Waldman convinced that modern scientific methods could achieve the artificial creation of life that alchemists had theorized but never successfully accomplished.

"I will discover the cause of generation and life," Victor declared to himself as he planned the course of study that would provide him with knowledge and techniques needed for the most ambitious scientific project in human history.

The curriculum that Victor pursued during his university years was designed to provide comprehensive understanding of chemistry, anatomy, and natural philosophy that would serve as foundation for legitimate medical research and humanitarian scientific advancement.

But Victor's approach to his studies was characterized by intensity that exceeded normal academic dedication and approached the kind of obsession that would ultimately corrupt his judgment about the relationship between scientific capability and moral responsibility.

"I attended lectures during the day," Victor explained to Captain Walton, "but spent my evenings and nights in private study that went far beyond what my professors required or recommended."

The laboratory work that accompanied Victor's theoretical education provided him with hands-on experience in chemical experimentation, anatomical dissection, and electrical investigation that revealed practical techniques for manipulating natural processes.

"I learned," Victor continued, "how chemical compounds could alter biological functions, how electrical forces could stimulate muscular activity in dead tissue, and how anatomical knowledge could guide surgical intervention in living systems."

But Victor's growing expertise in these areas was accompanied by social isolation that separated him from the moral influences that might have guided his scientific development in more responsible directions.

📜 University Obsession (Part 3)

"I stopped corresponding with my family," Victor admitted, "because I was too absorbed in my research to maintain personal relationships that seemed less important than the scientific discoveries I was pursuing."

The combination of increasing technical capability and decreasing moral accountability created conditions that would ultimately enable Victor to undertake experiments that no responsible scientist would attempt.

"I began to understand," Victor said, "that artificial creation of life was not just theoretically possible, but practically achievable through proper application of the scientific techniques I was mastering at the university."

By the end of his second year of study, Victor had moved beyond legitimate educational objectives toward research goals that would challenge fundamental assumptions about the relationship between human knowledge and divine prerogative.

*He stood on the threshold of becoming like God himself.*

📝 Grammar: Restrictive vs. Non-Restrictive Clauses and Commas

Learn:

Restrictive clauses are essential to the meaning and need no commas: 'The girl who told the stories survived.' Non-restrictive clauses add extra information and use commas: 'Scheherazade, who had read everything, told stories.' The difference changes meaning: 'Students who studied passed' vs. 'Students, who studied, passed.'

Example:

"Restrictive: 'The tales that changed the king came from many cultures.' Non-restrictive: 'Arabian Nights, which contains hundreds of tales, influenced world literature.'"

📰 The Science of Life: Galvanism and Electricity in the 1800s

In Mary Shelley's time, scientists were discovering electricity's power over life and death. These discoveries directly inspired Frankenstein's creation scene.

**Luigi Galvani's Experiments (1780s)**: Italian scientist Luigi Galvani discovered that electrical current could make dead frogs' legs twitch. He theorized that electricity was the life force itself. "Galvanism" became synonymous with using electricity to animate dead tissue.

**Giovanni Aldini's Public Demonstrations (1803)**: Galvani's nephew performed horrifying public experiments on executed criminals' corpses. When electrical current was applied to a hanged murderer's body in London, "the jaws began to quiver, the adjoining muscles were horribly contorted, and one eye actually opened." Witnesses thought he might come back to life.

**The Vitalism Debate**: Scientists argued whether life required a special "vital force" or was purely mechanical/chemical. Could chemistry and electricity alone create life, or was something mystical required?

**Real Scientific Limitations**: Despite sensational demonstrations, scientists couldn't actually revive the dead or create life. But they were discovering that electricity controlled muscles and nerves—suggesting life was partly electrical.

Shelley's genius was recognizing that even if science could create life, moral questions remained: Should we? What responsibility comes with creation? What if the created being suffers?

She set her novel in the near future (1790s) when galvanism was new and seemed to promise god-like powers. Victor Frankenstein represents the scientist who achieves the impossible but never considers whether he should.
