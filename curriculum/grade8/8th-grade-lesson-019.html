📜 Escalating Vengeance (Part 1)

The systematic destruction of Victor Frankenstein's remaining happiness began immediately after his creature witnessed the destruction of the female companion, proving that the threat delivered through the laboratory window had been a promise rather than simply an emotional outburst.

"The murder of my dear friend Clerval," Victor told Captain Walton, "occurred within days of my return from Scotland to Ireland, where I had planned to reunite with the companions who had been waiting for me while I conducted my secret experiments."

Henry Clerval had been Victor's closest friend since childhood—a young man whose enthusiasm for literature and philosophy had provided emotional balance during Victor's increasingly obsessive focus on natural science and artificial creation.

"Clerval possessed," Victor said, "everything that my scientific pursuits had caused me to neglect—love of poetry, appreciation for human relationships, and moral sensitivity that guided his choices toward activities that benefited others rather than simply satisfying intellectual curiosity."

The circumstances of Clerval's death revealed how precisely the creature understood human psychology and his creator's emotional vulnerabilities.

"Clerval was found strangled," Victor explained, "in a manner that left clear evidence of superhuman strength while also ensuring that suspicion would fall on me rather than on the true perpetrator who remained invisible to normal investigation."

The creature's strategy for framing Victor was sophisticated enough to result in his arrest and imprisonment for his friend's murder, demonstrating that his plan involved not just killing people Victor loved but also destroying Victor's reputation and freedom.

📜 Escalating Vengeance (Part 2)

"I was charged with murder," Victor continued, "based on circumstantial evidence that seemed convincing to people who had no knowledge of my creature's existence or capabilities. My presence in the area where Clerval's body was discovered, combined with witness testimony about my agitated behavior, created a compelling case for prosecution."

But the most psychologically devastating aspect of Clerval's murder was Victor's recognition that his friend had died because of his association with the scientist whose experiments had created an artificial being capable of systematic revenge.

"Clerval was innocent," Victor reflected with guilt that exceeded anything he had experienced after William's death or Justine's execution. "His only crime was friendship with someone who had chosen to pursue knowledge without accepting the moral responsibilities that accompanied scientific capability."

The legal proceedings that followed Clerval's murder provided Victor with direct experience of how his creature's actions were destroying not just the lives of innocent people but also the social relationships and legal protections that made civilized existence possible.

"I could not defend myself," Victor admitted, "without revealing information about my creature that would either be dismissed as evidence of insanity or would create panic that might be even more dangerous than the creature's continued secret existence."

The pattern that emerged from the creature's escalating violence was clearly designed to isolate Victor from every source of emotional support and social connection that made his life worth living.

"He was systematically destroying," Victor realized, "everyone whose death would cause me psychological pain while also eliminating the relationships that might have provided moral guidance or practical assistance in dealing with the crisis that my scientific irresponsibility had created."

📜 Escalating Vengeance (Part 3)

Victor's eventual release from prison came only after his father's intervention and the lack of conclusive evidence for conviction, but the experience had demonstrated how completely his creature could manipulate legal and social systems to achieve his revenge.

"I understood," Victor concluded, "that my creature possessed not only the physical power needed to kill anyone I cared about, but also the intelligence needed to ensure that I would be blamed for their deaths in ways that would destroy my social connections and legal standing."

The psychological strategy underlying the creature's campaign of vengeance was becoming clear—he intended to make Victor as isolated and desperate as he had been since the moment of his awakening into abandoned existence.

*The creature was systematically destroying Victor's life.*

🗣️ Language: Author's Purpose and Rhetorical Strategies

Learn:

Authors choose words to achieve specific purposes: inform, persuade, entertain, inspire. Rhetorical strategies include appeals to emotion (pathos), logic (logos), and credibility (ethos). Word choice, sentence structure, and figurative language all serve the author's purpose.

Example:

"Scheherazade uses narrative (storytelling) to persuade the king to change—this is a rhetorical strategy. She appeals to his emotions through compelling characters (pathos) and to his logic through cautionary tales (logos). Her credibility comes from her vast knowledge (ethos)."
