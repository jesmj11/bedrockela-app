📜 Years of Hidden Decay

The passage of time that should have marked Dorian Gray's transition from youth to maturity instead became a period during which his supernatural preservation allowed him to accumulate decades of moral corruption without visible consequences.

By his thirtieth birthday, Dorian appeared exactly as he had at twenty, while the portrait hidden in his locked room had become increasingly hideous as it reflected each selfish choice, each act of cruelty, each moment when he chose pleasure over responsibility.

"It's remarkable," observed Lady Narborough during one of the fashionable dinner parties where Dorian had become a celebrated guest, "how you never seem to age. You look exactly as you did when you first appeared in London society ten years ago."

The compliments about his preserved youth that Dorian received from London's social elite were accompanied by whispered speculation about the methods he might be using to maintain such extraordinary appearance.

"Some people say he's made a deal with the devil," Lady Windermere confided to her friend Mrs. Erlynne. "Others think he's discovered some secret of medical science that prevents aging. But everyone agrees that it's not natural for someone to look twenty when he's obviously lived enough to be forty."

But Dorian's unchanging beauty was matched by a reputation for corrupting everyone who became closely associated with him, though the specific details of his influence were never discussed openly in polite society.

📜 Years of Hidden Decay (Part 2)

"Young men who become his friends," noted Sir Thomas Burdon during a conversation at his club, "seem to develop expensive tastes and questionable moral habits. Several promising careers have been ruined through association with Gray."

The pattern of destruction that characterized Dorian's relationships was systematic and deliberate—he sought out innocent or vulnerable people whose corruption would provide him with entertainment while demonstrating his power to influence others.

"He has a talent," observed Dr. Geoffrey Clouston, "for identifying people's weaknesses and encouraging them to indulge those weaknesses until they become destructive obsessions."

The artistic community that had originally embraced Dorian because of Basil Hallward's championship had become wary of his influence after several young painters and writers had suffered career destruction following their friendship with him.

"He destroys people," concluded the critic Philip Wayne, "not through direct cruelty but through sophisticated encouragement of their worst impulses. He makes vice seem artistic and corruption appear sophisticated."

But perhaps most disturbing was Dorian's growing collection of beautiful objects that he acquired through methods that often involved the financial ruin or social disgrace of their previous owners.

📜 Years of Hidden Decay (Part 3)

"His house," reported Mrs. Cheveley after attending one of his exclusive dinner parties, "is filled with treasures that seem to have tragic histories. Beautiful things that were obviously sold by people who needed money desperately or quickly."

The portrait that recorded Dorian's moral development during these years of hidden decay had become so horrific that he could barely stand to look at it, even though it remained the only honest record of what he had become.

"Every sin," Dorian observed during one of his rare moments of self-reflection, "every selfish choice, every act of cruelty is written on that canvas while my face remains unchanged. I've achieved immortal beauty at the cost of eternal damnation."

*Every pleasure extracted had left its mark on the hidden canvas.*

📝 Grammar: Active and Passive Voice: Strategic Use

Active voice emphasizes the doer: 'Scheherazade told stories.' Passive voice emphasizes the action or receiver: 'Stories were told by Scheherazade.' Passive voice is useful when the doer is unknown, unimportant, or when you want to shift focus. Overusing passive voice weakens writing.

Example:

Active: 'The king executed the women.' Passive: 'The women were executed' (focuses on victims, not perpetrator). Sometimes passive voice is more appropriate for the meaning you want to convey.
