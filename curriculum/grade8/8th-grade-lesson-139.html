📜 The Great Hiatus

The period following Holmes's apparent death at Reichenbach Falls revealed how completely London had come to depend on one man's detective genius to solve crimes that baffled conventional investigation and protect society from criminal intelligence that exceeded ordinary law enforcement capabilities.

"The crime rate," Inspector Lestrade reported to the Commissioner of Police, "has increased dramatically since Holmes's death, with particularly sophisticated crimes going unsolved because our normal investigation methods cannot match the systematic criminal planning that has become increasingly common."

Watson's grief for his lost friend and partner was complicated by his recognition that Holmes's death had eliminated not just a personal relationship but a form of justice that could not be replaced by conventional police work.

"I find myself unable to read about crimes in the newspapers," Watson confided to Mrs. Hudson, "because I keep thinking about how Holmes would have approached each case and what solutions he would have found that are now permanently beyond reach."

But perhaps most disturbing were the reports that began reaching London from various parts of the world, describing mysterious incidents where impossible crimes had been solved by unknown individuals whose methods seemed remarkably similar to Holmes's detective techniques.

"A locked-room murder in Prague," Watson noted in his journal, "was solved by someone who used observation and deduction to identify a killer that local police had never suspected. The solution was elegant, logical, and completely consistent with Holmes's approach to similar problems."

📜 The Great Hiatus (Part 2)

The pattern of such reports suggested either an extraordinary series of coincidences or the possibility that Holmes had somehow survived Reichenbach Falls and was continuing his detective work under different circumstances.

"A counterfeiting ring in Vienna," Watson continued, "was exposed through analysis of physical evidence that revealed systematic criminal organization operating across national borders. The investigation methods were identical to techniques that Holmes had pioneered during his London career."

Mrs. Hudson's increasing hope that her lodger might still be alive was matched by Watson's careful reluctance to believe reports that might represent wishful thinking rather than factual evidence.

"I don't dare hope," Watson told her, "because accepting the possibility that Holmes survived would make discovering the truth about his death unbearably painful if these reports prove to be coincidental rather than evidence of his continued existence."

But the systematic nature of the reported detective work, combined with the specific techniques being used to solve seemingly impossible crimes, created a pattern that seemed unlikely to result from random coincidence.

"Someone," Watson realized, "is using Holmes's exact methods to solve crimes in locations where his presence would be unexpected and his identity could remain concealed from anyone who might recognize him."

📜 The Great Hiatus (Part 3)

The message that arrived at Baker Street three years after Reichenbach Falls was delivered by a street child who claimed to have been paid by a tall, thin man with a distinctive voice to ensure that it reached Dr. Watson personally.

"My dear Watson," the note read in handwriting that was unmistakably Holmes's, "the reports of my death were greatly exaggerated, and the time has come for me to return to London to complete the work that Moriarty's criminal network prevented me from finishing."

*But was the great detective truly dead?*

📝 Language: Author's Purpose and Rhetorical Strategies

Authors choose words to achieve specific purposes: inform, persuade, entertain, inspire. Rhetorical strategies include appeals to emotion (pathos), logic (logos), and credibility (ethos). Word choice, sentence structure, and figurative language all serve the author's purpose.

Example:

Scheherazade uses narrative (storytelling) to persuade the king to change—this is a rhetorical strategy. She appeals to his emotions through compelling characters (pathos) and to his logic through cautionary tales (logos). Her credibility comes from her vast knowledge (ethos).
