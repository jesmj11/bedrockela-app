📜 Sibyl's Tragedy (Part 1)

The news that reached Dorian Gray the morning after his cruel rejection of Sibyl Vane would provide him with the first test of whether he possessed any capacity for genuine human feeling or moral responsibility.

"I'm afraid I have tragic news," Lord Henry announced as he entered Dorian's breakfast room with a newspaper in his hand and an expression of carefully controlled concern. "It's about your actress friend, Miss Vane."

Dorian looked up from his morning correspondence with mild interest rather than the alarm that someone capable of love would have felt upon hearing such an announcement.

"What about Sibyl?" Dorian asked, though his tone suggested that her welfare was less important to him than the day's social engagements.

"She's dead," Henry replied with the detached manner he brought to discussing other people's tragedies. "Apparently she killed herself sometime after the theater closed last night. The newspaper says she drank poison in her dressing room."

The information that should have devastated someone who had claimed to love Sibyl Vane produced in Dorian only a momentary shock followed by something approaching relief that an embarrassing situation had resolved itself without requiring further action from him.

"How terrible," Dorian said with appropriate words but without genuine feeling. "I suppose I should feel responsible, since our conversation last night obviously contributed to her despair."

📜 Sibyl's Tragedy (Part 2)

But Lord Henry's response provided Dorian with sophisticated justification for avoiding any sense of guilt or moral responsibility for the tragedy his cruelty had caused.

"My dear boy," Henry said with the philosophical detachment that characterized his approach to human suffering, "you mustn't blame yourself for the emotional instability of someone you barely knew. Artists are notoriously prone to dramatic gestures, and suicide is simply the most extreme form of theatrical performance."

The conversation that followed demonstrated how completely Henry's influence had prepared Dorian to rationalize away any inconvenient moral feelings that might interfere with his pursuit of pleasure and self-interest.

"She was bound to end tragically," Henry continued, "because she confused art with reality, emotion with genuine understanding. You're better off without someone so unstable and melodramatic."

Dorian found these arguments convincing because they allowed him to avoid confronting the devastating truth that his rejection had driven someone who loved him completely to choose death rather than life without his affection.

"You're right," Dorian agreed with growing confidence in his own moral innocence. "I couldn't have saved her from her own weaknesses, and it would be foolish to let her suicide spoil my own happiness."

But the most significant aspect of Dorian's response to Sibyl's death was his recognition that he felt no genuine grief or remorse—only intellectual interest in his own emotional reactions and relief that an inconvenient attachment had been terminated.

📜 Sibyl's Tragedy (Part 3)

"I should probably attend the funeral," Dorian observed, "if only to maintain appearances and show proper respect for someone who meant something to me."

Henry's advice reflected his understanding that conventional expressions of mourning would interfere with Dorian's moral education in the proper pursuit of aesthetic pleasure.

"Absolutely not," Henry replied firmly. "Attending the funeral of someone who killed herself over you would create exactly the kind of morbid association that interferes with the development of a truly artistic temperament. You must learn to treat life as a work of art rather than a series of emotional obligations."

The decision that Dorian made about how to respond to Sibyl's death would establish the pattern for all his future moral choices—sophisticated rationalization would replace genuine feeling, and aesthetic pleasure would take precedence over human responsibility.

"You're absolutely right," Dorian concluded with obvious relief. "I'll remember Sibyl as she was at her best, and I won't let her weakness spoil my appreciation for the beautiful things in life."

*Her death was just the beginning of his moral descent.*

📝 Grammar: Parallel Structure in Lists and Series

Learn:

Parallel structure uses the same grammatical form for items in a series, creating rhythm and clarity. All items should be the same part of speech or phrase type: 'She was intelligent, determined, and resourceful' (all adjectives). Not parallel: 'She was intelligent, determined, and had resourcefulness.'

Example:

"Parallel: 'The king lost his faith, abandoned his principles, and destroyed his compassion.' Not parallel: 'The king lost his faith, his principles were abandoned, and destroying his compassion.'"
