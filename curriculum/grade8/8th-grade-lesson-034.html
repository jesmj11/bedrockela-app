📜 The Incident at the Window (Part 1)

The encounter that convinced Gabriel Utterson that his friend Dr. Jekyll was in serious danger occurred during what should have been a routine social call on a pleasant Sunday afternoon.

Utterson had decided to visit Jekyll's home directly rather than continuing his indirect investigation of the mysterious Edward Hyde. Three weeks of systematic inquiry had provided disturbing evidence that Hyde existed and that he had some kind of relationship with Jekyll, but no clear understanding of what that relationship involved or why Jekyll was facilitating it.

"Dr. Jekyll is not receiving visitors today," Poole, Jekyll's long-serving butler, informed Utterson when he arrived at the familiar Cavendish Square residence where he had enjoyed countless pleasant evenings over the past decade.

"Is he ill?" Utterson asked with genuine concern. Jekyll was typically enthusiastic about hosting friends for dinner and conversation, particularly on Sunday afternoons when his weekly laboratory work was suspended.

"Not ill, sir, but... occupied with important research that cannot be interrupted," Poole replied with obvious discomfort. "He has been working in his laboratory for the past four days without emerging for meals or social interaction."

This was highly unusual behavior for Jekyll, whose social habits were as regular as his scientific schedule. Utterson's lawyer instincts told him that something significant had changed in his friend's circumstances.

"Perhaps I could speak with him briefly from the garden," Utterson suggested. "I need only a few minutes to discuss a legal matter that requires his attention."

Poole's expression suggested that he was also concerned about his employer's recent behavior, and he led Utterson through the house to the back garden that provided access to Jekyll's private laboratory.

The laboratory was Jekyll's pride and scientific sanctuary—a modern facility that contained the most advanced chemical and research equipment available in London. Jekyll had spent considerable time and money creating a workspace that would support his experiments in what he described as "the fundamental nature of human psychology and moral development."

"Dr. Jekyll!" Utterson called from the garden, looking up toward the laboratory windows on the building's second floor. "It's Gabriel Utterson! I'd like to speak with you for just a moment!"

📜 The Incident at the Window (Part 2)

The face that appeared at the laboratory window was recognizably Jekyll's, but changed in ways that shocked Utterson with their implications.

His friend appeared to have aged years in the weeks since their last meeting. Jekyll's typically robust complexion had become pale and drawn. His eyes carried an expression of fear and guilt that suggested he was wrestling with problems that exceeded anything in Utterson's experience with troubled clients.

"Gabriel," Jekyll called down from the window, his voice carrying relief at seeing a trusted friend combined with obvious reluctance to engage in social conversation. "I'm afraid I cannot receive visitors at present. My research has reached a critical stage that requires complete concentration."

"Henry," Utterson replied, "I need to discuss with you certain legal arrangements that may require modification based on changed circumstances. Could we schedule an appointment for later this week?"

But even as Utterson spoke, he watched Jekyll's expression transform from uncomfortable embarrassment to absolute terror.

Jekyll's face went white as if he had suddenly remembered something horrible. His eyes widened with panic, and his mouth opened as if he intended to call out some kind of warning or plea for help.

Then, without a word of explanation or farewell, Jekyll slammed the laboratory window shut and disappeared from view.

"Sir?" Poole asked quietly, obviously as disturbed by his employer's behavior as Utterson was by his friend's transformation.

Utterson stared at the closed laboratory window for several minutes, trying to process the implications of what he had witnessed.

Jekyll had been afraid—not just uncomfortable or embarrassed, but genuinely terrified. The expression that had crossed his face suggested that he was confronting some immediate threat or overwhelming crisis that required desperate action.

📜 The Incident at the Window (Part 3)

"Poole," Utterson said as they returned through the house toward the front entrance, "how long has Dr. Jekyll been exhibiting this kind of behavior?"

"Perhaps six weeks, sir," Poole replied with obvious reluctance to discuss his employer's private affairs. "Since shortly after he began receiving visits from a gentleman who... who concerns the household staff."

"What gentleman?" Utterson asked, though he suspected he already knew the answer.

"A Mr. Hyde, sir. Small fellow, somewhat deformed, who has a key to the laboratory and comes and goes at unusual hours. The staff... well, sir, the staff finds him rather disturbing."

As Utterson returned to his own home, he understood that his investigation of Edward Hyde had moved beyond curiosity about an unusual will into genuine concern for Jekyll's safety and wellbeing.

His friend was obviously in some kind of serious trouble that involved the mysterious and disturbing creature who had so alarmed everyone who encountered him.

*Dr. Jekyll was afraid of something - or someone - in his own house.*

🗣️ Language: Word Roots: Greek and Latin Foundations

Learn:

Most academic vocabulary comes from Greek and Latin roots. Common roots: 'aud' (hear), 'dict' (speak), 'spec' (see), 'port' (carry), 'graph' (write), 'bio' (life), 'chrono' (time), 'geo' (earth). Knowing roots unlocks hundreds of words.

Example:

"'Spectator' (one who sees), 'inspect' (look into), 'retrospect' (look back). Same root, different prefixes change meaning. 'Biography' (writing about life), 'biology' (study of life)—same root, different suffixes."
