📜 The Corrupted Portrait (Part 1)

The discovery that would horrify all of London's high society began when the servants of Lord Henry Wotton's Mayfair mansion broke down the door to a room that had been locked for over twenty years.

Inside that sealed chamber, they found a portrait so hideous, so twisted with evil, that none of them could look upon it for more than a few seconds without feeling physically sick.

The painting showed a man whose face had been transformed by decades of cruelty, greed, and moral corruption. Every sin was written in the features—the mouth twisted with contempt, the eyes glittering with malice, the entire countenance bearing the accumulated weight of countless evil acts.

But beside this horrific canvas lay the body of one of London's most celebrated beauties: Dorian Gray, who had somehow maintained the appearance of a twenty-year-old man despite being well into his fifties.

📜 The Corrupted Portrait (Part 2)

"It's impossible," whispered Mrs. Leaf, the head housekeeper, as she stared at Dorian's corpse. "He looks exactly as he did when he first came to London thirty years ago. Not a line, not a gray hair, not a single sign of aging."

But even as they watched, Dorian's perfectly preserved features began to change with supernatural swiftness. Within minutes, the beautiful young face aged decades, becoming withered, ugly, and corrupted—exactly matching the hideous portrait that lay destroyed beside him.

"Dear God," breathed the butler, understanding finally what they had discovered. "The painting... it was aging for him. It was bearing the burden of his sins while he remained young and beautiful."

The newspapers would call it the most mysterious death in Victorian London's history. But those who had known Dorian Gray during his years of scandalous living understood that they had witnessed the final act of a supernatural bargain that had allowed beauty to mask the most corrupted soul in England.

📝 Grammar: Sentence Structure: Simple, Compound, Complex, Compound-Complex

Learn:

Understanding the four sentence types is essential for sophisticated writing. Simple sentences have one independent clause. Compound sentences join two independent clauses with a coordinating conjunction. Complex sentences combine an independent clause with one or more dependent clauses. Compound-complex sentences have multiple independent clauses AND at least one dependent clause.

Example:

"Simple: 'The king listened.' Compound: 'The king listened, and Scheherazade spoke.' Complex: 'When Scheherazade spoke, the king listened.' Compound-Complex: 'When Scheherazade spoke, the king listened, and the night passed quickly.'"
