📜 The Locked Laboratory

The crisis that would force Gabriel Utterson to take direct action in his investigation began when Poole appeared at his residence in a state of terror that exceeded anything the loyal butler had exhibited during fifteen years of faithful service to Dr. Jekyll.

"Mr. Utterson, sir," Poole said with obvious difficulty controlling his emotions, "I need your help with Dr. Jekyll. Something terrible has happened, and I don't know what to do."

Poole's account of his employer's recent behavior suggested that Jekyll's situation had deteriorated beyond anything that normal household management could address.

"Dr. Jekyll locked himself in his laboratory six days ago," Poole explained. "Since then, he has refused all food, declined all visitors, and responded to our inquiries only with written notes passed under the door. But sir, the notes are not written in Dr. Jekyll's normal handwriting."

"What do you mean?" Utterson asked with growing alarm.

"The handwriting changes, sir. Sometimes it looks like Dr. Jekyll's usual script. Sometimes it's completely different—smaller, more cramped, like someone else entirely is writing the messages. And the content..." Poole paused, obviously disturbed by what he had to report.

"The content alternates between Dr. Jekyll's normal polite instructions and demands written by someone who sounds like a completely different person. Someone angry, threatening, and...evil, sir. There's no other word for it."

Poole's description of the sounds emanating from Jekyll's laboratory was even more disturbing than the inconsistent handwriting.

"We hear voices, sir. Sometimes Dr. Jekyll's voice, speaking in his normal tone about scientific matters. But sometimes we hear another voice entirely—higher pitched, more aggressive, saying things that no gentleman would ever say in the presence of household staff."

📜 The Locked Laboratory (Part 2)

"Two people are in the laboratory?" Utterson asked.

"That's what we thought initially, sir. But we've been watching the laboratory entrance continuously for six days. No one has entered or left the room since Dr. Jekyll locked the door. Yet we clearly hear two distinct voices engaged in what sounds like arguments between people who hate each other."

The implications of Poole's testimony were so disturbing that Utterson initially resisted accepting their logical conclusion.

"You're suggesting that Dr. Jekyll is conducting conversations with someone who is present in a sealed room that no one has entered or left?" Utterson asked.

"I'm suggesting, sir, that something impossible is happening in that laboratory. Something that requires assistance from someone with legal authority to intervene in circumstances that are beyond normal household procedures."

Utterson's decision to accompany Poole to Jekyll's residence was motivated by genuine concern for his friend's safety, but also by his responsibility as a lawyer to investigate evidence of behavior that might require legal intervention.

The Jekyll household was in a state of barely controlled panic when they arrived. Servants who had worked for Jekyll for years were obviously terrified by whatever was happening in the laboratory, and several had requested permission to leave their employment rather than remain in the house.

"Listen, sir," Poole said as they approached the laboratory door. "You can hear for yourself."

The sounds coming from behind the locked door were unlike anything in Utterson's experience with normal human behavior.

📜 The Locked Laboratory (Part 3)

Jekyll's voice could be heard pleading desperately with someone, begging for cooperation or mercy from a listener who responded with laughter that carried pure malevolence.

"Please," Jekyll's voice said clearly enough to be understood through the heavy door. "You've had your time. You've done everything you wanted. Now let me return to normal consciousness before someone else gets hurt."

The response came in a higher-pitched voice that radiated contempt and amusement.

"Normal consciousness?" the other voice replied with obvious mockery. "Henry, you still don't understand, do you? There is no 'normal consciousness' anymore. There's only me."

As Utterson listened to what sounded like two people engaged in a life-or-death struggle for control of the same physical existence, he understood that his investigation had uncovered something that exceeded the boundaries of normal criminal behavior.

*Something inhuman was trapped in Jekyll's laboratory.*

📝 Language: Etymology: Word Histories and Evolution

Words have histories—they come from other languages, change meanings over time, and carry their origins with them. 'Sinbad' comes from Arabic. 'Scheherazade' is Persian. 'Genie' comes from Arabic 'jinn.' Knowing etymology deepens understanding and makes words memorable.

Example:

'Assassin' comes from Arabic 'hashashiyyin' (users of hashish), from a medieval group. 'Admiral' from Arabic 'amir al-' (commander of). Many English words have Arabic origins through trade and cultural exchange.
