📜 Cry for Heroes

The council that assembled in Hrothgar's private chambers three days after Grendel's first attack included the wisest men in Denmark and representatives from neighboring kingdoms who had come to offer assistance in a crisis that threatened all of northern civilization.

"We face," announced Hrothgar with the heavy dignity of a king who had never before admitted that any problem exceeded his ability to solve, "an enemy that cannot be fought with conventional weapons or defeated through normal military strategy."

The evidence that supported this grim conclusion was undeniable—thirty of Denmark's finest warriors had been slaughtered in a single night by a creature that left claw marks burned into stone and whose strength could tear armed men apart as easily as a child might destroy wooden toys.

"Every weapon we possessed," reported Aeschere, Hrothgar's most trusted counselor, "proved useless against this monster. Swords that could cleave through mail and helmet were turned aside by hide that seems harder than iron. Spears that could pierce shield and breastplate snapped against flesh that appears impervious to mortal weapons."

The wise men who had advised Danish kings for generations found themselves confronting a problem that exceeded all their accumulated knowledge of warfare, diplomacy, and statecraft.

"Our ancient lore," admitted Wulfgar, who knew more battle-songs and hero-tales than any man living, "speaks of such creatures in the oldest stories, but always as enemies defeated by heroes whose strength exceeded normal human capabilities."

📜 Cry for Heroes (Part 2)

The prayers and sacrifices that had been offered to secure divine assistance had brought no relief, no sign that Heaven was prepared to intervene in a conflict between supernatural evil and human civilization.

"God helps those who help themselves," observed Father Hrethric, the hall's chaplain, "but perhaps He expects us to find among ourselves the courage and strength that this battle will require."

The appeals that had been sent to neighboring kingdoms had produced expressions of sympathy and offers of normal military assistance, but no ruler possessed warriors capable of facing an enemy that human weapons could not harm.

"King Hygelac of the Geats," reported the messenger who had returned from that distant court, "sends word that his heart grieves for Denmark's suffering, but his army is composed of mortal men who would be slaughtered as easily as our own warriors have been."

But among the responses that reached Hrothgar's court was one message that offered hope different from the conventional military aid that other kingdoms could provide.

"There is," the Geatish messenger continued, "one warrior in Hygelac's court whose strength and courage exceed anything known among ordinary men. His name is Beowulf, and he has accomplished deeds that other warriors consider impossible."

📜 Cry for Heroes (Part 3)

The account of Beowulf's heroic achievements included battles with sea-monsters during swimming contests that lasted for days, single-handed victories against entire companies of enemy warriors, and feats of strength that seemed to exceed normal human capabilities.

"He has heard," the messenger concluded, "of Denmark's plight, and he has declared his intention to sail across the whale-road with chosen companions to test his strength against whatever evil has dared to attack King Hrothgar's hall."

The response that this news generated among the Danish court was a mixture of hope and skepticism—hope that supernatural heroism might match supernatural evil, skepticism that any single warrior, however brave, could succeed where thirty experienced fighters had failed.

*Word spread across the whale-road of Denmark's desperate need.*

📝 Grammar: Restrictive vs. Non-Restrictive Clauses and Commas

Restrictive clauses are essential to the meaning and need no commas: 'The girl who told the stories survived.' Non-restrictive clauses add extra information and use commas: 'Scheherazade, who had read everything, told stories.' The difference changes meaning: 'Students who studied passed' vs. 'Students, who studied, passed.'

Example:

Restrictive: 'The tales that changed the king came from many cultures.' Non-restrictive: 'Arabian Nights, which contains hundreds of tales, influenced world literature.'
