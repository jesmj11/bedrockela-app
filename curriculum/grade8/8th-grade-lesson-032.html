📜 The Strange Will

The document that would change Gabriel Utterson's understanding of his oldest friendship was locked in his office safe, waiting for his Monday morning review of pending legal matters.

Dr. Henry Jekyll's will was one of the most unusual documents Utterson had encountered during twenty years of legal practice. Most wills were straightforward expressions of a person's desire to transfer property and assets to family members or trusted friends after death. Jekyll's will was something altogether different—a legal arrangement that made no sense according to any rational understanding of human relationships or financial planning.

"In the event of my death or disappearance for a period exceeding three calendar months," the document read in Jekyll's precise handwriting, "all of my possessions, properties, and assets shall transfer immediately and without restriction to Mr. Edward Hyde."

Utterson had argued against the wisdom of this arrangement when Jekyll first proposed it six months earlier, but his friend had insisted with unusual emotional intensity that the will be prepared exactly as specified.

"Henry," Utterson had said during their discussion of the document's terms, "you are proposing to leave one of London's largest fortunes to someone I have never met, whom you describe only as 'a friend whose welfare concerns me greatly.' This is not responsible estate planning."

Jekyll's response had been uncharacteristically evasive for someone who typically approached legal and financial matters with scientific precision.

"Gabriel, there are circumstances in my life that require arrangements which may seem unusual to conventional thinking. I need your professional assistance in creating legal structures that serve purposes you may not fully understand."

📜 The Strange Will (Part 2)

At the time, Utterson had attributed his friend's secretiveness to some form of mid-life crisis or temporary emotional disturbance. Distinguished scientists sometimes developed peculiar obsessions or formed inappropriate attachments that required discreet legal accommodation.

But after Enfield's description of his encounter with Edward Hyde, Utterson was beginning to suspect that Jekyll's unusual will was connected to something far more troubling than a simple midlife emotional complication.

The physical description Enfield had provided—small, deformed, radiating malevolence—matched no one in Jekyll's normal social circle. Jekyll associated with respected scientists, prominent physicians, and established London society figures. Edward Hyde sounded like someone who belonged to an entirely different world of criminality and moral corruption.

"What hold could this Hyde creature have over the respected Dr. Jekyll?" Utterson wondered as he studied the will's bizarre provisions.

Blackmail was an obvious possibility. Hyde might have discovered some embarrassing secret from Jekyll's past and was using that knowledge to extract financial concessions. Such arrangements were not uncommon among London's professional classes, where maintaining respectability often required paying off people who possessed damaging information.

But blackmail typically involved ongoing payments rather than wholesale transfer of assets. Jekyll's will suggested something more permanent and more serious than simple extortion.

Another possibility was that Jekyll was being threatened with physical violence and had created the will as protection against harm. Perhaps Hyde represented criminal associates who had convinced Jekyll that his physical safety depended on ensuring their financial benefit from his continued survival.

📜 The Strange Will (Part 3)

But this explanation also seemed inadequate. Jekyll was a physically robust man with influential friends and adequate resources to protect himself from criminal threats through conventional means.

The most disturbing possibility was that Jekyll had formed some kind of voluntary association with Hyde—a relationship based on shared interests or activities that Jekyll was desperate to keep secret from his professional colleagues and social circle.

As Utterson locked the mysterious will back in his safe and prepared for his day's appointments, he made a decision that would draw him deeper into a mystery that challenged everything he thought he understood about human nature.

He would investigate Edward Hyde systematically and thoroughly until he understood exactly what kind of relationship existed between the respectable Dr. Jekyll and the creature who had so disturbed his cousin Enfield.

But Utterson had no way of knowing that his investigation would reveal truths about the potential for evil that existed within even the most civilized human beings—including people he had known and trusted for decades.

*What hold could this Hyde creature have over the respected Dr. Jekyll?*

📝 Language: Context Clues: Multiple Strategies Combined

Expert readers use multiple context clue types simultaneously: definition/restatement (author defines it), synonym/antonym (similar/opposite word nearby), example (specific instances given), inference (reasoning from situation). Look for signal words: 'or,' 'such as,' 'unlike,' 'however.'

Example:

From Arabian Nights: 'The king was consumed by a rage that was also grief—unlike simple anger, this sorrow-fury combination destroyed his judgment.' Multiple clues: definition ('sorrow-fury'), contrast ('unlike simple anger'), and effect (destroyed judgment).
