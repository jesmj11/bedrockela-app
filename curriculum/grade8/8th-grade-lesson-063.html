📜 The Philosopher of Pleasure

Lord Henry Wotton's first conversation with Dorian Gray introduced the young man to ideas that would ultimately corrupt his soul while appearing to offer sophisticated wisdom about the nature of life and pleasure.

"You must be Dorian Gray," Henry said with the charm that had made him one of London's most sought-after dinner companions. "Basil has told me so much about you, though he failed to adequately prepare me for such extraordinary beauty."

Dorian had been accustomed to compliments about his appearance throughout his life, but there was something in Lord Henry's manner that suggested deeper understanding of what beauty meant and how it could be used.

"I'm pleased to meet you, Lord Henry," Dorian replied with the polite formality that characterized his interactions with older members of London society.

But Henry had no intention of maintaining conventional social distance with someone whose youth and beauty represented such perfect raw material for the philosophical ideas he had been developing about life, pleasure, and the proper use of natural gifts.

"Tell me," Henry continued as he settled into a comfortable chair where he could observe both Dorian and Basil's artistic work, "what do you plan to do with such remarkable beauty? Surely you don't intend to waste it on conventional respectability?"

The question surprised Dorian, who had never considered his appearance as something that required planning or strategic use rather than simple acceptance.

📜 The Philosopher of Pleasure (Part 2)

"I hadn't thought about it," Dorian admitted. "My beauty is simply part of who I am, not something I use for any particular purpose."

Henry's response introduced Dorian to ideas about the relationship between beauty and moral responsibility that would reshape his entire understanding of how he should live his life.

"My dear boy," Henry said with the tone of someone sharing profound wisdom, "beauty is a form of genius—it is higher, indeed, than genius, as it needs no explanation. But it is also temporary. You have only a few short years to enjoy the power that such extraordinary appearance provides."

The philosophy that Henry outlined during their conversation was sophisticated, compelling, and ultimately poisonous in its effects on someone as impressionable as young Dorian Gray.

"The only way to resist temptation," Henry explained with obvious pleasure in his own cleverness, "is to yield to it. Resist it, and your soul grows sick with longing for the things it has forbidden to itself."

Basil listened to this conversation with growing alarm, understanding that his friend was introducing dangerous ideas to someone whose moral development was still forming and whose beauty made him particularly vulnerable to theories that justified self-indulgence.

"Henry," Basil interrupted with obvious concern, "you're filling Dorian's head with nonsense about the proper use of beauty. He should be thinking about developing his character, not about exploiting his appearance."

📜 The Philosopher of Pleasure (Part 3)

But Henry's response revealed how completely he rejected conventional morality in favor of aesthetic and hedonistic principles that prioritized individual pleasure over social responsibility.

"Character is what people fall back on when they have exhausted their beauty," Henry replied with the wit that made his conversation so dangerously attractive. "But Dorian has both beauty and the intelligence to use it properly, if he can be educated to understand what such gifts really mean."

The education that Henry began providing that afternoon would prove to be more destructive than any formal corruption could have achieved, because it was presented as sophisticated philosophy rather than obvious temptation.

"You have the most wonderful youth," Henry continued, "and youth is the only thing worth having. When your youth goes, everything goes with it. Make the most of it while you can."

*Words had power to reshape a soul.*

📝 Grammar: Restrictive vs. Non-Restrictive Clauses and Commas

Restrictive clauses are essential to the meaning and need no commas: 'The girl who told the stories survived.' Non-restrictive clauses add extra information and use commas: 'Scheherazade, who had read everything, told stories.' The difference changes meaning: 'Students who studied passed' vs. 'Students, who studied, passed.'

Example:

Restrictive: 'The tales that changed the king came from many cultures.' Non-restrictive: 'Arabian Nights, which contains hundreds of tales, influenced world literature.'
