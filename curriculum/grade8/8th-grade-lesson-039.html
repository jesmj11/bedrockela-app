📜 The First Taste of Freedom (Part 1)

Edward Hyde's first conscious moments were a revelation of power and possibility that no human being had ever experienced in the history of civilization.

Hyde possessed all of Jekyll's intelligence, education, and social knowledge, but none of his moral constraints, ethical concerns, or civilized inhibitions. For the first time in Jekyll's forty-year life, he could contemplate any action—no matter how cruel, selfish, or destructive—without experiencing guilt, shame, or moral conflict.

"The sensation was intoxicating beyond anything I had ever imagined," Jekyll would later write in his confession. "Every desire I had ever suppressed, every impulse I had ever controlled, every selfish thought I had ever rejected was now available for immediate gratification without consequence or remorse."

Hyde's first expedition into London society was an exploration of what unlimited selfishness could accomplish when supported by Jekyll's considerable intelligence and financial resources.

He walked through familiar neighborhoods, observing people and situations with eyes unclouded by sympathy, compassion, or moral concern. Every person he encountered was evaluated purely in terms of how they could serve his immediate desires or provide entertainment through their vulnerability.

"Children were particularly fascinating," Hyde noted with scientific detachment, "because their innocence and trust made them perfect subjects for psychological experimentation. I could observe the precise effects of fear, confusion, and betrayal by systematically violating their expectations about adult behavior."

The incident with the young girl that had so disturbed Enfield was not accidental—it was Hyde's first deliberate test of his freedom from moral constraints.

"I wanted to understand what it felt like to cause unnecessary suffering to someone who had done me no harm," Hyde recalled. "The physical act of stepping on the child was less important than the psychological experience of choosing cruelty over kindness simply because I preferred the more interesting emotional reaction."

The child's tears, the bystanders' shock, and Enfield's moral outrage provided Hyde with data about human emotional responses that Jekyll had only understood theoretically.

📜 The First Taste of Freedom (Part 2)

"Normal people," Hyde observed, "are controlled by their emotional reactions to others' suffering. They avoid causing pain because witnessing pain makes them uncomfortable. But when that emotional constraint is removed chemically, causing pain becomes an interesting intellectual exercise rather than a morally problematic choice."

Hyde's exploration of London's criminal underworld provided additional opportunities for psychological experimentation.

He visited gambling houses where desperate people risked money they couldn't afford to lose. He observed brothels where economic necessity forced women to submit to degradation for survival. He frequented taverns where alcohol revealed the barely contained violence that civilized men kept hidden beneath social respectability.

"Every location provided evidence that most people were restrained from evil behavior only by social pressure and fear of consequences rather than genuine moral conviction," Hyde noted with satisfaction. "Remove those external constraints, and the difference between 'good' and 'evil' people becomes merely a matter of opportunity and circumstance."

But Hyde's most significant discovery was that causing pain and fear provided positive emotional satisfaction rather than the negative emotional consequences that normal people experienced when they harmed others.

"Traditional morality assumes that evil behavior creates guilt and remorse that make such behavior unpleasant for the perpetrator," Hyde reasoned. "But when guilt and remorse are eliminated chemically, evil behavior becomes genuinely enjoyable rather than simply tempting."

The gradual escalation from psychological cruelty to physical violence was inevitable once Hyde understood that causing suffering provided pleasure rather than discomfort.

"Why settle for making people uncomfortable when you can make them genuinely afraid?" Hyde wondered as he contemplated increasingly ambitious experiments in human emotional manipulation.

The walking stick that would eventually be used to murder Sir Danvers Carew was acquired during this period of escalating violence—not as a mobility aid, but as a weapon that could provide precise control over the amount of pain inflicted on experimental subjects.

📜 The First Taste of Freedom (Part 3)

"Physical violence," Hyde discovered, "creates more intense emotional reactions than psychological cruelty, and therefore provides more satisfying feedback about one's power over other people's experiences."

As Jekyll's chemical transformation began to wear off and his normal moral consciousness returned, he was horrified by the detailed memories of everything Hyde had experienced during their first separation.

"I had expected that removing moral constraints would create a sense of neutral freedom," Jekyll wrote. "I had not anticipated that eliminating conscience would make cruelty actively pleasurable rather than simply permissible."

But even as Jekyll experienced revulsion at Hyde's actions, he found himself fascinated by the psychological insights that the experiment had provided.

For the first time in his life, he understood exactly what it felt like to be completely free from moral obligation—and the knowledge was simultaneously terrifying and addictive.

*Evil, once unleashed, demanded ever greater satisfaction.*

🗣️ Language: Connotation vs. Denotation: Precision in Word Choice

Learn:

Denotation is the dictionary definition; connotation is the emotional association. 'Childish' (negative) vs. 'youthful' (positive) vs. 'childlike' (neutral/positive)—same general meaning, different feelings. Authors choose words carefully for their connotations.

Example:

"'The king was stubborn' (negative) vs. 'The king was determined' (positive) vs. 'The king was resolute' (strongly positive). Same action, different judgment implied by word choice."
