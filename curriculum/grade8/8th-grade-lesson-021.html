📜 Wedding Terror

The marriage between Victor Frankenstein and Elizabeth Lavenza took place under the shadow of his creature's specific threat, creating a wedding celebration that was simultaneously the fulfillment of lifelong love and preparation for mortal combat.

"I could not abandon Elizabeth," Victor told Captain Walton, "despite my certain knowledge that our wedding would trigger the revenge that my creature had been planning since I destroyed his potential companion. To delay our marriage would have been to allow his threats to control our lives indefinitely."

The preparations that Victor made for their wedding night revealed his understanding that his creature's promise to be "with them" was not mere intimidation but a specific plan for violence that would require active resistance.

"I armed myself," Victor said, "with pistols and daggers that I hoped might prove effective against a being whose superhuman strength exceeded normal human capability. I also made arrangements for guards and security measures that might prevent access to our wedding chamber."

But Victor's focus on protecting himself from direct attack led him to misinterpret his creature's strategy, which was designed to cause maximum psychological damage rather than simply to eliminate Victor quickly.

"I expected," Victor admitted, "that the creature would attempt to murder me on my wedding night as revenge for destroying his potential companion. I prepared for personal combat rather than considering that his true target might be Elizabeth herself."

The wedding ceremony proceeded with beauty and joy that reflected the genuine love between Victor and Elizabeth, but also with an undercurrent of tension that suggested the groom's awareness that their happiness was under immediate threat.

📜 Wedding Terror (Part 2)

"Elizabeth radiated," Victor remembered, "the same pure joy and moral beauty that had characterized her throughout our lives together. She represented everything that was good about human nature, everything that made life worth protecting from the destructive forces that my scientific irresponsibility had unleashed."

But the creature's strategy was to destroy Victor's capacity for happiness by eliminating the person whose love provided meaning and purpose to his existence.

"I had stationed myself," Victor explained, "in a position where I could defend against attack through the windows or doors of our chamber. But while I was focused on perimeter security, the creature had already gained access to the room where Elizabeth was preparing for our first night as husband and wife."

The scream that alerted Victor to his tactical error was brief and final, suggesting that the creature's attack on Elizabeth had been swift and efficient rather than prolonged torture.

"I rushed to her chamber," Victor said, "and found her lifeless on the bed, strangled with the same superhuman strength that had characterized Clerval's murder. But her face showed not pain or terror, but surprise—as if she had been killed before she could understand what was happening."

The creature's presence at the scene of Elizabeth's murder was brief but deliberate, ensuring that Victor would see him and understand that this violence was personal rather than random.

"He appeared at the window," Victor continued, "for just long enough to confirm his responsibility for Elizabeth's death and to demonstrate his satisfaction at having successfully completed his revenge. Then he vanished into the darkness with speed that no human pursuit could match."

📜 Wedding Terror (Part 3)

The psychological destruction that resulted from Elizabeth's murder exceeded anything Victor had experienced during the deaths of William, Justine, and Clerval because it eliminated his last connection to happiness and moral purpose.

"Elizabeth represented," Victor reflected, "my final link to the loving family and social connections that had shaped my moral development before scientific obsession corrupted my judgment. Her death completed my transformation from creator to victim, from scientist to hunter."

The realization that his creature had achieved perfect revenge forced Victor to understand how completely his choices about scientific responsibility had determined not just his own fate but the survival of everyone he loved.

*Victor's greatest joy became his greatest loss.*

📝 Grammar: Subjunctive Mood for Hypotheticals and Wishes

The subjunctive mood expresses wishes, suggestions, or hypothetical situations. It often uses 'were' instead of 'was': 'If I were the king...' Common in formal writing after verbs like wish, suggest, recommend: 'I wish she were here.' 'I suggest that he study harder' (not 'studies').

Example:

Subjunctive: 'If Scheherazade were to fail, she would die.' 'The king wished that his wife were faithful.' Indicative (not subjunctive): 'Scheherazade was clever.'
