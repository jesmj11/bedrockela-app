📜 THE GIANT SQUID (Part 1)

The Nautilus entered warmer waters and continued north through the Atlantic. For a few days, life aboard settled into something almost like normalcy. I worked on my notes. Conseil organized specimens. Ned fished through the kitchen porthole and complained about captivity with renewed energy, which I took as a sign of good health. Then came the squid. Conseil spotted them first—dark shapes in the water, keeping pace with the ship. Through the salon window, we watched them approach: enormous creatures, each one forty to fifty feet long, with eight writhing arms and two longer tentacles, each lined with hundreds of suckers the size of dinner plates. Their eyes were huge and black and horribly intelligent. There were seven of them. "Giant squid," I breathed. "The kraken of legend." The Nautilus tried to outrun them, but one of the creatures wrapped its tentacles around the propeller and jammed it. The ship shuddered and stopped. More squid latched onto the hull, their arms snaking across the metal plating, their beaks scraping against the rivets. "We cannot use the electric charge," Nemo said grimly. "Their arms are too close to the hatches. We fight them hand to hand." He opened the hatches. The crew surged out with axes, harpoons, and knives. The squid attacked immediately—tentacles as thick as tree trunks whipped through the air, grabbing at men, coiling around limbs, dragging sailors toward gaping beaks. Ned was in the thick of it, his harpoon flashing. He drove it into the body of one squid, twisted, and yanked it free in a spray of black ink. Nemo fought beside him, hacking at a tentacle that had wrapped around a crewman's waist. Then a scream—a crewman was lifted into the air by a massive arm, his legs kicking helplessly. The squid pulled him toward its beak. Nemo and three men threw themselves at the tentacle, chopping desperately—but the creature dragged the man under the water. He was gone. The fight lasted fifteen minutes. It felt like hours. When it was over, the deck was slick with ink and blood. Six of the seven squid were dead or driven off. One crewman was lost. Nemo stood at the railing, staring at the water where his man had disappeared. Tears ran down his face—the first and only time I ever saw Captain Nemo cry.

📝 Grammar: Noun Clauses and Their Functions

Learn:

A noun clause acts as a noun in a sentence, often starting with that, what, who, whoever, whether, if. They can be subjects, objects, or complements: 'What she said convinced him' (subject). 'He knew that she was clever' (object). 'The question is whether he will change' (complement).

Example:

"Subject: 'Whoever tells the best story wins.' Object: 'The king realized that he had been wrong.' Complement: 'The problem was that he couldn't trust anyone.'"

📰 Antarctica: The Frozen Continent

Antarctica is Earth's southernmost continent, buried under ice up to 3 miles thick. It's the coldest, driest, windiest place on the planet. The lowest temperature ever recorded was -128.6°F at Antarctica's Vostok Station.

Despite the harsh conditions, Antarctica teems with life at its edges. Penguins, seals, and seabirds nest on the coast and islands. The Southern Ocean surrounding Antarctica is one of the world's richest marine ecosystems, home to krill, whales, and colossal squids.

Humans didn't set foot on Antarctica until 1821, and no one reached the South Pole until 1911, when Norwegian explorer Roald Amundsen won a deadly race against Britain's Robert Scott (who died on the return journey). The continent wasn't fully mapped until the 1950s.

Today, Antarctica is governed by the Antarctic Treaty (1959), which bans military activity and mining, dedicating the continent to scientific research. Climate change is now melting Antarctic ice at alarming rates. If the West Antarctic Ice Sheet collapses, sea levels could rise by 10 feet, flooding coastal cities worldwide.

When the Nautilus travels beneath the Antarctic ice, Verne imagined a frontier that was truly unexplored—it still largely is.
