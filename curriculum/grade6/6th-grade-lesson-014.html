📜 MAKING UP WITH BECKY (Part 1)

Something about Aunt Polly's kiss that morning lifted all the weight off Tom's shoulders. He felt light and happy for the first time in weeks. On his way to school, he ran into Becky Thatcher. "Becky, I acted awful mean the other day, and I'm so sorry. I won't ever do it again. Please—can we make up?" She stopped and gave him a cold stare. "I'll thank you to keep away from me, Tom Sawyer. I'm never speaking to you again." She tossed her braids and walked on. Tom was so stunned he couldn't even think of a comeback. His good mood evaporated. He stomped into the schoolyard, furious. For the rest of the morning, Tom noticed Becky laughing and running around the yard, always managing to end up close to him, clearly trying to get his attention. He pretended not to notice. When he saw her looking his way, he turned and talked loudly to Amy Lawrence instead. Becky's smile faded. She tried to look cheerful, but the light went out of her face. She drifted away. Meanwhile, something dangerous was happening. The teacher, Mr. Dobbins, kept a mysterious book in his desk—locked up, always hidden. Every kid in school was dying to know what it was. On this particular day, as Becky passed the teacher's desk, she noticed the key was in the lock. She glanced around. The room was empty. She turned the key and opened the book. The title page said Professor Somebody's Anatomy. She didn't know what that meant, so she started flipping through it—and came upon a picture of a human body, completely naked. At that exact moment, Tom walked through the door. Becky slammed the book shut in a panic—and tore the picture right down the middle. She shoved the book back in the desk, turned the key, and burst into tears. "Tom Sawyer! You are the meanest person alive, sneaking up on people!" "How was I supposed to know you were looking at anything?" "You're going to tell on me, and I'll get whipped, and I've never been whipped in school!" She stormed out, crying. Tom stood there, bewildered. "What a fuss over nothing," he thought. "Girls are so thin-skinned. I'm not going to tell old Dobbins. But he'll figure it out on his own. He'll ask every kid in the room, and when he gets to Becky, she won't be able to hide it—girls can never keep a straight face. She's going to get it. Too bad for her." He paused. "But she would have let ME get in trouble without thinking twice. All right, let her sweat." But he didn't feel right about it. That afternoon, Mr. Dobbins took the book from his desk. He settled into his chair to read it—and found the torn page. Every student sat up straight. Tom glanced at Becky. She looked like a trapped rabbit. Mr. Dobbins stood and faced the room. "Who tore this book?" Dead silence. You could hear a pin drop. "Benjamin Rogers, did you tear this book?" "No sir." "Joseph Harper, did you?" "No sir." One by one, the teacher worked his way through the class. Tom's heart pounded harder with each name. The teacher turned to the girls. "Amy Lawrence?" She shook her head. "Susan Harper?" No. "Rebecca Thatcher." Becky's face went white. Tom looked at her. Her eyes were wide with terror, her hands pressed together. "Did you tear this book?" Her lips moved, but no sound came out. Tom leaped to his feet. "I did it!" The whole school stared in shock. Mr. Dobbins said nothing for a long, terrible moment. Then: "Come here." Tom walked forward. He took the worst whipping Mr. Dobbins had ever given, plus two hours of detention after school. He didn't make a sound. And the whole time, he could feel Becky Thatcher's eyes on him—shining with gratitude and something that looked an awful lot like love. Tom went to bed that night still stinging from the whipping, but Becky's last words kept playing in his head like music: "Tom, how could you be so noble!"

🗣️ Language: Idioms, Colloquialisms, and Cultural Expressions

Learn:

Idioms are expressions whose meanings aren't literal: 'Break the ice' doesn't mean breaking actual ice. Understanding cultural expressions helps comprehension and shows how language reflects culture. Some expressions translate across cultures; others don't.

Example:

"'The king lost his head' (lost control, not literally lost his head). 'She kept him in the dark' (kept him uninformed). Cultural expression: 'A thousand and one nights' suggests something vast, uncountable—the title itself is an idiom."
