📜 THE FALL OF TROY

For ten long years, the Greeks had fought to capture the city of Troy. The war had begun because of a woman — Helen, the most beautiful woman in the world, wife of the Greek king Menelaus. She had been taken to Troy by the Trojan prince Paris, and every Greek king and warrior had sailed across the sea to bring her back. Achilles, the greatest fighter alive. Ajax, the mountain of a man. And Odysseus, king of Ithaca — not the strongest, not the fastest, but the cleverest of them all. It was Odysseus who ended the war. After ten years of siege, when brute force had failed, Odysseus devised a plan. He ordered the Greeks to build an enormous wooden horse — large enough to hold thirty warriors in its hollow belly. The Greeks left the horse at the gates of Troy and pretended to sail away. The Trojans, believing the war was over, dragged the horse inside their walls as a trophy. That night, while Troy slept, Odysseus and his men climbed out of the horse, opened the gates, and let the Greek army pour in. Troy burned. When the smoke cleared, Odysseus stood on the ruined walls and looked west, toward Ithaca — his small, rocky island kingdom. His wife, Penelope, was waiting there. His son, Telemachus, whom he had left as an infant, was now ten years old. "We go home," Odysseus told his men. He had twelve ships and over six hundred men. None of them knew that the journey home would take another ten years. None of them knew that most of them would never see home at all.

📝 Grammar: Active and Passive Voice: Strategic Use

Active voice emphasizes the doer: 'Scheherazade told stories.' Passive voice emphasizes the action or receiver: 'Stories were told by Scheherazade.' Passive voice is useful when the doer is unknown, unimportant, or when you want to shift focus. Overusing passive voice weakens writing.

Example:

Active: 'The king executed the women.' Passive: 'The women were executed' (focuses on victims, not perpetrator). Sometimes passive voice is more appropriate for the meaning you want to convey.
