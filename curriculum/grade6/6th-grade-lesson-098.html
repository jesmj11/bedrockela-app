📜 THE GARDEN (Part 1)

Food was our most critical concern. The island provided wild fruit, fish, and game, but we needed crops — reliable, renewable food sources that would sustain us not for weeks but for years. Among the supplies salvaged from the wreck were seeds: wheat, corn, barley, lettuce, turnips, and peas. Elizabeth also found seed potatoes, which she had wrapped carefully in canvas during the shipwreck and guarded like treasure through everything that followed. "Potatoes will keep us alive when nothing else will," she said. She was not exaggerating. In Switzerland, potatoes were the foundation of every farm. We cleared a plot of land near the river, where the soil was rich and dark. The boys dug until their hands blistered. I built a crude plow from ship timbers and hitched it to the donkey, who cooperated only after prolonged negotiations involving carrots. Fritz planted the wheat and corn in neat rows. Ernest, who had been reading a book on tropical agriculture that he'd found in the ship's library, advised on spacing and irrigation. Jack dug an irrigation channel from the river to the garden with more energy than precision — it flooded twice before Ernest redesigned it. Franz's job was chasing away the birds. He was surprisingly good at it, armed with a homemade scarecrow and a loud voice. We also planted sugarcane cuttings along the riverbank and transplanted cotton bushes closer to the camp. Elizabeth started an herb garden with wild plants she had identified: something that tasted like thyme, a mint-like plant that grew by the stream, and a spicy root that made an excellent tea. The first green shoots appeared two weeks later. I stood in the garden at dawn, watching the tiny plants push through the earth, and I felt something I hadn't felt since the shipwreck: hope. Not the desperate, moment-to-moment hope of survival, but the deep, steady hope of a future. We were planting for next year. That meant we believed there would be a next year.

📝 Grammar: Parallel Structure in Lists and Series

Learn:

Parallel structure uses the same grammatical form for items in a series, creating rhythm and clarity. All items should be the same part of speech or phrase type: 'She was intelligent, determined, and resourceful' (all adjectives). Not parallel: 'She was intelligent, determined, and had resourcefulness.'

Example:

"Parallel: 'The king lost his faith, abandoned his principles, and destroyed his compassion.' Not parallel: 'The king lost his faith, his principles were abandoned, and destroying his compassion.'"

📰 Tropical Plants and Their Uses

Tropical plants provided everything pre-industrial people needed. Coconut palms give food, drink, oil, rope (from husk fibers), and building material (from fronds and trunk). Bamboo grows fast and serves as construction material, water pipes, utensils, and even musical instruments. Banana plants provide fruit, and their leaves serve as plates, umbrellas, and roof material. Certain vines yield fresh water when cut. However, many tropical plants are poisonous—eating unknown plants is extremely dangerous. The Robinson family's European botanical knowledge helped them identify safe plants, but they likely made mistakes too. Indigenous knowledge, developed over centuries, far surpassed European understanding.
