📜 THE RAFT

My uncle wanted to cross the ocean. Of course he did. "Saknussemm crossed it," he said. "The tunnel continues on the far shore. We must cross." Hans built the raft. Using the tough, flexible stalks of the giant mushrooms and rope from our supplies, he constructed a platform roughly twelve feet square, with a mast made from a mushroom stalk and a sail made from our canvas tarpaulin. It was crude but effective — Hans tested it in the shallows, and it floated well. We loaded our supplies and pushed off from the shore. The underground sea stretched before us, gray and featureless, under the glowing ceiling of rock. There was no wind at first — we used makeshift paddles, and the raft moved painfully slowly. Then a breeze picked up. Hans raised the sail, and we began to move. For the first time since entering the earth, I felt something like peace. The gentle rocking of the raft, the sound of water against the logs, the vast open space above and around us — it was almost like being on a real ocean, under a real sky. If I squinted at the luminous ceiling and ignored the fact that it was rock instead of cloud, I could almost imagine we were back on the surface. My uncle spent every moment fishing — not for food, but for science. He dropped a line with a hook into the water and pulled up creatures that made his hands shake with excitement. Fish — real fish — but species that had been extinct for hundreds of millions of years. Fish with armored heads and bony plates instead of scales. Fish with eyes that had adapted to the dim light, growing enormous and sensitive. "These are living fossils," he breathed, holding up a specimen that looked like something from a nightmare. "Species that went extinct on the surface during the Devonian period. Three hundred and fifty million years ago. And here they are — alive." The water was deep. When Hans dropped a weighted line, it descended for hundreds of feet without touching bottom. Something was down there, in the darkness beneath us. I could feel it.

📝 Grammar: Parallel Structure in Lists and Series

Parallel structure uses the same grammatical form for items in a series, creating rhythm and clarity. All items should be the same part of speech or phrase type: 'She was intelligent, determined, and resourceful' (all adjectives). Not parallel: 'She was intelligent, determined, and had resourcefulness.'

Example:

Parallel: 'The king lost his faith, abandoned his principles, and destroyed his compassion.' Not parallel: 'The king lost his faith, his principles were abandoned, and destroying his compassion.'
