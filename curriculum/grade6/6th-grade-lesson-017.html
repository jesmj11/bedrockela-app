📜 A MISERABLE SUMMER (Part 1)

Tom joined the Cadets of Temperance, attracted by their fancy uniforms. He promised to stop swearing and smoking. Naturally, the moment he promised not to do these things, they were all he could think about. He quit after two days. The Fourth of July was a disappointment—it rained, and a visiting senator turned out to be a normal-sized man, which was deeply unsatisfying. A circus came to town. The boys played circus in the backyard for three days and then got bored. Becky Thatcher left for the summer to visit relatives in another town. Without her, nothing had any flavor. And underneath it all, like a toothache that never stopped, was the murder. The secret pressed on Tom's conscience every single day. Then came the measles. Tom was bedridden for two weeks, completely cut off from the world. When he finally dragged himself outside, everything had changed. A religious revival had swept through St. Petersburg. Everyone had "gotten religion"—even the boys. Tom went looking for a friendly face and found Joe Harper reading the Bible. Ben Rogers was delivering charity baskets. Jim Hollis told Tom his measles were God's warning to shape up. Even Huckleberry Finn greeted him with a Bible verse. Tom's heart sank. He went home and crawled into bed, convinced he was the only lost soul left in town. That night, a terrible thunderstorm struck. Lightning split the sky, thunder shook the house, and rain lashed the windows. Tom pulled the covers over his head, certain that all of this was aimed directly at him—that God had finally had enough of Tom Sawyer. But the storm passed, and he was still alive. Then the measles came back. He spent three more weeks in bed. When he finally recovered, the revival was over. Joe Harper was back to mischief. Ben Rogers had gone back to normal. Huck was eating a stolen melon behind a fence. Everything was just as it had been—except that the murder trial was about to begin.

🗣️ Language: Etymology: Word Histories and Evolution

Learn:

Words have histories—they come from other languages, change meanings over time, and carry their origins with them. 'Sinbad' comes from Arabic. 'Scheherazade' is Persian. 'Genie' comes from Arabic 'jinn.' Knowing etymology deepens understanding and makes words memorable.

Example:

"'Assassin' comes from Arabic 'hashashiyyin' (users of hashish), from a medieval group. 'Admiral' from Arabic 'amir al-' (commander of). Many English words have Arabic origins through trade and cultural exchange."
