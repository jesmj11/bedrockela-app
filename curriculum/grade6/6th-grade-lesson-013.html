📜 EXAMINATION DAY (Part 1)

Vacation was coming, and Mr. Dobbins was getting meaner by the day. He seemed to take a vicious pleasure in whipping students for the smallest mistakes. The younger kids spent their days in terror and their nights plotting revenge. They finally came up with a plan—and it was a beauty. The sign-painter's boy was in on it. His father rented a room to Mr. Dobbins, so the boy had his own reasons for wanting revenge. The plan required the teacher's wife to be out of town, and it required Mr. Dobbins to be drunk—neither of which was unusual. On Examination Evening, the schoolhouse was packed. Parents, town leaders, and visitors filled every seat. The teacher sat on a raised platform with a blackboard behind him, looking only slightly unsteady. Rows of students sat on a temporary stage—boys scrubbed raw and miserable in stiff clothes, girls in white dresses with ribbons in their hair. The performances began. A tiny boy recited a poem with robotic gestures and nearly fainted from stage fright. A girl lisped her way through "Mary Had a Little Lamb." Tom Sawyer stepped forward with grand confidence and launched into a dramatic speech—"Give me liberty or give me death!"—but froze halfway through. Stage fright hit him like a wall. His legs shook, his throat closed. The audience stared in painful silence. He gave up and slunk off the stage. More recitations followed. Then came the spelling bee, the Latin class, and finally the main event—original essays by the older girls. Every single essay, no matter the topic, ended with the same kind of preachy moral lesson. The audience clapped politely and pretended to be moved. Then Mr. Dobbins stood to lead the geography class. He turned to the blackboard and began drawing a map of America. But his hand was unsteady from drink, and the map came out crooked and wrong. A ripple of laughter ran through the crowd. He erased and tried again—worse. The laughter grew louder. He concentrated harder, determined to get it right. He didn't notice what was happening above him. In the ceiling, there was a small opening—a trapdoor to the attic. Through it, slowly, came a cat. She was tied to a rope and lowered inch by inch, a rag around her head to keep her quiet. She dangled and twisted, clawing at the air, getting closer and closer to the teacher's head. The audience was shaking with laughter now. The cat was six inches from Mr. Dobbins—four inches—two— She grabbed his wig with both paws and was yanked back up into the attic, taking the wig with her. The teacher's bald head gleamed in the lamplight. The sign-painter's boy had gilded it while the teacher napped. It shone like a mirror. The room exploded. The meeting was over. The boys were avenged. And vacation had begun.

📝 Grammar: Adjective and Adverb Clauses

What is it?

An adjective clause modifies a noun: 'Tom, who was very clever, tricked his friends.' An adverb clause modifies a verb, adjective, or adverb: 'Tom worked quickly because he wanted to finish.' Both add detail to sentences.

Example:

"Adjective: 'The fence that Tom painted stood in the yard.' / Adverb: 'Tom ran when he saw Aunt Polly.'"
