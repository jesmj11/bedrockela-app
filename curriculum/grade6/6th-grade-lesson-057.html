📜 THE POOR KNIGHT

Not all of Robin's encounters involved robbery. Some involved justice of a different kind. Sir Richard of the Lea was a knight — an honest man who had fallen on hard times. His son had accidentally killed a man in a tournament, and Sir Richard had borrowed four hundred pounds from the Abbot of St. Mary's to pay the legal fees. The Abbot had charged ruinous interest, and now the debt had grown to a sum Sir Richard could never repay. If he didn't pay by Michaelmas — just three weeks away — the Abbot would seize his lands, his home, and everything he owned. Sir Richard was riding through Sherwood Forest, head bowed, when Robin's men stopped him. They brought him to the camp, and Robin — following his custom of feeding every traveler before deciding whether to rob them — offered the knight dinner. Over roast venison, Sir Richard told his story. Robin listened carefully. "How much do you owe?" Robin asked. "Four hundred pounds. I have seventeen." Robin looked at Little John. Little John looked at Marian. Marian nodded. "We'll lend you the money," Robin said. Sir Richard stared. "You'll lend me four hundred pounds?" "We took it from people who stole it first. At least this way it goes to someone who deserves it. Pay us back when you can. No interest." Robin's men counted out four hundred pounds in silver and packed it in the knight's saddlebags. They gave him a new horse, a new cloak, and Little John as an escort to make sure he arrived safely. Sir Richard rode to St. Mary's Abbey and paid the Abbot in full. The Abbot, who had been counting on seizing the knight's lands, was furious. Sir Richard kept his home. And one year later, he rode back into Sherwood Forest and repaid Robin every penny — along with a gift of a hundred longbows and a hundred sheaves of arrows, the finest money could buy. "You saved my family," Sir Richard said. "That's what the greenwood is for," Robin replied.

📝 Language: Inference and Implication: Reading Between the Lines

Authors don't always state things directly—they imply them through word choice, actions, and context. Readers must infer (conclude based on evidence). Strong readers notice what's suggested but not said: a character's motives, unstated conflicts, thematic messages.

Example:

Text: 'The king listened without speaking.' Inference: He's processing something deeply, perhaps beginning to change. The author doesn't say 'he was changing'—the reader infers it from the behavior. Text: 'Scheherazade chose her next tale carefully.' Inference: She's strategic, not just entertaining.
