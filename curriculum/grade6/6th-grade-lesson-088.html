📜 THE LAST ARROW (Part 1)

Little John carried Robin to the window. The room overlooked a green meadow that sloped down to the edge of Sherwood Forest. The ancient oaks stood dark against the evening sky, their branches reaching upward like arms. Robin's great longbow was placed in his hands. He could barely hold it. The strength that had drawn that bow ten thousand times — that had won tournaments, defended the poor, and defied the most powerful men in England — was nearly gone. "Open the window," Robin said. Little John opened it. Cool forest air rushed in, carrying the scent of earth and leaves and rain. Robin breathed it in — the smell of home. "Wherever this arrow falls," Robin said, his voice thin but steady, "bury me there." He nocked the arrow. His hands trembled. Little John steadied his arm. Robin drew the bow — not with the full power of his youth, but with everything he had left. He aimed high, toward the green line of the forest, and released. The arrow flew. It arced across the twilight sky, a dark line against the fading light, and sailed over the meadow in a long, graceful curve. It cleared the stone wall of the priory, crossed the open ground, and disappeared into the trees at the forest's edge. Robin watched it fly. A faint smile crossed his face. "There," he whispered. "Bury me there. Under the greenwood tree." He closed his eyes. Little John held him. The great outlaw of Sherwood Forest — the archer, the trickster, the champion of the poor — was gone.

📝 Grammar: Rhetorical Devices: Parallelism, Antithesis, Anaphora

Learn:

Rhetorical devices create persuasive or memorable writing. Parallelism repeats structure: 'I came, I saw, I conquered.' Antithesis contrasts ideas: 'Not that I loved Caesar less, but that I loved Rome more.' Anaphora repeats words at the start: 'We shall fight on the beaches, we shall fight on the landing grounds...'

Example:

"Parallelism: 'She was not just intelligent but also brave and resourceful.' Antithesis: 'She offered stories, not arguments; patience, not force.' Anaphora: 'Night after night, she told tales. Night after night, he listened.'"

📰 Medieval Death and Burial

In medieval England, death rituals were deeply important. Bodies were prepared by family, buried quickly (usually within a day), and laid to rest in churchyards or significant locations. Outlaws couldn't be buried in consecrated ground—their deaths were unmarked, their graves forgotten.

Robin's burial at the edge of the forest, marked by his last arrow's flight, creates a legend instead of a forgotten grave. The choice of burial place matters: not in a church controlled by corrupt clergy, not in a town that rejected him, but at the boundary between civilization and wildness—where he always belonged.
