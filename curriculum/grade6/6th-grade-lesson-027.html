📜 ESCAPE (Part 1)

Tom followed the passage for what felt like hours. He played out the kite string as far as it would reach, then kept going on his hands and knees. He tried one tunnel, then another, then a third. At the very end of the third passage, he was about to turn back when he saw something—a tiny speck of light. Faint, far away, but unmistakable. He dropped the string and crawled toward it. The speck grew larger. Brighter. He pushed his head and shoulders through a narrow hole in the rock— And there was the Mississippi River, rolling broad and blue in the sunshine. He could have cried. He nearly did. He squeezed through the hole, ran back into the cave, and found Becky. She didn't believe him at first—she'd given up. But he pulled her to her feet, half-carried her through the passages, and brought her to the opening. When she saw daylight, she nearly collapsed. They crawled out onto a bluff overlooking the river. It was late afternoon. They were filthy, starving, and five miles downstream from the cave entrance. Some men in a rowboat came along, and Tom hailed them. The men didn't believe their story at first—"You're five miles below the cave," they said—but they took the children aboard, fed them, and brought them home. The town went wild. Bells rang. People poured into the streets. The news spread like fire. A procession of villagers filed through Judge Thatcher's house, weeping and laughing and hugging the children. Aunt Polly alternated between squeezing Tom and thanking God. Tom and Becky spent the rest of the week in bed, recovering. Three days and nights in the cave had nearly killed them. About two weeks later, Tom went to visit Huck, who had been sick with fever ever since the night he'd saved the Widow Douglas. Tom had plenty to tell him, but first he stopped at Judge Thatcher's house to see Becky. The Judge mentioned casually that no one would ever get lost in McDougal's Cave again. "Why not?" Tom asked. "Because I had the big door sealed with iron plating two weeks ago, and triple-locked. I've got the keys right here." Tom went white as paper. "What's the matter, boy? Someone get him some water!" "Judge—Injun Joe is in the cave!"

🗣️ Language: Inference and Implication: Reading Between the Lines

Learn:

Authors don't always state things directly—they imply them through word choice, actions, and context. Readers must infer (conclude based on evidence). Strong readers notice what's suggested but not said: a character's motives, unstated conflicts, thematic messages.

Example:

"Text: 'The king listened without speaking.' Inference: He's processing something deeply, perhaps beginning to change. The author doesn't say 'he was changing'—the reader infers it from the behavior. Text: 'Scheherazade chose her next tale carefully.' Inference: She's strategic, not just entertaining."
