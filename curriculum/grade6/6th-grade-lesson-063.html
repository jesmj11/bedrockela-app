📜 FIRST NIGHT

That first night on the beach was one of the longest of my life. We made camp between two large rocks, using sailcloth from the wreck to create a rough tent. Elizabeth built a fire — she was better at it than I was, though she was kind enough not to say so. We heated the soup she had made on the ship and ate it from tin cups, sitting on the sand, listening to the waves and the strange sounds of the forest behind us. The sounds were unsettling. Clicks, rustles, and cries we couldn't identify. Something large moved through the undergrowth beyond the firelight. Turk and Juno growled and pressed close to the boys. "What's out there?" Franz whispered. "We'll find out tomorrow," I said. "Tonight, we rest." I divided the night into watches. Fritz took the first, then me, then Ernest. Jack volunteered but fell asleep in four minutes, which was a new personal record for him. Elizabeth didn't sleep at all. I found her sitting by the fire in the small hours, staring at the dark ocean, and I knew what she was thinking — the same thing I was thinking. Were we the only survivors? Would anyone come looking for us? Would we ever see our home again? "The boys are safe," she said quietly. "That's what matters right now." She was right. She was usually right. Morning brought clarity and courage. The sun rose over the island in a blaze of gold, and in the daylight, our situation looked considerably less dire. The beach was broad and clean. A freshwater stream tumbled out of the forest and crossed the sand to the sea. Coconut palms lined the shore, their fruit hanging in heavy clusters. Beyond the palms, the forest was thick with trees I didn't recognize — enormous, ancient trees with trunks as wide as houses. "This island can sustain us," I told the family at breakfast. "It has water, food, shelter materials, and — based on the animal tracks I see — plenty of game. Our job now is to explore, to build, and to survive." Fritz wanted to explore immediately. Ernest wanted to make a complete inventory of our supplies. Jack wanted to climb the nearest tree. Franz wanted breakfast. We compromised. We would explore first, then inventory, then build. And everyone would get breakfast.

📝 Grammar: Restrictive vs. Non-Restrictive Clauses and Commas

Restrictive clauses are essential to the meaning and need no commas: 'The girl who told the stories survived.' Non-restrictive clauses add extra information and use commas: 'Scheherazade, who had read everything, told stories.' The difference changes meaning: 'Students who studied passed' vs. 'Students, who studied, passed.'

Example:

Restrictive: 'The tales that changed the king came from many cultures.' Non-restrictive: 'Arabian Nights, which contains hundreds of tales, influenced world literature.'
