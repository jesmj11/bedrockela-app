📜 THE PICNIC (Part 1)

On Friday morning, Tom got the best possible news: Becky Thatcher's family had returned to town. Even better, Becky's mother had agreed to let her have the long-promised picnic the very next day. Tom was ecstatic. By Saturday morning, a rowdy, laughing crowd of children gathered at Judge Thatcher's house and paraded through town to the ferry landing. The old steamboat carried them three miles downriver to a wooded hollow at the base of a steep, rocky hill. The kids scattered into the woods—chasing each other, eating from picnic baskets, playing in the creek. By late afternoon, someone shouted: "Who's ready for the cave?" Everyone was. They grabbed candles and scrambled up the hillside to McDougal's Cave. The entrance was shaped like the letter A—a great dark mouth in the rock. Inside, the air was cold and damp. The main passage was narrow, with high limestone walls that rose sixty feet overhead. Every few yards, smaller corridors branched off in every direction. The cave was a labyrinth—miles and miles of twisting, intersecting tunnels that led nowhere and everywhere. People said you could wander for days and never find the end. Most of the kids knew the first section of the cave and didn't venture beyond it. Groups chased each other through the dim passages, screaming and laughing, their candles throwing wild shadows on the walls. Tom and Becky ran with the rest, ducking through narrow openings, hiding in crevices, trying to scare each other. Eventually they wandered away from the main group. They found themselves in a part of the cave they'd never seen before—no names scratched in the walls, no candle-smoke graffiti. Just raw, untouched stone. Tom was thrilled. He wanted to explore. They followed the passage deeper and deeper, stopping to admire a cavern full of glittering stalactites, a crystal-clear underground spring, a vast room with pillars of stone where the ceiling dripped with thousands of sleeping bats. The bats were a problem. When the candlelight disturbed them, they came shrieking down in swarms. Tom grabbed Becky's hand and they ran, ducking into side passages to escape. When the bats were finally gone, they stopped to catch their breath. "Tom," Becky said, "I haven't heard the others in a long time." "Come to think of it—neither have I. We're pretty far down from them." "How long have we been walking?" "I don't know. We should probably start heading back." "Can you find the way?" "Sure. I think so." But after ten minutes of walking, nothing looked familiar. After twenty minutes, Tom wasn't talking as cheerfully. After thirty minutes, Becky noticed. "Tom, you didn't make any marks, did you?" His silence was answer enough. "We're lost," Becky whispered. "No—I'll figure it out. Come on." But he couldn't figure it out. The passages all looked the same. He tried one direction, then another, then another. Nothing. Becky started to cry. "We're lost, Tom! We're never going to get out!" Tom felt cold terror creeping up his spine. But he put his arm around her and said: "We'll find the way. Don't give up." They kept walking.

📝 Grammar: Noun Clauses and Their Functions

Learn:

A noun clause acts as a noun in a sentence, often starting with that, what, who, whoever, whether, if. They can be subjects, objects, or complements: 'What she said convinced him' (subject). 'He knew that she was clever' (object). 'The question is whether he will change' (complement).

Example:

"Subject: 'Whoever tells the best story wins.' Object: 'The king realized that he had been wrong.' Complement: 'The problem was that he couldn't trust anyone.'"
