📜 INJUN JOE'S FATE (Part 1)

Within minutes, the news was everywhere. A dozen boats launched from the riverbank. The ferryboat followed, packed with people. Tom Sawyer rode in the lead boat with Judge Thatcher. When they unlocked the heavy iron door of the cave, they found Injun Joe lying on the ground just inside. He was dead. His face was pressed against the crack at the bottom of the door, as if his eyes had been fixed on the light of the outside world until the very last moment. His bowie knife lay beside him, its blade broken in half. He'd spent his final days hacking at the great oak beam of the door—useless work, since solid rock formed the threshold beneath it. Even if he'd cut through the wood, he could never have squeezed under. He had eaten every candle stub left by tourists. He had caught and eaten bats. Near the door, a stalagmite rose from the floor, and on top of it he had placed a hollow stone to catch water that dripped from above—one drop every three minutes. A single spoonful every twenty-four hours. Tom stared at the cup and the slow-falling drop, and he understood exactly how the man had suffered—because he had nearly suffered the same way. Injun Joe was buried near the mouth of the cave. People came from miles around for the funeral. Some said they felt sorry for him. Tom did not feel sorry for him. He felt an overwhelming, bone-deep sense of relief. The weight he'd been carrying since that night in the graveyard was finally gone. The morning after the funeral, Tom pulled Huck to a private spot. "Huck, that money was never in Room Number Two at the tavern." "What?" "The money is in the cave." Huck's eyes went wide. "Say that again." "The treasure—the box of gold—it's in the cave. I know exactly where." "Tom, are you serious?" "Dead serious. Will you come with me to get it?" "I will if we can find it without getting lost." "I can take us right to it." They borrowed a small rowboat and headed downriver. Tom found the opening he'd escaped through—hidden behind a thick clump of bushes on the bluff. They crawled in with candles, kite string, and a couple of cloth bags. Tom led the way through the dark passages until they reached the spot where he'd seen Injun Joe's candle. On a big rock nearby, someone had traced a cross in candle smoke. "There it is, Huck. 'Number Two—under the cross.' This is where Joe kept his camp." They searched around the base of the rock and found a blanket, some gnawed bones, and a narrow opening hidden behind a stone. Tom squeezed through and held up his candle. The treasure box sat in a small natural vault, along with an empty powder keg, a couple of old guns, and some moccasins. Huck plunged his hands into the tarnished gold coins. "We're rich, Tom! We're actually rich!" The box weighed about fifty pounds. They stuffed the coins into their bags, dragged everything back through the cave, loaded it into the boat, and rowed home. They hid the bags in the Widow Douglas's woodshed and set off toward town—but on the way, they ran into the old Welshman, who grabbed them and marched them straight to the Widow Douglas's front door. "The widow's throwing a party," he said. "You two are coming." "But we're filthy—" "There are clean clothes inside. Get dressed."

📝 Grammar: Appositives

What is it?

An appositive is a noun or noun phrase that renames another noun right beside it. 'Tom, a clever boy, tricked his friends.' Appositives add detail without extra sentences. They're usually set off with commas.

Example:

"Tom's friend, Ben Rogers, fell for the trick."
