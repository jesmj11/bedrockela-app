📜 THE JOURNEY BEGINS

We traveled by train to Copenhagen, by ship to Reykjavik, and overland by horse across the most alien landscape I had ever seen. Iceland was extraordinary — and deeply unsettling. The land was bare rock, twisted lava fields, and steaming geothermal vents. Hot springs bubbled from the ground. Geysers erupted without warning, sending columns of boiling water fifty feet into the air. The sky was enormous, pale, and endless. The people were kind. In Reykjavik, we were welcomed by a Danish professor who provided supplies and recommended a guide for the journey to Sneffels. His name was Hans Bjelke. Hans was an Icelandic eiderdown hunter — a man who made his living climbing cliffs to collect feathers from eider duck nests. He was about thirty years old, lean and weathered, with a calm, expressionless face and hands like leather. He spoke almost no German and almost no French. His total daily conversation consisted of about six words, most of them "yes" or "no." He was the most competent human being I had ever met. Hans agreed to guide us to Sneffels and into the crater for a fixed weekly wage. He showed no curiosity about why two German professors wanted to climb into a volcano. He packed his bag, shouldered his tools, and was ready to leave in ten minutes. The three of us made an odd group: my uncle, burning with scientific obsession, talking constantly, gesturing wildly; Hans, silent and imperturbable, moving across the lava fields with the steady pace of a machine; and me, terrified, reluctant, and increasingly convinced that I was going to die underground. After four days of hard travel across Iceland's interior, we saw it. Sneffels Yökull rose from the horizon like a white crown — a massive volcano, its summit covered in glacial ice, its flanks scarred with ancient lava flows. It was beautiful and menacing, and my uncle looked at it the way a lover looks at a beloved's face. "There it is," he whispered. "The gateway."

📝 Grammar: Misplaced and Dangling Modifiers

Modifiers should be placed next to what they modify. Misplaced: 'She told a story to the king with great skill' (the king doesn't have the skill—she does). Dangling: 'Walking through the palace, the stories came to mind' (stories can't walk). Correct: 'Walking through the palace, she thought of stories.'

Example:

Misplaced: 'The merchant found treasure in the cave that was priceless' (the cave or the treasure?). Better: 'The merchant found priceless treasure in the cave.' Dangling: 'After telling the story, the king was impressed.' Better: 'After she told the story, the king was impressed.'
