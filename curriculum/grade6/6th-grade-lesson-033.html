📜 THE PEARL DIVERS

The Nautilus entered the Indian Ocean and made for the island of Ceylon—modern-day Sri Lanka. Nemo announced that he wished to visit the pearl-diving banks off the coast, and he invited us to join him. "Pearls?" Ned said, suddenly interested. "Real pearls?" "The finest in the world," said Nemo. "The oyster beds off Manaar are legendary. Some pearls are worth a king's ransom." We anchored near the coast and suited up for another underwater walk. This time, Nemo led us across a flat, sandy plain covered with thousands upon thousands of oysters. They lay in dense clusters, their rough shells half-buried in the sediment, each one potentially hiding a pearl. But Nemo wasn't interested in ordinary pearls. He led us to a hidden cave, partially concealed by an overhang of rock. Inside, in a natural basin, sat the largest oyster I had ever seen—or imagined. It was the size of a dining table, its thick shell coated with algae and barnacles. Nemo reached in and carefully pried the shell open. There, nestled in the creature's soft tissue, was a pearl the size of a coconut. I nearly choked on my air supply. It was flawless—perfectly round, gleaming with a soft, milky light. I calculated its value on the spot: it was worth millions of francs. Enough to buy a palace. Enough to fund a dozen scientific expeditions. Nemo looked at it the way a gardener looks at a prized rose—with pride and patience. He closed the shell gently and let it be. "I do not harvest this pearl," he said later, aboard the ship. "I allow it to grow. Year by year, layer by layer, it adds to its beauty. It is my treasure, and I will leave it in the sea where it belongs." This was the mystery of Captain Nemo. He had access to wealth beyond imagination—pearls, gold from sunken ships, the riches of the entire ocean—and he wanted none of it for himself. Or rather, he used it, but not the way any normal person would. As we would soon discover, Nemo had his own plans for that wealth. And they were not plans of a peaceful man.

📝 Grammar: Restrictive vs. Non-Restrictive Clauses and Commas

Restrictive clauses are essential to the meaning and need no commas: 'The girl who told the stories survived.' Non-restrictive clauses add extra information and use commas: 'Scheherazade, who had read everything, told stories.' The difference changes meaning: 'Students who studied passed' vs. 'Students, who studied, passed.'

Example:

Restrictive: 'The tales that changed the king came from many cultures.' Non-restrictive: 'Arabian Nights, which contains hundreds of tales, influenced world literature.'
