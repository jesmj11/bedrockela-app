📜 THE STORM (Part 1)

The mood aboard the Nautilus changed after the squid attack. A darkness settled over Captain Nemo that deepened with each passing day. He stopped joining me for meals. He stopped playing the organ. He spent hours alone in the salon, staring through the windows at nothing, or pacing the corridors late at night. When I tried to speak with him, he gave short, distracted answers and walked away. Meanwhile, Ned was growing desperate. We had been aboard the Nautilus for months. He could feel freedom slipping away with every mile. "We have to escape, Professor," he said one night, his voice low and urgent. "This man is losing his mind. I can feel it. Something is building inside him, and when it breaks, we don't want to be here." "Where would we go, Ned? We're in the middle of the ocean." "We're not always in the middle of the ocean. We pass near coastlines. The dinghy on the hull—it's seaworthy. If we time it right, on a night when Nemo is distracted..." I hesitated. Part of me—the scientist—never wanted to leave. The things I had seen aboard the Nautilus were beyond anything I had dreamed possible. But Ned was right about something: Nemo was changing. The noble, mysterious captain I had first met was being consumed by something—rage, grief, or madness. A massive storm struck the Atlantic. The Nautilus surfaced to ride it out—or rather, Nemo surfaced deliberately. He climbed to the platform at the top of the ship and stood in the full fury of the hurricane, lashed by rain and wind, waves crashing over him. Lightning split the sky. Thunder shook the hull. And Nemo stood there with his arms spread wide, his face turned up to the storm, as if daring it to take him. "He's completely mad," Ned said, watching from inside. I wasn't sure Ned was wrong.

🗣️ Language: Tone and Mood: Language Creates Atmosphere

Learn:

Tone is the author's attitude toward the subject (humorous, serious, ironic, respectful). Mood is the feeling created for the reader (suspenseful, hopeful, melancholy). Authors create tone and mood through word choice, sentence structure, imagery, and pacing.

Example:

"Serious tone: 'The king's decree devastated the kingdom.' Ironic tone: 'The king's "justice" brought only misery.' The quotation marks create irony. Suspenseful mood: 'The dawn approached. Would the king demand she continue or order her death?'"

📰 Ocean Pollution: Plastic and Chemicals

Every year, humans dump about 8 million tons of plastic into the ocean—the equivalent of one garbage truck per minute. This plastic doesn't biodegrade; it breaks into smaller and smaller pieces called microplastics, but never truly disappears.

Ocean currents collect this debris into massive "garbage patches." The Great Pacific Garbage Patch is twice the size of Texas—a floating island of trash. Sea turtles mistake plastic bags for jellyfish and starve with stomachs full of indigestible waste. Seabirds feed plastic to their chicks. Fish ingest microplastics, which then enter the food chain and end up on our dinner plates.

Chemical pollution is equally serious. Agricultural runoff carries fertilizers into rivers and ultimately the ocean, causing algae blooms that suck oxygen from the water, creating "dead zones" where nothing can survive. Heavy metals, oil spills, and industrial waste poison marine life.

In Verne's time, the ocean seemed infinite—a dumping ground that could absorb anything. We now know this was an illusion. The ocean is vast but not invincible. Nemo saw the sea as a refuge from human corruption. Today, there's no part of the ocean untouched by human impact.
