📜 FRITZ'S SECRET (Part 1)

Fritz had been acting strangely for weeks. He took the new boat — a second pinnace we had built to replace the Elizabeth — on long solo trips along the coastline, returning late and offering vague explanations. He was hiding something. Ernest noticed first. "Fritz found something," he told me quietly. "He won't say what." I confronted Fritz privately. He resisted, then relented. "I found this," he said, and pulled a glass bottle from his coat. Inside was a piece of cloth, and on the cloth, written in English, were the words: "Save me. I am alone on the smoking rock. A Christian soul. Jenny Montrose." A message in a bottle. Another castaway — a woman, alone, on an island she called "the smoking rock." Fritz had found the bottle wedged in a tide pool on the eastern shore, and he had been searching for the smoking rock ever since. "I think it's one of the islets to the east," Fritz said. "There's a rocky island out there with what looks like a volcanic vent — it sends up a thin column of smoke. I can see it on clear days." "How long has this message been floating?" I asked. "I don't know. Months. Maybe longer." My first instinct was caution. We didn't know who this person was, how she had gotten there, or what condition she was in. A rescue attempt in the eastern waters — the same waters that had sunk the Elizabeth — was dangerous. My second instinct overrode the first. There was a person out there. Alone. Asking for help. "We go tomorrow," I said.

📝 Grammar: Noun Clauses and Their Functions

Learn:

A noun clause acts as a noun in a sentence, often starting with that, what, who, whoever, whether, if. They can be subjects, objects, or complements: 'What she said convinced him' (subject). 'He knew that she was clever' (object). 'The question is whether he will change' (complement).

Example:

"Subject: 'Whoever tells the best story wins.' Object: 'The king realized that he had been wrong.' Complement: 'The problem was that he couldn't trust anyone.'"

📰 Indigenous Knowledge and Colonialism

The Swiss Family Robinson, written in 1812, reflects European colonial attitudes. The family treats the island as empty land waiting to be civilized, ignoring the possibility of indigenous inhabitants. In reality, most habitable islands were already occupied when Europeans arrived. Indigenous peoples had sophisticated knowledge of local ecosystems, developed over centuries. They knew which plants were edible or medicinal, best fishing methods, weather patterns, and much more. European colonizers often dismissed this knowledge as primitive, despite being far less equipped for survival. The Robinson family's fictional success depends partly on European tools and education, but real castaways benefited immensely from learning indigenous techniques—if they were humble enough to recognize local knowledge's value.
