📜 THE PROFESSOR

My uncle, Professor Otto Lidenbrock, was the most brilliant and the most terrifying man I had ever known. He was a professor of geology and mineralogy at the University of Hamburg, Germany, and he was famous — not just for his knowledge, which was vast, but for his temper, which was volcanic. He could identify any rock on Earth by sight. He could lecture for three hours without notes on the formation of granite. He could also throw a book across a room with startling accuracy when his experiments didn't go as planned. My name is Axel. I was nineteen years old, an orphan, and I lived with my uncle in his tall, narrow house on Königstrasse in Hamburg. I was his assistant, his student, and, more often than I liked, the target of his impatience. I was studying geology under his direction, though I would have preferred a quieter line of work — something involving a desk, a cup of tea, and absolutely no danger. I was also in love. Gräuben was my uncle's goddaughter — a sweet, calm, intelligent girl with blue eyes who lived nearby and who seemed, miraculously, to like me back. My greatest ambition in life was to finish my studies, marry Gräuben, and settle into a comfortable, predictable existence. My uncle had other plans. On the morning of May 24th, 1863, Professor Lidenbrock burst through the front door of our house, raced up the stairs, and locked himself in his study. I heard books slamming, furniture scraping, and what sounded like a small explosion — probably a paperweight hitting the wall. "Axel!" he shouted. "Come here immediately!" I went. When my uncle shouted, you went. It was a law of nature, like gravity. He was standing at his desk, holding an old book — very old, with a cracked leather cover and yellowed pages. His eyes were blazing with the light that meant he had found something important. I had seen that light before, and it always meant trouble. "Look at this," he said, thrusting the book toward me. "I found it at a bookseller's shop this morning. It's a twelfth-century Icelandic manuscript — incredibly rare. But that's not the important part." He opened the book and shook it. A small piece of parchment fell out and fluttered to the desk. It was covered in strange writing — letters I didn't recognize, arranged in what appeared to be a code. "What is it?" I asked. My uncle's grin was wild. "That," he said, "is what we are going to find out."

📝 Grammar: Subjunctive Mood for Hypotheticals and Wishes

The subjunctive mood expresses wishes, suggestions, or hypothetical situations. It often uses 'were' instead of 'was': 'If I were the king...' Common in formal writing after verbs like wish, suggest, recommend: 'I wish she were here.' 'I suggest that he study harder' (not 'studies').

Example:

Subjunctive: 'If Scheherazade were to fail, she would die.' 'The king wished that his wife were faithful.' Indicative (not subjunctive): 'Scheherazade was clever.'
