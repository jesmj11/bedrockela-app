📜 TRACKING NUMBER TWO (Part 1)

The treasure haunted Tom's dreams—but so did Injun Joe. The gold was out there somewhere, hidden in a place called "Number Two, under the cross." Tom had to find it. But he also had to avoid getting killed. He and Huck talked it through. "Number Two" had to be a room number—probably in one of the town's two taverns. Tom went to investigate. At the better tavern, Room Number Two was occupied by a young lawyer. At the cheaper tavern, the owner's son said Room Number Two was always locked. Nobody went in or out except late at night. He didn't know why. Most people figured it was haunted. "That's our place," Tom told Huck. "The back door of that room opens into the alley. You get every old key you can find, and I'll get Aunt Polly's keys. The first dark night, we'll try them all." "What if we run into Injun Joe?" "It'll be dark—he probably won't see us. And if he does—well, we run." "I don't like this one bit, Tom." "I don't love it either. But think about that gold." They tried on Thursday night. The alley behind the tavern was pitch dark—perfect. Tom crept up to the door, heart slamming against his ribs. He tried one key. It didn't turn. He tried another—a third— Then his hand closed on the doorknob, and the door swung open. It wasn't even locked. He eased inside, peeled back the towel he'd wrapped around his lantern, and— Something moved on the floor. A body. An arm. Tom's light fell on a face—Injun Joe, passed out drunk, sprawled on the floor in a puddle of whiskey. Tom didn't breathe. He didn't think. He ran. He burst out of the alley like a bullet and didn't stop until he found Huck three blocks away. "Run!" he gasped. "Run!" They didn't stop until they reached an abandoned shed on the edge of town. Rain began hammering the roof as they crouched inside, panting. "Tom, what happened?" "He's in there. Injun Joe. Asleep on the floor—drunk." "Did you see the box?" "I didn't wait to look around! I saw him and I ran." They agreed to keep watch on the tavern. Every night, Huck would patrol the area. If he ever saw Injun Joe leave, he would come straight to Tom's window and signal. Then they'd make their move. Huck took the first watch that night. And the next night. And the next. But Injun Joe never appeared.

🗣️ Language: Semantic Fields and Word Families

Learn:

Semantic fields are groups of words related by meaning: words for 'saying' (whisper, shout, murmur, declare, announce), 'intelligence' (clever, wise, shrewd, cunning, astute). Authors vary words within semantic fields to create precise meanings and avoid repetition.

Example:

"Instead of repeating 'said,' the author might use: 'whispered,' 'announced,' 'murmured,' 'declared'—each adds different meaning (volume, formality, emotion). Each word in the semantic field of 'speaking' creates a different effect."
