📜 FOLLOWING THE THREAD

The night came sooner than Irene expected.

She was in her room, getting ready for bed, when she felt the thread pull. Not a physical pull — she couldn't have described it to anyone — but a certainty, deep in her chest, that she needed to go. Now. Right now.

She put on her shoes, wrapped herself in a cloak, and followed the thread.

📜 FOLLOWING THE THREAD (Part 2)

It led her out of her room, down the hallway, through a door she had never noticed, and into a passageway that sloped downward — below the castle, into the rock of the mountain itself. The air grew cold. The darkness was absolute.

Irene was terrified. Every instinct told her to turn back, to run to Lootie, to hide under her blankets and pretend none of this was happening. She was eight years old, alone in the dark, in tunnels that led into goblin territory.

But the thread pulled, and she followed.

📜 FOLLOWING THE THREAD (Part 3)

She walked for what felt like hours. The tunnel twisted and branched. She could hear sounds in the darkness — dripping water, distant tapping, the scurrying of goblin creatures. Once, she heard goblin voices, harsh and close, and she pressed herself against the wall and held her breath until they passed.

The thread never wavered. It led her left when she would have gone right. It led her down when she wanted to go up. It led her deeper into the mountain, into the very heart of goblin territory, and every step was an act of faith.

Faith in the grandmother. Faith in the thread. Faith that the invisible could be more real than anything she could see or touch.

📝 Language: Word Roots: Greek and Latin Foundations

Most academic vocabulary comes from Greek and Latin roots. Common roots: 'aud' (hear), 'dict' (speak), 'spec' (see), 'port' (carry), 'graph' (write), 'bio' (life), 'chrono' (time), 'geo' (earth). Knowing roots unlocks hundreds of words.

Example:

'Spectator' (one who sees), 'inspect' (look into), 'retrospect' (look back). Same root, different prefixes change meaning. 'Biography' (writing about life), 'biology' (study of life)—same root, different suffixes.
