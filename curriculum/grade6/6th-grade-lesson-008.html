📜 THE TOWN IN SHOCK (Part 1)

By noon, the whole village knew. No one needed a telegraph—the news flew from mouth to mouth like wildfire. Dr. Robinson was dead. A bloody knife had been found near the body, and someone had recognized it as Muff Potter's. A man had spotted Potter washing himself in a stream at two in the morning, and Potter had run away when he saw he'd been noticed. Horsemen were sent out in every direction. The sheriff was confident Potter would be caught before nightfall. The whole town drifted toward the graveyard. Tom went too—not because he wanted to, but because something he couldn't explain dragged him there. He pushed through the crowd and saw the terrible scene: the open grave, the trampled grass, the bloodstains. Someone pinched his arm. He turned—it was Huck. Their eyes met, and both looked away instantly, terrified that someone might notice. All around them, people were talking: "Poor young fellow." "This should be a lesson." "Muff Potter will hang for sure." Tom shivered—because across the crowd, standing calm and still, was Injun Joe. Just then, a commotion rippled through the gathering. "It's him! He's coming!" "Who?" "Muff Potter!" Potter stumbled into the clearing, looking dazed and terrified. He wasn't trying to escape—he just seemed confused. He stopped in front of the body, and his whole body began to shake. He covered his face and burst into tears. "I didn't do it, friends. I swear I didn't." "Nobody's accused you," someone called out—but the words hit Potter like a slap. He looked around desperately, and his eyes landed on Injun Joe. "Oh, Joe—you promised you wouldn't—" The sheriff pushed through the crowd. "Is this your knife?" He held it up. Potter would have collapsed if people hadn't caught him. He sank to the ground and moaned, "Something told me I should've come back for that knife, but I was afraid..." Tom and Huck stood frozen, watching the stone-faced liar tell his story to the sheriff, calm as a summer morning. They expected lightning to strike him dead at any moment. But it didn't. The sky stayed clear, and Injun Joe walked away free. The boys made a new silent vow: they would never, ever open their mouths. In the days that followed, Tom's secret ate at him like acid. He talked in his sleep, which nearly gave everything away. One morning at breakfast, Sid said: "Tom, you toss and talk so much at night you keep me awake. Last night you kept saying, 'It's blood, it's blood, that's what it is!' and 'Don't torment me—I'll tell!'" Tom went pale. Luckily, Aunt Polly said, "That dreadful murder—I dream about it nearly every night myself." And the moment passed. But Tom began tying a bandage around his jaw at night, pretending he had a toothache, so that if he talked in his sleep, the words would be muffled. He never knew that Sid would sometimes untie the bandage in the dark and listen. Every day, Tom watched for a chance to visit the jail. It was a small brick building at the edge of a swamp—no guards, barely locked. Tom and Huck would sneak up to the grated window and pass through small gifts—tobacco, matches, whatever they could find. Potter's gratitude was heartbreaking. "You boys are the only friends I've got left," Potter would say, his voice cracking. "I used to mend kites for all the boys, and show them the good fishing spots. Now they've all forgotten me—except you two. I did an awful thing, boys. Drunk and crazy, that's the only way I can account for it. And now I've got to swing for it." Tom went home sick with guilt every time.

📝 Grammar: Parallel Structure in Lists and Series

Learn:

Parallel structure uses the same grammatical form for items in a series, creating rhythm and clarity. All items should be the same part of speech or phrase type: 'She was intelligent, determined, and resourceful' (all adjectives). Not parallel: 'She was intelligent, determined, and had resourcefulness.'

Example:

"Parallel: 'The king lost his faith, abandoned his principles, and destroyed his compassion.' Not parallel: 'The king lost his faith, his principles were abandoned, and destroying his compassion.'"
