📜 PIRATES OF JACKSON'S ISLAND (Part 1)

Tom's mind was made up. He was a friendless, miserable boy. Nobody loved him. Aunt Polly was disappointed in him. Becky Thatcher wouldn't speak to him. The secret of the murder was crushing his soul. Fine. If this was how the world wanted to treat him, he would leave. He would run away and never come back. Then they'd all be sorry. He was walking down Meadow Lane, feeling very dramatic about it all, when he ran into Joe Harper. Joe looked just as grim. It turned out Joe was running away too. His mother had whipped him for drinking cream that he'd never even touched—she'd thrown it out herself and forgotten. So Joe figured she was tired of him and wanted him gone. The two miserable boys walked together, swearing a solemn pact to stand by each other forever. Joe wanted to be a hermit and live in a cave, but Tom convinced him that being a pirate was much better. Three miles below St. Petersburg, in the middle of the wide Mississippi, lay Jackson's Island—a long, narrow strip of wooded land. Nobody lived there. It was perfect. They found Huck Finn and recruited him. He didn't care what they did—any career was fine with him. They agreed to meet at midnight on the riverbank, two miles above town, where a small log raft was tied up. At midnight, Tom arrived with a stolen ham and a few other supplies. The night was still and full of stars. The great river lay wide and dark, like a sleeping ocean. Tom gave a low whistle. It was answered from below the bluff. He whistled twice more. "Who goes there?" came a voice. "Tom Sawyer, the Black Avenger of the Spanish Main. Name yourselves." "Huck Finn the Red-Handed. Joe Harper, the Terror of the Seas." "Give the password." Two voices whispered the same word at the same time: "Blood!" Tom tumbled his ham over the bluff and climbed down after it. Joe had brought a side of bacon and some corn bread. Huck brought a skillet, some tobacco, and a few corn cobs for making pipes. They shoved off on the raft, Tom at the helm, giving whispered orders in proper pirate fashion: "Luff and bring her to the wind!" "Steady, steady!" "Let her go off a point!" None of these commands meant anything in particular, but they sounded magnificent. The raft drifted past the sleeping town. Two or three faint lights glimmered in the distance. Tom stood with his arms folded, taking one last dramatic look at the place he was leaving forever—or at least until next week. Around two in the morning, the raft grounded on a sandbar at the head of Jackson's Island. The boys unloaded their supplies, built a fire against a great fallen log, and cooked bacon in the skillet. They had never tasted anything so delicious. They lay on the grass afterward, staring at the flames, feeling like kings. "Isn't this great?" said Joe. "It's perfect," said Tom. "What would the boys at school say if they could see us?" "They'd die of jealousy," said Huck. "I don't ever want to go back. I never get enough to eat at home, and nobody bothers me here." "Pirates don't have to get up in the morning," said Tom. "They don't have to go to school, or wash, or any of that." They talked about pirate life for a while—treasure and ships and sword fights. Then, one by one, they fell quiet. The fire crackled. The river murmured. And the three pirates, who had sworn to never return to civilization, began to think—just a tiny bit—about home. They pushed those thoughts down and fell asleep under the stars.
