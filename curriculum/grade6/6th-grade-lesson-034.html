📜 THE SHARK

The next day, Nemo took us back to the pearl banks to observe the local divers at work. These were poor men from the coast who dove without any equipment—no suits, no helmets, no air tanks. They held their breath, dove to the bottom, scooped up oysters, and swam back to the surface. It was dangerous, exhausting work, and many divers died young. We watched from a distance, hidden among the rocks. One diver in particular caught my attention—a young man, thin but strong, who dove deeper and stayed down longer than any of the others. He was brave, but he was also reckless. On his fifth or sixth dive, I saw it before he did. A shadow passed over the sandy bottom—enormous, gray, and moving fast. A shark. Not just any shark—a great tiger shark, at least twenty feet long, with dead black eyes and a mouth that could swallow a man whole. The diver looked up and saw it. His eyes went wide with terror. He tried to swim for the surface, but the shark was faster. It turned, came at him with its jaws open— And Ned Land was there. I don't know how he moved so fast. One moment he was beside me, the next he was hurtling through the water like a torpedo, his knife—a long, heavy whaling knife—held out in front of him. He collided with the shark just as it reached the diver, driving the blade into the creature's belly with both hands. The water exploded into a cloud of blood and thrashing. The shark twisted, snapped, rolled—but Ned held on. He stabbed again and again, his face a mask of fury. Nemo was beside him in an instant, his own knife flashing. Between them, they killed the shark. It took several long, terrible minutes. When it was over, the water was dark with blood and the great fish sank slowly to the bottom, dead. The pearl diver floated nearby, unconscious but alive. Nemo swam to him, lifted him gently, and carried him to the surface. He reached into a waterproof pouch at his belt, pulled out a small bag of pearls, and pressed it into the unconscious man's hand. When we were back aboard the Nautilus, drying off and tending to Ned's cuts and bruises, I said to Nemo: "That was extraordinarily generous." He looked at me with those dark, unreadable eyes. "That man is one of the oppressed. As long as I live, I am his countryman." It was the first time Nemo had given any hint about his past—about who he had been before he became "no one." He was not simply a scientist or an eccentric. He was a man with a cause. A man with a wound. Ned Land said nothing about his own heroism. He just cleaned his knife, sharpened it on a stone, and went to bed.

📝 Language: Word Roots: Greek and Latin Foundations

Most academic vocabulary comes from Greek and Latin roots. Common roots: 'aud' (hear), 'dict' (speak), 'spec' (see), 'port' (carry), 'graph' (write), 'bio' (life), 'chrono' (time), 'geo' (earth). Knowing roots unlocks hundreds of words.

Example:

'Spectator' (one who sees), 'inspect' (look into), 'retrospect' (look back). Same root, different prefixes change meaning. 'Biography' (writing about life), 'biology' (study of life)—same root, different suffixes.
