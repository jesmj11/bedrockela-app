📜 THE TREEHOUSE (Part 1)

The paw print settled the question of where we would live. The beach was too exposed — too flat, too open, with no protection from animals or weather. We needed a home that was elevated, defensible, and sturdy. I thought of those enormous trees. The whole family hiked to the fig tree grove the next morning, loaded with tools and supplies from the wreck. When Elizabeth saw the trees, she stopped and stared upward. "You want to build a house in that tree," she said. It was not a question. "Look at those branches," I said. "They're as wide as floors. The trunk is impossible for any animal to climb. We'd be thirty feet off the ground, with a view in every direction." "It's perfect!" Jack shouted, and was halfway up the trunk before anyone could stop him. Building the treehouse took two weeks. It was the hardest work any of us had ever done, and it was also the most satisfying. Fritz and I hauled lumber from the wreck. Ernest designed a pulley system to lift heavy materials. Jack — who had no fear of heights whatsoever — worked on the highest branches, nailing planks and tying ropes with the agility of a monkey. Even little Franz carried supplies and held the ladder. Elizabeth was the architect. She had a practical eye for design that none of the rest of us possessed. She planned the layout: a main platform for cooking and eating, a sleeping area divided by canvas walls, a storage room for food, and a covered balcony with a railing where we could sit in the evening and look out over the island. We built a staircase that spiraled around the trunk — wide enough for two people, with a railing for safety. At the bottom, we constructed a gate that could be locked at night. When it was finished, we stood on the balcony as a family and looked out over the green canopy of the forest, the silver ribbon of the river, and the blue sea stretching to the horizon. The air was cool and clean. Birds sang in the branches above our heads. "What shall we call it?" Elizabeth asked. "Falconest," said Fritz, because a family of falcons nested in the highest branch. We had a home.

📝 Grammar: Verbals: Participles, Gerunds, and Infinitives

Learn:

Verbals are verb forms used as other parts of speech. Participles (-ing or -ed forms) act as adjectives: 'The sleeping king.' Gerunds (-ing forms) act as nouns: 'Storytelling saved her life.' Infinitives (to + verb) act as nouns, adjectives, or adverbs: 'To survive was her goal.'

Example:

"Participle: 'Scheherazade, speaking carefully, chose her words.' Gerund: 'Listening became the king's obsession.' Infinitive: 'To trust again would require courage.'"

📰 Food Preservation Without Refrigeration

Before refrigeration, preserving food meant survival. Salting, smoking, and drying were primary methods. Salt draws out moisture, preventing bacterial growth—this is how sailors preserved meat for long voyages. Smoking adds flavor and preserves by coating food in antibacterial compounds. Sun-drying works in hot climates for fruits, fish, and meat. The Robinson family would have known these techniques from European tradition and applied them to tropical foods. Without preservation, food spoils within hours in tropical heat. Learning to store food meant the difference between steady meals and constant hunger.
