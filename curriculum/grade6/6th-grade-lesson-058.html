📜 THE SILVER ARROW

Spring returned, and with it, another of the Sheriff's attempts to lure Robin into a trap. This time the prize was a silver arrow, and this time the Sheriff had hired professional archers from London — men who had never seen Robin Hood and wouldn't recognize him. Robin went anyway. He couldn't help himself. But Marian went with a plan. She positioned scouts at every exit from the tournament field. She arranged horses at three hidden points outside the city walls. She had Allan-a-Dale station himself in the crowd with a group of Robin's men, ready to create a distraction if things went wrong. Robin competed disguised as a tanner — a leather worker with rough hands and a simple brown tunic. He shot brilliantly, as always, and won the silver arrow. But the Sheriff had learned from his mistakes. This time, he didn't wait for Robin to reveal himself. The moment the tanner won, guards moved to surround the field. Every gate was locked. "Something's wrong," Robin murmured to Allan-a-Dale. Allan began to play his lute and sing — loudly, badly, and with great enthusiasm. The crowd gathered around him. In the confusion, Robin slipped through a gap in the guards and made it to the wall, where Will Scarlet was waiting with a rope. They were over the wall and on horseback before the Sheriff realized what had happened. But it was close — closer than the last time. An arrow grazed Robin's shoulder as he rode. Another killed the horse behind him. Back in the forest, Marian tended Robin's wound and said what no one else dared: "You're getting reckless. You go to these contests because you can't resist showing off. One day your pride will get you killed." Robin opened his mouth to argue. Then he closed it. She was right, and they both knew it.

📝 Grammar: Rhetorical Devices: Parallelism, Antithesis, Anaphora

Rhetorical devices create persuasive or memorable writing. Parallelism repeats structure: 'I came, I saw, I conquered.' Antithesis contrasts ideas: 'Not that I loved Caesar less, but that I loved Rome more.' Anaphora repeats words at the start: 'We shall fight on the beaches, we shall fight on the landing grounds...'

Example:

Parallelism: 'She was not just intelligent but also brave and resourceful.' Antithesis: 'She offered stories, not arguments; patience, not force.' Anaphora: 'Night after night, she told tales. Night after night, he listened.'
