📜 CURDIE THE MINER

Below the castle, in the town at the foot of the mountain, a boy named Curdie Peterson worked in the mines with his father.

Curdie was twelve years old, practical and strong, with rough hands and a sharp mind. His father, Peter Peterson, was one of the king's miners — men who dug deep into the mountain for gold and precious stones that were sent to the capital to fill the royal treasury.

The work was hard and dangerous. The tunnels were narrow, dark, and prone to collapse. The air was thick with dust. Miners worked by candlelight, swinging picks until their arms ached, filling carts with ore that other men hauled to the surface. Boys Curdie's age worked alongside men twice their size, because in a mining town, everyone worked.

📜 CURDIE THE MINER (Part 2)

But the mine held a secret that most people in the town preferred not to talk about.

Deep beneath the mountain, below the levels where the miners worked, there were other tunnels — older tunnels, carved not by human hands but by something else. Something that lived in the dark.

Goblins.

📜 CURDIE THE MINER (Part 3)

The miners knew about the goblins the way people know about earthquakes or wolves — a danger that was always present but rarely discussed. The goblins had lived under the mountain for centuries, driven underground long ago by the humans who had settled the surface. They were small, twisted creatures — stronger than they looked, clever in a cruel way, and filled with a bitter hatred for the people who lived above them.

The goblins kept to their own tunnels, mostly. They came out at night sometimes, creeping through the darkness, but they fled from light and noise. The miners stomped their feet and sang loud songs when they worked near goblin territory, because goblins hated the sound of singing and the vibration of stamping feet.

Curdie wasn't afraid of goblins. He was cautious — his father had taught him to be cautious — but he wasn't afraid. He had grown up in the mines. He knew the tunnels better than most grown men. And he had a quality that would prove more valuable than strength or speed: he paid attention.

📝 Grammar: Misplaced and Dangling Modifiers

Modifiers should be placed next to what they modify. Misplaced: 'She told a story to the king with great skill' (the king doesn't have the skill—she does). Dangling: 'Walking through the palace, the stories came to mind' (stories can't walk). Correct: 'Walking through the palace, she thought of stories.'

Example:

Misplaced: 'The merchant found treasure in the cave that was priceless' (the cave or the treasure?). Better: 'The merchant found priceless treasure in the cave.' Dangling: 'After telling the story, the king was impressed.' Better: 'After she told the story, the king was impressed.'
