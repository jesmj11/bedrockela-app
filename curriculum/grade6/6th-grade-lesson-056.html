📜 NEMO'S REVENGE (Part 1)

The event Ned had feared came without warning. Through the salon window, I spotted a ship on the surface—a large warship flying a flag I didn't recognize. Nemo appeared beside me. His entire body had gone rigid. His face was a mask of cold fury. "That ship," he said, in a voice I had never heard before. "That flag." "Captain, what—" "Leave the salon, Professor. Now. Go to your cabin. Do not come out until I say." "What are you going to do?" He turned to me, and for the first time, I saw something in his eyes that frightened me more than the deep ocean, more than the squid, more than the ice: hatred. Pure, absolute, burning hatred. "I am going to do what I was built to do," he said. We were locked in our cabins. Through the walls, I heard the engines roar to full power. I felt the ship accelerate, diving and then rising, building speed. Then—a colossal impact. The Nautilus shook from bow to stern. A grinding, tearing sound of metal on metal. Nemo had rammed the warship. Through the small porthole in my cabin, I saw it—the warship, broken nearly in half, sinking fast. Men were in the water, clinging to wreckage, crying for help. The ship's bow pointed straight up at the sky for a long, terrible moment, and then it slid under the waves. Bubbles. Debris. Silence. Nemo had killed them all. When he finally let us out, I found him in the salon, standing before a portrait on the wall—a woman and two small children. He was weeping. "That flag," he said, without looking at me. "That nation destroyed everything I loved. My family. My people. My country. They took it all." I said nothing. There was nothing to say. "I told you, Professor. I have severed all ties with the world above. But the world above has not severed its ties with me. It follows me. It hunts me. And when it finds me, I answer." I looked at the portrait of the woman and children. I looked at this brilliant, tortured man. And I understood—not forgave, but understood—what had driven Captain Nemo to the bottom of the sea.

📝 Grammar: Misplaced and Dangling Modifiers

Learn:

Modifiers should be placed next to what they modify. Misplaced: 'She told a story to the king with great skill' (the king doesn't have the skill—she does). Dangling: 'Walking through the palace, the stories came to mind' (stories can't walk). Correct: 'Walking through the palace, she thought of stories.'

Example:

"Misplaced: 'The merchant found treasure in the cave that was priceless' (the cave or the treasure?). Better: 'The merchant found priceless treasure in the cave.' Dangling: 'After telling the story, the king was impressed.' Better: 'After she told the story, the king was impressed.'"

📰 Ocean Acidification: The Other CO₂ Problem

Everyone knows carbon dioxide (CO₂) causes global warming, but fewer people know about its effect on the ocean. The ocean absorbs about 25% of the CO₂ humans emit. This might seem helpful—less CO₂ in the atmosphere means less warming—but it comes at a terrible cost.

When CO₂ dissolves in seawater, it forms carbonic acid, lowering the ocean's pH. The ocean is now 30% more acidic than it was before the Industrial Revolution, and acidity is increasing faster than any time in the last 300 million years.

This matters because many marine organisms build shells and skeletons from calcium carbonate—corals, mollusks, sea urchins, and many plankton species. Acidic water dissolves calcium carbonate, making it harder (sometimes impossible) for these creatures to grow and survive.

If ocean acidification continues, coral reefs will dissolve, shellfish populations will collapse, and the entire marine food web could unravel. Pteropods—tiny swimming snails that are a crucial food source for fish, whales, and seabirds—are already showing shell damage in some regions.

Verne's characters marvel at the ocean's abundance. Our challenge is ensuring that abundance survives for future generations.
