📜 THE BISHOP'S RANSOM

Winter came to Sherwood Forest, and with it, hardship. The ground froze. Game grew scarce. The band huddled around smoky fires, mending their clothes and sharpening their weapons. Even Robin's good humor was tested by the cold. Then word came from Marian's network: the Bishop of Hereford was traveling through Sherwood Forest with a wagon full of gold — tax money collected from the poorest villages in the county, bound for the King's treasury. The Bishop traveled with only a small escort, confident that his holy office would protect him. He was wrong. Robin and twenty of his men set an ambush at a narrow bend in the road where the forest pressed close on both sides. They dropped a tree across the path and waited. The Bishop's wagon appeared, creaking under the weight of its cargo. Six armed guards rode alongside. The Bishop himself sat in the wagon, fat and comfortable, wrapped in furs against the cold. "Good morning, Your Grace," Robin said, stepping out of the trees. "Terrible weather for traveling. Perhaps you'd like to rest a while." The guards reached for their swords. Little John stepped out behind them, his quarterstaff across his shoulders. Friar Tuck appeared on the other side of the road. Green-clad men rose from behind every bush and rock. The guards lowered their weapons. Robin didn't take everything — that was his rule. He took half the gold and left the Bishop enough to continue his journey. But before letting him go, Robin made the Bishop sit on a log and eat a simple meal of bread and cold venison — the same food the peasants lived on. "This is what the people you tax eat every day, Your Grace," Robin said. "Remember it." The Bishop left Sherwood Forest in a fury, vowing revenge. But the gold Robin took fed three villages through the winter. Children who would have gone hungry ate bread. Families who couldn't pay their rent were spared eviction. Friar Tuck, who had his own opinions about corrupt churchmen, said a prayer over the gold before it was distributed. "The Lord works in mysterious ways," he said. "Sometimes those ways involve highway robbery."

📝 Grammar: Misplaced and Dangling Modifiers

Modifiers should be placed next to what they modify. Misplaced: 'She told a story to the king with great skill' (the king doesn't have the skill—she does). Dangling: 'Walking through the palace, the stories came to mind' (stories can't walk). Correct: 'Walking through the palace, she thought of stories.'

Example:

Misplaced: 'The merchant found treasure in the cave that was priceless' (the cave or the treasure?). Better: 'The merchant found priceless treasure in the cave.' Dangling: 'After telling the story, the king was impressed.' Better: 'After she told the story, the king was impressed.'
