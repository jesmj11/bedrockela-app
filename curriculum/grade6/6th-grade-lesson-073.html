📜 GUY OF GISBOURNE (Part 1)

The Sheriff realized he could not catch Robin Hood with soldiers. He needed someone who could match Robin skill for skill, cunning for cunning. He needed an assassin. He found Guy of Gisbourne. Guy was a knight who had been stripped of his lands for acts of violence so extreme that even the other nobles were disgusted. He was tall, lean, and deadly — a master swordsman and archer who wore a cloak of rough horsehide and carried a black-handled dagger that had killed more men than anyone could count. He had no loyalty except to money, and no mercy at all. "Kill Robin Hood," the Sheriff said. "Bring me his head. I'll pay you his weight in silver." "Done," said Guy of Gisbourne. Guy entered Sherwood Forest alone, something no hired killer had dared before. He moved quietly, watching, listening, reading the forest like a hunter reads tracks. He was patient. He was dangerous. Robin found him — or rather, they found each other, meeting on a narrow forest path as if the forest itself had arranged it. They talked first. Both men took the measure of the other — the careful way a swordsman studies an opponent before the first blow. Guy pretended to be a traveler. Robin pretended to believe him. Then the pretending stopped. The fight was unlike anything Robin had experienced. Guy was fast, brutal, and absolutely without hesitation. He fought to kill — no honor, no rules, no pauses. His sword cut through Robin's sleeve, drawing blood. His dagger nearly found Robin's throat. Robin had always fought with joy — the thrill of the contest, the pleasure of testing himself. Against Guy of Gisbourne, there was no joy. There was only survival. The fight lasted twenty minutes. It ended when Robin caught Guy's sword arm in a bind, twisted, and drove his own blade home. Guy fell. Robin stood over him, breathing hard, blood running down his arm. He looked at the dead man's face and felt nothing but a cold, hollow relief. He took Guy's horsehide cloak and wore it into Nottingham, disguised as the assassin. When the Sheriff eagerly asked for Robin Hood's body, Robin revealed himself, mocked the Sheriff one final time, and escaped in the chaos. But the fight with Guy stayed with him. For the first time, Robin understood that this life — however merry they called it — had real consequences. People were trying to kill him, and someday, someone might succeed.

📝 Grammar: Semicolons, Colons, and Dashes for Emphasis

Learn:

Semicolons join closely related independent clauses: 'She was not afraid; she was determined.' Colons introduce lists, explanations, or quotes: 'She needed one thing: time.' Dashes create emphasis or mark interruptions: 'Her plan—dangerous but necessary—might work.'

Example:

"Semicolon: 'The night ended; the story continued.' Colon: 'Scheherazade understood one truth: stories change minds.' Dash: 'The king—once kind, now cruel—listened carefully.'"

📰 Medieval Assassins

Medieval England had professional fighters-for-hire called "knightly mercenaries." These were skilled warriors who fought for whoever paid them. Some were former knights who had lost their lands or been disgraced. With no loyalty except to money, they were dangerous and ruthless.

Guy of Gisbourne represents this type: a skilled fighter with no moral compass. The text describes him wearing horsehide (an unusual choice that suggests wildness) and carrying weapons designed only for killing. Unlike Robin's band, who rob but rarely kill, Guy has no code. This makes him more dangerous than any sheriff's army.
