📜 RAINY SEASON

The first rainy season hit us like a wall. For weeks, the sky turned dark and the rain fell in sheets — not the gentle drizzle of Switzerland, but a tropical deluge that turned the ground to mud, swelled the river to twice its normal width, and made any outdoor work impossible. Wind howled through the cliffs. Lightning cracked overhead. The ocean churned white. We retreated to Rockburg and waited. The first few days were almost pleasant. The boys played games. Elizabeth taught Franz to read from the books we had salvaged. I organized our tools and planned improvements for the dry season. Ernest worked on his natural history journal, drawing detailed illustrations of the plants and animals he'd cataloged. By the second week, the pleasantness wore off. Four boys in an enclosed space, no matter how large, is a recipe for conflict. Fritz and Ernest argued about everything — whose turn it was to cook, whose shelf was encroaching on whose space, whose scientific theory about the island's geology was correct. Jack couldn't sit still for more than ten minutes. Franz, usually cheerful, grew quiet and withdrawn. Elizabeth kept the peace with a schoolteacher's combination of firmness and creativity. She established a daily schedule: morning lessons in mathematics, reading, and writing; afternoon projects; evening entertainment. Fritz practiced his shooting technique with a makeshift indoor target. Ernest conducted experiments with island minerals. Jack built elaborate traps for imaginary animals. Franz learned to play a small flute that Elizabeth had found in the wreck. But the rain also brought time for reflection — and with reflection came the questions we had been too busy to face. "Do you think anyone is looking for us?" Ernest asked one evening, as rain hammered the cliff above our heads. "The ship was bound for a colony," I said carefully. "When it doesn't arrive, people will notice." "But will they look in the right place? We were blown hundreds of miles off course." I didn't answer. He was right. The chances of rescue were small, and they grew smaller with each passing month. Elizabeth caught my eye across the room. We had an unspoken agreement: honesty with the boys, but not hopelessness. We would tell them the truth, but we would also keep building, keep working, keep living as if the future mattered. Because it did — whether rescue came or not.

📝 Grammar: Semicolons, Colons, and Dashes for Emphasis

Semicolons join closely related independent clauses: 'She was not afraid; she was determined.' Colons introduce lists, explanations, or quotes: 'She needed one thing: time.' Dashes create emphasis or mark interruptions: 'Her plan—dangerous but necessary—might work.'

Example:

Semicolon: 'The night ended; the story continued.' Colon: 'Scheherazade understood one truth: stories change minds.' Dash: 'The king—once kind, now cruel—listened carefully.'
