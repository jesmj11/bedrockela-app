📜 THE BLOOD OATH

Tom and Huck ran until their lungs burned. They didn't stop until they reached the old tannery at the edge of town, where they burst through the door and collapsed in the shadows, gasping for breath. When they could finally speak, Tom whispered: "Huckleberry, what do you think will happen?" "If Dr. Robinson is dead, somebody's going to hang." "Do you really think so?" "I know it, Tom." Tom was quiet for a long time. Then he said: "Who's going to tell? Us?" "Are you crazy? What if Injun Joe didn't hang? He'd kill us for sure." "That's what I was thinking." "Listen, Tom—Muff Potter doesn't know what really happened. He was knocked out cold. He can't tell." "You're right. He was too drunk to see anything." "Tom, we have to swear—swear to keep our mouths shut." "I'm with you. We'll swear." Huck shook his head. "A regular swear isn't enough for something like this. There has to be writing. And blood." Tom's whole soul thrilled at this. It was dark and dangerous and perfect. He found a flat piece of pine in the moonlight, dug a bit of red chalk from his pocket, and slowly, painfully, scratching each letter by the light of the moon, he wrote: HUCK FINN AND TOM SAWYER SWEAR THEY WILL KEEP MUM ABOUT THIS AND THEY WISH THEY MAY DROP DOWN DEAD IF THEY EVER TELL AND ROT. Huck was impressed. Tom unwound thread from a needle, and each boy pricked his thumb and squeezed out a drop of blood. They signed their initials—Tom in shaky letters, Huck with a wobbly H and F. They buried the wooden oath near the wall, performed some made-up rituals over it, and felt that their tongues were now truly locked. The key was thrown away forever. A dark figure appeared at the other end of the ruined building—but the boys didn't see it. "Tom," Huck whispered, "does this mean we can never tell? Ever?" "Never. No matter what. We'd drop dead if we did." They whispered a while longer, then parted ways and crept home through the dark. Tom climbed back in through his window. He undressed as quietly as he could and fell asleep, not knowing that Sid had been lying awake the entire time, watching him. The next morning, Tom woke to find it was late. He hurried downstairs. The family sat at the table in strange, heavy silence. Nobody scolded him. Nobody even looked at him. There were only averted eyes and a gloomy air that chilled him to the bone. After breakfast, Aunt Polly took him aside. Tom almost felt hopeful—maybe she was going to punish him, and punishment he could handle. But she didn't punish him. She cried over him. She asked him how he could go on breaking her heart. She told him to go ahead and ruin himself and bring her gray hairs to the grave, because she just couldn't help him anymore. This was a thousand times worse than a whipping. Tom's heart was shattered. He cried and begged for forgiveness, promised to do better—and then was sent away, feeling that he'd only half been forgiven. He stumbled to school, miserable and distracted. He and Joe Harper both got whipped for skipping school the day before, but Tom hardly noticed. He had bigger things on his mind. He slumped into his seat and put his head in his hands. After a while, his elbow touched something. He picked it up—a little brass knob. His andiron knob. The one Becky had knocked to the floor. His heart broke all over again.

📝 Language: Figurative Language: Metaphor, Simile, Personification, Symbolism

Figurative language creates meaning beyond literal words. Metaphors assert identity: 'Her words were weapons.' Similes compare with 'like/as': 'Words like weapons.' Personification gives human qualities to non-human things: 'The story grabbed him.' Symbolism: objects representing ideas (Scheherazade's stories = hope, power of narrative).

Example:

Metaphor: 'The king's heart was stone.' Simile: 'His heart was like stone.' Personification: 'Anger consumed him.' Symbol: 'The thousand nights represent the slow process of healing and transformation.'
