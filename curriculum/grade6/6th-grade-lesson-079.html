📜 THREE YEARS

Time moves differently on an island. Without calendars, schedules, or the rhythm of a town's daily life, the days blend together. Seasons change — wet to dry, dry to wet — but the years accumulate quietly, unmarked by the milestones that measure time in civilization. It was Elizabeth who kept count. She marked each day on the wall of Rockburg with a small scratch — a habit she had started on our first morning ashore. By the time I thought to look, there were over a thousand marks. Three years. We had been on the island for three years. The boys had changed in ways that amazed and sometimes frightened me. Fritz was eighteen now — a man, tall and sunburned, with the confidence of someone who had faced wild animals, storms, and starvation and survived them all. He could sail the Elizabeth alone, shoot a bird on the wing, and track an animal through dense forest. He was also restless. The island, vast as it was, had begun to feel small to him. Ernest, at sixteen, had become the family's intellectual engine. His natural history journal had grown into multiple volumes — detailed observations of the island's plants, animals, weather patterns, and geology. He had taught himself basic chemistry by experimenting with island minerals. He was quiet, content, and deeply rooted in the life we had built. Jack, at fourteen, was still Jack — fearless, impulsive, and perpetually in motion. He rode Hurricane the ostrich across the grassland at speeds that terrified his mother. He had grown strong and wiry, with reflexes that made him the best hunter in the family after Fritz. Franz, at eleven, had lost his baby softness and become a thoughtful boy with a talent for music and languages. He spoke to the animals as if they could understand him, and sometimes I thought they could. Elizabeth had held us together through everything. She was the center — the steady, practical, unshakeable force that kept the family functioning when fear or frustration threatened to pull us apart. She had built a home from nothing, fed us from wilderness, educated four boys without a school, and done it all with a calm determination that I admired more than anything else in my life. And me? I was older. My hair had gone gray. My hands were rough and scarred from three years of building, farming, and improvising. But I was alive, and my family was alive, and we had created something remarkable on this forgotten island. Whether anyone would ever know about it was another question entirely.

📝 Language: Author's Purpose and Rhetorical Strategies

Authors choose words to achieve specific purposes: inform, persuade, entertain, inspire. Rhetorical strategies include appeals to emotion (pathos), logic (logos), and credibility (ethos). Word choice, sentence structure, and figurative language all serve the author's purpose.

Example:

Scheherazade uses narrative (storytelling) to persuade the king to change—this is a rhetorical strategy. She appeals to his emotions through compelling characters (pathos) and to his logic through cautionary tales (logos). Her credibility comes from her vast knowledge (ethos).
