📜 THE GOBLINS BELOW

One evening, Curdie stayed late in the mines — later than he should have, following a vein of quartz that glittered in his candlelight. He wandered deeper than usual and found himself in a part of the tunnel system he didn't recognize.

The walls were smoother here. The air was warmer. And there was a sound — a low, rhythmic murmur, like many voices speaking at once.

Curdie pressed himself against the wall and crept forward. The tunnel opened into a large cavern, lit by a sickly greenish glow. And in the cavern, seated on a rough stone throne, was the Goblin King.

He was larger than the other goblins — nearly the size of a man, but wider and heavier, with a flat, broad face and small, mean eyes. His queen sat beside him, tall and thin, with feet so long and flat that she walked on them like snowshoes. Around them, hundreds of goblins crowded the cavern floor.

📜 THE GOBLINS BELOW (Part 2)

They were having a council.

Curdie couldn't hear everything, but he caught enough. The Goblin King was angry. He was tired of hiding underground. He was tired of the miners digging into his territory, stealing the mountain's treasure. He wanted revenge.

And he had a plan.

The goblins had been tunneling — secretly, quietly, for months — toward the castle on the mountainside. They were going to break through the castle floor, storm the building, and capture the princess.

📜 THE GOBLINS BELOW (Part 3)

"The king's daughter," the Goblin Queen hissed. "She will marry our prince. Then we will have a claim to the surface. Then the mountain will be ours."

Curdie's blood went cold. He backed away slowly, his heart hammering, and ran for the surface.

📝 Language: Inference and Implication: Reading Between the Lines

Authors don't always state things directly—they imply them through word choice, actions, and context. Readers must infer (conclude based on evidence). Strong readers notice what's suggested but not said: a character's motives, unstated conflicts, thematic messages.

Example:

Text: 'The king listened without speaking.' Inference: He's processing something deeply, perhaps beginning to change. The author doesn't say 'he was changing'—the reader infers it from the behavior. Text: 'Scheherazade chose her next tale carefully.' Inference: She's strategic, not just entertaining.
