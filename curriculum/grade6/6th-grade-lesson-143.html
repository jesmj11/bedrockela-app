📜 THE GIANT (Part 1)

I still don't know if what I saw was real. We had left the bone shore and entered a dense forest of the giant prehistoric vegetation — ferns as tall as buildings, mosses thick enough to walk on, mushrooms towering overhead. The light was dim, filtered through the canopy of enormous leaves. I was walking ahead, pushing through the undergrowth, when I froze. In a clearing, perhaps a hundred yards away, stood a figure. It was enormous — at least twelve feet tall, with long arms and a broad, sloping forehead. It was humanoid but not human. It carried something — a branch, a weapon? — in one massive hand. It was watching a group of animals that grazed at the edge of the clearing: mastodons, living mastodons, their brown fur matted, their tusks yellowed. The giant was herding them. I didn't move. I didn't breathe. The creature — the being — stood motionless for what felt like an eternity, then turned and disappeared into the forest with a stride that covered ten feet. I ran back to my uncle and Hans. "I saw something," I gasped. "A giant. A — a person. Twelve feet tall. Herding mastodons." My uncle stared at me. Hans stared at me. "You're dehydrated," my uncle said carefully. "The dim light plays tricks." "I know what I saw." We went back to the clearing. The mastodons were gone. There were tracks in the soft ground — large tracks, but they could have been anything. The forest was silent. My uncle wrote it in his journal with a question mark. I have never stopped believing what I saw. Deep beneath the surface of the earth, in a world lit by electrical fire and populated by creatures that died on the surface millions of years ago, something human — or nearly human — still walks.

📝 Grammar: Noun Clauses and Their Functions

Learn:

A noun clause acts as a noun in a sentence, often starting with that, what, who, whoever, whether, if. They can be subjects, objects, or complements: 'What she said convinced him' (subject). 'He knew that she was clever' (object). 'The question is whether he will change' (complement).

Example:

"Subject: 'Whoever tells the best story wins.' Object: 'The king realized that he had been wrong.' Complement: 'The problem was that he couldn't trust anyone.'"

📰 Indigenous Knowledge and Colonialism

The Swiss Family Robinson, written in 1812, reflects European colonial attitudes. The family treats the island as empty land waiting to be civilized, ignoring the possibility of indigenous inhabitants. In reality, most habitable islands were already occupied when Europeans arrived. Indigenous peoples had sophisticated knowledge of local ecosystems, developed over centuries. They knew which plants were edible or medicinal, best fishing methods, weather patterns, and much more. European colonizers often dismissed this knowledge as primitive, despite being far less equipped for survival. The Robinson family's fictional success depends partly on European tools and education, but real castaways benefited immensely from learning indigenous techniques—if they were humble enough to recognize local knowledge's value.
