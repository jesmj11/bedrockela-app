📜 ALLAN-A-DALE

A young man came walking through the forest one morning, and Robin knew something was wrong before the stranger said a word. He was young, barely twenty, and he carried a lute on his back. His face was handsome but hollow with grief, and he walked like a man who no longer cared where his feet took him. "Good morning, friend," Robin said, stepping out from behind a tree. "You look like a man who's lost something." "I've lost everything," the young man said quietly. His name was Allan-a-Dale, and he was a minstrel — a traveling musician. He had fallen in love with a young woman named Ellen, and she loved him back. They had planned to marry. But Ellen's father owed money to a wealthy old knight named Sir Stephen of Trent. To pay the debt, the father had promised Ellen to Sir Stephen as a bride. The wedding was set for that very afternoon, at the church in Plympton, and there was nothing Allan could do to stop it. "She doesn't love him," Allan said, his voice breaking. "He's sixty years old and cruel. She wept when she told me. But her father says the debt must be paid." Robin's jaw tightened. "Where is this church?" One hour later, Robin Hood and a dozen of his men appeared at the church in Plympton. Robin was disguised as a wandering harper. Little John stood guard at the door. Friar Tuck — an actual ordained friar — stood ready. The ceremony had already begun. Sir Stephen, old and richly dressed, stood at the altar beside Ellen, who was pale and trembling. The bishop was reading the vows. Robin stepped forward and began to play his harp — loudly. "What is the meaning of this?" the bishop demanded. "A small change of plans," Robin said. He threw off his disguise, and his men blocked every exit. Ellen's father protested. Sir Stephen sputtered with rage. Robin turned to Ellen. "Do you love this man?" he asked, pointing to Sir Stephen. "No," she whispered. "Do you love Allan-a-Dale?" "With all my heart." Robin turned to Friar Tuck. "Marry them." Tuck performed the ceremony right there, over the bishop's objections and Sir Stephen's threats. Allan and Ellen were married before sunset. Robin paid off Ellen's father's debt with money taken from the bishop's own saddlebags. Allan-a-Dale joined the band that day, becoming their minstrel. His songs about Robin Hood would be sung for centuries.

📝 Grammar: Subjunctive Mood for Hypotheticals and Wishes

The subjunctive mood expresses wishes, suggestions, or hypothetical situations. It often uses 'were' instead of 'was': 'If I were the king...' Common in formal writing after verbs like wish, suggest, recommend: 'I wish she were here.' 'I suggest that he study harder' (not 'studies').

Example:

Subjunctive: 'If Scheherazade were to fail, she would die.' 'The king wished that his wife were faithful.' Indicative (not subjunctive): 'Scheherazade was clever.'
