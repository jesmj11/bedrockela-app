📜 THEIR OWN FUNERAL

Tom waded across the shallow sandbar, swam the deeper channel, and made his way through the dark woods to the riverbank opposite town. He slipped into the water again, swam to the ferryboat landing, and sneaked aboard a small rowboat tied to the stern. When the last ferry of the night crossed, Tom rode along underneath. He crept through quiet streets to his aunt's house. A light was burning in the sitting room. He pressed himself against the door, lifted the latch, and eased the door open—just a crack. Aunt Polly, Sid, Mary, and Joe Harper's mother sat together in the lamplight. They had been crying. They were talking about the boys. "He wasn't a bad boy," Aunt Polly was saying, her voice breaking. "Only mischievous. Just high-spirited, that's all. He never meant any harm, and he was the kindest-hearted boy in the world." She started crying again. "It was just the same with my Joe," Mrs. Harper sobbed. "Full of mischief, but as sweet and generous as could be. And to think I whipped him for drinking that cream, when I was the one who threw it out! I'll never see him again." "The Lord giveth and the Lord taketh away," Aunt Polly said. "But it's so hard. Only last Saturday, Tom filled the cat full of that terrible medicine, and I smacked him—and now I wish I could do it all over. I'd hug him instead." Tom was sniffling under the bed by now, feeling so sorry for himself—and for his aunt—that he could barely stand it. He learned that the funeral would be preached on Sunday morning, since no bodies had been found. The whole town assumed the boys had drowned while rafting across the river. After Mrs. Harper left, and Aunt Polly finally went to bed, Tom crept out. He stood over her sleeping form for a long moment. He took the piece of sycamore bark from his pocket and started to place it beside her candle—then stopped. A better idea had struck him. A magnificent idea. He put the bark back in his pocket, bent down and kissed his aunt's forehead, and slipped out into the night. He made it back to Jackson's Island by dawn. He found his friends still sleeping and collapsed beside them. When they woke, he told them everything—well, almost everything. He told them the town thought they were dead. He told them about the funeral. And then he told them his idea. Their eyes went wide. "Tom Sawyer," said Joe, "that is the greatest idea I have ever heard." Sunday morning came. The church bell tolled slowly—not its usual bright ring, but a single, mournful note. The villagers gathered in silence, dressed in black. The little church had never been so full. Aunt Polly entered, followed by Sid and Mary. Mrs. Harper came next with her family. They were led to the front pew while the whole congregation stood in respect. The minister spoke about the lost boys—their good hearts, their sweet natures, their playful spirits. He told stories about them that made people laugh through their tears. By the time he was done, every person in the church was weeping openly. Even the minister himself was crying. And then—a creak. The church door opened, just a little. Every head turned. Three boys came marching up the aisle: Tom in the lead, Joe next, and Huck bringing up the rear in his usual collection of rags, looking like he'd rather be anywhere else on earth. They had been hiding in the church gallery the whole time, listening to their own funeral. Aunt Polly, Mary, and Mrs. Harper threw themselves on the boys, smothering them with hugs and kisses and tears. Huck stood off to the side, uncomfortable and embarrassed, until Tom said: "Aunt Polly, somebody ought to be glad to see Huck, too." "And so I am!" she cried, and wrapped him up in a hug that made him squirm. The minister shouted: "Praise God! Now—sing! And put your hearts in it!" And they did. The old hymn rang out so strong it shook the rafters, and Tom Sawyer the Pirate looked around at the faces of every kid in town—all of them green with envy—and decided that this was the proudest moment of his entire life.

📝 Language: Academic Vocabulary: Tier 2 Words Across Disciplines

Tier 2 words (analyze, illustrate, demonstrate, evaluate, synthesize, contextualize) appear across subjects and are essential for academic success. They're more sophisticated than everyday words but not technical jargon. Mastering them improves reading and writing in all classes.

Example:

Instead of: 'Show how Scheherazade was smart,' write: 'Analyze how Scheherazade demonstrated strategic intelligence.' The tier 2 words (analyze, demonstrated, strategic) make thinking more precise.
