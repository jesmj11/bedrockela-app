📜 THE GRAVEYARD

That night, at half past nine, Tom and Sid were sent to bed. Sid fell asleep quickly, but Tom lay awake, restless and impatient. He stared into the darkness and waited. Every small sound seemed huge. The clock ticking. The stairs creaking. An owl hooting somewhere in the distance. Then—a dog howled, far away, and another answered from even farther. Tom shivered. At last, just when he thought he couldn't wait another second, he heard a faint sound outside: "Meow! Meow!" That was the signal. Tom eased open the window, crept across the roof on all fours, and dropped to the ground. Huckleberry Finn was waiting in the shadows, holding a dead cat by the tail. They set off through the dark streets toward the graveyard. It was an old cemetery on a hill about a mile outside of town—a run-down place with a crooked fence and weeds growing over everything. The wooden grave markers tilted at crazy angles, and the painted words on them had faded to nothing. The boys picked their way through the tall grass by moonlight. "Hucky," Tom whispered, "do you think the dead people mind us being here?" "I wish I knew. It sure feels spooky." "I bet it does." They found the fresh grave they were looking for—a man named Hoss Williams, recently buried—and hid behind a cluster of elm trees a few feet away. Then they waited. Time crawled. The silence was enormous. "Huck—do you think Hoss Williams can hear us?" "I reckon his spirit can." "I wish I'd called him Mister Williams." Tom was about to say something else when he froze. He grabbed Huck's arm. "Sh! Did you hear that?" "What?" "There—listen!" "Lord, Tom, they're coming. What do we do?" "Stay still! Maybe they won't see us." Three figures appeared in the darkness, moving through the graveyard, one of them carrying a lantern. Its light threw tiny spots across the ground. "It's the devils for sure!" Huck whispered. "Three of them! We're done for!" "Wait—those are people," Tom whispered back. "I know that voice. That's old Muff Potter." "Are you sure?" "Yes. And that other one—that's Injun Joe." Huck nearly stopped breathing. "Injun Joe! What are they up to?" The whispering died away as the three men reached the grave. The boys could see them clearly now. The third man was Dr. Robinson, a young doctor from town. Potter and Injun Joe had brought a wheelbarrow with shovels and rope. They began digging up the grave. "Hurry," the doctor said quietly. "The moon could come out any minute." The men grumbled but kept digging. After a while, they struck the coffin, pried off the lid, and hauled out the body—a horrible sight in the moonlight. They loaded it onto the wheelbarrow and tied it down. Potter cut the extra rope with his knife and said: "Now, Doc, you owe us another five dollars, or the body stays right here." "That's right," said Injun Joe. "What are you talking about?" said the doctor. "I already paid you." "Yeah, you did," said Injun Joe, stepping forward. His voice was low and dangerous. "But you did more than that. Five years ago, you threw me out of your father's kitchen when I came asking for food. You said I was up to no good. When I swore I'd get even, your father had me thrown in jail. Did you think I'd forget? I never forget." He was in the doctor's face now, fist raised. The doctor suddenly swung and knocked Injun Joe flat on the ground. Potter shouted, "Don't hit my partner!" and grabbed the doctor. The two men wrestled, trampling the grass, grunting and struggling. Injun Joe got to his feet, his eyes blazing. He snatched up Potter's knife from the ground. Then he crept around the fighting men, looking for an opening—quiet, patient, like a cat. The doctor broke free from Potter's grip, grabbed a heavy wooden headboard from the grave, and swung it. It cracked against Potter's skull, and Potter dropped like a stone. In the same instant, Injun Joe drove the knife into the doctor's chest. The doctor gasped, staggered, and fell—half on top of Potter. The clouds covered the moon, and the two boys ran, blind with terror, through the darkness. When the moonlight returned, Injun Joe stood over the two bodies, staring down at them. The doctor gave one last shudder and was still. "That score is settled," Joe muttered. Then he did something terrible. He took the bloody knife and placed it in Potter's hand. He sat down on the broken coffin and waited. A few minutes later, Potter groaned and woke up. He saw the knife in his hand. He saw the blood. He saw the doctor lying dead beside him. "Lord, Joe—how did this happen?" "It's a bad business," Joe said calmly. "What did I do? Joe, tell me honestly—did I do this?" Joe let a long moment pass. Then he said: "You two were fighting. He hit you with that headboard and knocked you cold. You got up, grabbed the knife, and stabbed him. Then you fell down again." "I—I don't remember any of it. It must have been the whiskey. I shouldn't have been drinking. Joe, please don't tell anyone. Promise me you won't tell." "I won't tell. You've always been fair with me, Muff. I won't go back on you." Potter stumbled to his feet, crying, and staggered away into the night. Injun Joe watched him go and muttered to himself: "He was so drunk he won't remember the knife. When he notices it's missing, he'll be too scared to come back." The graveyard was silent again. The murdered man, the open coffin, the empty grave—all of it lay under the cold moonlight while two horrified boys ran for their lives through the dark.

📝 Grammar: Verbals: Participles, Gerunds, and Infinitives

Verbals are verb forms used as other parts of speech. Participles (-ing or -ed forms) act as adjectives: 'The sleeping king.' Gerunds (-ing forms) act as nouns: 'Storytelling saved her life.' Infinitives (to + verb) act as nouns, adjectives, or adverbs: 'To survive was her goal.'

Example:

Participle: 'Scheherazade, speaking carefully, chose her words.' Gerund: 'Listening became the king's obsession.' Infinitive: 'To trust again would require courage.'
