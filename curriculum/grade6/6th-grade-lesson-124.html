📜 THE ECLIPSE

A boy came to my cell that night — a young page named Clarence, skinny and nervous, with big eyes and a quick mind. He had been sent to gawk at the prisoner, but I saw something useful in him: curiosity.

"What if I told you," I said, "that I am the greatest magician in the world? Tomorrow at noon, when they light the fire, I will blot out the sun."

Clarence ran to tell the court. The next day, they led me to the stake. The whole court was assembled — Arthur, Merlin, the knights, hundreds of common people.

I raised my arms and shouted: "I call upon the powers of darkness! Let the sun be destroyed!"

📜 THE ECLIPSE (Part 2)

Nothing happened. Merlin laughed.

Then the edge of the moon began to creep across the face of the sun.

The light dimmed. Stars appeared in the middle of the day. People fell to their knees, weeping, praying, begging for mercy.

King Arthur rushed forward. "Name your terms! Whatever you want — just bring back the sun!"

📜 THE ECLIPSE (Part 3)

"I want to be your chief minister. Second only to you. And I want Merlin locked up."

"Done! Anything!"

At the perfect moment, I raised my arms again. "Let the sun return!" The eclipse passed. Sunlight flooded back. The crowd erupted.

In five minutes, I had gone from a condemned prisoner to the most powerful person in England after the King. They called me "The Boss."

📝 Language: Word Roots: Greek and Latin Foundations

Most academic vocabulary comes from Greek and Latin roots. Common roots: 'aud' (hear), 'dict' (speak), 'spec' (see), 'port' (carry), 'graph' (write), 'bio' (life), 'chrono' (time), 'geo' (earth). Knowing roots unlocks hundreds of words.

Example:

'Spectator' (one who sees), 'inspect' (look into), 'retrospect' (look back). Same root, different prefixes change meaning. 'Biography' (writing about life), 'biology' (study of life)—same root, different suffixes.
