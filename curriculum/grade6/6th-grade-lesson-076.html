📜 THE OSTRICH HUNT

Jack was going to ride an ostrich. Nothing anyone said could stop him. We had discovered a flock of wild ostriches on the open grassland beyond the river — magnificent birds, taller than a man, with powerful legs that could outrun a horse. Fritz wanted to study them. Ernest wanted to sketch them. Jack wanted to ride one. "Absolutely not," Elizabeth said. "It'll be fine," Jack said, which was what Jack always said right before things went spectacularly wrong. He spent three days building a lasso from braided rope. On the fourth day, he crept into the grassland on his belly, got within range of a young male, and threw the loop around its neck. What happened next was chaos. The ostrich exploded into motion, dragging Jack across the grassland at terrifying speed. He clung to the rope with both hands, bouncing over rocks and bushes, his feet leaving the ground entirely. The rest of the flock scattered in every direction. "Help him!" Elizabeth shouted. Fritz and I ran after them, but no human can match an ostrich's speed. The bird ran for a quarter mile before it began to tire. Jack, battered and grass-stained but grinning, hauled himself hand-over-hand up the rope and actually managed to get onto the bird's back. The ostrich bucked. Jack held on. The ostrich spun in circles. Jack held on. The ostrich tried to bite him over its shoulder. Jack ducked and held on. Eventually, the bird gave up. It stood still, breathing hard, and Jack sat on its back like a king on a very annoyed throne. "See?" he called to us. "Fine!" He was covered in bruises and had lost a boot. Elizabeth gave him a lecture that lasted longer than the ride. But from that day forward, Jack had a riding ostrich — a bird he named Hurricane — and he became the fastest traveler on the island. Ernest shook his head. "He's going to kill himself." "Probably," Fritz agreed. "But you have to admire the commitment."

📝 Grammar: Subordination and Coordination for Sentence Variety

Coordination (using and, but, or, so) joins equal ideas. Subordination (using because, although, when, if) shows one idea is dependent on another. Varying these creates sophisticated writing: 'She told stories because the king needed to hear them' (subordination) vs. 'She told stories, and the king listened' (coordination).

Example:

Coordination: 'The tale ended, but another began.' Subordination: 'Although the tale ended, another began immediately.' Notice how subordination creates a different emphasis.
