📜 LIFE ON THE ISLAND

When Tom woke in the morning, he couldn't remember where he was. He sat up and looked around. A cool gray dawn filtered through the trees. The forest was perfectly still. Dew sparkled on every leaf and blade of grass. A thin ribbon of smoke rose straight up from the dying campfire. Then the woods began to wake up. A bird called. Another answered. A woodpecker hammered somewhere in the distance. A little green worm crawled onto Tom's leg, and he sat stone-still, watching it, because if a measuring worm walks all the way across you, it means you're going to get new clothes. Gradually the forest came alive—ants marching, a catbird singing, a blue jay flashing through the trees, a squirrel chattering at the boys from a branch. The sun broke through the canopy like golden spears. Tom shook the other two pirates awake, and within minutes they were all stripped down and splashing in the shallow water of the sandbar, laughing and ducking each other. No one missed the little village sleeping in the distance across the wide water. They came back to camp starving and got the fire roaring again. Huck found a cold spring nearby. Tom and Joe went to the riverbank with hooks and lines, and within minutes they had enough bass and catfish for a feast. They fried the fish with bacon and declared it the best breakfast in history. After the meal, they explored the island. It was about three miles long and a quarter mile wide, separated from the far shore by a narrow channel. They swam every hour or so, and by afternoon they knew the island like the back of their hands. But as the afternoon wore on and the excitement faded, a strange heaviness crept over them. They stopped talking. They stopped playing. They sat looking across the water at the distant town and thought about things they didn't want to think about. Suddenly, a deep, dull boom echoed across the river. "What was that?" Joe whispered. "I don't know," said Tom. "It isn't thunder," said Huck. They crept to the shore and peered through the bushes. The town's small ferryboat was in the river about a mile below the village. People crowded its deck. Then a great puff of white smoke burst from the boat's side, and after a pause, the same heavy boom rolled across the water. "I know!" Tom cried. "Somebody's drowned! They shoot a cannon over the water—it's supposed to make the body come up." "That's right," said Huck. "They did the same thing last summer when Bill Turner drowned." "I wonder who it is," said Joe. They watched the boat go back and forth. Then, slowly, it dawned on Tom. "Boys," he said, "I know who drowned. It's us!" They felt like heroes. They were missed! They were mourned! People were crying over them! They were the talk of the whole town! It was glorious. As night fell, the ferryboat gave up and went home. The pirates returned to camp, puffed up with pride. They caught more fish, cooked supper, and spent the evening imagining what the whole village must be saying about them. But when the fire burned low and the darkness closed in, the pride faded. Tom and Joe couldn't stop thinking about certain people at home who were not having a good time right now. They grew quiet and miserable. Joe timidly suggested that maybe they could go home—not right now, but maybe— Tom shut him down instantly. "Go home? A pirate doesn't go home! We'd be laughed at!" But later that night, after Joe and Huck had fallen asleep, Tom lay awake thinking. Carefully, quietly, he wrote something on two pieces of sycamore bark. He put one in his pocket and one in Joe's hat. Then he crept out of camp and disappeared into the night.

📝 Grammar: Active and Passive Voice: Strategic Use

Active voice emphasizes the doer: 'Scheherazade told stories.' Passive voice emphasizes the action or receiver: 'Stories were told by Scheherazade.' Passive voice is useful when the doer is unknown, unimportant, or when you want to shift focus. Overusing passive voice weakens writing.

Example:

Active: 'The king executed the women.' Passive: 'The women were executed' (focuses on victims, not perpetrator). Sometimes passive voice is more appropriate for the meaning you want to convey.
