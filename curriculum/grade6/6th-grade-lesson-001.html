📜 TOM AND AUNT POLLY (Part 1)

"Tom!" No answer. "Tom!" Still nothing. "Where has that boy gone? You, Tom!" The old woman pulled her glasses down and peered over them, scanning the room. Then she pushed them up and looked underneath them. She almost never looked through them—they were her fancy pair, meant for style, not for actually seeing anything. She could have seen just as well through a pair of oven doors. She looked annoyed but not truly angry. "Well, I swear, if I catch you, I'll—" She didn't finish, because she was already on her hands and knees, jabbing a broom handle under the bed. She found nothing but the cat. "I have never seen a boy like this one!" She marched to the open door and looked out at the tangled garden—tomato vines, weeds growing every which way. No Tom. She took a deep breath and hollered toward the distance: "Y-o-u-u, Tom!" A small noise came from behind her. She spun around just in time to grab a boy by the back of his shirt before he could escape. "There you are! What have you been doing in that closet?" "Nothing." "Nothing! Look at your hands. And your mouth. What is that stuff?" "I don't know, Aunt Polly." "Well, I know. It's jam. I have told you forty times—if you don't leave that jam alone, I will skin you alive. Hand me that switch." The switch hovered in the air. Things looked bad— "My! Look behind you, Aunt!" The old woman spun around, grabbing at her skirts. In that instant, the boy bolted. He scrambled up the tall board fence and vanished over the other side. His Aunt Polly stood there a moment, surprised, and then laughed softly. "That boy! Can't I ever learn? He's played that same trick on me enough times that I ought to know better. But he never does it the same way twice, so how am I supposed to keep up? He knows exactly how far he can push me before I lose my temper, and he knows if he can make me laugh, I can't bring myself to punish him. I'm not doing right by that boy, and that's the honest truth. But he's my dead sister's child, and I haven't got the heart to whip him. Every time I let him off, my conscience hurts me, and every time I do punish him, my old heart nearly breaks. Well—he'll skip school this afternoon, so I'll have to make him work tomorrow as punishment. Making him work on a Saturday, when all the other boys are free—that's the hardest thing in the world for him. But I've got to do my duty, or I'll be the ruin of that child." Tom did skip school, and he had a fine time doing it. He got home barely in time to help Jim, the hired boy, chop wood for supper—or at least he was there in time to tell Jim about his adventures while Jim did most of the work. Tom's younger half-brother, Sid, was already done with his chores. Sid was a quiet boy who never caused trouble and never looked for any. While Tom ate supper and snuck sugar whenever he got the chance, Aunt Polly asked him tricky questions, trying to catch him in a lie. She fancied herself a clever detective. She said: "Tom, it was pretty warm in school today, wasn't it?" "Yes ma'am." "Very warm, wasn't it?" "Yes ma'am." "Didn't you want to go swimming?" A flicker of alarm crossed Tom's face. He studied his aunt's expression, but she gave nothing away. "No ma'am—well, not too much." Aunt Polly reached out and felt his shirt. "But you don't seem too warm now." She was pleased with herself for checking whether the shirt was dry without anyone knowing that was her plan. But Tom was onto her, so he got ahead of the next move. "Some of us pumped water on our heads at school. Mine's still a little damp. See?" Aunt Polly was annoyed she'd missed that detail. Then she had a new idea: "Tom, you didn't have to undo the collar I sewed shut to pump water on your head, did you? Unbutton your jacket!" Tom opened his jacket. His shirt collar was securely sewn shut. "Hmph! All right, go on. I was sure you'd skipped school and gone swimming. But I forgive you, Tom. I suppose you're better than you look." She was half sorry her detective work had failed and half glad that Tom had actually behaved for once. But then Sid spoke up. "Funny—I thought you sewed that collar with white thread, Aunt Polly. But it's black." "Why, I did sew it with white! Tom—" But Tom was already out the door. "I'll get you for that, Sid," he called back. In a safe spot, Tom examined two needles stuck into his jacket—one threaded with white, one with black. He'd switched the thread before coming home. "She'd never have noticed if Sid hadn't opened his big mouth," Tom muttered. "Sometimes she uses white thread and sometimes black. I wish she'd pick one and stick with it. But I will get even with Sid." Tom was not the model boy of the village. He knew who the model boy was, though—and hated him. Within two minutes, Tom had forgotten every one of his troubles. Not because they were small troubles—they felt just as heavy to him as a grown man's worries feel to a grown man. But a new interest had pushed everything else aside. He'd just learned a new way of whistling from one of the older boys in town, and he was dying to practice it. The summer evening was long, and it wasn't dark yet. As Tom walked down the street, whistling happily, he spotted a stranger—a boy about his own size. A newcomer in the small village of St. Petersburg was a rare and impressive sight. This boy was well-dressed, too, which was downright amazing for a weekday. He wore a neat cap, a buttoned-up jacket, nice pants, and actual shoes—on a Friday! He even had a necktie. Everything about him screamed city boy. The fancier this stranger looked, the shabbier Tom felt. Neither boy spoke. They circled each other like cats. Finally Tom said, "I can lick you." "I'd like to see you try." "Well, I can do it." "No you can't." "Yes I can." "Can't." "Can!" There was an uncomfortable pause. Tom said, "What's your name?" "None of your business." "I'll make it my business." "I dare you to try." "You think you're something special, don't you? What a stupid hat." "You can knock it off if you don't like it." "You're a liar." "You're another." "You're a fighting liar and too scared to back it up." "Get away from here!" "Go away yourself." "I won't." "I won't either." They stood nose to nose, each with one foot braced, shoving with all their might. Neither could budge the other. Both were red-faced and sweating. Tom drew a line in the dirt with his toe. "Step over that line, and I'll fight you till you can't stand up." The new boy stepped over it immediately. "Now you said you would—so do it!" "Don't push me." "Well, do it!" "For two cents, I would." The new boy dug two pennies out of his pocket and held them out. Tom smacked them to the ground. In the next instant, both boys were rolling in the dirt, punching and pulling hair, covered in dust. When the dust settled, Tom was sitting on top of the new boy, hammering him with his fists. "Say uncle!" The boy struggled, crying—mostly from rage. "Say uncle!" At last the stranger choked out: "Uncle!" Tom let him up. "That'll teach you. Better watch who you mess with." The new boy slunk off, crying and threatening revenge. Tom jeered after him and started walking home, feeling like a champion. The moment Tom turned his back, the new boy snatched up a rock and hurled it, catching Tom right between the shoulder blades. Then he ran. Tom chased the boy all the way home and stood outside his gate for a long time, daring him to come out. But the boy only made faces through the window. Finally the boy's mother appeared, called Tom a wicked, vulgar child, and ordered him away. Tom got home late that night. When he climbed in through the window, he ran straight into an ambush: Aunt Polly was waiting. When she saw the state of his clothes, she made up her mind. His Saturday holiday was about to become a Saturday of hard labor—and there would be no getting out of it.

📝 Grammar: Sentence Structure: Simple, Compound, Complex, Compound-Complex

Learn:

Understanding the four sentence types is essential for sophisticated writing. Simple sentences have one independent clause. Compound sentences join two independent clauses with a coordinating conjunction. Complex sentences combine an independent clause with one or more dependent clauses. Compound-complex sentences have multiple independent clauses AND at least one dependent clause.

Example:

"Simple: 'The king listened.' Compound: 'The king listened, and Scheherazade spoke.' Complex: 'When Scheherazade spoke, the king listened.' Compound-Complex: 'When Scheherazade spoke, the king listened, and the night passed quickly.'"
