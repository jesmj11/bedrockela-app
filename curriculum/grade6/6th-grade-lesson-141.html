📜 THE PRINCESS ON THE MOUNTAIN

Princess Irene was eight years old, and she lived in a castle on the side of a mountain.

It was not a grand, glittering castle like the ones in fairy tales. It was a large, old, stone house — part fortress, part farmhouse — built into the rocky slope above a mining town. The town had no name that anyone remembered. People just called it "the town at the mountain," and the mountain had no name either, except among the miners, who called it the King's Mountain because the king owned everything underneath it.

Irene's father, the king, did not live in the mountain house. He was far away, governing the kingdom from the capital city, and Irene saw him only a few times a year. She lived with her nurse, Lootie, and a handful of servants who looked after her, fed her, and kept her safe.

Safe from what? Irene didn't know exactly. She only knew that she was never allowed outside after dark, and that when the sun began to set, Lootie would hurry her inside with a nervous look over her shoulder, as if the shadows themselves were dangerous.

📜 THE PRINCESS ON THE MOUNTAIN (Part 2)

"Why can't I stay out?" Irene would ask.

"Because it isn't safe, princess. That's all you need to know."

Irene was a good-natured child — trusting, curious, and braver than she looked. She had dark hair, bright eyes, and a way of asking questions that made adults uncomfortable, because her questions were often the ones nobody wanted to answer.

The mountain house was enormous and mostly empty. Dozens of rooms stood unused, their doors shut, their furniture covered in white sheets like sleeping ghosts. Irene had explored many of them during the long, rainy days when she couldn't go outside. She knew every corridor on the lower floors, every creaking step on the main staircase, every window seat where she could curl up with a book and watch the rain.

📜 THE PRINCESS ON THE MOUNTAIN (Part 3)

But the upper floors were different. They were older, dustier, and more confusing — a maze of narrow hallways and unexpected staircases that seemed to lead nowhere. Irene had been told not to go up there alone.

She went anyway.

📝 Grammar: Subjunctive Mood for Hypotheticals and Wishes

The subjunctive mood expresses wishes, suggestions, or hypothetical situations. It often uses 'were' instead of 'was': 'If I were the king...' Common in formal writing after verbs like wish, suggest, recommend: 'I wish she were here.' 'I suggest that he study harder' (not 'studies').

Example:

Subjunctive: 'If Scheherazade were to fail, she would die.' 'The king wished that his wife were faithful.' Indicative (not subjunctive): 'Scheherazade was clever.'
