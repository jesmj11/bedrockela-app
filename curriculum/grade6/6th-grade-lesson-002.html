📜 THE WHITEWASHING

Saturday morning came, bright and golden. The world was alive with summer—birds singing, flowers blooming, the sweet smell of blossoms in the air. Cardiff Hill rose green and inviting beyond the village, looking like a picture from a dream. And there stood Tom on the sidewalk with a bucket of whitewash and a long-handled brush. He stared at the fence. It was thirty yards long and nine feet high. All the happiness drained out of him. Life felt pointless. He sighed, dipped the brush, painted a single plank, then did it again. He looked at the tiny white streak compared to the vast unpainted fence, and sat down on a box, completely defeated. Jim came through the gate, singing on his way to fetch water from the town pump. Tom had always thought fetching water was miserable work, but it didn't look so bad now—at least there would be other kids at the pump to talk to. "Hey Jim, I'll get the water if you'll whitewash a little." Jim shook his head. "Can't, Mister Tom. Your aunt told me to fetch this water and not fool around with anybody. She said she figured you'd try to get me to whitewash, and she told me to mind my own business. She said she'd handle the whitewashing herself." "Oh, never mind what she said. She always talks like that. Give me the bucket—I'll only be a minute. She won't ever know." "I don't dare. She'd knock me sideways." Tom tried a white marble, his sore toe, and every trick he could think of. Jim was weakening—but then Aunt Polly appeared out of nowhere, smacked Jim with her slipper, and sent him running. Tom went back to whitewashing, and Aunt Polly marched away, satisfied. Tom's energy didn't last long. He started thinking about all the fun things he'd planned for today, and his mood sank lower. Soon the free boys would come walking past on their way to all kinds of adventures, and they would laugh at him for having to work. The thought burned like fire. He pulled out everything he owned—bits of toys, marbles, and junk. Not nearly enough to buy even half an hour of freedom from another boy. And then, at this dark moment, a magnificent idea hit him. A brilliant, world-class idea. He picked up his brush and went calmly back to work. Ben Rogers appeared around the corner—the very boy whose teasing Tom had dreaded most. Ben was prancing along, eating an apple, and pretending to be a steamboat. He was making engine noises, shouting orders to imaginary crewmen, and having a grand time. Tom kept painting. He didn't even look up. Ben stared. "Ha! You're stuck working, aren't you!" Tom turned, as if surprised. "Oh, it's you, Ben. I didn't notice." "I'm going swimming. Don't you wish you could come? But I guess you'd rather work, huh?" Tom studied the boy for a moment and said, "What do you call work?" "What do you mean? Isn't that work?" Tom went back to painting, nice and slow, like an artist. "Well, maybe it is, and maybe it isn't. All I know is, it suits Tom Sawyer." "Oh, come on. You don't mean you actually like it?" The brush kept moving. "Like it? Well, why shouldn't I? Does a boy get a chance to whitewash a fence every day?" That put everything in a new light. Ben stopped chewing his apple. Tom swept his brush carefully back and forth—stepped back to examine the result—added a touch here, fixed a spot there. Ben watched every move, getting more and more fascinated. Finally he said: "Hey Tom, let me whitewash a little." Tom thought about it, then shook his head. "No, I'd better not. Aunt Polly is real particular about this fence—it's right out front, you know. If it was the back fence, I wouldn't care. But this one has to be done just right. I bet there isn't one boy in a thousand who could do it the way it needs to be done." "Oh, come on, let me try. I'll be careful. I'll give you the core of my apple." "Well—no, Ben, I'm afraid—" "I'll give you all of it!" Tom handed over the brush with a show of reluctance but joy in his heart. And while Ben worked and sweated in the sun, Tom sat in the shade on a barrel, munching the apple and planning his next move. There was no shortage of victims. Boys came by every little while. They came to mock, but they stayed to whitewash. By the time Ben was worn out, Tom had traded the next turn to Billy Fisher for a kite. When Billy was done, Johnny Miller bought a turn for a dead rat on a string. And so it went, hour after hour. By mid-afternoon, Tom had gone from a boy with nothing to a boy rolling in treasure. He had: twelve marbles, a piece of blue glass, a tin soldier, a couple of tadpoles, six firecrackers, a one-eyed kitten, a brass doorknob, a dog collar (but no dog), the handle of a knife, and four pieces of orange peel. And the fence had three coats of whitewash on it. Tom had discovered a great law of human nature: in order to make someone want something, all you have to do is make it hard to get.

Answer these questions about today's chapter:

📝 Language: Context Clues: Multiple Strategies Combined

Expert readers use multiple context clue types simultaneously: definition/restatement (author defines it), synonym/antonym (similar/opposite word nearby), example (specific instances given), inference (reasoning from situation). Look for signal words: 'or,' 'such as,' 'unlike,' 'however.'

Example:

From Arabian Nights: 'The king was consumed by a rage that was also grief—unlike simple anger, this sorrow-fury combination destroyed his judgment.' Multiple clues: definition ('sorrow-fury'), contrast ('unlike simple anger'), and effect (destroyed judgment).
