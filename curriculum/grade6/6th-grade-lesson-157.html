📜 CURDIE DOESN'T BELIEVE

They sat on the hillside in the moonlight, catching their breath, and Irene told Curdie everything — the grandmother, the spinning wheel, the thread, the ring.

Curdie listened. His face was polite, but his eyes were skeptical.

"I believe that you believe it," he said carefully. "But I didn't see any thread. I didn't see any grandmother. You found me because you're brave and clever, not because of magic."

Irene felt a sharp pain in her chest — not anger, but disappointment. She had risked her life to save him, guided by something she trusted more than anything in the world, and he didn't believe her.

📜 CURDIE DOESN'T BELIEVE (Part 2)

"Come with me," she said. "I'll take you to the grandmother. You can see for yourself."

They climbed to the castle and up the stairs. Irene led Curdie through the hallways, found the door, climbed the winding staircase — and opened the door to an empty room.

No grandmother. No spinning wheel. No warm glow. Just bare stone and dust.

"She was here," Irene whispered.

📜 CURDIE DOESN'T BELIEVE (Part 3)

Curdie said nothing. He didn't mock her. He didn't call her a liar. He simply looked at the empty room and said, "I believe you're brave, Princess. That's enough for me."

But it wasn't enough for Irene. She knew the grandmother was real. She knew the thread was real. And she knew that someday, Curdie would believe it too.

The question was whether that day would come soon enough.

📝 Language: Figurative Language: Metaphor, Simile, Personification, Symbolism

Figurative language creates meaning beyond literal words. Metaphors assert identity: 'Her words were weapons.' Similes compare with 'like/as': 'Words like weapons.' Personification gives human qualities to non-human things: 'The story grabbed him.' Symbolism: objects representing ideas (Scheherazade's stories = hope, power of narrative).

Example:

Metaphor: 'The king's heart was stone.' Simile: 'His heart was like stone.' Personification: 'Anger consumed him.' Symbol: 'The thousand nights represent the slow process of healing and transformation.'
