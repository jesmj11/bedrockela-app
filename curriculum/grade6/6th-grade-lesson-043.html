📜 LITTLE JOHN

Robin was walking alone through the forest one morning when he came to a narrow bridge over a rushing stream — just a single log, wide enough for one person to cross at a time. On the other side stood the biggest man Robin had ever seen. He was nearly seven feet tall, broad as a barn door, with arms like tree limbs and a quarterstaff that looked like a young oak. His name, Robin would later learn, was John Little — though everyone would soon call him Little John, as a joke that never got old. "Step aside," Robin called. "I mean to cross this bridge." "Step aside yourself," the big man replied. "I was here first." Neither moved. Robin grinned. The stranger didn't. "I'll tell you what," Robin said. "We'll settle this the old-fashioned way. Quarterstaves. Whoever knocks the other into the stream gets the bridge." The big man shrugged. "Suits me. But you're half my size. It hardly seems fair." Robin cut himself a staff from a nearby oak — six feet of solid wood — and they met in the middle of the log bridge, balancing over the rushing water. They fought for nearly half an hour. Robin was faster, darting in with quick strikes, dancing back before the big man could land a blow. But Little John was immovable — every hit Robin landed felt like striking a stone wall. And when Little John swung, the air whistled. One massive blow caught Robin across the ribs. He staggered, slipped, and tumbled into the stream with a tremendous splash. He came up sputtering, soaked from head to toe, his dignity considerably damaged. The big man looked down at him from the bridge with a faint smile. Robin burst out laughing. "Well fought!" he said, hauling himself out of the water. "I haven't been beaten in years. What's your name?" "John Little." "Well, John Little, how would you like to join a band of merry outlaws? We live in the greenwood, we rob from the rich, and we eat better than most lords." John thought about it for approximately two seconds. "I'm in." Robin blew his hunting horn — three short blasts — and within minutes, his men appeared from the trees like green phantoms. They welcomed John Little with a feast, renamed him Little John, and from that day forward, he was Robin's closest friend and most trusted companion. He never lost another fight on a bridge. Mostly because no one was foolish enough to challenge him.

📝 Grammar: Semicolons, Colons, and Dashes for Emphasis

Semicolons join closely related independent clauses: 'She was not afraid; she was determined.' Colons introduce lists, explanations, or quotes: 'She needed one thing: time.' Dashes create emphasis or mark interruptions: 'Her plan—dangerous but necessary—might work.'

Example:

Semicolon: 'The night ended; the story continued.' Colon: 'Scheherazade understood one truth: stories change minds.' Dash: 'The king—once kind, now cruel—listened carefully.'
