📜 THE TRIAL

The trial of Muff Potter consumed the entire village. It was all anyone talked about. Tom couldn't escape it—every conversation, every rumor, every whispered opinion was like a needle in his conscience. He pulled Huck to a private spot to make sure they were still in agreement. "Huck, have you told anybody?" "Told anybody about what?" "You know what." "Of course I haven't. Not one word. Why?" "I was just making sure. I'm scared, Huck." "Me too. If Injun Joe ever found out—we wouldn't last two days." They swore their oath again, just to be safe. The trial dragged on for days. Witness after witness testified against Muff Potter. They described finding the knife, seeing Potter wash blood off himself in the middle of the night, his suspicious behavior. Potter's own lawyer barely asked a single question. The courtroom grew restless—did the defense attorney plan to just give up? Tom and Huck haunted the edges of the courthouse, drawn by the same horrible fascination. Every day, they heard worse news. The evidence was piling up. The verdict seemed certain. On the last day of the trial, the courtroom was packed. Potter sat in chains, pale and broken, his eyes empty. Injun Joe sat nearby, calm and expressionless as stone. The prosecution rested its case. Potter's lawyer stood and said something that shocked the room: "Call Thomas Sawyer to the stand." Every eye turned to Tom as he walked forward. He looked terrified. He was terrified. He took the oath and sat down. "Thomas Sawyer, where were you on the night of June seventeenth, around midnight?" Tom looked at Injun Joe's face—hard, dark, and still as iron. His tongue wouldn't work. The audience leaned forward. Silence stretched like a wire about to snap. Finally, Tom managed to push words out, barely above a whisper: "In the graveyard." "A little louder, please. Where were you?" "In the graveyard." Injun Joe's eyes flickered—just slightly. "Were you near the grave of Hoss Williams?" "Yes, sir." "How near?" "As near as I am to you right now." "Were you hidden?" "Yes, sir. Behind the elm trees." "Was anyone with you?" "Yes, sir." "Tell us everything that happened. Tell it in your own way. Don't skip anything." Tom began slowly, haltingly. But as the story poured out of him, his voice grew stronger. Every person in that courtroom stopped breathing. They sat frozen, mouths open, eyes fixed on this boy who was describing, in vivid and terrible detail, everything that had happened in the graveyard that night. "—and as the doctor swung the headboard and hit Muff Potter, Injun Joe jumped up with the knife and—" CRASH! Injun Joe hurled himself at the nearest window. Glass exploded outward. Before anyone could move, he was through it and gone—running, leaping over a fence, disappearing into the night. The courtroom erupted into chaos. But Muff Potter was free. And Tom Sawyer was a hero—again.

📝 Grammar: Appositives and Appositive Phrases

An appositive renames or explains a noun right beside it, usually set off with commas: 'Scheherazade, the vizier's daughter, had a plan.' Appositives add detail efficiently without needing separate sentences. Essential appositives (no commas) are rare: 'My sister Maria' (if you have multiple sisters).

Example:

Nonessential: 'The king, a broken man, listened to the tale.' Essential: 'The character Ali Baba discovered treasure.' (if there are other characters to distinguish from)
