📜 EXPLORING THE ISLAND

Fritz and I set out on the first expedition, leaving Elizabeth, Ernest, and the younger boys to guard the camp and organize supplies. We followed the stream inland, pushing through thick vegetation that closed over our heads like a green tunnel. The forest was alive with sound — birds calling, insects humming, leaves rustling in the warm breeze. I saw plants I recognized from my studies: sugarcane, cotton bushes, and what appeared to be a grove of rubber trees. Others were completely foreign to me. Fritz, who had a natural instinct for the wild, moved ahead with his musket ready. He was the best shot in the family and the most comfortable in rough terrain. He reminded me of a young soldier — alert, focused, and slightly too eager for danger. After an hour of walking, the forest opened onto a sight that took my breath away. A vast meadow stretched before us, green and rolling, dotted with clusters of trees. A river wound through the center, wider and deeper than the stream we had followed. Beyond the meadow, the land rose sharply into hills covered with dense forest, and beyond the hills, the island's central peak — a rocky mountain wreathed in clouds. "This isn't a small island," Fritz said, shading his eyes. "This is a whole country." He was right. The island was far larger than I had expected — miles across in every direction, with varied terrain: beaches, forests, meadows, hills, and what appeared to be marshy ground near the river's mouth. It could support a community, not just a family. On our way back, we made two important discoveries. The first was a grove of enormous trees — a species I would later identify as a type of tropical fig — with trunks thirty feet in circumference and massive branches that spread out like platforms high above the ground. They were the largest trees I had ever seen. The second discovery was less pleasant. Fritz spotted something in the underbrush — a track in the soft earth. It was a paw print, deep and wide, with the clear marks of claws. Something large lived on this island. Something with claws. We said nothing about it to Franz.

📝 Language: Word Roots: Greek and Latin Foundations

Most academic vocabulary comes from Greek and Latin roots. Common roots: 'aud' (hear), 'dict' (speak), 'spec' (see), 'port' (carry), 'graph' (write), 'bio' (life), 'chrono' (time), 'geo' (earth). Knowing roots unlocks hundreds of words.

Example:

'Spectator' (one who sees), 'inspect' (look into), 'retrospect' (look back). Same root, different prefixes change meaning. 'Biography' (writing about life), 'biology' (study of life)—same root, different suffixes.
